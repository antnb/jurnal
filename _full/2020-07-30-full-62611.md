<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Abstract*</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">This research discusses Indonesian language interference that occurs in Japanese essays written by SMK Penerbangan Cakra Nusantara students. This research aims to describe the types of interference found in the form of sentences in students' essays. This research used qualitative descriptive. The subject of this research are students of XII APP 1 class, meanwhile the object of this research are interference sentences in Japanese essays. The data was collected through note taking technique. The data was analysed using a theory proposed by Miles and Hubberman by performing data reduction, analysing data, and making conclusions. The results of this research are 1) the types of interference that occured based on linguistic aspects are morphological and syntactic interference; and 2) syntactic interference occured in three types of interference such as permutation of core constituents (I) and modifier (M), permutation of predicate (P) and object (O), and ommision of particles (</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">joshi</span><span class="font1">).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Introduction</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">As a language learner, especially a foreign language, people often make mistakes caused by the influence of their L1. The L1 greatly affected the learners because they are equating the rules of the L2 with their L1. The process of using different languages interchangeably by the same speaker makes a language contact situation. Through language contact, there is influence between the L1 and the L2 or vice versa, it will be easier if the structure of the L1 and L2 has many similarities or can inhibit the learning of the L2 if the structure of the L1 and L2 is very different. The different structures between the L1 and the L2 can lead to errors in the use of the second language that is referred as interference.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Language interference according to Weinreich (1970) is a form of deviation in the use of language from existing norms as a result of language contact or recognition of more than one language and used interchangeably by its speakers. According to Kridalaksana (2001) interference is a language error in the form of the language elements brought into the language or other dialects they learned.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Interference is a deviation in bilingualism in the L2 learning stage. Interference occurs due to the dominance of the L1 system that influences the use of L2 in events of communication, emotions, sensitivity, and attitudes of the speakers (Mustikawati, 2013). Interference can be</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">found in all components of language, it means that interference events can occur in phonology, morphology, syntax, lexicon, and semantics (Suwito, 1983: 55).</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Jendra (1991) divides interference into five based on linguistic aspects.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">1) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Phonological interference is interference that occurs in sound systems for example Javanese speakers who pronounce names starting with / b /, / d /, / g / and / j / like bandung becomes /mbandUŋ/.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">2) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Morphological interference is interference in word formation including the use of affixes.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">3) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Syntactic interference is interference in sentence structure.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">4) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Lexicon interference is interference that occurs in vocabulary in various forms such as in basic words, levels of groups of words, or phrases.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">5) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Semantic interference is interference that occurs in the level of meaning. Semantic interference divided into three (1) expansion semantics, (2) addition semantics, (3) replacement semantics.</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Interference often occurs in someone who learn a foreign language. The interference of the Indonesian language into the use of Japanese is found in the essays of senior high school students who study Japanese. The example is the phrase </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">neko no oneesan</span><span class="font2"> which is supposed to be </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">oneesan no neko</span><span class="font2"> which means ‘cat (belonging) brother’. There is a permutation between the controlled named head (H) and the being controlled named modifier (M) due to the influence of the Indonesian phrase structure. Indonesian phrase structure is the head (H) always precedes the modifier (M), while the phrase in Japanese the modifier (M) always precedes the head (H) (Sutedi, 2003). This interference is the type of syntactic interference.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Interference often occurs in speaking and writing skills. This is because speaking and writing are the active productive skill which is to produce expressions verbally or in writing (Sudipa, 2011). Thus, interference often found in these two language skills. Between these two language skills, writing skill is the focus of the research because the interference that students made often found in writing rather than speaking.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The research about language interference was conducted by Wulandari (2017) entitled Interference in &quot;The Use of Japanese Souvenir Traders in the Ubud Market&quot;. The results of the research show that there are three types of Indonesian language interference into Japanese languange by sellers in the Ubud market. The type of the interference are phonological interference, morphological interference, and lexical interference. Phonological interference occurs in </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">chouon</span><span class="font2"> (long vowels), </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">youon</span><span class="font2"> (adding smaller version of ya, yu or yo), </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">sokuon</span><span class="font2"> (double consonant), nasal consonant, and consonant addition. Morphological interference occurs in reduplication of words, changes in form and reversal of sentence structure. Lexical interference occurs in borrowing English and Indonesian when promoting merchandise, making transactions and ending transactions. Research conducted by Wulandari (2017) is the interference in speaking skill, while this research is on writing skills.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Other research on language interference was conducted by Suliman (2014) entitled “The Interference of Mother Tongue/Native Language in One's English Language Speech Production”. The study was aimed to describe the factors that cause interference to students in Malaysia. The result from the research that students rely on translation method from the mother tongue in comprehending certain instructions apart from producing utterances. The research conducted by Suliman (2014) is an interference in speaking skill. In addition, the research focuses on describing the factors causing interference in students who learn English while this research focuses on interference that occur in Japanese essays of SMK Penerbangan Cakra Nusantara students.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Other research on language interference was conducted by Thyab (2016) entitled &quot;Mother-Tongue Interference in the Acquisition of English Articles by L1 Arabic Students&quot;. The</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">research discusses the interference that occurs in Arab students who learn English, especially in the article system in English. The difficulty in learning English articles by Arabic L1 speakers is because their native language has an article system that functions differently. There are three types of the interference (1) omission of the definite article, (2) omission of the indefinite article, (3) wrong insertion of the definite article “the”</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">.</span><span class="font2"> The research focused on interference in the use of articles in English, while this study focuses on morphological and syntactic interference in Indonesian language in Japanese essays.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The previous research that has similar focus with this research is Gapur (2013) entitled “Interferensi Gramatikal Bahasa Indonesia dalam Pembelajaran Bahasa Jepang di Universitas Sumatera Utara”. The research discusses grammatical interference in students who learn Japanese language. The results obtained that there are four types of grammatical interference 1) interference (DM and MD) 40.62%, 2) ommision of particle (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">joshi</span><span class="font2">) 31.25%, 3) form of verb conjunctions 18.75%, and 4) grammatical interference S-K-O-P forms 9.38%.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The previous research can be used as a reference for this research. This research gives some information for teachers about deficiencies on aspects of writing Japanese essays found in students. Thus, the teacher can choose the best learning model or method or special technique that is suitable to solve the problem. Based on the problem, this research takes the topic of interference entitled “Indonesian Language Interference in Japanese Language Essays of SMK Penerbangan Cakra Nusantara Students”.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Methods</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">This research is a qualitative research with a qualitative descriptive approach. The subjects of the study were the 22 students of class XII APP 1 SMK Penerbangan Cakra Nusantara. The XII APP 1 class was chosen as the research subject since many Indonesian language interference in Japanese essay found in this class. The object of this research are interference sentences in Japanese essays.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The sentences which contain interference were taken from students' essays and collected in data table according to the types of interference. The data was collected through observation method and note taking technique. The results of students' essays were observed by taking note those sentences which contain the interference. The data was analysed using a theory proposed by Miles and Hubberman by performing data reduction, analysing data, and making conclusions.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Based on the theory of Miles and Hubberman (1984) data in the form of sentences that contain interference were collected then followed by data reduction process. Data reduction is done by summarizing, focusing the main points, and selecting important data to be analysed. The data presentation is done by describing a set of analysis descriptively and taking conclusions.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Informal method is used in this research to present the data analysis. The informal method is a method of presenting analysis using words and sentences in the form of discourse (Sudaryanto, 2015).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Discussions</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Based on the data collected through the note-taking technique, the sentences contain Indonesian language interference in Japanese essays from SMK Penerbangan Cakra Nusantara students are grouped into two types namely morphological and syntactic interference. Morphological and syntactic interference are described as follows.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">3.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Morphological Interference</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Morphological interference is interference in the formation of words including morphemes that form the words. Interference that found in students’ Japanese essays is verb morphology. There are </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gokan</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gobi</span><span class="font2"> in Japanese verb morphology. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Gokan</span><span class="font2"> is a morpheme that shows its lexical meaning, while </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gobi</span><span class="font2"> is a morpheme that shows its grammatical meaning (Sutedi, 2003). The final marker or </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gobi</span><span class="font2"> is connected behind the basic word is a very strong form that merge the basic word, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gobi</span><span class="font2"> is a marker of time, affirmation, and negation.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Japanese bound morphemes are also called auxiliary verb. This morpheme function is to give more meaning on the basis of verbs. For example, the verb in the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-masu</span><span class="font2"> form </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">tabemasu </span><span class="font2">means ‘eat’, {</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">tabe-</span><span class="font2">} is referred to as unchanged </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gokan</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">{-masu</span><span class="font2">} is the conjugated part called </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gobi</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Gobi</span><span class="font2"> {</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-masu</span><span class="font2">} can be conjugated according to the purpose of the speakers. For example, to explain the past activities ‘eat’ </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gobi</span><span class="font2"> {</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-masu</span><span class="font2">} was changed to {</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-mashita</span><span class="font2">} so that it became </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">tabe-mashita</span><span class="font2"> which means ‘eat (past)’. Examples of verbs form </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-masu</span><span class="font2"> are used because the verbs form which taught for high school students are the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-masu</span><span class="font2"> form. Here are morphological interference data found in students' essays.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(01) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Senshuu doubutsuen e </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">ikimasu</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(False)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Senshuu doubutsuen e </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">ikimashita.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(True)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘I went to the zoo last week’.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Morphological interference is shown in the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ikimasu</span><span class="font2"> which means ‘to go’. The adverb of time is explained by </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">senshuu</span><span class="font2"> which means ‘last week’, so that the verb should be turned into past form. The past form of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ikimasu</span><span class="font2"> verb is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ikimashita</span><span class="font2">. However, students wrote </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ikimasu</span><span class="font2"> which means the sentence still does not show past activities. The </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-masu</span><span class="font2"> form indicates the activities that will happen, while to explain past activities the verb should be changed to the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">– mashita</span><span class="font2"> form.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(02) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Kinou tomodachi wa uchi e </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">kimasu</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(False)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Kinou tomodachi wa uchi e </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">kimashita.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;(True)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘My friend came to my house yesterday’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Data (3-2) is also the type of morphological interference. In this sentence, morphological interference is indicated by the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kimasu</span><span class="font2"> which means ‘to come’. The interference found in conjugation of verb should be in past form. The adverb of the time is explained by the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kinou </span><span class="font2">which means 'yesterday' so that the verb should also be followed by past form. The </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gobi</span><span class="font2"> {</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-masu</span><span class="font2">} in the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kimasu</span><span class="font2"> should be changed to {</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-mashita</span><span class="font2">} so it will turn into </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kimashita</span><span class="font2"> which means ‘came’.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(03) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Yuube eiga o </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">mimasu.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(False)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Yuube eiga o </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">mimashita.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(True)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘I watched movie last night’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Morphological interference in data (3-3) is indicated by the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">mimasu</span><span class="font2"> which means ‘to watch’. The adverb of time is explained by </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">yuube</span><span class="font2"> which means 'last night', based on that the verb should follow the adverb of time into the past form. The past form of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">mimasu</span><span class="font2"> verb is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">mimashita</span><span class="font2">. In the sentence, it is written </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">mimasu</span><span class="font2"> which means the sentence still does not indicate past activity. The </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–masu</span><span class="font2"> form still indicates the activity that will happen, while to explain past activity the verb should be changed to </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–mashita</span><span class="font2"> form.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Based on these three data analyzed, it can be concluded that there was an Indonesian interference in student Japanese essays. The errors occurred because students are still influenced by the Indonesian system which does not need to change verbs into past form. Students explain</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">past activities only with adverb of time, meanwhile in Japanese explaining past activities should change the verb into the past form.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>3.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Syntactic Interference</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Syntactic interference is interference caused by the arrangement of L2 sentences that are influenced by the sentence structure of the L1. Syntactic interference occurs from the use of syntactic structure of the L1 while using the L2. </span><span class="font1">Syntax is one of grammar that discusses the formation of sentences, clauses, and phrases (Ramlan, 1987: 21). </span><span class="font2">Based on the theory, syntactic interference is found in sentences, clauses and phrases. In this research syntactic interference occured in sentences and phrases.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Typologically according to Tsujimura (1996) the structure of Japanese sentences is S-O-P (Subject-Object-Predicate), which is the predicate found at the end of the sentence. Japanese has S-O-P structure so that objects always followed by predicate, while Indonesian has an S-P-O (Subject-Predicate-Object) structure that is the predicate followed by the object (Alwi et al, 2003). Based on the statement, it can be seen that the sentence structure between Japanese and Indonesian has significant differences. The significant differences between Japanese and Indonesian are shown in the order of predicates and objects.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The significant differences cause errors in the order of Japanese sentences because they are still influenced by the structure of the Indonesian language. In addition, the pattern of phrase formation in Japanese is also different from Indonesian. The structure of Japanese phrases is the modifier (M) in front of the main phrase/head (H) (Koizumi, 1993). Modifier (M) is a word that explains, while the head (H) is the main of the phrase (e.g ‘red car’ phrase). In Japanese ‘red’ is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">akai</span><span class="font2"> while in Indonesian </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">merah</span><span class="font2">. In Japanese ‘car’ is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kuruma</span><span class="font2"> while in Indonesian </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">mobil</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">akai &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;kuruma</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(M) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(H) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(Japanese)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">mobil &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;merah</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(H) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(M) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(Indonesian)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">akai</span><span class="font2"> ‘red’ adjective as the attribute that describing the kuruma ‘car’ noun. The </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">akai </span><span class="font2">‘red’ attribute is used to explain the head constituent (H), </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kuruma</span><span class="font2"> ‘car’. From this description it can be concluded that the core phrase (H) in Japanese is in behind, while the formation of the Indonesian phrase is the core phrase (H) that is always in front of the modifier (M).</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Syntactic interference in student essays consists of three types: (1) permutation of core phrase (H) and modifiers (M), (2) permutations of predicate (P) and objects (O), and (3) omission of particles (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">joshi</span><span class="font2">). Here is the analysis based on the type of syntactic interference.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>3.2.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Permutation Main Phrase (H) dan </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Modifier</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> (M)</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Permutation of the core phrase (H) and modifier (M) is incorrect word order in the sentence. This type of interference is shown in the following data.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(04) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">Heya no watashi</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> wa kirei desu.</span><span class="font2"> (False)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">Watashi no heya</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> wa kirei desu.</span><span class="font2"> (True)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘</span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">My room</span><span class="font2"> is clean’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Watashi no &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;heya</span><span class="font2"> (Japanese)</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(M) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(H)</span></p></li></ul>

<p><span class="font2">In this phrase students are still influenced by Indonesian to express noun phrases by stating the main phrase (H) first and followed by a modifier (M). Based on this, to express 'my room' in Japanese it should be </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">watashi no heya</span><span class="font2">. From this data it can be seen clearly the influence of Indonesian in Japanese sentences. Another data also shows Indonesian language interference in Japanese essays, as follows.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(05) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Tsukue no ue ni </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">pen aoi</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> ga futatsu arimasu.</span><span class="font2"> (False) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Tsukue no ue ni </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">aoi pen</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> ga futatsu arimasu.</span><span class="font2"> (True) ‘There are two </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">blue pen</span><span class="font2"> on the table’</span></p>

This interference occurred because students are still influenced by the rules of phrase structure in Indonesian. Students translate directly from Indonesian into Japanese without noticing that the formation of phrases between these two languages is different.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>3.2.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Permutation Predicate (P) dan Object (O)</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Permutation of a predicate and object is an error in word order in sentence. In Indonesian the predicate always precedes the object, whereas in Japanese the object followed by the predicate. These differences caused mistakes in Japanese students' essays which are categorized in syntactic interference. Syntactic interference in student essays is shown in the following data.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(07) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Mai-asa </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">tabemasu o pan. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Mai-asa </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">pan o tabemasu. </span><span class="font2">‘I </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">eat bread</span><span class="font2"> every morning’ </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Pan &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;o &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;tabemasu</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">(Indonesian)</span></p>

<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(O) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(Part.) &nbsp;&nbsp;(P)</span></p></li></ul>

In that sentence has S-P-O structure that is the sentence structure of Indonesian, while the sentence structure in Japanese is S-O-P. That sentence should be in S-O-P structure. From the sentence we can see the existence of Indonesian language interference in Japanese sentence.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">From the data we can see that students are still influenced by the structure of Indonesian sentences. <p><span class="font2">Syntactic interferences in the form of permutations of predicates and objects are shown in </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">mimasu o terebi</span><span class="font2">. Each of the meanings of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">mimasu o terebi</span><span class="font2">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">mimasu</span><span class="font2"> means ‘to watch’, particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">o </span><span class="font2">as an object marker and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">terebi</span><span class="font2"> means ‘television’. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Mimasu</span><span class="font2"> in this sentence as predicate and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">terebi </span><span class="font2">as object.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The error is incorrect word order between the predicate and object. This is a form of error caused by students' L1. The position of predicates and objects in Japanese and Indonesian sentences is very different. Predicate precede objects in Indonesian, while Japanese objects precede predicate. Based on that, to state 'watching television' in correct Japanese is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">terebi o mimasu</span><span class="font2">. Based on the data presented, it can be seen that an error occurred in writing a Japanese essay, especially in this sentence, shows that the error occurred because students are still influenced by Indonesian. This is what is called language interference.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">3.2.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Omission of Particles (</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Joshi</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Particles in Japanese have important functions as auxiliary words in sentences. According to Iori (2003) particle (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">joshi</span><span class="font2">) is a word that cannot stand by itself, does not change and it is used to follow nouns, verbs and other word classes that have the function of helping and determining meaning, emphasis, questions, doubts, etc. in a Japanese sentence. Based on this statement the particle function in a sentence is very important and cannot be eliminated. Particle is also a special feature of Japanese from other languages.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Indonesian do not need particles to make a sentence. Japanese particles are something unusual for students. That is the causes interference found in sentences in Japanese students' essays. Here are the interferences of omission of particles (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">joshi</span><span class="font2">).</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(09) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Kesa 6 ji okimashita.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(False)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Kesa 6 ji </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">ni</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> okimashita.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(True)</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">‘I woke up this morning at 6 a.m’</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Interference in the sentence is indicated by the omission of the particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ni</span><span class="font2"> after the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ji</span><span class="font2">. It should be </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ni</span><span class="font2"> particle after word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ji</span><span class="font2">. Japanese particles have different functions. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Ni</span><span class="font2"> particle has functions to indicate the location or presence, declare time, show facing or heading or put in a direction or place and also shows the intended object (Iori, 2003). Based on this statement, it should be after 6 ji 'at 6' must be followed by </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ni</span><span class="font2"> particle to express the time, so that the appropriate statement to state ‘at 6’ in Japanese is 6 </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ji ni.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The omission of these particles is due to the influence of the Indonesian when making sentences in Japanese. Students do not fully understand the use of particles in Japanese and eliminate particles that should be in the sentence. It is because Indonesian sentences do not need particles. Here is another data that also shows the syntactic interference in the type of omission particle.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(10)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Mai-nichi gohan tabemasu.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(False)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Mai-nichi gohan </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">o</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> tabemasu.</span><span class="font2"> (True)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘I eat rice everyday’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Interference that found in the data is omission of particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">o</span><span class="font2"> after the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gohan</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Gohan </span><span class="font2">means 'rice' and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">tabemasu</span><span class="font2"> means 'to eat'. The function of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gohan</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">tabemasu</span><span class="font2"> are object and predicate in the sentence. The sentence needs particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">o</span><span class="font2"> as an auxiliary word to explain the object of verb. Based on data (3-10), students did not insert </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">o</span><span class="font2"> particles after the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gohan</span><span class="font2">. The missing particles is because students are not familiar with the Japanese language system. This is influenced by the Indonesian as their L1. There is no particle needed to explain the object from the verb. Based on this error, it is categorized as Indonesian interference in Japanese sentences.</span></p>

<h3><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Novelties</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">This study takes and contributes to applied linguistic especially in linguistic teaching and learning. This research gives some information for teachers about deficiencies on aspects of writing Japanese essays found in students. Therefore, the teacher can choose the best learning model or method or special technique that is suitable to solve the problem. From this research it can be found there are two types of interference based on the linguistic aspects, morphological and syntactic interference. Morphological interference was found in the form of conjugation of Japanese verbs into the past form. Syntactic interference was also found which are divided into three forms, (1) permutation of main phrase/head (H) and modifier (M), (2) permutation of predicate (P) and object (O), (3) omission of particles (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">joshi</span><span class="font2">).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Based on the results and discussion, it can be concluded that the Indonesian interference found in Japanese essays in SMK Penerbangan Cakra Nusantara students. There are two types of interference based on the linguistic aspects, morphological and syntactic interference. Morphological interference was found in the form of conjugation of Japanese verbs into the past form. Syntactic interference was also found and divided into three forms, (1) permutation of main phrase/head (H) and modifier (M), (2) permutation of predicate (P) and object (O), (3) omission of particles (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">joshi</span><span class="font2">). Syntactic interference is occurred more than morphological interference.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">This interference occurred because of the strong influence of the Indonesian when studying Japanese, the Indonesian system was applied in Japanese. The language rules between Indonesian and Japanese are so different so that the errors can affect the meaning in Japanese. For this reason, it is necessary to apply an appropriate learning model to reduce interference made by students.</span></p>
