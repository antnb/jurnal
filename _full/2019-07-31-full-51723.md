<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">Two steroids compounds, 7</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">α</span><span class="font5">-Hydoxy </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">β</span><span class="font5">-sitosterol (</span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">1</span><span class="font5">) and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">β</span><span class="font5">-sitosterol (</span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">2</span><span class="font5">), have been isolated from ethyl acetate extract of the fresh Super Red Dragon Fruit (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Hylocereus costaricensis</span><span class="font5">).The chemical structure of compounds </span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font5">and </span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font5">were identified by spectroscopic data including UV, IR, NMR-1D, NMR-2D and mass as well as by comparing with previously reported spectral data. Compounds </span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font5">and </span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font5">were reported for the first time from dragon fruit (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Hylocereus costaricensis</span><span class="font5">).</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">Keywords</span><span class="font5">: 7</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">α</span><span class="font5">-Hydoxy </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">β</span><span class="font5">-sitosterol, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">β</span><span class="font5">-sitosterol</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">, Hylocereus costaricensis</span><span class="font5">, steroids.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h1>
<p><span class="font6">Steroids is an important class of secondary metabolites, widely widespread in plants, animals, marines as well as fungi and have similarity to cholesterol in structure (Saeidnia et al., 2014), including β-sitosterol, campesterol, stigmasterol and cycloartenol (Ostlund, 2002). Sterols, especially β-sitosterol was reported to have interesting activity including anti-inflammatory (Prieto et al., 2006), inducing apoptosis (Chai et al., 2008; Park et al., 2007; Ju et al., 2004), chemoprotective or chemopreventive effects (Ovesna et al., 2004), hypocholesterolemic (Zak et al., 1990), angiongenic effect (Moon et al., 1999), anti-diabetic (Gupta et al., 2011;</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Jamaluddin et al., 1994; Radika et al., 2013), and anti-oxidant (Baskar et al., 2012; Vivancos and Moreno, 2005).</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Super Red Dragon fruit (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Hylocereus costaricensis</span><span class="font6">), known in Indonesia as “Naga Merah Super” is a promising tropical fruit which can be cultivated in different tropical and subtropical parts of the world such as Southeast Asia, and Central and South America. The demand for Super Red Dragon Fruit extensively increases and the fruit today can be found on almost all exotic fruit markets around the world (Salakpetch, 2000; Mohd, 2010).</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Previous phytochemical studies on the species of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">H. costaricensis</span><span class="font6"> have revealed the presence mostly of polyphenolic compounds</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">with interesting biological activities, including (Strack et al., 2003; Karamaae et al., 2006; Wong and Siow, 2015; Wybraniec et al., 2001).</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Although polyphenolic compounds of this species have been investigated previously, the steroid composition of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">H. costaricensis</span><span class="font6"> is yet to be reported. The isolation and structure identification of these isolated compounds are described herein.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">MATERIAL AND METHODS</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>General Experimental Procedure</span></h1>
<p><span class="font6">Melting points were measured on an electrothermal melting point apparatus and are uncorrected. The IR spectra were recorded on a Perkin-Elmer spectrum-100 FT-IR in KBr. Mass spectra were obtained with a Synapt G2 mass spectrometer instrument. NMR data were recorded on a Jeol ECZ-500 spectrometer at 500 MHz for <sup>1</sup>H and 125 MHz for <sup>13</sup>C, and TMS as internal standard. Column chromatography was conducted on silica gel 60 (Kanto Chemical Co., Inc., Japan). TLC plates were precoated with silica gel GF</span><span class="font2">254 </span><span class="font6">(Merck, 0.25 mm) and detection was achieved by spraying with 10% H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> in ethanol, followed by heating.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>Plant material</span></h1>
<p><span class="font6">The fresh fruit of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">H. costaricensis</span><span class="font6"> were collected in Plantation Plant at Cicalengka District, West Java Province, Indonesia in April 2017. The plant was identified by the Mr. Joko Kusmoro, staff of the Laboratory of Plant Taxonomy, Department of Biology, Universitas Padjadjaran and a voucher specimen was deposited at the herbarium.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Extraction and isolation</span></h1>
<p><span class="font6">The fresh fruit (25 kg) was extracted with methanol (30 L) at room temperature for 3 days. After removal of the solvent under vacuum, the concentrated of MeOH extract (120.5 g) was first suspended in H</span><span class="font2">2</span><span class="font6">O and then partitioned with </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font6">-hexane, EtOAc, and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font6">-BuOH, successively. Evaporation resulted in the crude extracts of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font6">-hexane (10.3 g), EtOAc (30.4 g), and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font6">-BuOH (21.6 g), respectively. The </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font6">-hexane soluble fraction (10.3 g) was fractionated by vacuum liquid chromatography on silica gel 60 using a gradient </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font6">-hexane and EtOAc to give nine fractions (A–I). Fraction A (6 g) was chromatographed on a column of</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">silica gel, eluted successively with a gradient of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font6">-hexane–CH</span><span class="font2">2</span><span class="font6">Cl</span><span class="font2">2 </span><span class="font6">(10:0–1:1) to give ten subfractions (A01–A10). Subfraction A03 was chromatographed on a column of silica gel, eluted with </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font6">-hexane:CHCl</span><span class="font2">3 </span><span class="font6">(9:1) to give </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font6">(12.4 mg). Fraction B (12.4 g) was fractionated by column chromatography on silica gel using a gradient </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font6">-hexane and EtOAc to give eight fractions (J–Q). Fraction K (927.6 mg) was chromatographed on a column of silica gel, eluted successively with a gradient of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font6">-hexane–EtOAc (10:0–0:10) to give </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font6">(10.2 mg).</span></p>

<p><span class="font6">showed the presence of six methyl groups, two tertiary methyl groups resonating at δ</span><span class="font2">H </span><span class="font6">1.15 (Me-18), 0.71 (Me-19), three secondary methyl groups resonating at δ<sub>H</sub> 0.90 (3H, d, </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font6"> = 6.2 Hz, Me-21), 0.86 (3H, d, </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font6"> = 6.5 Hz, Me-26), and 0.85 (d, </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font6"> = 5.2 Hz, Me-27), one primary methyl group resonating at δ<sub>H</sub> 1.81 (t, </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font6"> = 5.2 Hz, Me-29), which was indicate the presence of stigmastane-type steroid skeleton (Cayme and Ragasa, 2004, Farabi et al., 2017). The proton pairing was also confirmed with the <sup>1</sup>H-<sup>1</sup>H COSY spectrum (Figure 2). <sup>1</sup>H-<sup>1</sup>H COSY cross peak observed at H-2/H-3/H-4 indicated that position of hydroxy group at C-3. The cross peak also observed at H-6/H-7/H-8 that indicated the position of double bond at C-5/C-6. The <sup>13</sup>C-NMR (CDCl</span><span class="font2">3 </span><span class="font6">125 MHz) and HMQC and DEPT 135° spectra showed the presence of six methyl groups, one olefinic methine, one olefinic quartenary carbon, and a oxygenated methine group, resonating at δ<sub>C </sub>71.5 (C-3) and 74.5 (C-7), indicated the characteristic of stigmastane-type steroid (Cayme and Ragasa, 2004; Farabi et al., 2017). These functionalities accounted for one of total five degree of unsaturations. The remaining four degrees of unsaturation were consistent with the stigmastane-type steroid. Correlation of H-2 and H-4 to an oxygenated carbon at </span><span class="font0">δ</span><span class="font6"><sub>C </sub>71.5, indicated that hydroxyl group attached at C-3. Another hydroxyl group was located at C-7 based on the correlation of H-6 and H-8 to oxygenated carbon at C-7 (</span><span class="font0">δ</span><span class="font6"><sub>C</sub> 74.5). A comparison of the NMR data of </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font6">with the data for 7</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">α</span><span class="font6">-Hydroxy-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">β</span><span class="font6">-sitosterol (Chaturvedula and Prakash, 2012; Farabi et al., 2017), revealed that the structure of the two compounds were very similar, consequently compound </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font6">was identified as a 7</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">α</span><span class="font6">-Hydroxy-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">β</span><span class="font6">-sitosterol, which shown in this plant for the first time.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Compound </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font6">was obtained as a white needlelike crystal. The IR spectra showed absorption peaks at 3480 cm<sup>-1</sup> (OH), 2960 and 2865 cm<sup>-1 </sup>(aliphatic), 1472 cm<sup>-1</sup> (C=C), 1382 and 1238 cm<sup>-1</sup> (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">gem</span><span class="font6">-dimethyl groups), and 1040 cm<sup>-1 </sup>(C-O). The NMR spectra of </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font6">very similar with </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">1</span><span class="font6">, the main difference was that compound </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font6">the absence of hydroxyl group at [</span><span class="font0">δ</span><span class="font6"><sub>H</sub> 4.21 (1H, m, H-7), </span><span class="font0">δ</span><span class="font2">C </span><span class="font6">74.5] and the presence of the methylene signal at [</span><span class="font0">δ</span><span class="font2">H </span><span class="font6">2.56 (1H, m, H-7a),</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">2.72 (1H, m, H-7b), </span><span class="font0">δ</span><span class="font2">C </span><span class="font6">32.4], suggested that </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font6">is 7-dehydroxy derivative of </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">1</span><span class="font6">. In comparison of </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font6">with literature data of a stigmast-5-en-3β-ol (β-sitosterol) (Chaturvedula and Prakash, 2012; Harneti et al., 2014; Farabi et al., 2017), showed good agreement, therefore compound </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font6">was identified as a stigmast-5-en-3β-ol (β-sitosterol), which shown in this plant for the first time.</span></p>
