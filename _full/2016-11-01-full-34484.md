<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Abstrak</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Metafora dan simile adalah bagian dari bahasa kiasan yang biasanya dipakai dalam pecakapan sehari hari dan penulisan berbagai literatur. Dalam penerjemahan metafora dan simile yang dalam pemakaiannya dipengaruhi oleh kebudayaan bahasa setempat memiliki teknik khusus dalam proses pengalihan bahasanya. Penelitian ini bertujuan untuk menganalisis jenis-jenis Metafora dan Simile serta strategi yang di gunakan dalam process penerjemahan Metafora dan Simile di novel Laskar Pelangi karya Andrea Hirata. Data dikumpulkan melalui proses membaca dan mencatat lalu membandingkan bahasa sumber dan bahasa terjemahannya. Metafora dan Simile dibagi menjadi dua jenis yaitu Dead dan Live. Dalam proces penerjemahan terdapat lima teknik yang dapat dipakai untuk menerjemahkan Metafora dan simile yang di usulkan oleh Larson(1968). Kelima teknik tersebut dapat di gunakan dalam penerjemahan metafora dan simile selama pesan yang ingin di sampaikan dari bahasa sumber dapat tersampaikan dengan baik dan benar sesuai dengan konteks dan situasinya.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Kata Kunci: Penerjemahan, metafora, simile.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li><a name="caption1"></a>
<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Metaphor and simile which are a part of figurative language, often used in daily conversation and also literature. Metaphor and simile are used to compare two things. Metaphor is the comparing expression which uses the basic similarity of the first idea to the second idea implicitly. Simile is close to metaphor since it has the same function, that is, to compare things. However, simile compares something explicitly using the word “as” or “like”.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Every language usually has their own ways to use figurative language especially for metaphor and simile. This situation occurs because figurative language in general is influenced by the culture and social situation in which the language is spoken, For example, the metaphor such as “life is a fashion show” which becomes “hidup adalah</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">panggung sandiwara”; this sentence is not translated literally but it is translated by substituting the metaphor by a metaphor that has the same idea in SL.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In translation process, the difference of metaphor and simile form in each language could be a concern. In translating a translator needs to maintain the meanings that are transferred into the target language. Related to metaphor and simile, it is difficult to find the perfect equivalent meaning between two languages since every language has different culture; therefore, sometimes the translator should use words that are really different in lexical meaning. According to Larson through her book </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Meaning-Based Translation - A Guide to Cross-Language Equivalence</span><span class="font2">, In general there are 2 kinds of translation; we can translate SL literally or we can translate SL idiomatically based on the concept that is supposed to be transferred.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Method</span><br><br><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark8"></a>4.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Data Source</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The data to support this writing were taken from the novel by Andrea Hirata entitled </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Laskar Pelangi</span><span class="font2">. This novel is written in Indonesian language and translated into English language in 2013 </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">The Rainbow Troops</span><span class="font2"> by Angie Kilbane; both of those novels were published by Bentang. This novel was chosen because it contains language that reflects</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">the Malayan culture and the author uses many metaphors and similes in it. It was also chosen in order to investigate how metaphors and similes in Indonesian language are translated, and how the translator transferred the meaning naturally into English.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark9"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark10"></a>4.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Method and Technique of Collecting Data</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The method of library research was used in collecting the data. There are several techniques that used to collect the data. First, the data werre collected by reading intensively and focused on the metaphors and similes used in </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Laskar Pelangi</span><span class="font2"> and underlined the expressions found in the novel. Furthermore, the words or phrases that are metaphors and similes found in the source language and their translation equivalents were written down using the note taking technique. Then, the data between the Source Language and the Target Language were compared.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark11"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark12"></a>4.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Method and Technique of Analyzing Data</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">In this study, the qualitative method was applied. The data were listed, classified and analyzed descriptively. After collecting the data by reading and identifying or listing the metaphors and similes found in the novel </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Laskar Pelangi</span><span class="font2"> and its translation into English </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">The Rainbow Troops</span><span class="font2">; the data were classified based on Larson’s theory about the type of translating figurative language. Then, the data already collected were analyzed by comparing and identifying the techniques used to translate the source language. The other theory was proposed by Nida (1975) about loss of information, gain of information in translation were also used as the supporting theory.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark13"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark14"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Result and Discussion</span><br><br><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>5.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Metaphor and Simile in the Novel Laskar Pelangi</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Metaphor and simile as proposed by Larson has two types; Live and Dead. (Larson.1998:274). Live metaphor and simile are the metaphor and simile that are understood after paying attention to the context of the comparison being used; for the dead metaphor and simile we did not have to look for the context. Here are some examples of the Metaphor and simile found in the novel </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Laskar Pelangi</span><span class="font2"> novel.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font3"><a name="bookmark17"></a>•</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Dead Metaphor</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">:Ia adalah penyeimbang perahu kelas kami</span><span class="font2"> (Hirata,2005:139)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL :We now discovered that </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Mahar balanced out the ship of our school </span><span class="font2">(Kilbane, 2013:121)</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font3"><a name="bookmark19"></a>•</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Dead Simile</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Karena penamplan pak harfan agak seperti beruang madu, maka pertama kali kami melihatnya kami merasa takut.</span><span class="font2"> (Hirata,2005:21)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL: Because Pak Harfan looked quite like a grizzly bear, we were scared the first time we saw him. (Kilbane, 2013:24)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In the examples above, the metaphorical word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">perahu</span><span class="font2"> or ship as a methaphor and Beruang as simile are usually used in daily conversation. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Perahu</span><span class="font2"> is a common word that idiomatically refers to journey of life. The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">beruang madu</span><span class="font2"> is used in order to illustrate how </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">pak harfan</span><span class="font2"> looks like also automatically gives us the image of someone who looks big and scary. The image that comes into our mind when we read those figurative languages is not their primary meaning as ship and the grizzly bear but automatically the idiomatic meaning. Therefore, this metaphor and simile belong to dead metaphor and dead simile.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font3"><a name="bookmark21"></a>•</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Live Metaphor</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Aku menghampiri kotak merpati saat ia menjulurkan kapur</span><span class="font2"> (Hirata,2005:251) TL: I approached the pigeon cage door. She slid her hand out. (Kilbane, 2013:205)</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font3"><a name="bookmark23"></a>•</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Live Simile</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Pria itu berpotongan seperti pohon cemara angin yang mati karena di sambar petir, hitam, meranggas, kurus, dan kaku</span><span class="font2"> (Hirata,2005:10)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL:(He resembled a pine tree struck by lightning: black, withered, thin and stiff.) (Kilbane, 2013::10)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Kotak merpati</span><span class="font2"> which is translated into </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">pigeon cage door</span><span class="font2"> above is the metaphorical image; however, the meaning of the metaphor is a little bit vague and so is the image of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">pohon cemara angin yang mati karena di sambar petir</span><span class="font2"> or </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">a pine tree struck by lightning</span><span class="font2">. In order to understand such metaphorical images, we have to identify the</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">context of the novel. This kind of metaphor and simile is made by the author itself and belongs to the live metaphor and live simile</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark24"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark25"></a>5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Translation of Metaphors and Similes</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Larson proposed 5 ways to translate Metaphor and 3 ways to translate simile (the techniques for simile start from number 3.3.3). The analysis is as follows.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark26"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark27"></a>5.2.1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The metaphor may be kept if the receptor language permits</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Agaknya selama turun temurun keluarga </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">laki-laki cemara angin </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">itu….(Hirata, 2005:11)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL: Without a doubt, all previous generations of men from </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">this pine tree man</span><span class="font2">’s family…. (Kilbane, 2013:11)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">As long as the meaning of metaphor could be delivered to receptor language, literal translation could be applied. This translation process is the example of metaphor to metaphor translation with the same image, in which </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lelaki pohon cemara</span><span class="font2"> is translated literally as </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">pine tree man</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark28"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark29"></a>5.2.2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Metaphor translated as a Simile</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: Lintang adalah mercusuar. (Hirata, 2005:431)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL: Lintang was like a lighthouse. (Kilbane, 2013:411)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Sometimes in translating metaphor the translator chooses to explicitly express the comparison and changes the metaphor into simile. In this example, the Indonesian metaphor which literally means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Lintang is the flashlight</span><span class="font2"> was translated into </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lintang was like a flashlight</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark30"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark31"></a>5.2.3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Substitution</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">•</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Metaphor</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Kami adalah para duta besar </span><span class="font2">yang berkuasa penuh selama musim hujan (Hirata, 2005:171)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL: </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">We were all-powerful VIPs </span><span class="font2">during the rainy season. (Kilbane, 2013:140)</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">•</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Simile</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: Sekali lagi para pendukung kami berjingkat jingkat histeris </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">seperti doger monyet. </span><span class="font2">(Hirata, 2005: 382)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL: Our supporters jumped around </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">like dancing monkeys. </span><span class="font2">(Kilbane, 2013: 317)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In order to achieve the equivalent and acceptable translation the translator should sometimes change the metaphor into the other form which still has the same meaning in the receptor language. The metaphor </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Duta Besar</span><span class="font2"> above is translated using the substitution technique</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">. VIP</span><span class="font2"> has more broad meaning as it refers to someone who is really important; this word is usually used in daily conversation and sounds more natural. Then, for the simile</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">, Doger monyet</span><span class="font2"> is a metaphorical image that is used to compare the act of the very happy supporter who are Lintang and his team.. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Doger Monyet</span><span class="font2"> was translated into dancing monkey which is not the exact meaning but this word is the closest natural equivalent word. When this phrase was translated into </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Dancing Monkey</span><span class="font2"> it certainly loses some elements of the original word but it does not matter as long as it can express the meaning correctly to the reader.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark32"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark33"></a>5.2.4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Meaning Explain (Metaphor)</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">•</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Metaphors</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: Hal ini sedikit banyak berhubungan dengan urusan </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">cinta monyet. </span><span class="font2">(Hirata, 2005: 251)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL: My sudden change of heart more or less had to do with </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">cinta monyet</span><span class="font2">— monkey love, or puppy love. (Kilbane, 2013: 204)</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">•</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Simile</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: Rapor terakhir mereka memperlihatkan deretan angka merah </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">seperti punggung dikerok </span><span class="font2">. (Hirata, 2005: 402)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL: Red numbers lined their report card </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">like someone’s back had been dikerok</span><span class="font2">—scraped by a coin as part of a traditional massage. (Kilbane, 2013:386)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The metaphor above </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">cinta monyet</span><span class="font2"> also the simile that use the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Dikerok</span><span class="font2"> is quite difficult to translate and difficult to understand. Due to that reasons the translator put</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">some information by adding more information about the meaning of the metaphor itself. This additional information will help the reader to understand the word meaning of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">cinta monyet</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">dikerok</span><span class="font2"> easily.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark34"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark35"></a>5.2.5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Without keeping the imagery metaphor</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">•</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Metaphor</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: Tiba-tiba sekarang </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">dua ekor tornado </span><span class="font2">ini ingin bersekutu (Hirata, 2005: 356)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL: now </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">they </span><span class="font2">wanted to be united (Kilbane, 2013: 281)</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">•</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Simile</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">SL: Seorang perfeksionis berwajah </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">seindah rembulan. </span><span class="font2">(Hirata, 2005:74)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">TL: He was a perfectionist </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">with a most handsome face. </span><span class="font2">(Kilbane, 2013:61)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Every language uses a different way to use a metaphor, sometimes a translator translates a metaphor into its meaning to make it easier to understand. The image </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">dua ekor tornado</span><span class="font2"> that means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">two tornado tail</span><span class="font2"> was literally translated into </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">they</span><span class="font2"> by the translator. But, the translator translated it by changing the metaphor into its non-metaphorical meaning </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">they</span><span class="font2"> to avoid ambiguity. Also the simile, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">rembulan</span><span class="font2"> or </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">moon</span><span class="font2"> when it is being translated, the translator decided not to keep the metaphorical image in English. She translated directly into the meaning </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">a most handsome face</span><span class="font2"> to express the metaphor.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark36"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark37"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Metaphor and simile found in the novel </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Laskar Pelangi</span><span class="font2"> can be categorized into two groups; Dead and Live. Dead metaphor and dead simile are those which are a part of the idiomatic constructions of the lexicon of the language while Live Metaphor and live simile are those which are constructed on the spot by the author or speaker to teach or illustrate (Larson, 1998). Some metaphor and simile here have been discussed with the theory proposed by Larson about the technique of translating metaphor and simile. From 5 techniques mentioned by Larson; 1) The metaphor may be kept if the receptor language permits, 2) A metaphor may be translated as a Simile, 3) Substitution, 4) Meaning Explain, 5) Without keeping the imagery metaphor; the technique mostly used is by keeping the language if the receptor language permits it, all these techniques were</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">found in the translation of metaphors and similes in the novel </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Laskar Pelangi</span><span class="font2"> into its English version.</span></p>
