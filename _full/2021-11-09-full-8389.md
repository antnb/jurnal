<p><span class="font2">THE TRANSLATION OF CHAIRIL ANWAR’S POEM “AKU” INTO ENGLISH “I”</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>Abstrak</span></h1>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Artikel ini berjudul “The Translation of Chairil Anwar's Poem &quot;Aku&quot; Into English. Puisi ini menarik untuk dikaji karena sudah berulangkali diterjemahkan ke bahasa Inggris. Penelitian ini bertujuan untuk menganalisa apakah terjemahannya sepadan di bahasa sasaran.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Penelitian ini menggunakan teori terjemahan dari Eugene Nida, Mildred Larsson, J.P. Vinay dan Jean Darbelnet, serta teori struktur dan elemen puisi dari I.A. Richards. Berdasarkan analisis yang dilakukukan, terjemahan puisi ini dapat dikategorikan sepadan dengan bahasa sumbernya. Namun ada penyempitan makna dibanding puisi aslinya yang berbicara tentang kekuatan manusia dalam menghadapi tantangan hidup baik berupa perpisahan, pengalaman hidup baru bahkan kematian.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Kata Kunci: Terjemahan, Puisi, Kesepadanan</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Introduction</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Poem is one of the interesting objects to be translated. Many argue that it is very difficult to translate poem for many different reasons. But until now we still see many poems translated into another language. The high degree of difficulty from this process, at the same time may also be the most interesting challenge to the translators.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">All types of literature have two significant characteristics, namely beauty and expression. However, the situation is more complicated because the beauty of poem is not only shown through the choice of words, but sometimes the poet creates rhythm, cadence, and the other typical emotions in various typical expressions too, which is sometimes written without following the formal rules.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The object of this article is a poem &quot;Aku&quot; written by Chairil Anwar and its translation &quot;I&quot; by Burton Raffel. This poem is one of the most well-known and interesting from Chairil. Although the poet himself has now gone and the poem was written in 1953, its echoes are still heard until now. One line of the poem</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">which is the most popular, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">aku ingin hidup seribu tahun lagi (I want to live a thousand years) </span><span class="font2">has been cited for many times and always burns the spirit of the people who read it.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Problem of The Study</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The problem that the writer tries to answered is: what is the translation of the poem &quot;Aku&quot; in English as the target language (TL)?</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Aim of The Study</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">This research is intended to know the translation of the poem &quot;Aku&quot; into English as target language (TL).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Method</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">This research adopted some data collecting techniques related to the library research through close reading and taking notes over some sources (books). The analysis of the data of this research commenced by comparing the SL poem and the TL poem. Reading the poem thoroughly, line by line and stanza per stanza, while watching carefully the surface structure of each sentence along the poem, the writer tries hard to find the initial comprehension of the whole and the parts of the poem.</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">Raffel translated the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">comes </span><span class="font2">into </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">sampai </span><span class="font2">in the first line, which has meaning about something which is coming, reaches something, or something that comes after to the persona of &quot;I&quot;, but it has less significant meaning when it is compared to the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">sampai</span><span class="font2">. In Indonesian, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">sampai </span><span class="font2">does not only mean to achieve something, get somewhere, but it is also </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">stop</span><span class="font2">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">over</span><span class="font2">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">finish</span><span class="font2">. That impression is not obtained in </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">comes </span><span class="font2">though it is still show shades of ambiguity as the original version. In English, it is often found the statement about death that comes after to someone, but it is only limited to come after. There is no element of the end.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">For the second line, Raffel uses addition strategy by adding a clause </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">to hear </span><span class="font2">after </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">want</span><span class="font2">. The effect is that the pattern becomes very similar to the original. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">To hear </span><span class="font2">is also associated with </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">cries </span><span class="font2">because when someone is crying he or she must be heard by the other. Apart from addition, there is also deletion of word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">kan </span><span class="font2">which in Indonesian version gives the impression of candor and free from the confine of grammar.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">But the use of word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">cries </span><span class="font2">by Raffel also raises question. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Merayu </span><span class="font2">in Indonesian literally means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">persuade</span><span class="font2"> until someone wants to do something, while </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">cries </span><span class="font2">means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">menangis</span><span class="font2">. In terms of the sounds of cries (-es) with comes (-es) on the first line does indicate similarity, rhythmic, but there is a very noticeable difference when it is compared to the original meaning. Unless Burton really thinks that the meaning of the first line is only about death or separation.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Tidak juga kau </span><span class="font2">in the third line comes from formal pattern </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kau juga tidak (merayu</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">, </span><span class="font2">if it is connected with the second line). Due to consideration of &quot;taste&quot;, style and novelty, Chairil deliberated to writes in that way. This sentence shows a clear rejection message, even to </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">you </span><span class="font2">(in this case, the close friend or relative of him).</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">But in Raffel, the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">tidak </span><span class="font2">is not translated as </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">not</span><span class="font2">, but </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">nor </span><span class="font2">and </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">you </span><span class="font2">turn into a possessive pronoun, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">yours</span><span class="font2">. This is related to the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">cries </span><span class="font2">on the second line which if it is combined, the meaning is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">tidak juga tangismu (milikmu)</span><span class="font2">. So Raffel's translation openly refers to the tears belonging to </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">you</span><span class="font2">. While the strategy which is used in this line classified as transposition. The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">juga </span><span class="font2">in Chairil's poem actually shows a higher intensity of rejection, not merely the persona &quot;you&quot; suddenly becomes one from the group that is rejected by the persona &quot;I&quot;.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Raffel translates the fourth line with a different pattern from the original. He uses the strategy of addition as well as descriptive equivalent. The phrase </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">tak perlu </span><span class="font2">is translated into </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">away</span><span class="font2">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">sedu sedan </span><span class="font2">into </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">cry </span><span class="font2">and </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">itu </span><span class="font2">into </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">with all who</span><span class="font2">. When it is translated literally will be </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">menjauhlah semua yang menangis.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">And when the fourth line from Raffel is combined with the previous line, there is no problem with the line. Moreover, Raffel consistently uses the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">cry </span><span class="font2">that shows sadness. However, when referring to the original line, the impression is different. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Away </span><span class="font2">and </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">tak perlu </span><span class="font2">equally show rejection and may be regarded as equivalent translation, but </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">cry </span><span class="font2">is not enough to represent </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">sedu sedan</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Sedu sedan </span><span class="font2">is one kind of cry which is not only the tears that come out from the eye, but also the sound of sobbing and gasping from the mouth. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Sedu sedan</span><span class="font2"> is one particular form of the general forms, cry. By replacing it does not indicate deepest meaning.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Raffel’s translation for the fifth line uses transposition and additional strategies. He also splits the sentence from the original into two and separates them by comma. Transposition occurs in the place of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">here </span><span class="font2">in front </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">I am</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Here </span><span class="font2">is used to translate </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">ini</span><span class="font2">, which literally means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">this</span><span class="font2">. But in the context of the fifth line, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">this</span><span class="font2"> is not right because the function of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">ini </span><span class="font2">is to show the existence of a person, to introduce persona of &quot;I&quot;. It seems that </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">here </span><span class="font2">actually refers to the place (where persona of &quot;I&quot; is used to belong) in the Raffel’s translation feels more equivalent. The change in location of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">here </span><span class="font2">to the front and the phrase </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">I am </span><span class="font2">which is written in full form, not abbreviated into I'm, gives the same intensity as </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">aku ini </span><span class="font2">in Chairil version.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The addition occurs in the translation above with the inclusion of the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">a </span><span class="font2">in front of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">wild beast</span><span class="font2">. The addition is not an option, but a necessity because the</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">article </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">a</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">an</span><span class="font2"> are required in English structure for the objects that can be calculated and referred for the first time or not known from its context.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Raffel uses </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">wild beast </span><span class="font2">for </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">binatang jalang </span><span class="font2">in the sixth line. The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">jalang </span><span class="font2">and </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">wild </span><span class="font2">if being compared do not show equivalence of meaning. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Jalang </span><span class="font2">in Indonesian does not only mean </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">liar</span><span class="font2">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">wild</span><span class="font2"> but also means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">tidak tahu malu</span><span class="font2">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">shameless</span><span class="font2">, in the phrase of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">perempuan jalang</span><span class="font2"> for example. While </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">wild </span><span class="font2">only means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">liar</span><span class="font2">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">buas</span><span class="font2">. Although the </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">beast </span><span class="font2">itself not only means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">binatang (buas)</span><span class="font2"> but also </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">jahanam</span><span class="font2">, still the full meaning of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">jalang </span><span class="font2">in Indonesia, is not delivered.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The line </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">dari kumpulannya terbuang </span><span class="font2">actually has an ambiguous meaning. Persona of &quot;I&quot; is excluded from his group or he comes from a group that consists of excluded person, or unwanted people. Both of these meanings are important and relevant. The translation by Burton demonstrates the first meaning.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Raffel’s translation uses addition strategy by the presence of the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">the </span><span class="font2">in front of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">herd </span><span class="font2">as a definite article according to English structure. The sentence pattern is different from Chairil's version and when we translate it literally, it means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">diusir dari kawanannya</span><span class="font2">. The form is passive.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Raffel uses transposition strategy, as seen in the possessive pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">-ku </span><span class="font2">which shows change in position to the front of noun, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">my skin</span><span class="font2">. The change is also present in the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">may </span><span class="font2">behind the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">bullets</span><span class="font2">. This pattern creates repeated sound effects that are similar between </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">my </span><span class="font2">and </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">may</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Raffel translates eight line using descriptive equivalent strategy. He uses </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">keep on </span><span class="font2">to express </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">meradang menerjang</span><span class="font2">. When we read the eight line only, without reading the seventh line in Raffel version, we do not get any clue, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">but I'll keep on </span><span class="font2">(what?). Compared to line eight by Chairil which is already full of meaning. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Meradang menerjang </span><span class="font2">is not only brilliant on the firmness of meaning, a sense of restrained anger and an effort to attack forward, but also has a beautiful sound repetition in prefix </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">me- </span><span class="font2">and the suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">-ang </span><span class="font2">in both words. This sound is more beautiful when they are combined to the </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">binatang (-ang) jalang (-ang)</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">But I'll keep on </span><span class="font2">does really not represent the intensity of the meaning from its original version.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Transposition occurs in the ninth line when the predicate in form of verb, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">carrying forward </span><span class="font2">is placed in the front. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Ku </span><span class="font2">which in the original version has an</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">object is turned to </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">my </span><span class="font2">as possessive pronoun in </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">my wound and my pain</span><span class="font2">. The effect from repetition of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">my </span><span class="font2">creates sound repetition too, slightly similar to the </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">luka dan bisa</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Raffel uses </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">forward </span><span class="font2">to explain </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">berlari</span><span class="font2">. These two words do have meaning to move. Something that is not revealed is in </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">berlari, </span><span class="font2">there is an intensity of physical leg movement, while in the </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">forward </span><span class="font2">does not exist. This word only describes the direction of movement, to the front. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Berlari </span><span class="font2">itself means the movement toward a &quot;goal&quot; in front of him, and also means leaving something behind, running from something. The direction is the same, but the purpose is different.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The use of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">pain </span><span class="font2">to translate </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">bisa </span><span class="font2">is categorized as descriptive equivalent. This word describes only one of the effects of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">bisa</span><span class="font2">, which in Indonesian is more known as venom of animal, the snake for example. So the </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">pain </span><span class="font2">is no longer present in form of metaphor in the English version, but as the literal meaning. That means that this translation results a reduction of language style eventhough this is still equivalent in the target language.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Raffel translates the tenth line which originally only consists of one line into two lines. This also has a correlation with the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">berlari </span><span class="font2">which he did not translate in the ninth line and is inserted into tenth line.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">As we have discussed earlier, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">berlari </span><span class="font2">is very different from </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">attacking</span><span class="font2">. This word will be relevant only if it is associated with the previous line, where persona of &quot;I&quot; does challenge death by ignoring a barrage of bullets. The emergence of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">attacking </span><span class="font2">twice can be said as a delayed emotion or slower phase than the original poem or two other translations. In the original poem, the desire to attack has emerged since </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">menerjang meradang</span><span class="font2">. At Raffel, the seventh stanza is only translated into </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">but I'll just keep on</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The eleventh line from Raffel is classified as transposition and componential analysis. Burton simplifies the phrase </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">pedih peri </span><span class="font2">into one word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">suffering</span><span class="font2">. It does not just eliminate the rhythmic sound of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">pedih peri</span><span class="font2">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">suffering, </span><span class="font2">but also does not show the impression of restrained anger and resentment, either it only shows </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">penderitaan</span><span class="font2">. So the message is less complete.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The line </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">dan aku lebih tidak perduli </span><span class="font2">from Chairil is actually the same as the sentence </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">dan aku makin tidak perduli.</span><span class="font2"> The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">lebih </span><span class="font2">shows the intensity of a</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">&quot;double&quot; act from something done by persona of &quot;I&quot;. Burton uses literal strategy. But the sentence </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">and I won't care anymore </span><span class="font2">which literally means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">dan aku tidak akan peduli lagi</span><span class="font2"> is not in the same level as </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">dan aku lebih tidak perduli. </span><span class="font2">In that sentence there is no sign of high increasing level of &quot;not care&quot; as in the original version.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Raffel uses the strategy of literal translation and transposition for the thirteenth line. He chooses the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">another </span><span class="font2">to replace </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lagi</span><span class="font2"> and put it in front of </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">thousand years </span><span class="font2">in accordance with English structure. The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">another </span><span class="font2">in Indonesian also means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lagi</span><span class="font2"> actually, not only </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">yang lain</span><span class="font2">. As the same as in the sentence: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">give me another banana, I have eaten this one</span><span class="font2">, for example.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Based on the analysis of the translation above, the most significant differentiator from the original version appears in the second line </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">ku mau tak seorang kan merayu</span><span class="font2">. In the original poem, the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">merayu </span><span class="font2">makes the poem ambiguous. Manny interpretations come, especially when it is associated with the first line, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">bila sampai waktuku</span><span class="font2">. &quot;Aku&quot; by Chairil affirms the spirit to face the challenges and difficulties of life, with all of its risks and suffering, whether it is separation, death or new adventure.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In the translation, this line really affects the change of meaning from original poem. In Raffel's work, the message of the poem is narrowed down to just the extent of suffering of death, especially since he consistently uses the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">cries </span><span class="font2">to replace </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">merayu</span><span class="font2">, and also cry to replace </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">sedu sedan</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font0">6.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Finally, this study comes to some conclusions. The first, the translator use different strategies, namely literal, transposition and componential analysis in translating the poem. The different strategies and the different words used also affect the meaning of each line. Second, Raffel’s translation is categorized as equivalent in the target language, but the reduction of meaning can not be avoided, though it is not to the extent that changes the whole theme.</span></p>
