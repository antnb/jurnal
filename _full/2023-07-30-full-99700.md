<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>Determinants of Financial Reporting Local Government Organization Transparency and Accountability as a Mediator</span></h1>
<p><span class="font4">Ietje Nazaruddin<sup>1</sup></span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">*</span><span class="font4">, Yustisia Melasari Rahmandani<sup>2.</sup> Salsabila Putri Aryati Sibuea<sup>3</sup></span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstract</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">This study examines the role of accountability as a mediating variable in understanding how internal control and external pressure influence financial reporting transparency. Data was sourced from a survey, garnering responses from 214 participants. The purposive sampling technique was adopted, focusing on responses from heads of finance departments, financial treasurers, and financial staff supporting local government organizations (OPD). For hypothesis testing, the study employed Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) grounded on the Partial Least Squares (PLS) approach. Results revealed that internal control significantly promotes financial reporting transparency in OPD, with accountability serving as a mediator. In contrast, external pressure alone is inadequate to guarantee the transparency and accountability of financial reports within local governments. Consequently, OPDs are advised to bolster their internal control and accountability mechanisms to enhance the trustworthiness of their financial statements.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">Keywords: </span><span class="font4">internal control, external pressure, accountability, and financial reporting transparency</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>Introduction</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">Financial reporting transparency has garnered significant attention from researchers. A lack of transparency in disseminating financial information to beneficiaries, stakeholders, and the public can impede effective monitoring of local government performance </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Arkorful et al., 2021; Mahdi</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">&amp;&nbsp;Santoso, 2019)</span></a><span class="font4">.</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Piotrowski &amp;&nbsp;Bertelli (2010) </span></a><span class="font4">posited that public concern often centers on the ease of access to government-held information. This focus stems from the intricate relationship between transparency and a myriad of issues, including ethics, corruption, administrative malpractices, and accountability </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Adiputra et al., 2018)</span></a><span class="font4">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">In Indonesia, the Law Number 14 of 2008 mandates that local governments must ensure public information disclosure. Consequently, websites have become an effective tool for disseminating information to the public </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Adiputra et al., 2018; Davici, 2018; Krah &amp;&nbsp;Mertens, 2020;</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">Paranoan &amp;&nbsp;Totanan, 2018; Prihatin &amp;&nbsp;Ritonga, 2020; Sofyani et al., 2020)</span></a><span class="font4">. However, a review of several official local government websites, such as</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">those in Kalimantan Province, shows a lack of transparency in financial reporting. An assessment by the Open Budget Index (OBI) highlighted that many local governments in Kalimantan Province fall short of optimal transparency standards </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font3" style="font-variant:small-caps;">(P</span><span class="font2" style="font-variant:small-caps;">rihatin</span><span class="font4"> &amp;&nbsp;</span><span class="font3" style="font-variant:small-caps;">R</span><span class="font2" style="font-variant:small-caps;">itonga</span><span class="font3" style="font-variant:small-caps;">,</span></a><span class="font3" style="font-variant:small-caps;"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">2020)</span></a><span class="font4">. Furthermore, several regencies lack official websites altogether. Notably,</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Syamsul</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(2020) </span></a><span class="font4">reported that 7% of the 411 regency governments in Indonesia, including the Sukamara Regency Government, do not maintain official online portals. A significant impediment to transparency is the absence of punitive measures in most local regulations, hampering their implementation and enforcement </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Pebriani et al., 2021)</span></a><span class="font4">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">For instance, the Lamandau Regency Government's official website shows fragmented and incomplete financial management reports, such as the Reports on Budget Outcomes (LRA), Opinions on Local Government Finances Report (LKPD), Budget Implementation Document of the Head of Local Government Financial Management Office (DPA PPKD), and the General Procurement Plan (RUP) as per the records from pemda.lamandaukab.go.id in 2022. This fragmentation poses challenges for the public seeking comprehensive information. Similarly, while the West Kotawaringin Regency Government disclosed financial reports on its website, it received an Unqualified Opinion for the years 2019, 2020, and 2021. Nevertheless, the Audit Board (BPK) identified weaknesses in the internal control system, as detailed on portal.kotawaringinbaratkab.go.id in 2022.</span></p>
<p><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">Rasmini &amp;&nbsp;Masdiantini (2018) </span></a><span class="font4">contend that weaknesses in internal control adversely affect financial reporting transparency. The imperative for a robust internal control system within each local government organization (OPD) is articulated in Government Regulation Number 60 of 2008. This regulation emphasizes the comprehensive and effective implementation of the Internal Control System to oversee OPD activities, thereby enhancing financial reporting transparency </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Antika et al., 2020;</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah, 2022; Rasmini &amp;&nbsp;Masdiantini, 2018; Sumartono &amp;&nbsp;Pasolo, 2019)</span></a><span class="font4">. Several studies have shown a positive correlation between internal control and the transparency of financial reporting </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Fathmaningrum &amp;&nbsp;Mukti, 2019; Setyowati &amp;&nbsp;Yuliani, 2020;</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">Sumartono &amp;&nbsp;Pasolo, 2019; Wintari &amp;&nbsp;Suardana, 2018)</span></a><span class="font4">. However, findings from</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Budiartini</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">&amp;&nbsp;Putra (2021) and Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah (2022) </span></a><span class="font4">differ, rendering the relationship between internal control and transparency as somewhat ambiguous.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">On the other hand, in order to produce transparent financial reports, local governments must respond to external pressures </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah, 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. These pressures arise from the varied interests of multiple parties within the local government's financial framework </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Davici, 2018)</span></a><span class="font4">. Research conducted by</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Wirawan et al. (2019)</span></a><span class="font4"> indicated that external pressures have a positive impact on the transparency of financial reporting by OPDs. However, this conclusion contrasts with findings from earlier studies by</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Davici (2018), Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah (2022), and Setyowati &amp;&nbsp;Yuliani (2020).</span></a></p>
<p><span class="font4">From the discussion above, there are evident inconsistencies in prior research concerning the influence of internal control and external pressure on financial reporting transparency. To address these discrepancies, introducing a mediating variable might be essential to further define the relationships between these variables and bolster the research findings </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Ghozali &amp;&nbsp;Latan, 2012)</span></a><span class="font4">. Specifically, introducing the 'accountability' variable adds a novel dimension to this research. This is particularly noteworthy as this variable is infrequently employed in studies focusing on financial reporting transparency, especially as a mediating factor. Accountability plays a crucial role in ensuring</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">transparency to the public. If OPDs uphold and effectively implement this accountability, it can lead to increased public trust </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Asrida et al., 2018; Umaira &amp;&nbsp;Adnan, 2019)</span></a><span class="font4">. Given this perspective, research on financial reporting transparency in OPDs is of paramount importance.</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Adiputra et al. (2018) </span></a><span class="font4">and</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Pina et al. (2010) </span></a><span class="font4">posit that institutional theory can elucidate the financial reporting practices of local governments.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Within the framework of institutional theory, the control function's role in revealing financial information in local governments is viewed as a mechanism to ensure organizational functions garner legitimacy from the community </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Adiputra et al., 2018;</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">Wintari &amp;&nbsp;Suardana, 2018)</span></a><span class="font4">. One primary means of assuring the community is by robustly and effectively enhancing internal control. When executed correctly, this amplifies the accountability of financial reporting, subsequently promoting good governance </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Bonsu et</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">al., 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. Previous studies consistently indicate that internal control exerts a positive and significant influence on accountability </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Antika et al., 2020; Bonsu et al., 2022; Hardiningsih</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">et al., 2020; Widyatama et al., 2017; Yesinia et al., 2018)</span></a><span class="font4">. Based on this evidence, the hypothesis can be articulated as.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">H₁: Internal control positively influences accountability.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">External pressures can emanate from other organizations or stem from cultural expectations of the community in which an organization operates </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(DiMaggio &amp;&nbsp;Powell,</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">1983)</span></a><span class="font4">. Such pressures can foster the establishment of good governance by advocating for transparency and public trust in accountability </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Diansari et al., 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. The presence of external pressures serves to monitor relevant stakeholders on the performance of local governments in fulfilling their duties in accordance with established laws and regulations. Furthermore, it provides a platform to address community needs </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Diansari et al., 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. The enhancement of accountability is further fueled by oversight and demands from external entities, compelling OPDs to be answerable for their actions. This observation is consistent with prior studies indicating that external pressure has a positive influence on accountability </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Mada et al., 2017; Medianti et al., 2018; Asrida et al., 2018; Diansari et al.,</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">2022; Umaira &amp;&nbsp;Adnan, 2019)</span></a><span class="font4">. Thus, the hypothesis can be formulated as.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">H₂: External pressure positively influences accountability.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Accountability entails the responsibility of the entrusted party to report, explain, and disclose all undertakings to the trustor, who possesses both the right and the duty to demand such transparency. This concept underscores the importance of informing the public about all activities of local governments </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Budiartini &amp;&nbsp;Putra, 2021; Fathmaningrum</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">&amp;&nbsp;Mukti, 2019; Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah, 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. Reflecting on past studies, it's evident that accountability has a positive bearing on the execution of financial reporting transparency </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Asroel et al., 2016; Budiartini &amp;&nbsp;Putra, 2021; Fathmaningrum &amp;&nbsp;Mukti, 2019; Muraina &amp;</span></a><span class="font4">&nbsp;</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">Dandago, 2020; Rasmini &amp;&nbsp;Masdiantini, 2018; Wibisono et al., 2023)</span></a><span class="font4">. Consequently, the hypothesis can be presented as.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">H₃: Accountability has a positive impact on the practice of financial reporting transparency.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Achieving transparency in regional financial reporting lays a foundational bedrock for improved governance. Ensuring transparency and accountability in public funds bolsters public trust in the government's management capabilities. It suggests that heightened internal control aligns with an enhanced application of regional financial reporting transparency </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah, 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. Prior studies indicate that internal control indeed has a positive influence on the application of financial reporting</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">transparency </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Asroel et al., 2016; Fathmaningrum &amp;&nbsp;Mukti, 2019; Setyowati &amp;&nbsp;Yuliani,</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">2020; Sumartono &amp;&nbsp;Pasolo, 2019; Wintari &amp;&nbsp;Suardana, 2018)</span></a><span class="font4">. Based on this information, the hypothesis can be articulated as.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">H<sub>4</sub>: &nbsp;Internal control positively influences the application of financial reporting</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">transparency.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">An OPD that emphasizes legitimacy will generally conform to both external and societal expectations. This alignment often subjects the OPD to institutional pressures, leading to homogenization in their approaches </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Pebriani et al., 2021)</span></a><span class="font4">. Coercive isomorphism, an external pressure facet, necessitates an organization's adherence to rules for the achievement of objectives </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Pebriani et al., 2021; Pradita et al., 2019)</span></a><span class="font4">. The role of external pressure is pivotal in augmenting the application of financial reporting transparency; a rise in external pressures corresponds to an increase in local government financial transparency </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Pradita et al., 2019)</span></a><span class="font4">. Previous studies have documented that external pressure favorably impacts an OPD's financial reporting transparency </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Budiartini</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">&amp;&nbsp;Putra, 2021; Fathmaningrum &amp;&nbsp;Mukti, 2019; Pebriani et al., 2021; Pradita et al., 2019;</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">Wirawan et al., 2019)</span></a><span class="font4">. From this discussion, the hypothesis can be framed as.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">H<sub>5</sub>: External pressure has a positive influence on the implementation of financial reporting transparency.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Research by</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Antika et al. (2020) </span></a><span class="font4">and</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Bonsu et al. (2022) </span></a><span class="font4">underscored the potential of internal control to shape accountability in the regional financial management process. Effective internal control boosts public confidence in the OPD's ability to adhere to financial guidelines, thereby meeting accountability standards </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Arfiansyah, 2020; Diansari</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">et al., 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. When such accountability standards are achieved, it is incumbent upon the OPD to disclose financial activities and outcomes to the public, thus facilitating transparent financial reporting </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah, 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. Hence, internal control can indirectly elevate regional financial reporting transparency by promoting accountability. Consequently, the hypothesis can be expressed as:</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">H<sub>6</sub>: Internal control indirectly enhances the implementation of financial reporting transparency via accountability.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">To curb deviant behavior, the central government has promulgated regulations that oversee the control mechanisms of an OPD </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah, 2022; Sari, 2017)</span></a><span class="font4">. Institutional pressures and external environmental factors favorably influence the deployment of a performance accountability system </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Lestari &amp;&nbsp;Salomo, 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. Additionally, the tenet of accountability, when buttressed by transparency, embodies the principle of openness. This ensures the public has access to information concerning regional financial management </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Budiarto &amp;&nbsp;Damayanti, 2020)</span></a><span class="font4">. Therefore, external pressure can indirectly bolster regional financial reporting transparency via the conduit of accountability. Based on this analysis, the hypothesis can be articulated as:</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">H<sub>7</sub>: External pressure indirectly enhances the implementation of financial reporting transparency through accountability.</span></p>

<p><a name="bookmark13"></a><span class="font4">In this study, a self-reporting survey was used, which could introduce a normative bias, commonly referred to as Common Method Variance (CMV). If a single latent factor contributes over 50% to the variance, bias is considered significant </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Podsakoff et al., 2003)</span></a><span class="font4">. Therefore, all these indicators can be considered valid </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Ghozali &amp;&nbsp;Latan,</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">2015)</span></a><span class="font4">. For the discriminant validity test indicator, the square root of AVE was examined, as shown in the Fornell-Larcker table within the PLS algorithm report. If the square root of the AVE for each variable is greater than the correlation between the variables, then discriminant validity is achieved </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Fornell &amp;&nbsp;Larcker, 1981)</span></a><span class="font4"> (refer to</span><a href="#bookmark15"><span class="font4"> Table 5)</span></a><span class="font4">.</span></p>
<p><a href="#bookmark15"><span class="font4">Table 5, </span></a><span class="font4">based on the Fornell-Larcker criterion, indicates that the square root of the AVE for the accountability variable was 0.789. This value was greater than the correlation between the accountability variable and external pressure on financial reporting transparency. Thus, all variables in the study are validated. The study's reliability test employed both Cronbach's alpha and composite reliability. A construct is deemed reliable if it has a Cronbach's alpha value greater than 0.7 and a composite reliability exceeding 0.7 </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Ghozali &amp;&nbsp;Latan, 2015)</span></a><span class="font4">. As evident in</span><a href="#bookmark16"><span class="font4"> Table 6,</span></a><span class="font4"> all constructs satisfied the stipulated validity and reliability benchmarks.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The hypothesis testing findings, presented in</span><a href="#bookmark17"><span class="font4"> Table 7, </span></a><span class="font4">revealed that internal control had a positive and indirect impact on the transparency of financial reporting. Nevertheless, neither direct nor indirect external pressure impacted the transparency of financial reporting.</span></p>
<p><a href="#bookmark17"><span class="font4">Table 7 </span></a><a name="bookmark15"></a><span class="font4">demonstrates that internal control positively influences the implementation of financial reporting transparency. This finding aligns with research by </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">Budiartini &amp;&nbsp;Putra (2021), Erni &amp;&nbsp;Giri (2019), Rinie et al. (2019), Setyowati (2020), and</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">Wintari &amp;&nbsp;Suardana (2018).</span></a><span class="font4"> Moreover, the outcomes are consistent with prior studies indicating that the internal control system has a positive impact on accountability </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Antika et al., 2020; Bonsu et al., 2022; Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah, 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. These results suggest that effective internal control directly enhances the transparency of financial reporting.</span></p>

<p><span class="font4">When internal control is robust, the ensuing financial reports become more transparent and credible. Furthermore, the study uncovered a positive correlation between internal control and the application of OPD financial reporting transparency, with accountability acting as a partial mediator. One of internal control's pivotal roles is to guarantee the accuracy, completeness, and reliability of an organization's financial statements. A sound internal control system bolsters OPD's assurance of financial reporting's integrity and accountability, thereby elevating public trust in OPD and reinforcing both legitimacy and transparency. These insights resonate with research by </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">Mualifu et al. (2019) and Oktavia (2022),</span></a><span class="font4"> which assert that enhanced internal control levels correlate with increased accountability, subsequently boosting financial reporting transparency. Moreover, this study's findings bolster the institutional theory, suggesting that OPDs with effective internal control and accountability systems are more inclined to embrace transparent financial report practices, strengthening their community and stakeholder legitimacy.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Drawing from institutional theory, coercive isomorphism describes an organizational compulsion to conform to specific standards under external pressures, be it from the state, other entities, or societal expectations </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(DiMaggio &amp;&nbsp;Powell, 1983)</span></a><span class="font4">. The inherent coercive force within prevailing regulations amplifies legitimacy </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Davici,</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">2018; Pradita et al., 2019)</span></a><span class="font4">, leading external parties to recognize organizational authenticity </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Adiputra et al., 2018; Asrida et al., 2018)</span></a><span class="font4">. As</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Frumkin &amp;&nbsp;Galaskiewicz (2004)</span></a><span class="font4"> suggest, heightened external pressures catalyze greater organizational openness. Consequently, as external pressure on the OPD escalates, so does its transparency </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Davici, 2018)</span></a><span class="font4">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Contrary to expectations, the research findings did not validate the hypothesis that external pressure would robustly promote the implementation of financial reporting transparency in local governments and accountability. This outcome could be attributed to the lax regulations concerning financial reporting in local administrations, which failed to stimulate transparency in financial reporting. This observation aligns with earlier studies, suggesting that the presence or absence of external pressure doesn't significantly influence regional financial reporting transparency </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Davici, 2018)</span></a><span class="font4">. Additionally, if the OPD is reluctant to disclose financial statements, it simply won't </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah, 2022)</span></a><span class="font4">. This less-than-transparent organizational culture is evident in prior research, such as</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Asrida et al. (2018),</span></a><span class="font4"> which disclosed that external pressures haven't significantly bolstered accountability. Thus, OPD's financial reporting transparency to the public remains limited. Other studies, including those by</span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4"> Davici</span></a><span class="font4"> </span><a href="#bookmark6"><span class="font4">(2018), Oktavia &amp;&nbsp;Aliyah (2022), and Setyowati &amp;&nbsp;Yuliani (2020),</span></a><span class="font4"> concur that external pressures don't substantially influence the practice of financial reporting transparency.</span></p>

<p><span class="font4">In this context, external pressures haven't sufficiently enlightened OPDs on regulatory transformations that could potentially enhance local government financial report transparency and accountability.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">This study's findings yield both theoretical and practical insights. Theoretically, the outcomes contribute to references and empirical literature probing the relationship between internal control, external pressure, and financial management transparency, with a spotlight on accountability as a mediating factor. On a practical note, this study advises OPDs to emphasize internal control, given that external pressures have not significantly shaped OPDs' comprehension of prevailing regulations and policies. Neither external pressures nor public opinion seem potent enough to spur financial reporting transparency. Hence, to augment financial reporting transparency, robust accountability and internal control systems are imperative.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>Conclusion</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">This study sought to investigate and assess the influence of internal control and external pressure on financial reporting transparency, with accountability serving as a mediating factor. The findings indicate that a robust internal control system can ensure that OPD's financial reports are precise, dependable, and accountable, which in turn augments public trust in the OPD and bolsters its legitimacy and transparency. Conversely, external pressure neither directly nor indirectly affected the implementation of financial reporting transparency, even when considering accountability as a mediating or intervening factor.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The research was limited to regencies within the Central Kalimantan Province, which necessitates prudence when extrapolating these results to a wider setting. For enhanced generalizability, subsequent studies might contemplate including a more varied array of provinces across Indonesia. Additionally, for a more comprehensive data collection, future investigations might employ interview techniques, offering a deeper understanding of respondents' perspectives on transparency advancements in financial reporting within the regencies of Kalimantan Province. Drawing from these insights, it is advocated that OPDs amplify their internal control mechanisms to heighten the clarity of their financial disclosures. Furthermore, it is imperative for OPDs to grasp the crucial role of accountability in bridging the gap between internal control and lucid financial reporting. Such initiatives will elevate their overall financial administration, ensuring the authenticity and trustworthiness of their financial statements.</span></p>
