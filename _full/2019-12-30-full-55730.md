<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>A study of essential oil from an invasive </span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Piper aduncum</span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"> L.</span></h1>
<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font5"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Kajian minyak atsiri dari tumbuhan invasif </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Piper aduncum</span><span class="font5"> L.</span></h2>

<h3><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTISARI</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">Tumbuhan invasif </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Piper aduncum</span><span class="font4"> merupakan spesies tumbuhan yang berpotensi menghasilkan minyak atsiri. Penelitian ini bertujuan untuk menentukan persentase, senyawa kimia yang terkandung, dan sifat anti-jamur dari minyak atsiri yang dihasilkan dari daun dan buah </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4">. Penelitian menunjukkan bahwa daun dan buah </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> mengandung masing-masing 0,30% dan 0,33% minyak atsiri. Apiol adalah senyawa kimia paling melimpah yang diperoleh dalam minyak atsiri daun dan buah </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum,</span><span class="font4"> masing-masing dengan konsentrasi 57,10% dan 66,31%. Kami menyimpulkan bahwa minyak atsiri yang diperoleh dalam penelitian ini mampu menghambat pertumbuhan </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Aspergillus niger</span><span class="font4"> dan </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Cladosporium</span><span class="font4"> sp. namun tidak mampu menghambat </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font4"> dan </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium solani</span><span class="font4">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci: Antijamur, apiol, analisis GC-MS, IAPs, fitokimia, penyakit tanaman</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">An invasive </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Piper aduncum</span><span class="font4"> is a plant species that potentially produces essential oil. The study aims to determine the percentage, phytochemical compounds, and anti-fungal properties of essential oil produced from the leaves and fruits of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Piper aduncum</span><span class="font4">. The study showed that the leaves and fruits of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum </span><span class="font4">contain 0.30% and 0.33% of essential oil, respectively. Apiol is the most abundant phytochemical compound obtained in essential oil of leaves and fruits of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> with 57.10% and 66.31%, respectively. We conclude that essential oil obtained in this study is able to inhibit the growth of both </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Aspergillus niger</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Cladosporium</span><span class="font4"> sp. but not for </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium solani</span><span class="font4">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Keywords: Antifungal, apiol, GC-MS analysis, IAPs, phytochemistry, plant disease</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Piper aduncum</span><span class="font4"> is firstly introduced to Indonesia as an ornamental plant in 1860, and now has been widely distributed throughout Indonesia (Hartemink, 2010; Tjitrosoedirdjo et al., 2016). The species is neotropic plant that grows to an altitude of 2,000 meters and generally in open and disturbed areas, such as secondary forest (Jan et al., 2002; Tjitrosoedirdjo et al., 2016), for example in Borneo</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">land clearing for road construction is reported to have contributed to the spread of this species (Padmanaba and Sheil, 2014). Despite being an invasive plant, in Papua New Guinea this species is used for raw material of farming equipment, pesticides, fertilizers, construction of houses, and windbreaks (Siges et al., 2005).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Piper aduncum</span><span class="font4"> contains an essential oil which is reportedly able to prevent bites of the </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Aedes albopictus</span><span class="font4"> mosquito (Misni et al., 2009). In addition</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">to being a mosquito repellent, the essential oil is widely used in the drug industry, as food flavor, in cosmetics and fragrances (Rizal et al., 2009). Essential oils are also known to inhibit fungal growth. Essential oil from lemons is known to inhibit the growth of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Aspergillus parasiticum </span><span class="font4">fungus, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">A. carbonarius</span><span class="font4">, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Cladosporium cladosporioides</span><span class="font4">, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Eurotium herbariorum</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Penicillium chrysogenum</span><span class="font4"> (Dimic et al., 2014). Essential oil from </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Thymus vulgaris</span><span class="font4"> has also been known to inhibit the growth of the fungus </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizopus oryzae</span><span class="font4"> (de Lira Mota et al., 2012).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Efforts to control Invasive Alien Plants (IAPs) are constrained by costs. However, some invasive plant species are considered having an economic value that can be created as part of control costs (Stafford &amp;&nbsp;Blignaut, 2017). This study aims to determine the yield, phytochemical content and potential anti-fungal properties of essential oil obtained from the leaves and fruits of an invasive</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Piper aduncum</span><span class="font4">. Also, this present study can hopefully provide a preliminary information to anyone who interested on the topic.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">METHODS</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Study area</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">All the processes and antifungal testing of essential oil of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> were conducted at Laboratory of Applied Botany of Bali Botanical Garden, while sequencing of phytochemical was carried out at the Development Unit for Clean Technology - LIPI. The sample materials were the leaves and fruits of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. Aduncum</span><span class="font4"> obtained from Bali Botanical Gardens with registered number XV.B.229 (Figure 1). We selected leaves which are not too young and not too old, while the fruits were mature (but not ripen yet). We considered picking period for sample materials to yield an optimum amount of essential oil.</span></p>

<h3><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>Extraction of Essential Oil</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">A hundred grams of fresh leaves and fruits of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> were distilled using </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Schott Duran</span><span class="font4"> steam distiller. Heating was about five hours at temperature of 100°C. The samples were three</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">times replication to reduce the data errors. The essential oil was separated with the water using </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Schott Duran</span><span class="font4"> separator funnel (Handa, 2008). The separated oil was then analysed using GC-MS. The percentage of essential oil was calculated using the following formula:</span></p>

<h3><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>Analysis of Chemical Components</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">The essential oil samples were then analysed using Shimadzu GC-MS QP 2010. About 1 μL of the sample was injected into Rtx-5MS column (60mx0.25mm) with a film thickness of 0.25μm, the pre-programmed oven temperature was 50 - 280°C with a temperature rise rate of 5°C/minute. The carrier gas used was helium with a pressure of 101kPa, a total flow rate of 46.5 mL/minute, a column flow rate of 0.85 mL/minute, cleaning flow rate of 3.0 mL/minute, and a split ratio of 1:50.</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>Antifungal Assay</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">The essential oil samples were tested for antifungal properties against several species of fungi, such as </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Aspergillus niger</span><span class="font4">, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Cladosporium</span><span class="font4"> sp. </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium solani</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">F. oxysporum</span><span class="font4"> using agar</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">diffusion method. The method was carried out by laying moistened filter paper with essential oil on the media that had been planted with the fungus with three replications. Clear area formation was observed for three days. The formation of clear area on the surface indicated the presence of fungal death (Pratiwi, 2008).</span></p>

<h3><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>DISCUSSION</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">Apiol and Myristicin found in the essential oil of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> fruits and leaves are toxic to insects (Lichtenstein &amp;&nbsp;Casida, 1963; Lichtenstein et al., 1974; Fuhremann and Lichtenstein, 1979; Bernard et al., 1995; Walia et al., 2004), thus the presence of these compounds in high concentrations promotes</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">an opportunity to use </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> essential oil as a natural insect repellent. In addition to Apiol, in a large percentage, the essential oil of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum </span><span class="font4">leaves also contains caryophyllene. This compound is often found in essential oils of various species of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Piperaceae</span><span class="font4"> family member, including </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. cernuum </span><span class="font4">and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. regnellii</span><span class="font4"> (Costantin et al., 2001; da Silva et al., 2017).</span></p>
<div><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/55730-3.jpg" alt="" style="width:368pt;height:274pt;">
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Figure 3. </span><span class="font2">Comparison of some dominant chemical compounds in essential oils, obtained in fruits and leaves of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
</div><br clear="all">
<p><span class="font4">Some dominant compounds obtained in the fruits are also found in the leaves with different concentrations. The ratio of such compounds can be seen in Figure 3. This present study showed that the</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">leaves and fruits of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> are both dominated by terpenoids, these compounds are known having many biological activities, such as antimicrobial, antifungal and antibacterial (Cavaleiro, 2015).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The inhibitory ability is likely to correlate with the presence of beta-Ocimene compounds. Cavaleiro et al. (2015) reported beta-Ocimene is a compound with antifungal properties. The compund is also reported having anti-inflammatory and antiviral properties (Terpene, 2018). On the contrary, ineffectiveness in inhibiting the growth of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">F. oxysporum</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">F. solani</span><span class="font4"> was probably because the nature of active ingredients has no wide spectrum, it means the essential oil only inhibits the growth of some fungus species (Neu and Gootz, 2001).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Previous studies of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> extraction showed various yields of essential oil. For example, leaves and branches samples of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> from eight different regions of the Amazon produce essential oil ranging from 1.2% to 3.3% (Maia et al., 1998). </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Piper aduncum</span><span class="font4"> leaves, native to Papua New Guinea, yields essential oil of 0.35%, while </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> leaves from Cuba yields essential oil of 0.96% (Pino et al., 2004; Rali et al., 2007). The difference in yields of essential oil is probably due to different soil macronutrients in each region. Kahkashan et al. (2015) stated that soil types affected the amount of essential oil produced by </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Mentha arvensis</span><span class="font4"> L. Suryawati &amp;&nbsp;Murniyanto (2011) showed that essential oil produced by </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Zingiber officinale</span><span class="font4"> L. is directly proportional to the availability of macronutrients in the soil. A lack of available groundwater can also result in changing secondary metabolites of plants than in normal conditions (Trisilawati &amp;&nbsp;Pitono, 2012).</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark24"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark25"></a>CONCLUSION</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">The present study concluded that essential oil of the leaves and fruits of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> contained Apiol compounds in high concentrations. The essential oil has been confirmed to inhibit the growth of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Aspergillus niger</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Cladosporium</span><span class="font4"> sp. The study promoted possibilities that essential oil of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">P. aduncum</span><span class="font4"> could be developed into industrial production of fungicides and insecticides. However, natural products should respect to cultivation, collection, and used in sustainable manner which conserves both of the plants materials and the environment.</span></p>
