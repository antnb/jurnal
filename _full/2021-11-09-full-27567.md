<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstrak</span></h2>
<p><span class="font0" style="font-style:italic;">Skripsi ini berjudul “Elipsis pada Klausa Koordinatif Bahasa Inggris pada Novel Berjudul Rowan’s Mill oleh Elizabeth Walker”. Penelitian ini bertujuan untuk mengetahui jenis-jenis elipsis dan menganalisis terjadinya elipsis pada klausa koordinatif. Topik ini dianalisis karena sering terjadinya ambiguitas dalam menentukan unsur-unsur pada klausa yang dapat dielipsis ketika dua klausa atau lebih digabungkan. Sumber data dari penelitian ini adalah novel berjudul Rowan’s Mill oleh Elizabeth Walker. Metoda yang digunakan dalam pengumpulan data adalah metoda dokumentasi dengan teknik pencatatan. Penelitian ini menggunakan metoda deskriptif kwalitatif untuk menganalisa data yang telah terkumpul dimana data yang telah dianalisis dengan teori yang diusulkan oleh Quirk, dkk. (1972) yang dipresentasikan dengan metoda deskriptif.</span></p>
<p><span class="font0" style="font-style:italic;">Ada enam jenis utama elipsis pada klausa koordinatif, yaitu: elipsis subjek, elipsis tambahan, elipsis subjek dan tambahan, ellipsis predikasi, elipsis kepala frase kata benda, dan elipsis subjek pelengkap. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Hasil penelitian ini menunjukkan bahwa semua jenis elipsis dalam novel Rowan Mill oleh Elizabeth Walker ditemukan dan elipsis subjek adalah jenis elipsis yang paling sering terjadi. Dalam klausa koordinatif, elipsis dapat terjadi pada klausa bagian pertama dan klausa berikutnya. Elipsis disebut anaforis, cataforis, atau anaforis maupun cataforis. Namun, elipsis sebagian besar anaforis dengan kemunculan item diwujudkan dalam bagian pertama dari serangkaian klausa.</span></p>
<p><span class="font0" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci:Elipsis, klausa koordinatif, anaforis, cataforis.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Language is very important in human life. It is a system of vocal sounds and combinations of such sounds to which meaning is attributed. The use of the expression or communication of such a system is a written representation of such a system (Agnes, 2005:805). The most important function of language is to communicate or give information to other people. However, sometimes we do not use the full grammatical structure in communication. People mostly come across sentences in which some elements are deleted. Indeed, they do not realize that their interpretation of the sentence is hindered by such omission known as ellipsis.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In English, the sentences that are joined by the coordinating conjunction are called coordinated clauses and ellipsis is frequently involved in the coordination of the clauses. It means that there is such avoiding repetition in which certain elements of the clauses are often omitted.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Quirk et al. (1972: 569) in their book entitled </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">A Grammar of Contemporary English</span><span class="font1"> claimed that ellipsis in coordinated clauses is intended to avoid repetition of the same elements of the clauses and the ellipsis can be divided into: ellipsis of subject, ellipsis of auxiliary, ellipsis of subject and auxiliary, ellipsis of predication, ellipsis of head of noun phrase and ellipsis of prepositional phrase. Indeed, a sentence with ellipsis is acceptable only if the same sentence is acceptable after ‘the deleted’ words are restored.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Ellipsis in coordinated clauses is an interesting phenomenon to be discussed because there is often repetition when we link one or two clauses using coordinate conjunctions (</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and</span><span class="font1">, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">or</span><span class="font1">, and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">but</span><span class="font1">). Beside the repetition, sometimes there is also ambiguity in determining elements of the clauses that can be deleted when we combine two or more clauses.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In order to avoid the repetition and ambiguity, it is important to know the rules of ellipsis, particularly the ellipsis which occur in coordinated clauses. Therefore, this study focuses on ellipsis in coordinated clauses using the coordinators </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and</span><span class="font1">,</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">or</span><span class="font1">, and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">but</span><span class="font1">. The data were taken from a novel entitled </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Rowan’s Mill</span><span class="font1"> by Elizabeth Walker.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis of Ellipsis in English Coordinated Clauses in the Novel Entitled </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“Rowan’s Mill”</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"> by Elizabeth Walker</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The discussion presents the analysis of types and the occurrences of ellipsis in coordinated clauses found in the novel entitled </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“Rowan’s Mill”</span><span class="font1"> by Elizabeth Walker using the theory proposed by Quirk, et al (1972).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>5.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis of Types of Ellipsis in English Coordinated Clauses</span><br><br><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark16"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Subject</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">He</span><span class="font1"> ran across to his Jaguar and climbed in beside his wife (P. 2).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The example above indicates that only by the occurrence of the subject subsequent to the first to be deleted. In the first example the subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">he</span><span class="font1"> in the first clause is identical with the subject in the second clause. Therefore, the subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">he </span><span class="font1">is deleted in the second clause.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">He </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">ran across to his Jaguar and […] climbed in beside his wife.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">He </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">ran across to his Jaguar and [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">he</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] climbed in beside his wife.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark17"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark18"></a>2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Auxiliary</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">So much better than the lukewarm dribble at the mill cottage, she told herself, where the towel </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was</span><span class="font1"> rough and the bathroom freezing (P.193).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">This example shows that the only the auxiliary is deleted. The auxiliary can be deleted in the last clause, where the deleted auxiliary in this example is </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">So much better than the lukewarm dribble at the mill cottage, she told herself, where the towel </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">rough and the bathroom […] freezing.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">So much better than the lukewarm dribble at the mill cottage, she told herself, where the towel </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">rough and the bathroom [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] freezing.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark19"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark20"></a>3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Subject and Auxiliary</span></h2></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">I &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;was</span><span class="font1"> blind, and wet, and thrusting (P. 214).</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical items can be found in the second and last clauses. Apart from the auxiliary, the subject can also be deleted in those clauses since the auxiliary and the subject are identical. The auxiliary </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was</span><span class="font1"> and the subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">I</span><span class="font1"> are deleted in this example.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">I was </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">blind, and […] wet, and […] thrusting.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">I was </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">blind, and [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Iwas</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] wet, and [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Iwas</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] thrusting.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark21"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark22"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Predication</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The ellipsis of predication can be treated under four main headings and each of them will be analyzed in this section.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark23"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark24"></a>4.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of the First Part of Predication</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The first part of the predicate or of the predication may be deleted, and the subject may be deleted as well.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Lexical Verb</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">She looked</span><span class="font1"> wistful and strange (P. 188).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">she </span><span class="font1">and the lexical verb </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">looked </span><span class="font1">which occur in the first clause are identical with the second clause, based on this condition they are allowed to be deleted in the second clause.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">She looked </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">wistful and […] strange.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">She looked </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">wistful and [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">she looked</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] strange.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Verb Including Auxiliary</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">He was wearing</span><span class="font1"> jeans and an open-necked shirt (P. 13).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The coordinated clauses with coordinating conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">and</span><span class="font1"> have the auxiliary </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was</span><span class="font1"> and the verb </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">wearing</span><span class="font1"> which are deleted. Beside the auxiliary </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was </span><span class="font1">and the verb </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">wearing</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">,</span><span class="font1"> the subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">he</span><span class="font1"> is also deleted in the second clause.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">He was wearing </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">jeans and […] an open-necked shirt.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">He was wearing </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">jeans and [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">he was wearing</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] an open-necked shirt.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">c. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Verb and Subject Complement</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Ellipsis of verb and subject complement are analyzed as follows:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">For examples:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">One sharp word or gesture and all confidence </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was gone</span><span class="font1"> (P. 247).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The verb and subject complement are deleted in the first and the second clauses in order to avoid the repetition namely; </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">gone</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">. </span><span class="font1">The realized items are only present in the last clause.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">One sharp word […] or gesture […] and all confidence </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was gone</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">One sharp word [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was gone</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] or gesture [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was gone</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] and all confidence </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">was gone</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">d. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Verb and Object</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">I knew I should </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">stop you</span><span class="font1"> but I couldn’t (P. 32).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical items occur in the second clause where the verb </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">stop</span><span class="font1"> and the object </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">you</span><span class="font1"> are deleted.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">I knew I should </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">stop you </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">but I couldn’t […].</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">I knew I should </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">stop you </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">but I couldn’t [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">stop you</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">].</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark25"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark26"></a>4.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Whole Predication</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Saul and Rowan </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">padded after her</span><span class="font1"> (P.211).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The ellipsis of the whole predication </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">padded after her</span><span class="font1"> takes place in the first clause with the realized item is in the last clause only.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Saul […] and Rowan </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">padded after her</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Saul [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">padded after her</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] and Rowan </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">padded after her</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark27"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark28"></a>4.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Subject Complement or Direct Object</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">She</span><span class="font1"> slipped and dropped </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the case</span><span class="font1"> (P. 228).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In this example the direct object </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the case</span><span class="font1"> in the first clause and the subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">she </span><span class="font1">in the second clause are deleted.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical forms:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">She </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">slipped […] and […] dropped </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the case</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete forms:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">She </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">slipped [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the case</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] and [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">she</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] dropped </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the case</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark29"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark30"></a>4.4 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Adverbial</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Rowan</span><span class="font1"> picked up the round pink glob and nibbled at it </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">cautiously</span><span class="font1"> (P.208).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The adverbial </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">cautiously</span><span class="font1"> is deleted cataphorically in the first clause but it is realized in the last clause and the subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Rowan</span><span class="font1"> is deleted anaphorically in the last clause.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Rowan </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">picked up the round pink glob […] and […] nibbled at it </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">cautiously</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Rowan </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">picked up the round pink glob [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">cautiously</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] and [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Rowan</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] nibbled at it </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">cautiously</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark31"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark32"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Head of Noun Phrase</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">They</span><span class="font1"> were to spend </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">t</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">hree </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">days</span><span class="font1"> in Tokyo and then go on to Tokyo for </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">two </span><span class="font1">(P. 194).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The head </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">days</span><span class="font1"> in the noun phrase </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">three days</span><span class="font1"> is deleted anaphorically. Besides , the ellipsis of subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">they</span><span class="font1"> was also found in this example where the subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">they </span><span class="font1">is deleted in the last clause.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">They </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">were to spend three </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">days </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">in Tokyo and then […] go on to Tokyo for two […].</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">They </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">were to spend three </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">days </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">in Tokyo and then [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">they</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] go on to Tokyo for two [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">days</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">].</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark33"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark34"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis of Complement of Prepositional Phrase</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Because I often go to </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Japan</span><span class="font1"> and should love to show around (P. 189).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complement of the prepositional phrase </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Japan</span><span class="font1"> and the subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">I</span><span class="font1"> are deleted in the second clause, where the identical items lie in the first clause.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Because I often go to </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Japan </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and […] should love to show around […].</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Because I often go to </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Japan </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">I</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] should love to show around [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Japan</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">].</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark35"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark36"></a>5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis the Occurrences of Ellipsis in English Coordinated Clauses in the Novel Entitled </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“Rowan’s Mill”</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"> by Elizabeth Walker</span><br><br><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark37"></a>a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Anaphoric</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">He looked</span><span class="font1"> very tall and very powerful (P. 194).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">This example says anaphoric since the subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">he</span><span class="font1"> and the verb </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">looked</span><span class="font1"> are deleted in the second clause and the realized items are in the first clause.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">He looked </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">very tall and […] very powerful.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">He looked </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">very tall and [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">he looked</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] very powerful.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark38"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark39"></a>b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Cataphoric</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Saul and Rowan </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">padded after her</span><span class="font1"> (P. 211).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In example (47), the elliptical items </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">padded after her</span><span class="font1"> takes place in the first clause with the realized item in the last clause only.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Saul […] and Rowan </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">padded after her</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Saul [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">padded after her</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] and Rowan </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">padded after her</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark40"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark41"></a>c. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Both Anaphoric and Cataphoric</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Saul</span><span class="font1"> reached out and took </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the cup</span><span class="font1"> away from her (P. 213).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In example 48, the subject </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Saul</span><span class="font1"> is deleted anaphorically while the direct object </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the cup</span><span class="font1"> is deleted cataphorically.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The elliptical form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Saul </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">reached out […] and […] took </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the cup</span><span class="font1"> away from her.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The complete form:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Saul </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">reached out [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the cup</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] and [</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Saul</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">] took </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the cup</span><span class="font1"> away from her.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark42"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark43"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Based on the discussion in the previous chapter, it can be concluded that all of the types of ellipsis in the novel </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Rowan’s Mill</span><span class="font1"> by Elizabeth Walker were found. All of those types are ellipsis of subject, ellipsis of auxiliary, ellipsis of subject and auxiliary, ellipsis of lexical verb, ellipsis of verb including auxiliary, ellipsis of verb and subject complement, ellipsis of verb and object, ellipsis of whole predication, ellipsis of subject complement or direct object, ellipsis of adverbial, ellipsis of head of noun phrase, and for the last is ellipsis of complement of prepositional phrase. The result of this study shows that ellipsis of subject is the most frequently type of ellipsis that occurred in the data.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In coordinated clauses, the ellipsis can occur in the first and in the subsequent clauses. Ellipsis may be simple or complex. In the simple ellipsis, either ellipsis occurs in only one of the conjoined clauses or identical items are deleted in two or more conjoined clauses. In the complex ellipsis, the elliptical elements are both anaphorically and cataphorically deleted in the same sentence. Ellipsis can be anaphoric with the realized items in the first of series of clauses, cataphoric with the realized items in the last of a series of clauses or both anaphoric and cataphoric. However, the ellipsis is mostly anaphoric with the realized items in the first of a series of the clauses.</span></p>
