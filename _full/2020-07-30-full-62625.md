<h3><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Introduction</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Passive and antipassive are constructions that can be formed to reduce the valency of a verb (Dixon and Aikhenvald, 2000: 20). The term passive is used for a derivative structure from an underlying clause in accusative language, whereas antipassive refers to a derivative structure in an ergative language. If in passive construction, the decrease in valency or the reduction of core arguments is done through the deletion of the A argument, in antipassive construction, the deletion is not on A but P/O (Palmer, 1994 and Dixon 2012). For more details, the antipassive form can be seen in the example below:</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(1a) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Gopnyan teungoh </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">geu-sampah </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">broh</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">He &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;PROG 3SG-sweep garbage</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘He is </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">sweeping </span><span class="font2">the garbage’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(1b) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Gopnyan teungoh </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">geu-s-eum-ampah</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">He &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;PROG 3SG-AP-sweep</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘He is </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">sweeping</span><span class="font2">’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Sentence (1) is an example of Acehnese antipassive construction taken from Hanafi (2006: 28). The underlying construction is the construction of a transitive sentence with two core arguments; </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Gopnyan</span><span class="font2"> 'he' as A and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">broh</span><span class="font2"> 'garbage' as O. When forming the antipassive construction, the A </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Gopnyan</span><span class="font2"> becomes S and the original O </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">broh</span><span class="font2"> is deleted. Deleting the O argument resulting the verb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">sampah</span><span class="font2"> 'sweep' in getting the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-eum-</span><span class="font2"> infix as an antipassive marker (AP) and reducing the valency of the verb to an intransitive verb with a single argument (S). The prefix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">geu-</span><span class="font2"> attached to the verb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">sampah</span><span class="font2"> is a third-person marker cross-referenced to the A/S (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Gopyan</span><span class="font2">).</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Research related to passive and antipassive in Austronesian languages has been carried out by many language researchers in the world. In Sasak language itself, which is an Austronesian language, there are at least quite a number of published articles discussing passive construction including those conducted by Muhid (2014) and Johandi (2017). The research conducted by Muhid (2014) on Sasak language used in central Lombok shows that the passive construction of the dialect can be formed by using prefix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">te-</span><span class="font2">, confix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">te- -an/in, ke- -an</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ken- -an</span><span class="font2">, which functions as a passive marker (PAS). This research also shows that passive construction can appear with unmarked verbs. All of these passive forms are complemented by the ‘by phrase’ with the preposition </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">isiq</span><span class="font2">, in which its presence can be removed (optional). Differ to Muhid, Johandi (2017) examined Sasak language used in the eastern part of Lombok. The result of his research shows that passive construction can be formed by adding prefixes </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ke-</span><span class="font2">, confix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ke- -an</span><span class="font2"> and no morphological markers. In passive with no morphological marker, the A is undeletable. This usually occurs when the A is a third person singular (3SG).</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">For antipassive construction, researches related to Sasak language so far has only been conducted by foreign researchers. One of them is Asikin-Garmager (2017) who discusses the Sasak antipassive construction in his dissertation entitled 'Sasak Voice'. His findings show that in Sasak language used in some parts of the central, eastern and northern Lombok the antipassive markers (AP) lay on the use of nasal prefixes (</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">m-</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ace</span><span class="font2"> ‘read’</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">nen-</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">alѐt</span><span class="font2"> ‘plant’</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">meny-</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ѐran ‘</span><span class="font2">hunt’) attached to the transitive verbs. The addition of the nasal prefix causes the A to change its function into S and the O from the underlying construction is deleted.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">By looking at the results of the previous researches on Sasak passive and antipassive construction, it would be very interesting to also see the formation of these constructions specifically in Sasak Kuto-Kute dialect (SKD). The purpose of this study is to look at the forms of passive and antipassive constructions in SKD in terms of valency decrease mechanism, which are limited to the list of 70 verbs from Andrej Malchukov and Bernard Comrie (2010).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Method</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The research method used in this study is a qualitative descriptive method due to the purpose of this method is in accordance to the purpose of the study that wants to describe the language data naturally. The data is collected based on the language phenomena that are indeed used by the speakers of SKD while still taking into account that the collected data is grammatically and semantically acceptable. In its implementation, there were three stages conducted: (1) the initial stage; the qualitative researcher see everything that is still in place in general, (2) the second stage; the stage of reduction or focus by selecting which data is important, useful, and new to be grouped into various categories that are determined as the focus of the research, and (3) the third stage or selection stage; the researcher describes the focus into more detail and conduct in-depth analysis of the data and the obtained information. The data were collected using the list of 70 verbs proposed by Andrej Malchukov and Bernard Comrie (2010) and analyzed using the theory of linguistic typology proposed by Dixon (2012). The data</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">collection was carried out in 7 villages within the Tanjung district, namely the villages of Sigar Penjalin, Sokong, Medana, Tanjung, Teniga, Jenggala and Tegal Maja.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Discussion</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Passive and antipassive are two constructions among several that can be used to reduce the valency of a verb. Generally, both constructions derived from transitive clauses forming intransitive clauses. Both constructions have several similarities and differences. To know the characteristics of these two constructions, Dixon (2010: 206 - 208) distinguishes the two as follows. The criteria for canonical passive derivative forms that can be applied to transitive clauses include:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(a) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;apply to the underlying transitive clause and forms a derivative intransitive clause;</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">(b) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;the NP of the original object (O) becomes the subject (S) of the intransitive clause;</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">(c) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;the NP of the original agent (A) enters the peripheral function, marked by a non-core case, preposition, and so on; this NP can be deleted with an option of including it; and</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">(d) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;there are some explicit formal markers in passive construction, such as the morphological process (affixation) on the verb or periphrastic verbal construction as found in English.</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The criteria for canonical antipassive derivative forms include:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(a) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;apply to the underlying transitive clause and forms a derivative intransitive clause;</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">(b) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;the NP of the original agent (A) becomes the subject (S) of the intransitive clause;</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">(c) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;the NP of the original object (O) enters the peripheral function, marked by a non-core case, preposition, and so on; this NP can be deleted with an option of including it; and</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">(d) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;there are some explicit formal markers of antipassive construction (probably similar to those as in passive construction).</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Based on the mentioned criteria, it is known that the differences between passive and antipassive constructions lay in points (b) and (c) where the main focus is on deriving the S and O functions. In the passive construction, the S function in the intransitive clause is filled by O from the transitive clause and the original A becomes the peripheral argument, whereas the antipassive construction applies vice versa: the S function is filled by A from the transitive clause and O becomes the peripheral argument. To figure out the forms of passive and antipassive constructions in SKD, the next discussion will explain some examples of sentences found in the data.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>3.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Passive</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Passive construction in SKD can appear in several forms. One of them is through the marking on the verb or called the morphological marker. An example of this construction is found in sentence (2):</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(2)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ku &nbsp;</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ke-lele-n</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-nya</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">1SG PASS-laugh-</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font2">-3SG</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">‘I </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">was laughed (at) </span><span class="font2">(by him)’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Sentence (2) has a morphologically passive marker on the verb. The marker is the confix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ke- -n</span><span class="font2"> (an allomorph of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ke- -an</span><span class="font2">), which is marked PAS (passive) and attached to the verb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lelѐq </span><span class="font2">(laugh). Example (2) is the derivation of the transitive active sentence </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Ia ngelelѐn ku</span><span class="font2"> ‘He laughed at me’. In the passive construction, the A (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Ia</span><span class="font2"> ‘He’) of the active sentence moves to the position after the verb and the O (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ku</span><span class="font2"> 'I') occupies the grammatical S position. The movement of A into the peripheral position, in this case, is not marked by the use of the 'by' preposition or in SKD is known as the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq</span><span class="font2"> preposition, therefore the A argument can appear as a post-clitic attached to the</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">verb. However, the post-clitic is optional because it is a peripheral argument, which can be seen clearly through its complete construction </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Ku kelelѐn siq ia</span><span class="font2"> ‘I was laughed (at) by him’. It should be noted that in SKD if the A is a 3SG its appearance in the passive construction can be in the form of post-clitic.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Differ to sentence (2), sentences (3) - (8) are passive constructions that are syntactically marked. See the following examples:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(3)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Terijen nu &nbsp;</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ng-isin siq </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Dimas</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Jerry can DEF N-fill PREP Dimas</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘The jerry can </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">is filled </span><span class="font2">by Dimas’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(4)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Kupi </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">m-bait </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq Andi</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Coffee N-take PREP Andi</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘The coffee </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">was taken </span><span class="font2">by Andi’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(5)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Sampi nu </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">taliq-ang </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq Rudi kon lolon kayuq</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Cow DEF tie-</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ang</span><span class="font2"> PREP Rudi on tree</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘The cow </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">was tied </span><span class="font2">by Rudi on the tree’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(6)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Jejojaq baronya </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">demen-ang </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq beaq mama nu</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Toy new-POSS like-</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ang</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;PREP child boy DEF</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘The new toy </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">was liked </span><span class="font2">by the boy’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(7)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Beaq berik nu &nbsp;</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ng-aran-in </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq tau lokaq nu</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Child little DEF N-name-</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">in</span><span class="font2"> PREP man old DEF</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘The child </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">was given a name/named </span><span class="font2">by the old man’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(8)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Gabahnya &nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ng-goro-in </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq amaq</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Grain-POSS N-dry-</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">in</span><span class="font2"> PREP father</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘His grain </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">was dried </span><span class="font2">by father’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The six sentences use different forms of verbs in their passive construction. Data (3) and (4) take the nasal form, data (5) and (6) use verbs with suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-ang</span><span class="font2">, while data (7) and (8) use verbs with confix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">N- -in</span><span class="font2">. However, there are similarities among the six sentences, namely the use of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq</span><span class="font2"> preposition as the passive marker. These sentences derive from active transitive constructions with two core arguments: A and O. When forming a passive construction, the valency of the verbs decreases to intransitive verbs with a core argument functioning as grammatical S and a peripheral argument marked by the preposition </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq</span><span class="font2"> 'by' positioned before the NP. O is promoted into grammatical S, while A is demoted into a peripheral argument with preposition </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq</span><span class="font2">. In this case, the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq</span><span class="font2"> preposition is the only syntactic marker that states the six sentences as passive constructions.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In contrast to Dixon (2012: 206) who stated that there are explicit formal markers on passive constructions, in SKD it was found that there are passive constructions with no morphological or syntactic markers. Here are a few examples:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(9)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ku </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">takut-in-nya &nbsp;</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kon rorong</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">1SG scare-in-3SG on road</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘I </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">was scared/frightened </span><span class="font2">(by him) on the road’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Sentence (9) shows that the form of a passive sentence in SKD does not necessarily have to be marked by a morphological or syntactic marker. The above construction is known as a passive construction because it is a derivation of the active construction </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Ia nakutin ku kon rorong </span><span class="font2">'He scares me on the road', where A falls into the position of a non-core argument and O occupies the grammatical S position. The use of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq</span><span class="font2"> preposition in the above sentence can be placed before the non-core argument (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ia</span><span class="font2"> 'he'), however, in general, this form is rarely used. Based on the observation, if the A of the active sentence is a singular pronoun, either first (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ku</span><span class="font2"> 'me'), second (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">diq</span><span class="font2"> 'you') and third (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ia</span><span class="font2"> 'he'), as well as first plural pronoun (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kami</span><span class="font2"> 'us'), then the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq</span><span class="font2"> preposition can be removed. However, if the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq</span><span class="font2"> preposition is deleted, only the third singular pronoun (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ia</span><span class="font2">) can turn into a post-clitic (-</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">nya</span><span class="font2">) attached to the verb as seen in example (9). As for other pronouns (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ku, diq</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kami</span><span class="font2">), the forms remain the same and they cannot be changed into postclitics. The following examples illustrate this:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(10)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Cangkir nu </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">taoq </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">diq kon bon meja</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Cup DEF put 2SG on top table</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘The cup </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">was placed </span><span class="font2">(by you) on top of the table’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(11)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Bale nu </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">pengaq </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kami</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">House DEF built 1PL</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘The house </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">was built </span><span class="font2">by us’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(12)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Meong nu </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">pelewas </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ku</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Cat DEF throw 2PL</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘The cat </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">was thrown </span><span class="font2">(by me)’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>3.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Antipassive</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Antipassive construction was also found in the data of SKD. Antipassive is a construction that can be used to reduce the verb’s valence by making the A in the transitive clause into S in the intransitive clause, which is then followed by changing the function of the O to a non-core/peripheral argument or omitting/deleting the O argument. The examples of antipassive sentences in SKD are as follow:</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(13a) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Amangku </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">meny-ѐran </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">baq gawah</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Father-POSS AP-hunt on rice field</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘My father </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">hunt </span><span class="font2">on the rice field’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(13b) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Amangku </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ny-ѐran </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">terata baq gawah</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Father-POSS ACT-hunt chicken forest on rice field</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘My father </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">hunt </span><span class="font2">forest chicken on the rice field’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(14a) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Tau mama nu </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ng-enang-ang</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Person man DEF N-go-AP</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘The man has </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">left/died</span><span class="font2">’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(14b) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Tau mama nu </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ng-enang </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">balen amanya</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Person man DEF ACT-go house-POSS father-POSS</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘The man </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">left </span><span class="font2">his father’s house’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(15a) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Ia girang </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">me-lakoq-lakoq </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kon jalan</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">3SG like AP-ask-ask on road</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘He likes </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">to beg </span><span class="font2">on the road’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(15b) </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Ia girang </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">lakoq </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kepeng kon jalan</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">3SG like Øask money on road</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">‘He likes </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">begging </span><span class="font2">for money on the road’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Sentences (13a) - (15a) are antipassive constructions, while sentences (13b) - (15b) are the underlying active transitive constructions. From the four examples, it can be seen that in the antipassive constructions there are formal markers attached to the verb. The antipassive (AP) markers are the nasal prefix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">meny-</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">me-</span><span class="font2"> attached to the base word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">seran</span><span class="font2"> 'hunt’, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">saluk</span><span class="font2"> 'wear' and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lakoq</span><span class="font2"> 'ask', and the confix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ng- -ang</span><span class="font2"> attached to the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">enang</span><span class="font2"> 'go'. All the S in the antipassive constructions are the A from transitive active constructions. In these sentences, it can be seen that the two core arguments, which were in the position before the verb (S and A) are both actors. The use of these affixes has reduced the valency of verbs that initially required two core arguments to verbs with only one core argument.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Novelties</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Referring to Dixon’s statement (2012: 206) that passive constructions have explicit markings either on the verbs directly or as a periphrastic verbal construction, it was found that in this dialect some passive sentences have no markings at all. The examples are on sentences (10) – (12). The case was found to apply only to sentences where the A of the underlying transitive clauses are singular pronouns (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ku</span><span class="font2"> ‘I’, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">diq</span><span class="font2"> ‘you’ and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ia</span><span class="font2"> ‘s/he’) or the plural pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kami</span><span class="font2"> ‘we’.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">From the above analysis, it can be concluded that the passive construction in SKD can appear in 3 forms, namely the use of morphological markers on the verb through confix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ke- -n</span><span class="font2">, the use of syntactic markers </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">siq</span><span class="font2"> 'by' preposition, and the passive construction, which can only be detected from the movements of the arguments (no markers). For the third form, this construction can only appear if the A in the active sentence is a singular pronoun, either first (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">I</span><span class="font2"> 'me'), second (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">diq</span><span class="font2"> 'you') and third (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ia</span><span class="font2"> 'he'), and first plural pronoun (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kami</span><span class="font2"> 'we'). The syntactic passive marker in SKD is very productive and more common compared to the morphological marker. As for the antipassive construction, in this dialect, it was found that the construction is only formed through morphological marking. The AP markers found in the data are nasal prefixes of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">meny-</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">me-</span><span class="font2">, and confix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ng- -ang</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
