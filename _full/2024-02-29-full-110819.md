<h4><a name="bookmark9"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark10"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h4>
<p><span class="font3">Malaria is an disease caused by </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium</span><span class="font3"> genus and transmitted by female Anopheles mosquitoes via bites. The occurrence of antimalarial drug resistance in various places, such as Chloroquine and Artemisinin Combination Therapy, be a challenge for researchers to find new herbal and effective alternative antimalarial drugs. Pandan Wangi roots (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pandanus amaryllifolius Roxb.</span><span class="font3">) contain compounds with antimalarial properties. This study aims to determine the effect of Pandan Wangi roots ethanol extract at doses of 1, 100, and 1,000 mg/kgBW (Body Weight on the degree of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3"> parasitemia </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">in vivo</span><span class="font3">. This study employs an experimental in vivo method using the Post-Test Only Controlled Group Design. Sample of 24 mice were divided into one negative control group and three treatment groups. The data analysis technique employed is One Way ANOVA and Post Hoc Test. In this study, the mean degree of parasitemia in negative control group was 32.60%, treatment group with dose of 1 mg/kgBW was 22.27%, treatment group with dose of 100 mg/kgBW was 18.49%, and treatment group with dose of 1,000 mg/kgBW was 14.88%. The One Way ANOVA analysis resulted in p-value of 0.001, which is &lt;&nbsp;0.05, indicating a significant difference in the average degree of parasitemia among the three treatment groups. Thus, the effect of Pandan Wangi roots ethanol extract has antimalarial activity. Administration dose of 1,000 mg/kgBW proved to be the most effective in suppressing the growth of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3"> parasitemia.</span></p>

<h4><a name="bookmark11"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark12"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h4>
<p><span class="font3">Malaria is an disease caused by </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium</span><span class="font3"> genus and transmitted by female Anopheles mosquitoes via bites.<sup>1</sup> It has become one of the most severe infectious diseases, threatening nearly half of the world's population, particularly in regions like Africa.<sup>2</sup> As a result, malaria poses a life-threatening risk and a significant global health threat. Annually, approximately two billion people are at risk of contracting malaria.<sup>3</sup> Looking at malaria elimination in Indonesia, none of the provinces in the eastern part of the country has achieved elimination. Papua has the highest Malaria Morbidity Rate (Annual Parasite Incidence/API) at 52.99 per 1,000 population.<sup>4</sup> Symptoms of this disease include fluctuating fever, chills, diaphoresis, myalgia, nausea, vomiting, headache, muscle pain, and stiffness.<sup>5</sup></span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Resistance to antimalarial drugs, such as chloroquine, has been observed in most parts of the world, including Africa.<sup>6</sup> Resistance to artemisinin in </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium sp </span><span class="font3">infections<sup>7</sup> presents a challenges for researchers to discover new and effective selective antimalarial drugs.<sup>8</sup></span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Given the reality of resistance to chloroquine and artemisinin, efforts are needed to develop new herbal alternative antimalarial drugs. One such attempt involves</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">using the pandan wangi plant (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pandanus amaryllifolius Roxb.</span><span class="font3">), which is readily available in tropical and subtropical regions like Indonesia. This plant is known for its pleasant fragrance, containing chemical compounds such as alkaloids, saponins, steroids, flavonoids, terpenoids, tannins, polyphenols, and coloring agents.<sup>9</sup></span></p>
<h4><a name="bookmark13"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark14"></a>MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></h4>
<p><span class="font3">This study employs an experimental </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">in vivo</span><span class="font3"> method using the Post-Test Only Controlled Group Design conducted from March to August 2023. The sample used in this study consisted of 24 male Balb/c strain mice that had been infected with </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3">. The selection of these mice was based on inclusion requirements, specifically healthy male Balb/c strain mice weighing 25-30 grams and aged approximately 2-3 months. Exclusion criteria included mice that died or went missing. After meeting the criteria, the mice were divided into one negative control group and three treatment groups. The negative control group received distilled water and ethanol. In contrast, the three treatment groups were given ethanol extract suspension of pandan wangi roots at doses of 1 mg/kgBW, 100 mg/kgBW, and 1,000 mg/kgBW, respectively. The antimalarial activity in</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">this study was measured by calculating the percentage of parasitemia in each mice samples infected with </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3"> using The Peters' 4-day suppressive test method. The research involved the utilization of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3">, which was acquired from the Department of Parasitology, Faculty of Medicine at Universitas Udayana. This study has obtained ethical clearance with the number 786/UN14.2.2.VII.14/LT/2023 issued by the Research Ethics Commission (KEP) of the Faculty of Medicine, Universitas Udayana.</span></p>
<h4><a name="bookmark15"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark16"></a>Preparation of Ethanol Extract from Pandan Wangi Roots (</span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Pandanus amaryllifolius Roxb.</span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">)</span></h4>
<p><span class="font3">Fresh pandan wangi roots are cleaned thoroughly using running water. Subsequently, they are blended until they turn into powder. A total of 1 kg of pandan wangi root powder is then filtered or sieved again to obtain a finer powder, which is then dried at room temperature for 8 days (with an indicator of water content &lt;10%).<sup>9</sup> The dried powder sample is then macerated with 80% ethanol for 1 day. It is shaken for 3 hours using a shaker at a speed of 120 rpm. Afterward, it is filtered to get the filtrate and concentrated using a rotary evaporator to obtain a concentrated extract.<sup>10</sup> The extract is administered to the treatment groups according to the predetermined daily doses for 4 consecutive days (H</span><span class="font1">0</span><span class="font3">-H</span><span class="font1">3</span><span class="font3">) using a probe.</span></p>
<h4><a name="bookmark17"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark18"></a>Preparation and Grouping of Test Animals</span></h4>
<p><span class="font3">This study utilized a sample of 24 male Balb/c strain mice that had been infected with </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3">, each weighing between 25-30 grams and aged approximately 2-3 months. All mice must meet the inclusion criteria. The total number of cages required corresponds to the number of mice groups, which is four cages, with each cage housing six mice. Each mice is labeled differently for easy identification during the research. The mice are divided into one group as the negative control (K) and three as treatment groups (P1, P2, P3) with their respective predetermined doses.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3"> is first propagated within the bodies of 5 donor mice and left undisturbed for 2-3 days. If the parasitemia level in the donor mice reaches &gt;10%, they can be inoculated or transferred to the test mice. The inoculation process is carried out intraperitoneally with a volume of 0.2 cc.<sup>11</sup> The negative control group (K) was given distilled water and ethanol. In contrast, the treatment groups (P1, P2, and P3) were administered ethanol extract of pandan wangi roots at doses of 1 mg/kgBW, 100 mg/kgBW, and 1,000 mg/kgBW, respectively. The extract is administered orally to the mice for 4 days (H</span><span class="font1">0</span><span class="font3">-H</span><span class="font1">3</span><span class="font3">) using a probe.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">Preparation of Thin Blood Smears and Observation of Parasitemia Degree</span></p>

A drop of blood is collected from the mice's tail, and the smear is fixed with methanol and then air-dried. After air-drying at room temperature, the smear is stained with 10% Giemsa for 30 minutes, followed by washing with running water and air-drying again at room temperature. The examination of thin blood smears is conducted utilizing a light microscope at a magnification of 1,000 times. The calculation of the percentage of parasitemia is done by tallying the count of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3">-infected erythrocytes per 1,000 erythrocytes.<sup>12 </sup>Subsequently, the percentage of parasitemia and the inhibition percentage of the test substance against parasite growth are calculated using the following formula:<sup>13</sup></span></p>

<h4><a name="bookmark19"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark20"></a>Data Analysis</span></h4>
<p><span class="font3">The data in this study were analyzed using the </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Software Statistical Package for the Social Science</span><span class="font3"> (SPSS) version 26. The data analysis tools in this research are as follows:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To determine the antimalarial activity of ethanol extract of pandan wangi roots &nbsp;&nbsp;(</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pandanus</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">amaryllifolius Roxb.</span><span class="font3">), a descriptive analysis was conducted.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To assess the differences in parasitemia levels for doses of 1 mg/kgBW, 100 mg/kgBW, and 1,000</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">mg/kgBW of ethanol extract of pandan wangi roots (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pandanus amaryllifolius Roxb</span><span class="font3">.) compared to the negative control group, a two-sample mean difference test was employed. This was carried out using Post Hoc Test through Tukey analysis at a significance level (α) of 5%.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To examine the differences in parasitemia levels among doses of 1 mg/kgBW, 100 mg/kgBW, and 1,000 mg/kgBW of ethanol extract of pandan wangi roots (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pandanus amaryllifolius Roxb</span><span class="font3">.), a One-Way Analysis of Variance (One Way ANOVA) was utilized at a significance level (α) of 5%. The One Way ANOVA test requires two assumptions, namely the normality of distribution and the homogeneity of variances. Therefore, before conducting the One Way</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">ANOVA test, the Shapiro-Wilk test was utilized to assess normality, while Levene's test was employed for checking homogeneity of variance.</span></p>

<h4><a name="bookmark31"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark32"></a>DISCUSSION</span></h4>
<p><span class="font3">The results of this study indicate that the administration of ethanol extract from pandan wangi roots (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pandanus amaryllifolius Roxb.)</span><span class="font3"> has been proven to have an inhibitory effect on the growth of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3"> in male Balb/c mice. The administration of ethanol extract from pandan wangi roots in treatment 3 (P3) with a dose of 1,000 mg/kgBW has been proven to be the most effective in suppressing the parasitemia growth of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The ethanol extract of pandan wangi roots (Pandanus amaryllifolius Roxb.) administered to male Balb/c mice, which were already infected with </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei </span><span class="font3">parasites, contains alkaloid compounds with lactone/alkaloid lactone groups<sup>9</sup> compounds have been proven to have antimalarial activity when tested against </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium falciparum in vitro</span><span class="font3">.<sup>15</sup> Theoretically, the lactone alkaloid have potential as antimalarials and their mechanism of action is similar to the aminoquinoline antimalarial drugs.<sup>9</sup> Lactone alkaloids are among the alkaloids present in pandan wangi extract. These alkaloids act as antimalarials by inhibiting the detoxification of parasite haem in the food</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">vacuole, as well as acting as blood schizonticides and gametocides.<sup>16</sup> Alkaloids are also known as compounds that can influence the immune system, acting as immunostimulants.<sup>9</sup> In addition to alkaloids, pandan wangi root extract is also proven to contain active ingredients such as flavonoids, saponins, steroids, and terpenoids, which not only have the potential as antimalarial secondary metabolites but may also act as immunomodulators.<sup>15</sup></span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Another study conducted by Irmayanti and colleagues, in which they investigated the effectiveness of pandan wangi root extract as an antimalarial on the eosinophil counts with concentrations of 6.5%, 13%, and 26%, proved to reduce parasitemia levels more effectively than the negative control group, with average parasitemia degrees of 10.6%, 9.4%, and 6.8%, respectively.<sup>16</sup> Another study examining the effectiveness of pandan wangi root extract as an antimalarial on the neutrophil count was conducted by Sari and colleagues. They concluded that a concentration of 26% pandan wangi root extract demonstrated the best antimalarial activity, characterized by a decrease in parasitemia levels and an increase in neutrophils.<sup>15 </sup>Furthermore, supporting this research is a study by Mustofa and colleagues that investigated the effectiveness of methanol extract from pandan wangi roots on the lymphocyte count. The study concluded that the optimal extract concentration was 26%, exhibiting antimalarial activity with an 8.6% reduction in parasitemia levels.<sup>9</sup></span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Various herbal plants have been studied to prove the effectiveness of alkaloid compounds as antimalarials, such as papaya leaf extract (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Carica papaya</span><span class="font3">), guava leaf extract (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Psidium guajava</span><span class="font3">),<sup>17</sup> merkubung leaf extract (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Macaranga gigantea</span><span class="font3">),<sup>18</sup> gempol leaf extract (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Nauclea orientalis L.</span><span class="font3">),<sup>19 </sup>and noni fruit extract (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Morinda citrifolia L.</span><span class="font3">).<sup>20</sup> Based on the previous studies outlined above, it is evident that many herbal plants have the potential as antimalarials.</span></p>
<h4><a name="bookmark33"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark34"></a>CONCLUSIONS AND SUGGESTIONS</span></h4>
<p><span class="font3">Ethanol extract of pandan wangi roots (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pandanus amaryllifolius Roxb.</span><span class="font3">) exhibits antimalarial activity. The highest average parasitemia degree occurred in the negative control group (K), reaching 32.60%, with a parasite inhibition percentage (suppression) of 0.00%. On the other hand, the lowest average parasitemia degree occurred in the mice of treatment group 3 (P3), at 14.88%, with a parasite inhibition percentage (suppression) of 54.36%. The administration of ethanol extract from pandan wangi roots (Pandanus amaryllifolius Roxb.) at a dose of 1,000 mg/kgBW proved to be the most effective in suppressing the growth of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3"> parasitemia.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Further research on the effectiveness of doses of ethanol extract from pandan wangi roots </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">(Pandanus amaryllifolius Roxb.)</span><span class="font3"> is needed. This is because, considering that the most effective dose in suppressing the growth of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Plasmodium berghei</span><span class="font3"> parasitemia (administration of a dose</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">of 1,000 mg/kgBW) in this study only achieved a suppression (parasite inhibition percentage) of 54.36%, it is hoped that a more effective dose with more significant suppression percentage than 54.36% can be identified through further research.</span></p>
