<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTISARI</span></h2>
<p><span class="font9">Kelapa sawit (</span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Elaeis guineensis)</span><span class="font9"> merupakan salah satu komoditas utama di Provinsi Riau. Secara morfologi, batang kelapa sawit mempunyai lingkungan yang sesuai bagi pertumbuhan paku-pakuan epifit, karena bagian pangkal tangkai daun yang melebar sehingga dapat menampung serasah organik dan materi anorganik lainnya. Tujuan dari kajian ini adalah untuk mengetahui keanekaragaman jenis paku epifit yang tumbuh pada batang kelapa sawit. Sebanyak 125 individu kelapa sawit dari tujuh area kajian di Pekanbaru, Riau telah diteliti. Jumlah jenis paku epifit yang diidentifikasi pada penelitian ini adalah 16 jenis yang tergolong enam famili.</span></p>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci : paku epifit, kelapa sawit, Pekanbaru</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h2>
<p><span class="font9">Oil palm (</span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Elaeis guineensis</span><span class="font9">) is one main commodity in Riau Province. Morphologically, the trunk of oil palm has suitable environment to the growth of epiphytic fern, due to its broaden base of petiole that may accumulate organic and inorganic debris. The objective of this study was to investigate the diversity of epiphytic fern on the oil palm tree. A total of 125 oil palm trees from seven study sites in Pekanbaru, Riau were observed. The number</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">of epiphytic ferns identified in this study was 16 species</span></p>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Keyword: epiphytic fern, oil palm tree, Pekanbaru</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font9">The palm oil (</span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Elaeis guineensis)</span><span class="font9"> is one of the main commodity in Riau Province. The fruit of palm oil can be processed to produce palm oil. This species is a monoecious tall palm with leaf-bases adhere, the tree height may reach up to 20m. The petiole is 1.3-2.3 m long, 12.5-20 cm wide, broadened at base. Therefore, the base of petiole may accumilate leaves debrish or other litters and makes this part is moist and has suitable micro environment for the growth of epiphytic flora, especially fern. Epiphytic flora are plant which rely on other plants non-parasitically for support. It grows on trunks and branches rather than rooting themselves to the ground, or sometimes upon some other object. The epiphytic species may occur on three types of tree growth, i.e. vertical trunk, horizontal branch and horizontal crothch (Wooley and Lacher, 2008) depends on the root type of each species. Generally, the root of epiphytic plants has ability to derive support and assimilate water and nutrient from surface.</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">Fern is a botanical group known as Pteridophyta. This group has very distinct generation alternation from independent inconspicuous and sort-lived gametophyte (sexual) to conspicuous and dominant sporophyte (asexual) stages. Unlike mosses, fern have vascular bundle consisted of xylem and floem, therefore it is grouped into vascular plant but have neither seeds nor</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">belongs to six families.</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">flowers. The reproduction of fern uses spore. According to Schuettpelz (2007), almost 10% of leptosporangiate fern are epiphytic fern. These plant group need unique microhabitat on host bark or trunk (Roberts </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font9">. 2005)</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">The aims of this study was to investigate the diversity off the epiphytic fern on the oil palm tree from Pekanbaru, Riau. The oil palm plantations in this region are commonly located in both side of village road or folk garden, with various number of tree ranged from about 50 to 500 trees depends on the area. The age of oil palm trees in this study were ranged from 5 to 7 year.</span></p>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">MATERIAL AND METHOD</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Study sites and data collection</span></h2>
<p><span class="font9">Floristic data were collected from seven sites in Pekanbaru, Riau Province and a total of 125 oil palm trees were examined (Table 1). A survey was carried out to know which oil palm tree that had high epiphytic fern coverage and diversity. The selection of trees was conducted using purposive sampling based on the high diversity of epiphytic fern, and not based on the tree age.</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">Field observation included position of fern on trunk, the abundance of the species and the color of rhizome, petiole, leaf and sori. The abundance of epiphytic species that determine based on the precentage of each species occurance on tree selected was explained as follow, very</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">rare (&lt;20%), rare (21-40%), abundant (41-70%) and very abundant (&gt;70%). All collected specimens were labelled and dried to make herbarium. Herbarium specimens were prepared in Laboratory of Botany, Department of Biology, Faculty of Math and Natural Science, University of Riau. The morphological characters of each fern was carefully examined for species identification. All photographs were taken using two Canon and Nikon D70.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>Species identification</span></h2>
<p><span class="font9">All fern specimens were identified to species level based on Piggeot (1996). The morphological characters used for identification including vegetative characters (rhizom and leaf) and generative characters (sori and spora) The accepted name of each species were checked using The Plant List site (</span><a href="http://www.theplantlist.com"><span class="font9">www.theplantlist.com</span></a><span class="font9">). <h2><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>DISCUSSION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font9">The polypod ferns are grouped in Polypodiaceae family. Polypod group has long creeping and branched rhizome (Smith </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font9">. 2006), simple or pinnate fronds (Negi </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font9">. 2009). Species identified in this study i.e. </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Microsorum punctatum</span><span class="font9">, </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Microsorum pustulatum</span><span class="font9">, </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Phymatosorus scolopendria, Pyrrosia piloselloides</span><span class="font9"> and </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Goniophlebium percussum.</span><span class="font9"> Among these five polypod fern, only </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Pyrrosia piloselloides</span><span class="font9"> was an dimorphic fern, with diferent shape of fertile and sterile frond. </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Microsorum punctatum</span><span class="font9"> was characterized by its simple frond and small sori that irregularly arranged on both sides of costa. Morphologically, </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Microsorum pustulatum </span><span class="font9">and </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Phygmatosorus scolopendria</span><span class="font9"> had similar pinnatified pinnae with larger sori. The abundance of all of these three Polypodiaceae species were less abundant than Davaliaceae member. They were commonly found in the midle to the top of oil plam trunk, as these part are easier to be attached by uncovered sori carried by the wind.</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">Aspleniaceae is the family with the largest number of species within the Pteridophyta (Ekrt and Stech, 2008). This family is remarkable with its large morphological diversity (Sleep, 1983). The species of Aspleniaceae identified in this study were belong to genus </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Asplenium</span><span class="font9">, i.e. </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">A. nidus A. crinicaule</span><span class="font9"> and </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">A</span><span class="font9">. </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">monanthes.</span><span class="font9"> Among these three species, </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">A. monanthes</span><span class="font9"> was more abundant than </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">A. nidus</span><span class="font9"> and </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">A. crinicaule</span><span class="font9">. Morphologically, </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">A. monanthes</span><span class="font9"> and </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">A. crinicaule</span><span class="font9"> are more similar to each other, they had pinnate leaflet and long stipes, while </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">A. nidus</span><span class="font9"> had simple, long frond and short stipes. In spite of their diverse frond shapes, </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Asplenium</span><span class="font9"> species have similar sorus position and shape. Sori are linear, aranged regulary on both side of costa.</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">The most abundant epiphytic ferns found in oil palm trunk belong to Davalliaceae family, with three species identified, i.e.. </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Nephrolepis biserrata, Nephrolepis hirsutula</span><span class="font9"> and </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Davallia denticulata.</span><span class="font9"> The occurance of the two </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Nephrolepis</span><span class="font9"> species was very abundant in all study sites and sometime covered from basal to upper part of the oil palm trunk. According to Gauchan </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font9">. (2008), that in nature, this fern grow in a wide variety of situation, such as in the soil, among rocks or as an epiphyte. All of the </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Nephrolepis</span><span class="font9"> species are fertile all year, and spread by spores, rhizome, stolon and tuber. But tuber production apparently limeted to plants growing in humus (Nauman, 1981). If </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Nephrolepis</span><span class="font9"> species occured trought out the stem, </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Davallia denticulata</span><span class="font9"> was densely found in the basal of oil palm stem and rarely found in the top of stem. This species is dimorphic fern, with different fertile and sterile frond. The fertile frond is ussually narrower than steril one. Both fronds have triangular shape.</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">Blechnaceae and Gleicheniaceae with one species each, </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Stenochlaena palustris</span><span class="font9"> and </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Dicranopteris linearis</span><span class="font9"> were commonly found in the basal of the trunk. </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Stenochlaena palustris</span><span class="font9"> was dimorphic, creeping or climbing fern. The sterile pinnae is wider than fertile frond. </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Dicranopteris linearis</span><span class="font9"> is isomorphic and climbing fern. Figure 1. presents the morphology of all of the 16 epiphytic fern identified from oil palm tree.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>Morphological characters</span></h2>
<p><span class="font9">The morphological characters were observed directly in the field and also in the Botany Laboratory. The caracters of each species are presented below based on the observation in this study, and compared with the description by Piggeot (1996).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">1.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Asplenium nidus</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> L.</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Epiphytic or litophytic. Rhizome short, erect or ascending, dark brown. Roots scaly. Stipes stramineous to dark, 2-5cm long, scaly at the base. Fronds rossete, simple, up to 150 or more, 12–20cm broad, broadest in the middle, gradually narrowing toward both apex and base, light green, paler below; midrib raised on upper survace, flat below. Sori elongare along veins. Indusia present.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">2.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Asplenium crinicaule</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> Hance</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Epyphytic. Rhizome short, erect, densely scaly. Stipes usually up to 10cm long, dark brownish-purple to nearly black. Frond narrowly lanceolate, gradually narrowing towards both apex and base, 40cm long or more, 13cm wide or more. Lateral pinnae about 25 pairs, sessile, narrowing subtriangular to elliptic, apex acute, indistinctly lobed. Lobe with a few teeth. Sori long, crescent-shaped along posterior veinlet. Inducia entire. </span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">3. </span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Asplenium monanthes</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> L.</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">Rarely epiphytic. Stems erect or nearly erect, scaly. Leaves monomorphic. Pinnae extremely diverse, simple to 4-pinnate. Veins free to anastomosing. </span><span class="font9" style="text-decoration:underline;">Sori</span><span class="font9"> elongate along vein, ± lunate to linear. </span><span class="font9" style="text-decoration:underline;">Indusia</span><span class="font9"> usually present. </span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">4. </span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Stenochlaena palustris</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (Burm. f.) Bedd.</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome long, dark brown, creeping, climbing on tree trunk. Stipes ut to 17cm long. Frond 40–75cm long or more, bearing pinnae up to 15 pairs, opposite. Steril pinnae stalked, articulate, 18cm long or more, about 4cm cm wide, apex acuminate. Veins simple or forked. Fertile pinnae about 20cm long, 3mm wide, sporangia on the whole lower surface.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">5.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Nephrolepis biserrata</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (Sw.) Schott</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome ascending, brown, bearing a few fronds drooping at apex, numerous roots and stolon. Stipes up to 70cm or more, densely scaly with narrower scales. Laminae large, usually more than 140cm long, 20cm wide, lanceolate, narrowing toward both apex and base, pinnate. Lateral pinnae 50 pairs or more, linier-lanceolate, apex acuminate to caudate, base cuneate, sessile, serrate at the margin of posterior part. Sori round, in row at 1/3-1/2 way from margin to costa. Indusia reniform.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>6.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Nephrolepis hirsutula</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (G. Forst.) C. Presl</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome ascending, brown, bearing a few fronds drooping at apex, numerous roots and stolon. Scales dense, bicoloured with dark brown in the midle and paler in the margin. Stipes up to 60cm or more, scaly trhoughtout. Laminae large, usually more than 60cm long, 20cm wide, lanceolate, narrowing toward both apex and base, pinnate. Lateral pinnae 50 pairs or more, linier-lanceolate, apex acuminate to caudate, base cuneate. Sori round, submarginal. indusia reniform.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>7.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Davallia denticulata</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (Burm. F.) Mett. Ex. Kuhn</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome long, light to dark brown, creeping, densely</span></p>
<p><span class="font9">scale throughout, brown to dark brown. Laminae subtriangular, gradually narrowing toward apex, quadipinnatifid, 50cm long or more, 25cm wide or more. Pinnules oblong, lobed at the margin. Sori small, at every margin of lobes. Indusia cup-shaped.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">8.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Dicranopteris linearis</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (Burm. f.) Underw</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome long, brownish green, creeping or climbing on other plant. Stem grows from rhizome, green to brown. Main rachis dichotomous, devided into rachis branches. The branches fork 3-4 times, up to 7m or more. The two leafly branch has equal length 20cm long or more, 18cm broad or more, deeply pinnatified lobe, comb-shaped, , ligh green. Sori at the underside of the lobes. Indusia absent.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">9.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Microsorum pustulatum</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (G.Forst.) Copel</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Epiphyte. Rhizome climbing. Stipes short, scaly, swollen at the base. Fronds simple, shiny, light green, well-spaced along the rhizome, erect or weeping, 40cm long or more, 10cm wide or more, variable in shape (oblong to linear-lance shaped, unevenly three-pronged, and irregularly divided / pinnatifid). Sori globe-shaped, 2-4 mm in diametre. Indusia absent.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">10.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Microsorum punctatum</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (L.) Copel</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome creeping, dark or glaucous on the upper surface, scaly. Stipes not distinct from laminae, light green or stramineous. Laminae narrowly oblong to lanceolate, gradually narrowing toward both apex and base, up to 100cm long or more, 15cm broad or more. Midrib raised on bot surface. Sori small, round, iregullary aranged on the under surface of laminae.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark24"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark25"></a>11.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Phymatosorus scolopendria</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (Burm. f.) Pic. Serm</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome long, creeping, brown, bearing fronds more than 1cm apart, scaly. Stipes up to 50cm long, stramineous. Laminae oblong-ovate to subdeltoid, pinnatifid with broadly winged rachis, up to 40 long or more, 30cm broad or more. Lateral lobes up to 6 pairs, margin entire. Sori round or elongated, spead out on each mid rib side.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">12.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Pyrrosia piloselloides</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (L.) M.G. Price</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome long, creeping, brown, bearing fronds, 1–4cm apart, densely scaly. Fronds simple, distinctly dimorphic. Steril frond nearly circular, oblong, apex round, 4cm long or more, 1.5cm broad or more, fleshy, green. Fertile frond linear-lanceolate, apex round, gradually narrowing towards base, up to 15cm long or more, 1,5cm broad or more. Sori line-shape on the margin.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark26"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark27"></a>13.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Goniophlebium percussum</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (Cav.) Wagner &amp;&nbsp;Grether</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome long, brown to almost black, creeping, scaly. Stipe long, up to 150cm long or more. Fronds pinnate. Laminae dark green, 150cm long or more, widest at the base, margin entire to crenate, apex acuminate. Sori uniserial, costal, deeply sunken.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">14.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Vittaria ensiformis</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> Sw.</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome short, creeping, dark brown, scaly. Fronds simple, lamina extending to the rhizome, stipes absent. Laminae linear, 20cm long or more, 1 cm broad or more, narrowing toward both apex and base, light green, margin. Sori in two continuous lines inside the lamina margin.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;">15.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Vittaria graminifolia</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> Kaulf</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome short, creeping, dark brown, densely scaly. Frond simple, young frond bright red, paler toward base. Laminae pendent, linear, dark green at the middle toward base, redish at the apex, glossy, up to 20 cm long or more, 0.5cm wide or more, margin entire, apex acute. Sori in single submarginal groove on each side of midrib, dark brown, 0,5mm wide.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark28"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark29"></a>16.</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Vittaria scolopendrina</span><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"> (Bory) Schkuhr ex Thwaites</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font9">Rhizome short, dark brown, creeping, bearing close fronds, scaly. Lamina sessile, up to 40cm long or more, 3cm broad or more, gradually narrowing toward both apex and base. Sori in the groove inside the margin.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark30"></a><span class="font9" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark31"></a>CONCLUSION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font9">Oil palm (</span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Elaeis guneensis</span><span class="font9">) has suitable micro environment of epiphytic fern. A total of 16 epiphytic fern species from six families were identified in this study. The member of Davalliaceae, especially </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Nephrolepis bisserata </span><span class="font9">and </span><span class="font9" style="font-style:italic;">Nephrolepis hirsutula</span><span class="font9"> were the most abundance species, while Vittariaceae species was very rare species ocur in oil palm tree.</span></p>
