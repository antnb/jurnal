<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstrak</span></h1>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Penelitian yang berjudul Deverbal Adjectives in English with Reference to the Reader’s Digest Magazine dilakukan dengan tujuan untuk mengidentifikasi bentuk dan menganalisa fungsi dari kata sifat deverbal dalam bahasa Inggris yang ditemukan di majalah Reader’s Digest. Teori yang digunakan untuk menganalisa data adalah teori yang dikemukakan oleh Bauer (1983) dalam bukunya yang berjudul English WordFormation dan Huddleston dan Pullum (2005) dalam bukunya yang berjudul A Student’s Introduction to English Grammar. Hasil dari penelitian ini menunjukan bahwa ada dua bentuk dari kata sifat deverbal, yaitu bentuk dengan akhiran –ing dan – ed. Kemudian, arti dari kata sifat deverbal dapat berubah menjadi bentuk negative dengan menambahkan awalan –un. Selanjutnya, fungsi dari kata sifat deverbal bisa dalam bentuk frasa ataupun klausa. Fungsi dari kata sifat deverbal pada level frasa adalah sebagai subjek dan objek. Sedangkan, fungsi dari kata sifat deverbal pada level klausa adalah sebagai predikat.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Kata kunci: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">kata sifat deverbal, bentuk dan fungsi</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;What forms of deverbal adjectives are there in the sentences found in the printed English magazine</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Reader’s Digest</span><span class="font2">?</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">b) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;What are the functions of the deverbal adjectives in the sentences found in the printed English magazine </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Reader’s Digest</span><span class="font2">?</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Aims of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The aims of this study are as follows:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To identify the forms of deverbal adjectives, especially the participle –ing and –ed in the sentences found in the printed English magazine </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Reader’s Digest</span><span class="font2">.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">b) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To analyze the functions of the deverbal adjectives, especially the participle -ing and –ed in the sentences found in the printed English magazine </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Reader’s Digest</span><span class="font2">.</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Method</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The data were taken from the printed English magazine </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Reader’s Digest</span><span class="font2"> 1980 October edition. The magazine consists of 30 articles with 144 pages and contains deverbal adjectives.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The method used in this study was the documentation method, because the data were in the written text, that is, a magazine. The data were collected using the theories that are relevant to the topic discussion; the magazine containing 30 articles were read intensively; the data were noted down to make the data easily analyzed.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The collected data were analyzed using the qualitative method. The theory proposed by Bauer (1983) in his book </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">English Word-Formation</span><span class="font2"> was used to identify the forms of the deverbal adjectives. The Huddleston’s and Pullum’s theory (2005) in their book entitled </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">A Student’s Introduction to English Grammar</span><span class="font2"> was used to analyze the functions of the deverbal adjectives.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Result and Discussion</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Forms of Deverbal Adjectives</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.1.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Derivational Process of Deverbal adjectives</span></p></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">1 They are </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">starving</span><span class="font2"> (1980: 2)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The base form of the word starving is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">starve</span><span class="font2">. The verb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">starve</span><span class="font2"> appears in a mute (unpronounced) –e. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Starve</span><span class="font2"> means suffering or death that is caused by a lack of food. It is a free morpheme which can form a new word or can be turned into deverbal adjectives by adding the participle –ing. However, the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">starveing</span><span class="font2"> does not have any meaning. If the word ends in a mute (unpronounced) –e, then this –e has to be dropped. For instance, starv + -ing = starving. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Starving</span><span class="font2"> is a deverbal adjective which is used in a dramatic way to say that someone is very hungry.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The streets are </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">unpaved</span><span class="font2"> (1980: 45)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">unpaved</span><span class="font2"> is formed through some processes. The first process is the derivational process which changes the verb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">pave</span><span class="font2"> into the adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">paved</span><span class="font2">. The verb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">pave</span><span class="font2"> appears in a mute (unpronounced) –e. It is a verb used to cover the ground with a firm surface. According to Quirk </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">.(1985: 461), if the word ends in a mute (unpronounced) –e, this e will be dropped. Meanwhile, in the second processes, the adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">paved</span><span class="font2"> used to describe something that covered with a firm surface. It, however, can form a new word like </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">unpaved</span><span class="font2"> by adding the prefix un-. It changes the meaning into the negative form </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">unpaved</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Functions of Deverbal Adjectives</span><br><br><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark10"></a>5.2.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Functions of Deverbal Adjectives in Phrasal Level</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">1</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;A terrified mother</span><span class="font2"> handed me her baby (1980:4)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">A terrified mother</span><span class="font2"> is a noun phrase which consists of the determiner </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">a</span><span class="font2">, pre- modifier adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">terrified</span><span class="font2"> and head of the noun phrase </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">mother</span><span class="font2">. This noun phrase is functioned as the subject of the sentence.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The story poses </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">an interesting question</span><span class="font2"> (1980:71)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">An interesting question</span><span class="font2"> is a noun phrase that is structured out of three syntactic categories: the determiner </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">an</span><span class="font2">, the pre-modifier adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">interesting</span><span class="font2"> and the head of the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">question</span><span class="font2">. It follows the rules that a noun phrase consists of a pre-modifier and a head noun. It is functioned as the object of the sentence.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark11"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark12"></a>5.2.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Function of Deverbal Adjective in Clausal Level</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The story poses an interesting question (1980:71)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">The story poses an interesting question</span><span class="font2"> is a </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">clause</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">The story</span><span class="font2"> is a ‘noun phrase’ that consists of the determiner </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the</span><span class="font2"> and the head of the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">story</span><span class="font2">. Similarly, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">poses</span><span class="font2"> is classified as a verb phrase and itcan be taken as the head element.</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">An interesting question</span><span class="font2"> is a noun phrase that consists of the determiner </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">an</span><span class="font2">, the modifier adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">interesting</span><span class="font2"> and the head of the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">question</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In terms of syntactic functions</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the story</span><span class="font2"> functions as ‘the subject’ of the clause, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">poses an interesting question</span><span class="font2"> is ‘predicate’; within the VP that forms the predicate, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">an interesting question</span><span class="font2"> is ‘an object’ and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">poses</span><span class="font2"> is ‘predicator’. Huddleston and Pullum (2005: 63) stated that the two major elements in the clause are called </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">subject</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">predicate</span><span class="font2">. Meanwhile, predicator is used for the head of the VP.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The streets are unpaved (1980:45)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">The street are unpaved</span><span class="font2"> is a</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">clause</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">The streets</span><span class="font2"> are ‘noun phrase’ that consists of the determiner </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the</span><span class="font2"> and the head of the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">streets</span><span class="font2">. Similarly, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">are</span><span class="font2"> is classified as a verb phrase and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">are</span><span class="font2"> can be taken as the head element. It is in the form of a linking verb.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In terms of syntactic functions</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the streets</span><span class="font2"> functions as ‘the subject’ of the clause, while, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">are unpaved</span><span class="font2"> is ‘predicate’; within the VP that forms the predicate and</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">are</span><span class="font2"> is the ‘predicator’ that is used for the head of the VP.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark13"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark14"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">It can be concluded that the deverbal adjectives are built up by the base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">verb</span><span class="font2"> and suffixes -</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ing</span><span class="font2"> or -</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ed</span><span class="font2">. Furthermore, the meaning of deverbal adjectives can be changed into the negative form by adding prefixes </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">un-</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">It is shown in the structure of the phrase that deverbal adjectives have two functions. The deverbal adjective in phrasal level can function as </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">subject</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">object</span><span class="font2">. Meanwhile, in the structure of the clause, the function of deverbal adjective is as </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">predicate</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
