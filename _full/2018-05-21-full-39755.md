<h1><a name="bookmark3"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark4"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h1>
<p><span class="font4">Isolation and identification of arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) in cashew plants was carried out in Datah Village, Abang District of Karangasem Regency. This study aims to determine the diversity of mycorrhizal spores in the cashew rhizosphere and AMF infections in the roots of cashew plants. The result in microscopic observations found 5 genera of AMF spores such as </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus, Racocetra, Entrophospora, Septoglomus</span><span class="font4">, and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Acaulospora</span><span class="font4"> and the population of the genus </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus</span><span class="font4"> were found most dominant in the rhizosphere of cashew plants. The result of staining root with trypan blue obtained the internal structure of AMF in the form of vesicles, arbuscules and hyphae found in root tissue of cashew plants. The result of AMF DNA amplification with primer NS31 and AML2 showed 560 bp bands in electrophoresis with 1% agarose gel determined AMF infection in root of cashew plants.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Keywords</span><span class="font4">: Cashew, Arbuscular Mycorrhizal Fungi, Microscopic, Staining root, PCR</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark5"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark6"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h1>
<p><span class="font4">Cashew (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Anacardium occindentale</span><span class="font4"> L.) is one of the commodities that have been known by the people of Indonesia, especially the people of Bali. Cashew is one of Bali’s main commodities besides coconut, coffee, and cocoa (</span><span class="font3">Dinas Perkebunan Provinsi Bali</span><span class="font4">, 2013). Based on the statistics of </span><span class="font3">Dinas Perkebunan Provinsi Bali </span><span class="font4">in 2014, the area of cashew plantation in Bali is 12,285 ha with production of 3,498,72 tons or productivity of 445 kg / ha. Karangasem regency became the largest cashew production center in Bali with plantation area of 9,480 ha. Karangasem as the center for cashew production is located in three sub-districts with the largest area of</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">cashew plantations are Kubu, Abang, and Karangasem.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Abang sub-district has the largest dry land in Karangasem regency, which is 12,658 ha followed by Kubu, Rendang, and Karangasem sub-districts. Low potential dry land (marginal land) in terms of fertility and low rainfall are mostly found in the subdistricts of Kubu, Abang, and Karangasem (Bappeda Karangasem, 2003). The development of cashew plants in areas with marginal and dry climate conditions show that it is can compete with other plantation crops. However, cashew resilience against drought led to the production of only the range of 2-3 kg / tree / year. (Trisilawati et al., 2012).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The existence and use of mycorrhizal fungi as biological tool in agriculture can improve plant growth, productivity and quality without degrading the quality of soil ecosystems. In addition, the application of arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) can assist in the rehabilitation of critical land and increase the productivity of agricultural crops, plantations, forestry on marginal lands (Syah et al., 2007). Utilization of arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) is one of the ways in the development of cashew plants on critical land. The use of adaptive mycorrhiza to critical and dry land was needed therefore it is necessary to isolate and identify mycorrhizal either microscopically or molecularly on cashew plant in critical and dry land.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">This study aims to determine the diversity of mycorrhizal spores in the cashew rhizosphere and AMF infections in the roots of cashew plants.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark7"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark8"></a>MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></h1>
<p><span class="font4">The research was conducted on December 2016 until February 2017. Samples of soil and root of cashew were collected by diagonal sampling at the cashew plantation in Datah Village, Abang district, Karangasem. Analysis of the2 samples obtained was carried out at the Udayana University's Genetics and Biomolecular Resource Laboratory. AMF spores were isolated from 100 g soil samples by wet</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">sieving technique and were analyzed under compound microscope. Spore identification was done manually based on INVAM (at </span><a href="http://invam.caf.wvu.edu"><span class="font4">http://invam.caf.wvu.edu</span></a><span class="font4">) in relation to morphological characteristics as shape, size, colour, presence of structures like sporiferous saccule, and subtending hypha.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Internal structure of AMF on root were observed using 25 pieces (1 cm) of the cashew roots. The roots were subjected to bleaching in a solution of KOH 10 % at 100<sup>o</sup>C for 45 min, washed in water and subsequently immersed in </span><span class="font3">H</span><span class="font2">2</span><span class="font3">O</span><span class="font2">2 </span><span class="font3">3% at </span><span class="font4">100<sup>o</sup>C for </span><span class="font3">45 min, </span><span class="font4">washed in water and subsequently immersed in HCl 1 % for 30 min, followed by staining in 0.05 % trypan blue for 24 h thereafter remove the 0.05 % trypan blue then add lactoglycerol heated at 250°C for 5 min and were analyzed under compound microscope. The percentage of root infections by AMF was calculated with the slide method (Brundett et al., 1996). The colored roots were cut into 1 cm long pieces and arranged in a row of glass preparations 5 roots each sample. Infected root by AMF are known by the structure of AMF (vesicles, arbuscules, and hyphae) on roots.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Analysis infection of AMF in the roots with PCR technique analyzed using primer NS31 and AML2. Approximately 1 g of roots for each sample w</span><span class="font1">ere </span><span class="font4">ground in liquid nitrogen with mortar and pestle to break the cell wall and DNA was extracted</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">with a Geneaid Genomic DNA Mini Kit (Plant) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Each PCR mixture (20 µl) contained 1 µl DNA, 1 µl each primer), 5 µl PCR master mix solution, and 2 µl free water. An initial denaturation for 3 min at 94°C was followed by 30 cycles of 30 sec at 94°C, 1 min at 40°C and 1 min at 72°C and a final extension for 10 min at 72°C.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark9"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark10"></a>RESULT AND DISCUSSION</span></h1>
<p><span class="font4">The results of microscopic observation of AMF spores in this study found 5 genera of AMF spores such as </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus, Racocetra, Entrophospora, Septoglomus,</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Acaulospora</span><span class="font4">. The population of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus </span><span class="font4">were found most dominant in the cashew rhizosphere. According to Walker et al. (1992 in Warouw and Kainde, 2010) AMF spore populations are high when there are 14-</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">160 spores / 100 g of soil. The number of spores obtained in each sample can be seen in the Table 1.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">In each sample was found 2-4 different types of the AMF genera. The AMF genera found in sample 1 are 3 genera namely </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus, Racocetra and Entrophospora</span><span class="font4">. In sample 2 there are 4 genera of spore that are </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus, Racocetra, Entrophospora</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Septoglomus</span><span class="font4">, in sample 3 found 2 genera namely </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Acaulospora</span><span class="font4">, in sample 4 there are 3 genera of AMF that is </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus, Acaulospora</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Septoglomus</span><span class="font4">, and in sample 5 are 2 genera spores that are </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Racocetra</span><span class="font4">. The diversity of the genera of AMF spores found in cashew tree rhizosphere namely </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus, Racocetra, Entrophospora, Septoglomus</span><span class="font4">, and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Acaulospora</span><span class="font4"> showed that cashew plants can associate more than one genus of AMF.</span></p>

<p><span class="font4">According to Barker and Weeks (1991 on Wulandari et al., 2013) site differences and rhizosphere lead to difference in species diversity and population of fungi.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The genus </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus</span><span class="font4"> is the most dominant spore of the AMF on cashew rhizosphere compared to other spores. According to Husin et al. (2007) </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus </span><span class="font4">spores are found dominant in various rhizosphere in the critical land of Sumatra. Suamba et al. (2014) reported </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus</span><span class="font4"> spores were also found to be dominant in citrus plant rhizosphere in Kerta Village, Payangan</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">District, Gianyar Regency. According to Brundrett et al. (1996 in Delvian 2010), the genus </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus</span><span class="font4"> has an excellent adaptability to various environmental conditions. One of the advantages of adaptation of the genus </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus</span><span class="font4"> in the environment is the spores germitate faster. </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus</span><span class="font4"> spores only take 4-6 days of germination. Wang and Jiang (2015) report, the dominant number of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus </span><span class="font4">spores in the rhizosphere is due to the spores of the genus </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Glomus</span><span class="font4"> can produce more spores compared to other AMF genera in the same environment.</span></p>

<p><span class="font4">The results of microscopic observation on AMF spores in cashew root in this study found that there were infection of AMF on all samples. According to Wulandari et al. (2013), infection of AMF can be known with the presence of the internal structure (hypha, vesicles, and arbuscules) that was formed by AMF. The percentage of root infections by</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">AMF based on the calculation using the slide method, obtained AMF infection that occurs in the roots of cashew plants is 100%. According to Aldeman et al., (2006)., mycorrhizal infection can increase plant growth and its ability to utilize the nutrients present in the soil, especially P, Ca, N, Cu, Mn, K, and Mg elements. Mycorrhizal</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">colonization of plant roots can expand the root absorption plane in the presence of external hyphae that grows and develops through roots (Mosse, 2001). The precentage of AMF infection on cashew roots can be seen in the Table 2.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Internal structure of AMF on cashew root in this study found that the vesicles were round, slightly round, oval, and dark blue colored. According to INVAM (2017), vesicles were generally are oblong to elliptical in shape and usually stain darkly in trypan blue or other stain. Arbuscules were found to be shaped as colonization of branched trees on plant root cells. According to INVAM (2017), the shape of the arbuscula may vary within the same root or between roots depending on the homogeneity of stain diffusion and the reactivity to the stain. Arbuscules is a colonizing unit that has reached the deeper cortical cells and penetrates the cell wall to forms a complex</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">hyphae branch system, they usually seen as a small tree with branches (Gunawan, 1993 on Setiadi and Setiawan 2011). Internal hyphae were found shaped as blue thin threads on root tissues . According to INVAM (2017), internal hyphae are commonly found branched or unbranched and interconnected, they usually stain darkly in trypan blue and other stains. Pictures of the internal structure of AMF on cashew roots can be seen in Fig. 2.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">According to Smith and Read (2008) the most important structure in a symbiotic system with plant roots is arbuscules. Arbuscules are structures formed by internal hyphae and connect fungi with root cortical cells. This structure plays a role in nutrient traffic from fungi to plants and vice versa. The size of the infection, the development of external hyphae, and the production of spores depend on the environmental conditions of an area.</span></p>
