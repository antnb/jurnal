<h2><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h2>
<p><span class="font7">Some of fruit flies have been reported as the important pest on fruits and vegetables in the world</span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">.</span><span class="font7"> Agricultural Quarantine Agency Denpasar reported that there was new coming species (exotic) of fruit flies in Bali in 2014 based on the morphological identification, namely </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Bactrocera occipitalis.</span><span class="font7"> However </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Bactrocera dorsalis</span><span class="font7"> complex have similar morphological characters and have a less distinctive character for taxonomic identification, therefore it is difficult to identify fruit flies accurately. Based on that phenomena, the accurate identification is needed. One of the more accurate identification techniques is based on molecular identification using DNA-based barcode. To identify fruit flies, DNA-based barcode using mitochondrial cytochrome oxidase I (COI) gene has been conducted. PCR analysis using Fruit Fly MT-CO1-F (FFMT-CO1-F) 5’-GGAGCATTAATYGGRGAYG-3’ as forward primer and HCO 5’-TAAACTTCAGGGTGACCAAAAATCA-3’ as reverse primer was successfully amplified around 600 bp of COI gene of fruit flies. Based on similarity of sequence product, the species was identifiedas </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Bactrocera occipitalis</span><span class="font7"> and same result was revealed using morphological identification. Phylogenetic analysis of </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">B. occipitalis</span><span class="font7"> based on COI genes showed that </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">B. occipitalis</span><span class="font7"> from Bali were in the same groups with Bactrocera species from Tarakan and Philippines. In addition, </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Bactrocera occipitalis</span><span class="font7"> as exotic fruit fly is a new report in Bali, Indonesia.</span></p>
<p><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Keywords</span><span class="font7">: fruit flies, COI, Molecular identification, Phylogenetic analysis</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font7">Fruit flies (Diptera: Tephritidae) are among the most destructive agricultural pests in the world, eating their way through acres and acres of citrus and other fruits at an alarming rate and forcing food and agriculture agencies to spend millions of dollars in control and management measures. Genus of Bactrocera is a group of fruit fly with 450 members of species (Drew and</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">Hancock, 2000). In addition some of spesies </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Bactrocera</span><span class="font7"> were reported as an importance pest on fruits and vegetables (Allwood </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font7">, 1999). However, until now, the study of fruit flies has been traditionally biased towards applied aspects including the morphological identification, and lack of molecular approach. The traditional method for identification based on morphological characteristic has importance role to</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">understand species of fruit flies. On the other hand, the morphological identification has some weakness and not completely accurate. A morphological character occasionally is not describing the relationship with the genetic characters and some factors are caused by environmental interaction (Smith </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font7">, 2003; Siwi, 2004). Based on those phenomena the more accurate identification is needed, one of them is molecular identification. Molecular identification has been utilized for improving the accurate information from morphological character, molecular characters more stable compared with morphological character (Hidayat, 2005). DNA barcoding has gradually been verified as an effective tool for identifying species in a wide range of taxonomic groups (Jiang </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font7">., 2014). DNA barcoding using Gen Mitokondrial </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Cytochrome Oxidase Subunit I</span><span class="font7"> (MT-CO1) has been utilized for identification of fruit flies. The MT-COI barcode sequences for the diagnosis of fruit flies using 1426 sequences for 73 species of Bactrocera distributed worldwide was reported by Jiang </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font7">. (2014). In addition, mitochondrial DNA also has been used to describe the genetic variation of animal (Loftus </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font7">., 1994; Suryanto, 2003). As new information, Agricultural Quarantine Agency Denpasar reported that in 2013-2015 there was a new coming species or exotic fruit fly in Bali namely </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Bactrocera occipitalis</span><span class="font7"> based</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">on their morphological characteristics. In this study, DNA barcoding using MT-COI gene was performed to reconfirm both species.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></h2>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>Fruit fly Trapp</span></h2>
<p><span class="font7">The method of trapping of fruit fly was conducted base on protocol of survey of pests and diseases by Agricultural Quarantine Agency. Trapping area was designed all regencies around Bali using </span><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">(</span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Trapping</span><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">) </span><span class="font7">tipe Steiner </span><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">(</span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Steiner Trap</span><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">) </span><span class="font7">with </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Cue lure</span><span class="font7"> and </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Methyl eugenol</span><span class="font7"> attractant</span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">.</span><span class="font7"> The collecting of fruit fly was conducted every week to collect sample for morphological and molecular identification. Sample </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">B. occipitalis</span><span class="font7"> Tarakan was collected from Laboratory of Agricultural Quarantine Agency Tarakan.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>Morphological identification</span></h2>
<p><span class="font7">The morphological identification was conducted as the first method to recognize the species of fruit fly. However, the method requires expert examination and may require additional supporting evidence such as the molecular diagnosis or host association records. Morfological identification was conducted based on identification key on book guide </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">”Fruit Flies of Indonesia : Their Identification, Pest Status and Pest Menegement”</span><span class="font7"> by Griffith University, Brisbane, Australia and </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">The Australian Handbook for the Identification of Fruit Flies </span><span class="font7">(Plant Health Australia, 2011). Key features</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">used for the morphological diagnosis of adult fruit flies include: wing morphology and infuscation, presence or absence of various setae, and relative setal size. (Note: Chaetotaxy, the practice, of setal taxonomy, is not as important in this group as some others.), overall colour and colour patterning, as well as presence, shape and color of thoracic vittae, a vitta is a band or stripe of colour (Plant Health Australia, 2011).</span></p>
<p><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">Molecular Identification</span></p>
<p><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">DNA extraction and MT-COI gen amplification</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">Qiagen DNeasy® (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) Blood and Tissue kit was used to extract the DNA of fruit flies. The result of DNA extraction was amplified using PCR with general primers MT-CO1 Fruit Fly MT-CO1-F (FFMT-CO1-F)5’-GGA GCA TTA ATY GGR GAY G-3’ as forward primer and HCO 5’-TAA ACT TCA GGG TGA CCA AAA ATC A-3’ as reverse primer (Plant Health Australia, 2011). PCR amplification was performed in a 25 μl final reaction volume consisting of master mix 12,5 µl (1x </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">BSA</span><span class="font7">, 10x </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Buffer</span><span class="font7">, dNTP’s, MgCl2, dan NEB Taq), 1 µl LCO1490-F, 1 µl HCO-R, DNA template 3 µl, dan </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Nuclease free water</span><span class="font7"> 7,5 µl., was conducted in </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Thermal cycler.</span><span class="font7">The PCR thermal cycle program consisted of initial denaturation at 94 °C for 2 min, followed by 40 cycles of denaturation at 94</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">°C for 30 s, annealing at 52 °C for 30 s and extension at 72 °C for 1 min with final extension at 72 °C for 7 min and finally holding at 4 °C until analyzed. (Plant Health Australia, 2011).</span></p>
<p><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">Electrophoresis, sequencing and phylogeny analysis</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">The PCR products with 1 µl loading dye were analyzed by gel electrophoresis on 2 % agarose gel at 70 V for 120 min in 1× TAE Buffer. DNA amplification product was visualized and captured by UV light using UV transilluminator at 302 nm wavelengths. The DNA target of both of fruit flies is </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">around</span><span class="font7"> 600 bp (Delomen </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font7">, 2013, Pramudi </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font7">, 2013).</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">PCR product of fruit flies was analyzed in First Base Laboratorium, Malaysia for DNA sequencing., The sequence of nucleotide was used to search similarity in </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">GeneBank</span><span class="font7"> with Program </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Basic Local Alignment Tool</span><span class="font7"> (BLAST) (NCBI 2014) on site of </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">the European Bioinformatic Institute</span><span class="font7"> (EBI) </span><a href="http://www.ebi.ac.uk/"><span class="font7">(www.ebi.ac.uk)</span></a><span class="font7">. The homolog sequence of nucleotide of fruit flies was aligned to understand the similarity score using program ClustalW. Data from the sequencing of MT-COI DNA of fruit flies were analyzed using BioEdit 7.0.1 to determine the consensus sequences based on the conservative sequences and MEGA (Molecular Evolutionary Genetics Analysis)</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">6: 06 program to construct phylogeny trees (Tamura </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font7">, 2013).</span></p>
