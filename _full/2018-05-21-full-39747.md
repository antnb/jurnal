<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h1>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Leucaena leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2">) provides the highest amount of proteins compared to other greens. However, its use is restricted due to the content of mimosine, an antinutrition non-protein amino acid known to be toxic to animals. This study aimed to observe the effect of administration of more than 10% of processed </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal to the level of T3, T4 and the thyroid histopathology. In this study, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaves were soaked in distilled water for 12 hours. The processed of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal was made into pellets containing 0%, 7.5%, 15%, and 22.5% leaf meal of total feeds respectively, and was fed to the rats during pregnancy and lactation. The level of triiodothyronine (T3) and thyroxine (T4), and the thyroid histological features were the parameters observed. The collected data were statistically analyzed by SPSS 20.0 for Windows using One-way ANOVA followed by Duncan Multiple Range Test to observe any significant difference among the 4 treatment groups (α=5%). Meanwhile, the presence of hyperplastic cells and follicle lumens filled with vesicles and colloids was descriptively analyzed by means of available literatures. The analysis of T3 and T4 level revealed that there was no any significant difference between the control and treatment groups. The epithelial cells of thyroid follicles in the treatment groups of P1, P2 and P3 showed hyperplasia and were detached from their respective basal membranes.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Keywords: leucaena,</span><span class="font1"> detoxification, thyroid, rats</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">INTRODUCTION</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Leucaena leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2">) leaves contain proteins as high as 25-35% of their dry weight (Gosh and Bandyopadhyay, 2007). In other hand, also contain mimosine, an antinutrition nonamino acid substance which has been known to cause several disorders in cattle</span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:line-through;">s</span><span class="font2">, one of which is the inhibition of thyroid hormone synthesis due to its anti-thyroid activity (Komari, 1993). Additional 75% </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal in cow’s feed for more than 22 days caused a decline in serum level of T3 (triiodothyronine) and T4 (thyroxine) (Gosh </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">., 2007). The decline of T3 and T4 blood serum level could cause thyroid gland enlargement, a condition called goiter. Ly </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">. (2007) reported that excessive consumption of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> by animals could lead to decrease in body weight, thyroid dysfunction, alopecia, and decline in both quality and quantity of</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">semen, thus impairing reproductive function. Mimosine exhibits negative effects not only in ruminants but also in monogastric animals, in which administration of more than 10% </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal was conducted (Rai </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">., 1992).</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The mimosine content of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> depends on the age of the plant, drying method (treatment), and season (Bray, 1994). Recently, researchers are focused to find a way to reduce the mimosine level contained in </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf, thus reducing the exhibited hazard in animal wellbeing. The detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala </span><span class="font2">leaves by means of water-soaking method reduced the mimosine content up to 73,31% (Wiratmini </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">., 2014). The study also reported no abnormalities in the development of embryos while detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala </span><span class="font2">leaf meal was incorporated into the feeds of the rats in pregnancy period</span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:line-through;">e</span><span class="font2">. Meanwhile, there is no research regarding the effect of detoxified leucaena by soaking in water to the subject of the thyroid gland. Therefore, it is of interest to study for the subject matter to the livestock that was given detoxified leucaena during pregnancy and lactation. In this study, albino rats (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Rattus norvegicus</span><span class="font2">) were used as models for monogastric cattle. The serum level of T3 (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Triiodothyronine</span><span class="font2">) and T4 (thyroxine) and thyroid histological features were used as indicators of thyroid gland activity.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">The Processing of Treatment Feed</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaves were hand-picked from the trees that grow at the roadside of Bypass Ngurah Rai Street, Denpasar, Bali. The leaves were removed from its stem and let detoxified by soaking in cold water (pH 8.5) at 1 kg leaves<sup>-1</sup> 30 litre of water for 12 h. Treated leucaena were then collected by losing the water through sieve and immediately air-dried up until the constant dry weight is achieved. The leaves were later milled and sifted to produce </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala </span><span class="font2">leaf meal according to Zakayo </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font2">, (2000) which then made into pellets as such: The feeds used for the control group (P0) contained 0% of detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala </span><span class="font2">leaf meal and consisted only of commercial pig feed CP 551, while the one used for treatment groups was a mix of CP 551 and detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal designated as P1, P2, and P3. The mixing process was done by homogenization with the addition of 1.5% CMC and water. The mixture was then processed in pelleting machine and the resulting pellets were airdried in a 60<sup>o</sup>C oven. The finally processed pellets were stored in sealed clear plastic bags (Wiratmini </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">., 2014).</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>Experimental Procedure</span></h1>
<p><span class="font2">Female albino rats (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Rattus norvegicus</span><span class="font2">), approximately 3 months old and with an</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">average live weight of 180-200 g, were acclimatized inside a 33x25x14 cm plastic container for 7 days. The room where acclimatization took place was set in 25-26<sup>o</sup>C temperature with alternating 12 hours light and dark cycles. During this period, the rats were fed on CP 551 (produced by PT Charoen Pokphan Indonesia) with </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ad-libitum </span><span class="font2">access to water. Estrous cycle was determined by vaginal smear and Giemsa staining method. Females in estrous were mated with males in the afternoon. A vaginal plug or sperm found on the next day indicated that copulation had occurred and this was taken as being the 0 day of gestation.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The Completely Randomized Design was used in this study, involving four treatment groups (P0, P1, P2 and P3) based on the level of incorporating </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal in the given feeds, each group comprising eight rats. Thirty-two of albino rats in their 1<sup>st</sup> day of gestation were randomly grouped into the aforementioned treatment groups. The control group (P0) was fed only on CP 551 while P1, P2, and P3 were fed on treatment feed incorporated with 7.5%, 15%, and 22.5% of detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal, respectively. The experiment was conducted for 42 days, counted from the 1<sup>st </sup>day of gestation until the end of lactating period. On the 43<sup>rd</sup> day, all rats were sacrificed by means of intramuscular ketamine injection and dissected to collect</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">the thyroid glands and blood samples. The blood samples were drawn from the orbital sinus using microhaematocrite tubes and collected in 1.5 ml microtubes. Collected blood samples were left at room temperature for 45 minutes, and then centrifuged for 10 minutes at 3000 RPM. The serum was transferred to other tubes and then evaluated for both the level of T3 and T4 measured by </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Vidas Test Kit</span><span class="font2"> from </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Biomerieux</span><span class="font2"> according to the steps instructed by the manufacturers. Dissected thyroid glands were made in histological preparations embedded in paraffin wax, stained by Haematoxylin-Eosin (HE), and observed by means of light microscope equipped with an </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Optilab</span><span class="font2"> camera in 400 x magnifications.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>Data Analysis</span></h1>
<p><span class="font2">SPSS 20.0 for Windows was used to statistically analyze the collected data. The T3 and T4 serum level were analyzed by One Way ANOVA for normally distributed data and followed by Duncan Multiple Range Test (DMRT) for significantly different ones. Thyroid gland histopathology was descriptively analyzed by referring to Kumar </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">. (2007).</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">(p&gt;0.05). This implicates that incorporated detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal up to 22.5% did not cause any decline on the serum level of T3 and T4.</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">The serum level of T3 and T4 did not decline significantly due to the fact that the detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal used in this study contained less than 1% mimosine. Wiratmini </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">. (2014) reported that the mimosine contain in 12-hours detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaves decreased up to 73.31%, which implicates that the mimosine level in the feeds of P3, that contained the highest amount of incorporated detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal (22.5%), was only of 0.64%.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Monogastric livestock was reported to be intolerant of undetoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal if given more than 10% the total feed (Rai </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">., 1992). According to Wiratmini (2014), undetoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal contained 10.64% mimosine, which means that feed incorporated with 10% </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">meal would contain 1.06% mimosine. Monogastric livestock, for example rabbits, were intolerant to even more than 1% of momosine as reported by Fayemi (2011). </span><span class="font1">Chancay and Poosaran (2009) reported that monogastric livestock fed </span><span class="font2">on more than 10% undetoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal eventually developed several disorders.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Gosh </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">. (2007) reported that cows fed on undetoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal during lactation exhibited decreased of T3 and T4 serum level as well as goiter. Aside from cows, undetoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal also caused goiter in goats (Sastry </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">., 2008). This phenomenon is due to the fact that mimosine is structurally similar to tyrosine, an amino acid that binds with iodide during the process of thyroid hormone synthesis (Hegarty </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">., 1964). Therefore, mimosine acts as the competitive</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">inhibitor of tyrosine in terms of iodide binding, thus leading to the depletion of iodide ions as substrates for the synthesis of T3 and T4.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Wiratmini </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">. (2014) reported that incorporation of up to 22.5% detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal into the feeds of albino rats did not cause either abortions, postnatal death, developmental abnormalities, or decrease in the litter size. This implies that detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala </span><span class="font2">leaf meal during the gestation period does not interfere with thyroid hormone synthesis in rats. Thyroid hormones serve as growth control, differentiation, as well as metabolism of all somatic cells (Grummer </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">al</span><span class="font2">., 1988). During gestation, thyroid hormones function in the maturation and providing nutrition for the uterus, including placental growth, fetus metabolism, and production of other hormones related to growth (Glinoer, 1997). Inadequate level of thyroid hormones could lead to infertility and impaired reproductive functions (Airin, 2011).</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">+ found</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The histological features of thyroid glands found in the control group were of organized follicles filled with pinkish colloids, arranged by cuboidal epithelial cells that were attached to their respective basal membrane. A colloid is a mixture of proteins</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">consisting of thyroglobulins which would be broken down to T3 and T4 by endopeptidase enzyme. The resulting hormones would be released into the bloodstream before reaching their target organs (Ganong </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">., 2008). Meanwhile, the histological features found in</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">P1, P2, and P3 were of hyperplastic cells that were detached from their respective basal membrane, which lead to the disorganized appearance of the follicles that contained neither colloids nor hemorrhage (Fig. 1). Those mentioned findings in P1, P2, and P3</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">suggested that mimosine contained in detoxificated </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal was responsible for producing such histological features. Hyperplasia of follicle cells causes enlargement of the thyroid gland, a condition known as goiter.</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">Hyperplasia is the main response of tissues due to physiological and pathological stress to achieve a new equilibrium state and ensure survival (Kumar </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2">., 2007). In this condition, the cells are said to be undergoing adaptation process. If the insulting factors were to be removed before further cellular</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">damage took place, those cells would return to their initial state.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>CONCLUSIONS</span></h1>
<p><span class="font2">The incorporation of detoxified </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">L. leucocephala</span><span class="font2"> leaf meal to the feeds of albino rats during gestation-lactation period did not</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">cause any decline in the serum level of T3 and T4 while the histological features of thyroid glands suggested hyperplasia of epithelial cells that were detached from their respective basal membrane.</span></p>
