<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstrak:</span></h1>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Bahasa slang merupakan bahasa tidak resmi yang memiliki dua kategori, yaitu bahasa slang primer dan sekunder. Salah satu bahasa slang yang populer digunakan adalah slang Amerika dan banyak dijumpai dalam percakapan pada naskah film. Sumber data yang digunakan dalam skripsi ini adalah sebuah film berjudul “Pirates of the Caribbean – The Curse of the Black Pearl”.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Data tersebut diperoleh melalui metode perekaman dan pengunduhan dari sebuah situs internet, beserta langkah penganalisaan data seperti identifikasi, klasifikasi, dan kesimpulan. Terdapat dua kategori bahasa slang Amerika, yaitu slang primer dan sekunder yang ditemukan dalam analisa data. Slang Primer meliputi: </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">‘em, aye, hell, missy, mate, dammit, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">dan </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">yep</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">, sedangkan slang sekunder meliputi: </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">hearties, poppet, parlay, fulcrum, avast, slack-jawed, jinx, slit, blast, bloody, blighter</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">. Perkembangan bahasa slang dipengaruhi oleh dinamisnya perubahan makna di setiap periode. Asal muasal dan sejarah bahasa tersebut juga sangat menarik untuk diketahui agar memudahkan pembaca dalam menelusuri dinamika dan fenomena bahasa yang terjadi dalam masyarakat.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Kata kunci: </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Bahasa Slang, slang Amerika, dan sejarah bahasa</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Language is a set of symbols used mainly for communication. The symbols may be spoken or written. There are some classification of the language users based on age and gender. For instance, men and women do not speak in exactly the same way in any community. Pitch, vocabulary, pronunciation and grammar can differentiate age groups. Moreover, from among children and adults have their own</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">style of slang. These differences include the use of swear words and slang. Slang is being developed dynamically from the secret word which is only spoken by certain people to become the daily communication. The most popular one is American slang. It is unique and seems impolite to be used in the daily conversation, especially when it is used in the formal conversation. In some periods, American slang expressions can be found in the art works and entertainment subjects, such as novel, drama, poetry, song lyrics, movie, and many others. Therefore, many American slang expressions are found in some movies, especially comedy movie.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">There are several examples of primary and secondary slang found in </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Pirates of Caribbean – The Curse of the Black Pearl</span><span class="font1"> movie script. The discussion below are the slang words and expressions uttered by the characters in </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Pirates of Caribbean – The Curse of the Black Pearl</span><span class="font1"> movie script when communicating and they can be categorized as primary and secondary slang. As stated by Chapman (1998), the primary slang was general expression used by people in their daily communication and those slang expressions are generally used in everyday talk, and secondary slang is chosen not so much to fix one group as to express one’s attitudes and resourcefulness by pretending momentarily, and hence to express someone’s contempt, superiority, cleverness by borrowing someone else verbal dress. It is also a matter of stylistic choice rather than true identification. Moreover, the analysis of etymology of the slang expressions were analyzed by reading the main dictionary by Harper (2012) in </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Online Etymology Dictionary</span><span class="font1"> and supported by several dictionaries, such as by Spears (1993) in </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">NTC’s Thematic Dictionary of American Slang,</span><span class="font1"> Lighter (1994) in </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Dictionary of American Slang Volume I &amp;&nbsp;II</span><span class="font1">, Collins (2009) in </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Collin’s English Dictionary</span><span class="font1"> and additional information from </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Wikipedia, the Free Encyclopedia</span><span class="font1"> (2013).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>5.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Primary Slang a. ‘em</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The slang word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">‘em</span><span class="font1"> can be found in scene one through the conversation</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">below:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">YOUNG ELIZABETH: (standing at the bow of a ship and singing) “…… </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">We pillage, we plunder, we rifle and loot. Drink up me hearties yo ho! We kidnap and ravage and don’t give a hoot. Drink up me hearties yo ho! Yo ho, yo ho a pirate’s life for me. We extort, we pilfer, we filch and sack. Drink up –“</span><span class="font1"> (Gibbs puts his hand on her shoulder which surprises her).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GIBBS &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“Quiet, miss! Cursed pirates sail these waters. You</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">don’t want to bring </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">‘em </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">down on us now?”</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">There are two characters involved in the conversation above, they are young Elizabeth and Gibbs. Based on the situation in the movie, the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">‘em</span><span class="font1"> used by Gibbs as the reference to the pirates, he believes the myth that who is singing about pirates’ song will bring the pirates to their ship and cause danger, because Elizabeth was singing about pirate on the ship which carried the Governor and his crew including Gibbs.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Secondary Slang</span><br><br><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark22"></a>a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Hearties</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">hearties</span><span class="font1"> can be found in the scene one through the act below:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">YOUNG ELIZABETH: (standing at the bow of a ship and singing) </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“…… We pillage, we plunder, we rifle and loot. Drink up me </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">hearties </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">yo ho! We kidnap and ravage and don’t give a hoot. Drink up me hearties yo ho! Yo ho, yo ho a pirate’s life for me. We extort, we pilfer, we filch and sack. Drink up –“</span><span class="font1"> (Gibbs puts his hand on her shoulder which surprises her).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GIBBS &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“Quiet, miss! Cursed pirates sail these waters.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">You don’t want to bring ‘em down on us now?”</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Elizabeth and Gibbs are involved in the data above. It happened on the Governor’s ship named </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Dauntless</span><span class="font1">. The situation in the movie showed that Elizabeth sings a special song of pirates on the ship deck, there is the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">hearties</span><span class="font1"> which referred to a pirate and imagined herself as a pirate who sails the sea. The word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">hearties</span><span class="font1"> is an addressing term among pirates at that time.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark23"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark24"></a>b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Parlay!</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The slang word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">parlay</span><span class="font1"> found in the movie script actually has a meaning as a part of the mythical set pirate laws. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Parlay</span><span class="font1"> entitles its invoker with free passage to negotiate with a ship’s captain until negotiations are complete. The word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">parlay</span><span class="font1"> can be found in the scene six through the conversation below:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">ELIZABETH : </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“Parlay!</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">”</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">RAGETTI : </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“What?”</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">ELIZABETH : </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“Parlay. I invoke the right of parlay. According to the</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Code of the Brethren, set down by the pirates Morgan and Bartholomew, you have to take me to your Captain.”</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">PINTEL : </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“I know the code.”</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">ELIZABETH : </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“If an adversary demands parlay you can do them no harm until the parlay is complete.”</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">RAGETTI : </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“To blazes with the code.”</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">PINTEL : </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“She wants to be taken to the captain. And she’ll go without a fuss. We must honor the code.”</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">There are three characters involved in the conversation above. They are Elizabeth, Ragetti, and Pintel. The situation in the movie showed that Elizabeth used </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">parlay</span><span class="font1"> in her words as her right to negotiate with the ship’s captain in order to avoid her danger of being killed by Ragetti, Pintel, and other pirates on her detention in the pirates’ ship. In the movie, the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">parlay</span><span class="font1"> is used as a secret term among pirates at that time.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark27"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark28"></a>b. Parlay</span></h1>

Since 1942 its meaning developed </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">to exploit to advantage</span><span class="font1">. Later in modern English, it means a series of two or more bets set up in advance that the original stake plus its winnings are risked on the successive wagers. (Harper, 2012)</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The broad sense appeared as the fresh risking of an original stake together with its winnings. It has a synonym to </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">accumulator</span><span class="font1"> in which the meaning of a single bet links together two or more individual wagers. It has a benefit of much higher profits, (Spears, 1993). In some movies which told a story of pirates especially </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">in Pirates of Caribbean – The Curse of the Black Pearl</span><span class="font1"> (2003) the term of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">parlay</span><span class="font1"> was used as a part of the mythical set pirate laws. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Parlay</span><span class="font1"> entitles its invoker with free passage to negotiate with a ship’s captain until negotiations are complete. <h1><a name="bookmark29"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark30"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Based on the previous discussion and analysis, the types of slang used by the characters in the movie script are mostly secondary slang. It pretends to be more stylish and appealing by borrowing someone else’s verbal dress in order to exclude the true identification, especially used by the characters of pirates in the movie, as a member of criminal group. There are nine scenes which contained the data of primary and secondary slang. Moreover, every slang word has its own history, its meaning changes dynamically for several people in any parts of the world. For the American, there are no limitations in using slang expressions for their communication even the origin of the expressions are not American. If the words long last enough, they may become very well known and indeed unavoidably become standard English.</span></p>
