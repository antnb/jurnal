<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Abstract</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">The study focused on analysing the types of derivational suffixes. The data was taken from The Dry by Jane Harper and analysed by using the theory from Plag (2003) and Carstairs-Mccarthy (2002). The data collected was analysed by using a descriptive qualitative method. The results of this study were explained using both formal and informal methods. The findings showed that there are four types of derivational suffixes found in this novel, such as nominal suffixes, verbal suffixes, adjectival suffixes, and adverbial suffixes. The occurance found in this study is 194 data which consist of nominal suffixes are 66 data. The second, in verbal suffixes are 1 data. Next, adjectival suffixes are 69 data. The last suffix is adverbial suffixes are 58 data.what does this numeral data mean?</span></p>
<h4><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h4>
<p><span class="font3">Linguistics is the scientific study of languages. Linguistics can be classified into two groups based on the aspect of the study: macro linguistics and micro linguistics. One branch of micro linguistics that is concerned about word and its formation is known as Morphology. The study of the internal structure of words is known as morphology (Katamba, 1993). Morphology is also defined as the study of words and their structure (Bauer, 2003). And according to Lieber (2009), morphology is the study of how words are produced, including how new words are created in different languages throughout the world and how word forms alter depending on how they are used in sentences. It is possible to conclude that morphology is the study of how the words in a language are formed and their internal structure. It involves understanding how morphemes, which are the smallest units that have meaning, are combined to form larger words. Morphology allows us to understand how words undergo shape changes or are added with elements such as suffixes, prefixes, or reduplication, thus forming new meanings or changing grammatical functions. Morphemes can be classified into two main types: free morphemes and bound morphemes. According to Yule (2010), Free morphemes are morphemes that can stand alone as single words, for example, open and tour. Different from the free morpheme, bound morphemes cannot function as standalone words but must be attached to other morphemes to convey meaning. Affixes, such as prefixes and</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">suffixes (e.g., &quot;un-&quot; or &quot;-ed&quot;), are examples of bound morphemes. The bound</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">morpheme is separated into inflectional morpheme and derivational morpheme based on its functional role. Derivational morphemes frequently create a new word, which affects the class as well as the meaning of the term. Nominal suffixes, verbal suffixes, adjectival suffixes, and adverbial suffixes are four types of derivational suffixes.</span></p>

<h4><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>SUFFIXES</span></h4>
<p><span class="font3">According to Plag (2003), there are four types of derivational suffixes.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Nominal suffixes is often employed to derive abstract nouns from verbs, adjectives, and nouns, such as; -age, -al, -ance, -ant, -cy, -dom, -ee, -eer, -er, -ery, -ess, -ful, -hood, -(i)an, -ing, -ion, -ism, -ist, ity, -ment, -ness, -ship.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Verbal suffixes is verbs derived from other categories, primarily adjectives and nouns, such as -ate, -en, -ify, and -ize.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Adjectival suffixes is created by combining nouns, verbs, and adjectives. such as; -able, -al, -ary, -ed, -esque, -ful, -ic, -ing, -ish, -ive, -less, -ly, -ous.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Adverbial suffixes come from another category (adjective and noun). There is suffix -ly and suffix -wise.</span></p>

<h4><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Derivational Nominal Suffixes</span></h4></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">Derivational nominal suffixes are often employed to derive abstract nouns from verbs, adjectives, and nouns. And the analysis of the data was explained below:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Noun Derived from Verb</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">“Falk took his in </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">astonishment</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">”.</span><span class="font3"> (Pg. 26)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">[[astonish]</span><span class="font0">v - </span><span class="font3">ment]</span><span class="font0">n</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Based on the data presented above, the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Astonishment</span><span class="font3"> derived from verb to noun. Word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">astonishment</span><span class="font3"> (N) is derived from the base noun </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">astonish</span><span class="font3">. The base word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">astonish</span><span class="font3"> means surprise greatly Oxford Learner’s Pocket Dictionary (2008) By stanching the suffix -ment after the base word it forms the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">astonishment</span><span class="font3"> (N) means very surprising or unexpected. Therefore, the word class is changed by adding the suffix -ment to the original word.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Nouns Derived from Nouns</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">“The </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">farmer </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">shifted his large body slightly and fixed Falk properly in his gaze for the first time”.</span><span class="font3"> (Pg. 6)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">[[Farm]</span><span class="font0">n </span><span class="font3">-er]</span><span class="font0">n</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The process of the base </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">farm</span><span class="font3"> connects with the suffix -er making the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">farmer</span><span class="font3">. This suffix changes the meaning but not the word class; it provides the suffix -er a function to maintain the word class. The word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">farm</span><span class="font3"> has the meaning of an area of land and associated structures used for raising food and breeding animals. Moreover, the meaning of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">farmer</span><span class="font3"> is the person who owns or manages a farm Oxford Learner’s Pocket Dictionary (2008). The suffix -er in the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">childhood</span><span class="font3"> can be identified as a classmaintaining suffix.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">c. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Nouns Derived from Adjective</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">“When he looked back, time had moved on and Luke and Karen were smiling with stiff </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">formality </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">on their wedding day</span><span class="font3">”. (Pg. 9)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">[[ Formal]</span><span class="font0">adj </span><span class="font3">-ity]</span><span class="font0">n</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Based on the data, the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">formality</span><span class="font3"> is a noun built with two morphemes. The base </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">formal</span><span class="font3"> (Adj) is the first morpheme then the base word is added by the second morpheme which is the suffix -ity. The base </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">formal</span><span class="font3"> has meaning done by rules of etiquette; appropriate for or comprising an official or significant setting or occasion. Meanwhile, the meaning of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">formality</span><span class="font3"> is the rigid observance of rules of convention or etiquette Oxford Learner’s Pocket Dictionary (2008) The process added of the suffix -ity in the base word makes the suffix -ity function as the class-changing suffix.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h4><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Derivational Verbal Suffixes</span></h4></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">The process of creating new words of verbs there are formed by taking a basic word from another lexical category and combining them with certain suffixes.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">a. Verbs Derived from Adjectives</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">“</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Even those who didnʼt </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">darken </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">the door of the church from one Christmas o the next could tell there would be more mourners than seats”.</span><span class="font3"> (Pg. 1)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">[[ Dark]</span><span class="font0">adj </span><span class="font3">-en]</span><span class="font0">v</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Dark</span><span class="font3"> is an adjective before it is added by the suffix -en at the end of the word. The base becomes a verb and changes to the </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">darken</span><span class="font3"> because of the suffix -en which is put at the last of the word. The function of the suffix -en beside changes the word class, this also changes the meaning. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Dark</span><span class="font3"> means with little or no light. Meanwhile, </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">darken</span><span class="font3"> is making or becoming dark or darker Oxford Learner’s Pocket Dictionary (2008). From this analysis, it can be proven that the suffix -en is part of class-changing suffixes.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h4><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Derivational Adjectives Suffixes</span></h4></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">English adjectival suffixes are classified into two categories: relational adjectives and qualitative adjectives.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adjectives Derived from Verbs</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">“We wanted something small and </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">manageable </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">after- ʻshe hesitated for a beat”.</span><span class="font3"> (Pg. 25)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">[[Manage]</span><span class="font0">v </span><span class="font3">-able]</span><span class="font0">adj</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">manageable</span><span class="font3"> to take from the base </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">manage</span><span class="font3"> as a verb then added with the suffix -able. From the verb in the word class, it is changed to an adjective because suffix -able. The base manage from the meaning succeed in doing something, especially something difficult. Meanwhile, </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">manageable</span><span class="font3"> means capable of being handled, controlled, or done without much trouble, Oxford Learner’s Pocket Dictionary (2008). This becomes class-changing because there is a phenomenon of changing the word class and its meaning.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adjective Derived from Nouns</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">“</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Just here in a </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">personal </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">capacity,ʼ Falk said.</span><span class="font3"> (Pg. 35)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">From the data, it is visible that there is the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">personal.</span><span class="font3"> The word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">personal</span><span class="font3"> comes from the free morpheme </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">person</span><span class="font3"> with the meaning of a human being regarded as an individual. The word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">person</span><span class="font3"> is a noun in the word class, then the free morpheme </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">person </span><span class="font3">added by suffix -al becomes </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">personal</span><span class="font3"> and changes from noun to adjective, </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">personal </span><span class="font3">means of, affecting, or belonging to a particular person rather than anyone else Oxford Learner’s Pocket Dictionary (2008). From the analysis, the suffix -al is classified into class-changing suffixes.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">c. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adjectives Derived from adjectives</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">“Say Luke wanted to hide something </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">smallish</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">”.</span><span class="font3"> (Pg. 37)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">[[Small]</span><span class="font0">adj -</span><span class="font3">ish]</span><span class="font0">adj</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">In the word class, the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">small</span><span class="font3"> is an adjective, it has the meaning of a size that is less than normal. This changes the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">smallish</span><span class="font3"> after the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">small,</span><span class="font3"> followed by the suffix -ish. After this process, the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">smallish</span><span class="font3"> is an adjective in the word class, it means not changing the word class. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Smallish</span><span class="font3"> means fairly small Oxford Learner’s Pocket Dictionary (2008). Suffix -ish is identified as class maintaining suffixes because it just changes the meaning of the word.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h4><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Derivational Adverbial Suffixes</span></h4></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">Derivational adverbial suffixes are the process of creating new words of adverbs that are formed by taxing a basic word from a word in another lexical category part of speech.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adverbs Derived from Adjectives</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">“They both now glanced </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">awkwardly </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">at Gretchen”.</span><span class="font3"> (Pg. 17)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">[[Awkward]</span><span class="font0">adj </span><span class="font3">-ly]</span><span class="font0">adv</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Awkward</span><span class="font3"> means causing difficulty; hard to do or deal with. In the word class, word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">awkward</span><span class="font3"> is adjective. Meanwhile, </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">awkwardly</span><span class="font3"> is adverb with the meaning in an ungainly manner; not smoothly or gracefully Oxford Learner’s Pocket Dictionary (2008). Suffix-ly is the part of class changing suffixes because after put suffix-ly in the last of the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">awkward,</span><span class="font3"> it changes the word class.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">“They were mingling </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">solemnly</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">, balancing plastic cups and plates of chocolate ripple cake”.</span><span class="font3"> (Pg. 15)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">[[Solemn]</span><span class="font0">adj </span><span class="font3">-ly]</span><span class="font0">adv</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">It is clear from the data that there is word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">solemnly</span><span class="font3">. Word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">solemnly</span><span class="font3"> comes from the free morpheme </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">solemn</span><span class="font3"> with the meaning formal and dignified, the word </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">solemn</span><span class="font3"> is an adjective in the word class. Then the free morpheme </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">solemn</span><span class="font3"> added by suffix -ly become </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">solemnly</span><span class="font3"> and changes from adjective to adverb, </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">solemnly</span><span class="font3"> means in a formal and</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">dignified manner Oxford Learner’s Pocket Dictionary (2008). From the analysis above proves that the suffix -ly is part of class-changing suffixes.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adverb Derives from Noun</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">“</span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Gamely </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">attempting to bring geography or woodwork or something else alive for bored teenagers”.</span><span class="font3"> (Pg. 5)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">[[Game]</span><span class="font0">n </span><span class="font3">-ly]</span><span class="font0">adv</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Game</span><span class="font3"> is classified as a noun. In the word game, after being added by the suffix -ly, the category of the word class becomes </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">gamely</span><span class="font3"> as an adverb. The addition of the suffix -ly at the end of the word changed the meaning of the word. A </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">game</span><span class="font3"> means of play or sport, particularly one that is competitive and is decided by skill, strength, or chance. Meanwhile </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">gamely,</span><span class="font3"> means in a brave, spirited way Oxford Learner’s Pocket Dictionary (2008). The suffix -ly in the word gamely changed the word class, this proves that the suffix -ly is identified as the class-changing suffix.</span></p>
