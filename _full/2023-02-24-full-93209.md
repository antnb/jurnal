<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Abstract</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">This study aims at finding out the translation variations as the lexical equivalences of the English noun ‘love’ into Indonesian, and analyzing the translation shifts. The data were taken from the parallel corpus containing the English expressions with the noun ‘love’ and their translations. The data were collected through the documentation method and analyzed qualitatively. The results show that the translations of the English noun ‘love’ are varied with 21 lexicons. The translations are categorized into three types, those that are translated into the base words, the affixed words, and the phrases. Besides that, there are also zero translation and borrowing terms used. The TL noun ‘cinta’ is found at 72.2% and it is indeed equivalent with the SL noun ‘love’. The other variations are the synonyms and related lexicons used based on the certain contexts. Two kinds of shifts namely class and unit shifts are found in the translations.</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">Equivalence in translation is a concept indicating that a source language (SL) and a target language (TL) share some kind of ‘sameness’ (Panou, 2013, p. 2). This is in line with Larson's (1998, p. 169) ‘lexical equivalents’ that refers to concepts in the source language which are known (shared) in the target language.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">A word is generally defined as a basic meaningful element in a language, however, meaning can be understood by units that are more complex than the single word and by various structures and linguistic devices (Baker, 2018, p. 10).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The adjective ‘unhappy’ for instance, is a word, but it has two morphemes ‘un-’ as the bound morpheme that carries the negative meaning and ‘happy’ as the free morpheme. The negation in different languages is not the same, like in Indonesian, the negation is ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">tidak’</span><span class="font4"> or ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">bukan’,</span><span class="font4"> so, ‘unhappy’ becomes ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">tidak bahagia’</span><span class="font4">. A word may also have more than one category or class. It is often understood by considering the context of the phrase or clause where the word is used. Take the examples of the emotion base words ‘love’ and ‘fear’ that have two classes in English, one is as a noun, and the other is as a verb. When those base words (i.e., categorized as nouns)</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">are translated into Indonesian, they might have several translations in different word classes.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Emotion literally refers to someone’s feelings, and it can be expressed through words. Emotion words often have more than one word class. Shaver et al. (2001, p. 215) reported that emotion in Indonesian can be categorized into five basic-level lexicons, they are </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4"> (love)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, senang</span><span class="font4"> (happy)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, marah</span><span class="font4"> (anger)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, kawatir/takut</span><span class="font4"> (anxiety/fear)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">,</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">sedih </span><span class="font4">(sadness). The categories of lexicon in Indonesian are all in adjectives, meanwhile the English categories vary in adjective (happy), noun and verb (love, fear) and nouns (anger, anxiety, sadness). This becomes a consideration in translation because the equivalence of emotion lexicons seems to have the class shift (Catford, 1965, pp. 78–79) in the target language.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The study that analyzed the translation procedures of the English emotion word ‘happy’ as the adjective into Indonesian with reference to Kṛṣṇa Text has been conducted by Suryasa et al. (2019) Emotion word ‘happy’ is translated into eight words: </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">bahagia </span><span class="font4">(happy)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, senang</span><span class="font4"> (happy)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, suka</span><span class="font4"> (like)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, lega</span><span class="font4"> (relieved)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, kesenangan</span><span class="font4"> (happiness)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, gembira ria</span><span class="font4"> (joyous)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, riang</span><span class="font4"> (cheerful)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, ceria</span><span class="font4"> (cheerful)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">,</span><span class="font4"> and two phrases: </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">patah hati</span><span class="font4"> (broken heart) and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">tenteram</span><span class="font4"> (serene). Transposition procedure is reported as the most relevant one because it has an alignment to the source language culture. The adjective in the source language can be translated into a noun as in ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">kesenangan</span><span class="font4">’. It involves the class shift. Besides, the adjective is also translated into phrases as in ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">gembira ria</span><span class="font4">’ and ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">patah hati</span><span class="font4">’.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The equivalent translations of the source language ‘happy’ are found in ten variations (Suryasa et al., 2019). They can be considered as the synonyms that express the positive feeling: </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">bahagia </span><span class="font4">(happy)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, senang</span><span class="font4"> (happy)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, suka</span><span class="font4"> (like)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">,</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">lega</span><span class="font4"> (relieved)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, kesenangan</span><span class="font4"> (happiness)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, gembira ria</span><span class="font4"> (joyous)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, riang</span><span class="font4"> (cheerful)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">, ceria</span><span class="font4"> (cheerful)</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">,</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">tenteram</span><span class="font4"> (serene). However, there is one translation that shows the negative feeling </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">patah hati </span><span class="font4">(broken heart). There might be a question whether the negative emotion phrase is the near-synonym or the lexical equivalence of ‘happy</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">’.</span><span class="font4"> The source language sentence where ‘happy’ occurs is “We should try instead to be happy”, and the translation is “</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Malah sebaiknya, kami menikmati rasa </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">patah hati </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">ini</span><span class="font4">.” (Suryasa et al., 2019, p. 743). Modulation procedure of translation (Vinay &amp;&nbsp;Darbelnet, 2000, p. 89) is applied in this case considering the Indonesian culture in which a positive message may be expressed in the reverse form, and it is the target language orientation. It shows that the kind of equivalence can be achieved based on its context (Baker, 1993, p. 236), in order to reproduce the closest natural equivalent of the SL message into the TL, first in terms of meaning, and style (Nida &amp;&nbsp;Taber, 1982).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Analysis of the translation of emotion words from English into Indonesian is interesting to be conducted. Since there are not many translation analyses focusing on the lexical equivalence of emotion words, this study takes the analysis of one of the emotion words that is ‘love’ with the noun class. This study aims at finding out the translation variations as the lexical equivalence of the English noun ‘love’ into Indonesian and showing their number of occurrences. It then proceeded to analyze the translation shifts.</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>METHOD AND THEORY</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">Parallel corpora are the collections of texts containing original source language-texts in one language and their translated versions in another language (Baker, 1995, p. 230; Bowker, 2002, p. 348; McEnery &amp;&nbsp;Hardie, 2012). The data provided in parallel corpus are the</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">languages in use in huge numbers that enable researchers to conduct the comparative analysis (Rajeg &amp;&nbsp;Rajeg, 2022).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The data in this study were taken from a parallel corpus containing the English expressions with the noun ‘love’ as the key word and their translations in Indonesian. It is considered as the secondary data provided in 2018, and downloaded &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;from</span></p>
<p><a href="https://github.com/prasastoadi/parallel-corpora-en-id/"><span class="font4">https://github.com/prasastoadi/parallel-corpora-en-id/</span></a><span class="font4"> (file name: IWSLT17.train).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The clauses/sentences containing the base word ‘love’ as a noun and their translation are collected through the documentation method. This lexicon is chosen due to the number of expressions found is 295, and is representative for the analysis compared to the other emotion words such as happiness, anger, fear, lust, sadness, pride, shame, and guilt (Kövecses, 1990) which are less found.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The corpus data were downloaded and saved into .txt format. The data show the Key Word In Context (KWIC), that is a list of the occurrences generated from the search of a specified keyword in the corpus. Each of them is set in the middle of one line of context/expression (Baker, 1993, p. 226).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The data were opened by using </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">AntPConc</span><span class="font4"> (Anthony, 2017) software. The results were then saved in the Excel Table. The saved data were classified by labeling the columns into: CASE (number of data), LEFT (words on the left of the node), NODE (searched word), RIGHT (words on the right of the node), TRANSLATION NODE (translation of node), dan TRANSLATION_POS (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Part-of-Speech</span><span class="font4"> of node translation).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The collected data were analyzed by applying the qualitative approach (Creswell, 2009). The variations of the translations of the noun ‘love’ into Indonesian are presented in Tables. The SL and TL expressions showing the</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">context of utterances are added within paragraphs as well as the analysis of the translation shifts (Catford, 1965).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Shifts may occur in translation (Catford, 1965, p. 73) due to the different grammar and sentence structure in the SL and TL. Shifts are grouped into two major types namely level shifts, and category shifts. The first major type may occur where there are different tenses and aspects in two languages. English is the language that has the tense and aspect markers, while Indonesian shows the past tense with the past adverb, and the continuous activity with the word ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">sedang</span><span class="font4">’. The second major type is divided into structure shifts, class shifts, unit shifts, and intra-system shifts. A structure shift may occur when a TL shows different positions of Subject, Predicate, Object/Complement with an SL. A class shift refers to the changes of word class from SL to TL as in ‘angry’ (Adj) to ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">kemarahan</span><span class="font4">’ (N). A unit shift is shown when an SL phrase is translated into a word and vice versa, for example, ‘love’ becomes ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">rasa cinta</span><span class="font4">’. The intrasystem shift is described as a word in an SL has a typical form as in ‘trousers’ or ‘scissors’ that is said as the plural noun in the dictionary is then translated into ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">gunting</span><span class="font4">’ in Indonesian which shows the singular form. Translation shifts shows the message and information transfer from one to another language to overcome its language barrier (Finsen &amp;&nbsp;Puspani, 2019).</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>RESULT AND DISCUSSION</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">The translation variations of the English noun ‘love’ into Indonesian and the translation shifts analysis are divided into three subsections showing the categories of 1) translations into the base words (6 lexicons); 2) translations into the affixed words (10 lexicons); and 3) translations into the phrases (5 lexicons). Each subsection has a Table reporting the translation variations in Indonesian with</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">the number of occurrences to show the lexical equivalence, the near-synonyms of the lexical equivalence, and the other lexicons found. The SL and TL samples are shown to describe the contexts of the utterances, as well as the analysis of the translation shifts occurring in the translations.</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>The translations into the base words</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">The base lexicons as the translation of ‘love’ are ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4">’ that occurs 213 times and as the most frequent translations found, followed by ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">kasih</span><span class="font4">’ that occurs 11 times, and ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">senang</span><span class="font4">’, ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">simpati</span><span class="font4">’, ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">suka</span><span class="font4">’, ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">gairah</span><span class="font4">’ respectively occur once. All the base words are the noun class except ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">suka</span><span class="font4">’ which is a verb, and ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">senang’</span><span class="font4"> an adjective. <p><span class="font4">The English noun ‘love’ that is translated into the noun ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4">’ is considered the exact equivalence because of the frequent occurrence in the translations. In the Indonesian Dictionary (KBBI), ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4">’ is an adjective, however, it can also be a noun without or with the addition of affixes such as ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">ke-cinta-an</span><span class="font4">’.<sup>1</sup></span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The noun ‘love’ is usually used in noun phrases like ‘romantic love’ where ‘love’ is the head and ‘romantic’ is the</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">modifier. The translation is ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta yang romantis</span><span class="font4">’, ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4">’ is the head of the phrase placed at the beginning of the phrase, ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">romantis</span><span class="font4">’ is the modifier, and a conjunction ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">yang</span><span class="font4">’ is added in the middle, and it is used optionally in that context. ‘Love’ can also function as a modifier as in ‘love songs’ and the translation is ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">lagu cinta</span><span class="font4">’. English and Indonesian noun phrases have the reverse order of the modifier and the head positions, and it results in the structure shift in the translations.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The lexicon ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4">’ in Indonesian has two-word classes. As a noun, it can be interpreted as something that is objectified. It can be used as a subject, an object, or a complement in a clause. In addition, it cannot be preceded by the word ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">tidak’</span><span class="font4"> (negative form). Meanwhile, ‘love’ as an adjective has: a) the attributive function in which it describes the nature of a noun, b) the predicative function, where ‘love’ is used as the predicate of a clause, and c) the substantive function that is used as a complement to the subject.</span><span class="font0"><sup>2</sup> </span><span class="font4">In the example ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">lagu cinta</span><span class="font4">’, though ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">lagu</span><span class="font4">’ is the head of the phrase, ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4">’ is not the adjective because it does not have the attributive function. It means ‘songs about love’ or ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">lagu tentang cinta</span><span class="font4">’ in which ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4">’ is the topic/theme of the songs. The meaning of ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4">’ based on the context shows the relationship between two people who fall in love.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The noun ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">kasih</span><span class="font4">’ occurs in the sentence “</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Tenangkan dengan </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">kasih</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">…</span><span class="font4">” as the translation of “Tempered with love…”. It means the feeling of love or affection of someone who helps another person to be calm.</span><span class="font7"><sup>3</sup> </span><span class="font4">It has a slightly different context with ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4">’.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The adjective ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">senang</span><span class="font4">’ is found in the clause “</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Tidak ada hubungan antara </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">senang </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">akan Lego dan berapa banyak yang dibangun…</span><span class="font4">” that has been translated from the source language “There was no relationship between the love of Legos and how much people built…”. It describes that someone likes something (a thing).<sup>4</sup></span></p>
<p><span class="font4">‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Simpati</span><span class="font4">’ is also found as the translation of the word ‘love’ that is defined as someone who has the empathy to other people<sup>5</sup>, in this case for workers or members of a community. The clause is “The people in our communities who do these jobs deserve our attention, our love and our deepest support” and its translation is “</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Orang-orang yang melakukan pekerjaan semacam ini perlu perhatian kita, </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">simpati </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">dan dukungan penuh kita</span><span class="font4">”</span></p>

<p><span class="font4">The last two phrases ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">kasih sayang</span><span class="font4">’ and ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">belahan jiwa</span><span class="font4">’ are the collocations. ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Kasih sayang</span><span class="font4">’ is used in the SL clause “But I confidently believe that your act of love and caring …” and its translation is “</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Namun saya sangat yakin bahwa tindakan </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">kasih sayang </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">dan kepedulian Anda</span><span class="font4">…”. This collocation can be found in a phrase ‘act of love’ that means the action of affection and love.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The collocation ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">belahan jiwa</span><span class="font4">’ occurs in the SL “And he released himself into the water, and Doaa watched as the love of her life drowned before her eyes.” And the translation is “</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Dan ia melepaskan dirinya ke dalam air, dan Doaa menyaksikan </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">belahan jiwanya </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">tenggelam di depan matanya.</span><span class="font4">” The second collocation is used in the SL collocate phrase ‘love of my life’ that means someone’s lover.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">There are two more variations found in the TL. The first one is there are sixteen borrowing terms or the source language that is borrowed and used as it is in the target language, the second one is there is one datum that is not translated or has zero translation. The borrowing term is found because it is used as the title of movie such as ‘Eat, Pray, Love’ (9 data), used as the abbreviation like “L is Love” (2 data), used as quotations (3 data), and used in song lyrics (2 data) where the word ‘Love’ is used in the TL. The zero translation is found in the SL expression “Oh, for the love of God”, and is translated into the expression “</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Demi</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Tuhan</span><span class="font4">”. Though it has zero translation, it is acceptable in the TL culture.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The variations of the TL lexicons show that translation is a craft involving the attempt to transfer the SL message to the TL (Newmark, 1988, p. 7). The TL noun ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta’</span><span class="font4"> is the equivalence of the SL noun ‘love’ since it occurs 213 times out of 295 data. The SL words ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">kasih’, ‘senang’, ‘suka’,</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">‘sayang</span><span class="font4">’, based on the meanings in the contexts are the synonyms, though they are not the absolute synonyms. Every word basically has its own characteristics and distinctive features, even though it has similar meaning (Edmonds dan Hirst, 2002: 108).</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>CONCLUSION</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">The translations of the English noun ‘love’ into Indonesian are varied enough with 21 variations. The translations are categorized into three types, those that are translated into base words (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta, kasih, suka, senang, simpati, gairah</span><span class="font4">), affixed words (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">kekasih, pasangan, kecintaan, percintaan, kesukaan, kegemaran, kesenangan, dicintai, menyayangi, menyukai</span><span class="font4">), and phrases (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">rasa cinta, rasa sayang, perasaan sayang, kasih sayang, belahan jiwa</span><span class="font4">). Besides the translated lexicons found, there are also zero translation and borrowing terms used. The noun ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta’ </span><span class="font4">is found 72.2% and it is indeed the equivalence of the SL noun ‘love’. The other variations such as ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">kasih’, ‘suka’ ‘senang’</span><span class="font4"> as well as the affixed words, including ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">menyayangi’, and menyukai’, </span><span class="font4">and the phrases containing those three base words are considered the synonyms of the noun ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">cinta</span><span class="font4">’. The other words like ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">simpati’, ‘gairah’, ‘pasangan’, ‘kegemaran’,</span><span class="font4"> and ‘</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">belahan jiwa’</span><span class="font4"> are the related lexicons that are used based on the certain contexts.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Several different word classes and forms in the SL indicate that there are category translation shifts. The SL noun</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">translated into the verb and adjective bases, as well as into the affixed verbs show the class shift. Moreover, the translations in the form of noun phrases show the unit shift where one word is translated into two words.</span></p>
