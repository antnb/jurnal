<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstrak</span></h1>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Jurnal berjudul Syntactic Structure of Relative Clauses with Reference to John Green’s Novel Looking for Alaska berfokus pada analisis tentang klausa relatif, khususnya pada struktur dan jenis klause relatif. Tujuan dari penelitian ini ialah untuk memaparkan jenis-jenis klausa relatif dan menganalisis struktur klausa relatif yang ditemukan dalam novel yang ditulis oleh John Green berjudul Looking for Alaska yang pertama kali diterbitkan pada tahun 2005. Dalam menganalisa struktur klausa relatif dalam penelitian ini, digunakan teori tentang phrase structure rule dari Wekker dan Haegeman (1985) dalam buku mereka yang berjudul A Modern Course of English Syntax dan didukung oleh sebuah teori dari Haegeman tentang Wh-movement dalam bukunya yang berjudul Introduction to Government and Binding Theory 2<sup>nd </sup>Edition dan dalam menganalisis jenis-jenis klausa relatif, digunakan teori dari Quirk et al (1985) dalam bukunya yang berjudul A Comprehensive Grammar of the English Language. Analisis data disajikan menggunakan tree diagram dan phrase structure rule. Hasil analisis dalam penelitian ini menunjukan bahwa semua jenis klausa relatif ditemukan dalam data. Jenis-jenisnya yaitu klausa relatif restrictive dan klausa relatif non-restrictive yang diperkenalkan oleh kata ganti who, which, whom, whose, that, zero atau dengan prasa yang berisi kata ganti contohnya: for which, to which dan lain sebagainya. Dalam fungsinya, klausa relatif dapat berfungsi sebagai subjek atau objek yang memberikan informasi mengenai kata sebelumnya sebagai anteseden atau hanya memberikan informasi tambahan pada kata sebelumnya. Selain itu, analisis dalam penelitian ini juga menunjukan bahwa terdapat wh-movement dalam struktur klausa relatif.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Kata kunci: Struktur Sintaksis, Klausa Relatif, Wh-movement</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">One of the main aspects of language besides semantic, pragmatic, and phonology is syntax. Syntax is the way that word and phrases are put together to form a sentence in a language (Hornby, 2010:1515). A sentence itself is a linguistic unit that consist a group of words that are grammatically linked. Sentences are either simple or multiple, in which a simple sentence consists of an independent clause whereas a multiple sentence contains more than one clause. Multiple sentences are compound or complex (Quirk </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et a</span><span class="font2">l., 1985:719). A compound sentence consists of two or more coordinated main clauses in which the main clauses of a compound sentence have equivalent function, whereas, a complex sentence consists of one main clause or an independent clause which is joined by one or more subordinate clauses. A subordinate clause or also known as a dependent clause is a clause that cannot stand by itself as a sentence. There are some different types of dependent clause, such as: noun clauses, adverbial clauses and relative clauses. Relative clause is a subordinate clause that modifies a noun or a noun phrase. Relative clause is introduced by a relative pronoun such as: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">who, which, whom, whose, that</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">zero</span><span class="font2">. This study analyzed the syntactic structure of relative clauses found in one of John Green’s novels entitled </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Looking for Alaska</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>

<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Result and Discussion</span><br><br><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark14"></a>5.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Types of Relative Clause by Quirk </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> (1985)</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">In analyzing the types of relative clause found in John Green’s novel </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Looking for Alaska,</span><span class="font2"> theory proposed by Quirk </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et</span><span class="font2"> al was used. Quirk </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2"> (1985) stated, according to their semantic relation between the clause and its antecedent, there are two kinds of relative clauses: restrictive relative clause and non-restrictive relative clause.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark15"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark16"></a>5.1.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Restrictive Relative Clause</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Restrictive relative clause is a subordinate clause that aims to modify the meaning of its head word, it gives detailed information defining the noun that comes before it (its antecedent). For examples:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The lunch discussion centered on the girl </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">who was supposed to have been Alaska’s roommate.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;We are driving through the place </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">that she could not drive through.</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.1.2</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Non-restrictive Relative Clause</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Non-restrictive relative clause is a subordinate clause that aims to provide additional information, so this clause does not have a big effect to the meaning of the sentence. For examples:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Hyde talked about </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the Gospel of Mark</span><span class="font2">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">which I hadn’t read until the day</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">before.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I only registered the name Takumi, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">whom Alaska had mentioned yesterday.</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark17"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark18"></a>5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Syntactic Structure of Relative Clause</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">In analyzing the syntactic structure of relative clause, phrase structure rule theory by Wekker and Haegeman (1985) was used. Wh-movement theory by Haegeman (1994) was also used as a supporting theory in analyzing the syntactic structure of relative clause.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">1.</span><span class="font2"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I see a girl </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">who loves books</span><span class="font2">. (Green, 2005 :83)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">A relative clause can modify the subject or object of the main clause. In this clause the relative clause modifies the object of the main clause. The structure of the sentence above is as follows:</span></p>

<p><a href="#bookmark20"><span class="font2">NP</span><span class="font0">1</span></a></p>
<p><span class="font2">VP</span><span class="font0">1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="font2">VNP</span></p>
<p><a href="#bookmark21"><span class="font2">NP &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;DetN S'</span></a></p>
<p><span class="font2">S'Rel S</span></p>

<p><a href="#bookmark23"><span class="font2">NP</span><span class="font0">2</span></a></p>
<p><span class="font2">VP</span><span class="font0">2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="font2">VNP</span></p>
<p><a href="#bookmark24"><span class="font2">NPN</span></a></p>
<p><span class="font2">Based on the structure above the tree diagram of the example 4 is as follows:</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/33493-1.png" alt="" style="width:324pt;height:332pt;">
<p><span class="font2">I &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;see a girl who</span><span class="font0">i &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="font2">t</span><span class="font0">i &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="font2">loves &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;books</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">Wh-movement</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">From the tree diagram above it can be seen that the relative clause which is embedded to its antecedent </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">a girl who loves books</span><span class="font2"> is clearly an NP which is filled by </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">a girl</span><span class="font2"> modified by a relative clause </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">who loves books</span><span class="font2">. It also can be seen that there is a wh-movement applied in that sentence since the wh-pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">who</span><span class="font2"> which must originate within S of the S' moves outside S to leftmost constituent S-bar (S'):</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">[</span><span class="font0">S</span><span class="font2">I see [</span><span class="font0">NP </span><span class="font2">a girl [</span><span class="font0">S' </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">who</span><span class="font2"> [</span><span class="font0">S </span><span class="font2">— loves books]]]]</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The relative wh-pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">who</span><span class="font2"> is moved from its original position marked by — to its superficial position and leaving a coindex trace in its original position (t</span><span class="font0">i</span><span class="font2">). The movement is indicated schematically below:</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">Wh-movement</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The sentence above </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">I see a girl who loves books</span><span class="font2"> consists of two clause: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">I see a girl</span><span class="font2"> as the main clause, and the subordinate clause </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">a girl loves books</span><span class="font2">, however the subject of the subordinate clause </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">a girl</span><span class="font2"> is moved from its original position [NP, S of S'] to the higher position [Rel, S'] and changed into a wh-pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">who</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark25"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark26"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">A relative clause is an embedded clause which is added in a free clause or a sentence. It is embedded to the noun or noun phrase that has a function as subject or object. Moreover, from the structure analysis of relative clauses in this study, it is found that wh-movement is applied in the structure of relative clause. It is applied when the relative wh-pronoun is moved out from its original position within the S of S-bar (S') into its superficial position Rel of S-bar (S').</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Based on the types, according to the theory used by Quirk </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font2"> (1985) based on their semantic relation between the relative clause and its antecedent, there are two types of relative clauses, and both of those types were found in John Green’s novel </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Looking for Alaska,</span><span class="font2"> those are: restrictive relative clause and non-restrictive relative clause. Restrictive relative clause is the type of relative clause that aims to modify the meaning of its antecedent whereas non-restrictive relative clause is type of relative clause that only aims to give additional information.</span></p>
