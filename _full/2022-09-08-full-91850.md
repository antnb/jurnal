<p><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">Abstract--</span><span class="font6">This research was carried out based upon the habitual speaking attitude of Singaporeans in Singapore Colloquial English by adding discourse particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">. Data were gathered from </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership </span><span class="font6">YouTube channel in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership Hits the Streets</span><span class="font6"> section from January to December 2020. There were two objectives; finding out the types and explaining the assigned functions of discourse particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> of SCE found in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership</span><span class="font6"> videos. It was conducted through applying qualitative approach which observation, document analysis, discourse analysis, and descriptive-interpretative method were employed. Theories applied to this research were discourse particles classification by Wong (2004), roles of discourse particles by Low &amp;&nbsp;Deterding (2003), and context of situation by Halliday &amp;&nbsp;Hasan (1985). The results showed that four types of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and three types of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> were used by Singaporeans as seen in the videos; perlocutionary </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">, vocative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">, emphatic </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">, </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> as question marker, impositional </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">, propositional </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">, and persuasive </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">. Two functions were identified; utterance-final marker and audible comma particle. On the frequencies, 38 occurrences of particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and 62 occurrences of particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">Keywords</span><span class="font6">— </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">discourse particles, SCE, ah, lah</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Introduction</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font6">The revolutionary spread of English over the world has invented changes in the sociolinguistic profile of the language and resulted in new varieties of English (A.Al-Mutairi, 2019:85). A number of English varieties have been recognised worldwide, such as Indian English, Australian English, and Singaporean English. Of the several varieties, one intriguing variety which still takes the concern of linguists is Singaporean English. There are two varieties of English used in Singapore i.e., Singapore Standard English (SSE), which is similar to Standard English over the globe, and Singapore Colloquial English (SCE).</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">SCE is commonly known as Singlish and differs significantly from Standard English at all levels (Wong, 2014:6). It is mutually understood and used within the multi-racial Singaporean community. According to Qizhong (2009:17-18), there are seven typical features found in SCE, they are copula deletion, subject and object deletion, lack of tense and agreement features on the verb, aspectual markers, tag questions, passive constructions, and discourse particles.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Of the existing features, discourse particle is the common to be recognised. Discourse particles express the speakers’ attitude towards the addressee or the situation spoken about, encompassing their assumption, intention, and emotion (Wierzbicka, 1991:341 as cited in Wong, 2004:740). Singaporeans are naturally used to adding discourse particles in speaking, which according to Leimgruber (2011:54), there are ten particles i.e., </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah, hor, lah, leh, lor, hah, mah, what/wot, meh,</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ya</span><span class="font6">. Among them all, </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> are the most prevalent particles used in everyday speaking which is also in line with the findings in ICE-SIN (International Corpus of English-Singapore) in spoken category that they have the highest frequency. (Vivien, 2005:36).</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Concerning on several considerations – that SCE is one of the most-learnt varieties of English that is discussed continuously, the use of SCE and its discourse particles are increasingly becoming a native variety of many Singaporeans, and is functioned as an impactful means for expressing</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">national identity – it is suggested that the topic is worth-discussing and needs to be seen more.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">In addition to this, some previous analysis done by Wulandari (2016) and Febryanto (2018) have attempted to investigate the use of SCE discourse particles. This indicates that this topic has been acknowledged to be put into discussion. Those previous studies examined scripted communication in the form of movies and comedy-themed videos which the conversations containing </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> are more scripted. This present study, instead, examines real communication events among Singaporeans in the form of informal interview audio-visuals in which the utterances are more natural, less-scripted, and accurate, in order to answer the gap that has not been provided in the previous studies in terms of factualness.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Viewing this phenomenon, research questions are formulated in attempts to investigate the use of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">. First, what types of discourse particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> of SCE found in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership </span><span class="font6">videos. Second, what functions assigned to discourse particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> of SCE in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership</span><span class="font6"> videos. Furthermore, the objectives of this study are finding out the types and explaining the assigned functions of discourse particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> of SCE found in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership</span><span class="font6"> videos.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Although some studies regarding this topic have been done previously, this topic, however, is still rare to be found in discussions. Therefore, it is believed that this study is relevant in present day due to the incorporation of new, recent elements and, hence, enriches the literature regarding SCE and its discourse particles.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Method</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font6">Real speech events among Singaporeans in the form of informal interview audio-visuals were utilised as the data wherein they are taken from </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership</span><span class="font6"> YouTube channel. The examined videos were those in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership Hits the Streets </span><span class="font6">section wherein the staff randomly interviews people on the street for discussing a certain issue. The ones published in January until December 2020 were being focused on. The speech events are natural and less-scripted since the videos are in the</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Vol. 29 No.2</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">form of informal interview with the society. Thus, the phenomenon can be identified obviously and is accurate.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">The process of collecting data was undertaken through observation and document analysis method. Observations were done in three steps. First, a general observation was done in the collection of videos in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership</span><span class="font6"> YouTube channel. After observing generally, only relevant videos were gathered. In this case, the videos from </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership Hits the Street</span><span class="font6"> section uploaded on January to December 2020 were collected. Lastly, the selected videos were observed thoroughly to find out the phenomenon of particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">. Altogether with it, document analysis method was conducted in several steps. First, the selected videos were watched comprehensively, and simultaneously conducted note-taking to make transcriptions. Then, the transcriptions were reviewed to find out the appearance of particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah </span><span class="font6">and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> by marking them. Lastly, they were listed in order to simplify the process of analysing data.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">The data was analysed through discourse analysis and descriptive-interpretative method. Conducting the analysis, it was done in several steps. First, the collected data was processed through discourse analysis method and theory of context of situation by Halliday &amp;&nbsp;Hasan (1985) to gain comprehensive understanding of the usage and to identify the social context. Then, making interpretation through the context to find out the equal meanings in Standard English to assist in elaborating explanation. Lastly, analysing the occurrences of particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> by applying theory of discourse particles classification by Wong (2004) and of roles of discourse particles by Low &amp;&nbsp;Deterding (2003). Tables and checklists were also provided. In addition, the number of occurrences were also noted to provide information regarding the distribution. Having done with the analysis, it proceeded to elaborating analysis through descriptive method.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>3.1.1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Perlocutionary </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ah</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font6">Perlocutionary </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> is intended to affect an addressee by saying something, including to frighten, sympathize, persuade, etc. Based on the findings, there occurs 20 perlocutionary </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership Hits the Streets</span><span class="font6"> videos from January to December 2020. The detailed analysis of three subtypes of it are presented below.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">Interrogative </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ah</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">Data 1</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Title &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: What Do People in Singapore</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Think About the Idea of Cruise to Nowhere?</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Context &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: Person 1 shared his thoughts about</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">what couples usually do on a staycation with a sense of humour and tried getting validation by asking back.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Interviewer : What do you think people usually do on staycations?</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Person 1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: I mean if we friends, drink. Then</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">play games, poker, something like that </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">. That’s all I know </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">,</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Vol. 29 No.2</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">for now.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Interviewer : What about couples?</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Person 1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: Uh, couples, tsk, the usual thing</span></p>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">. You know </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ah?</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Data 1 shows an informal interview between a guy and an interviewer. Person 1 shares his thought about what people do on staycations. Moreover, he adds about a more specific thing couples usually do on a staycation that everyone would have known by putting some jokes around. Trying to get validation on it, he asks the interviewer back by saying ‘you know’ with additional particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> spoken in rising tone to strengthen the sense of interrogative, expecting the interviewer validate it. Looking to the occurrence and context, particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> in data 1 is best classified as interrogative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">. It is obvious that the use of interrogative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> is intended to achieve an effect, making the hearer aware that the utterance is a question to validate the speaker’s idea.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>Imperative </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ah</span><br><br><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark8"></a>Data 2</span></h2>
<p><span class="font6">Title &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: How Tech-savvy are Seniors in</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Singapore?</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Context &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: The interviewer concluded the</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">session after having enough ideas of young people contribution to the seniors in Singapore to cope with today’s digitalization by commanding the audience.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Interviewer : So, young people out there, do help your uncle, aunty, </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">makcik</span><span class="font6">, </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">pakcik</span><span class="font6">, </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ah!</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Data 2 shows an utterance from the interviewer conveyed to the audience which relates to how the seniors in Singapore cope with digitalization and how young people take their parts. Finished interviewing some people she met on the streets, she brings about a conclusion that the help of young people is critical. Thus, she tells them to be willingly helping the seniors in using technology. <h2><a name="bookmark40"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark41"></a>3.3.1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Audible Comma Particle</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font6">The idea of audible comma particle is observable by paying attention to the position where the particle is placed. An audible comma particle is inserted in the utterance which has more than one point of concern, phrase, or even clause. Thus, the insertion here is intended to mark the boundary between them. Referring to the findings, there are 16 occurrences of particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and 24 occurrences of particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> wherein they are functioned as an audible comma particle. Consider the representative data 10 below to see how this function assigned to those two particles.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark42"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark43"></a>Data 10</span></h2>
<p><span class="font6">Title &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: Singapore GE2020: Where</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">do Old People Get Election Updates from?</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Context &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: Person 1 shared what he</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">thought of the new way of rallies of 2020 Singapore General Election and stated a reason afterward.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Interviewer : Do you know that there are online</span></p>
<p><span class="font0">rallies now?</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Vol. 29 No.2</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Person 1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: I believe online is better</span></p>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">,</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">because don’t have to go to the</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">place.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Data 10 presents an interview happens between an old man and an interviewer wherein they discuss about 2020 Singapore General Election. With the current situation, person 1 believes that the online way of holding rallies is a better option. He also adds a following reason right after stating the answer by inserting discourse particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> to mark the boundary between the different points. The occurrence showed in data 10 clearly denotes the function as an audible comma particle. It is viewed from the middle position where the particle is placed and its aim that more to make a separation between two idea, phrases, or clauses, thus the utterance can be more obvious and understandable.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark44"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark45"></a>3.3.2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Utterance-Final Marker</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font6">The function of particle as an utterance-final marker is to mark the final part of what is being conveyed and occurs at the final of the utterance. In accordance with the findings, it is found that both particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> are functioned as utterance-final marker 22 times and 38 times. In order to get a deeper understanding, see the representative data 11 provided below.</span></p>

It is identified from the final position where the particle is placed and its aim that is more to mark an end to the utterance.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark48"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark49"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusions</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font6">The analysis and discussion in this study come with insightful findings to which clear conclusions can be drawn upon. In regard to the types of discourse particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">, it appears that four types as stated in the applied theory are found in the data. They are perlocutionary </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah </span><span class="font6">(consisting of interrogative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">, imperative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">, and declarative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">), vocative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">, empathic </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6">, and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> as question marker. More on the findings, emphatic </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> appears as the prevalent type to occur with 15 occurrences. Having 10 occurrences, imperative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> follows afterward. Behind it, declarative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> appears 9 times. The rest of the frequencies includes </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> as question marker with 2 occurrences, interrogative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> with 1 occurrence, and last but not least, vocative </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> with 1 occurrence. It can be inferred that the use of discourse particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership</span><span class="font6"> videos is more frequent as an expression that implies a strong sense to emphasise something being concerned, to give a highlight, and is done without any doubt.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Vol. 29 No.2</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Concerning the types of discourse particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">, three classifications as provided in the theory are clearly identified in the data. The types comprise impositional </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">, propositional </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">, and persuasive </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">. To add up more on the frequencies, the prevalent type to occur is persuasive </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> with 26 occurrences. Following afterward, there is impositional </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> that occurs 23 times. The latter, there is propositional </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> with 13 occurrences. Viewing the distributions has brought about an inference that the use of discourse particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership </span><span class="font6">videos is more frequent as an expression that implies the sense of persuading, convincing, or sometimes requesting someone to influence ones’ thought.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">In respect of the functions of particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6">, the two functions as stated in the applied theory are observable in the data. The functions assigned to discourse particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> comprise utterance-final marker and audible comma particle. Furthermore, both particles </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah </span><span class="font6">and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> are frequently functioned as utterance-final marker wherein each of which reaches 22 and 38 occurrences. On the other part, their function as an audible comma particle appears less in which particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> reaches 16 occurrences and particle </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> reaches 24 occurrences. These distributions lead to a statement that both </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font6"> in </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mothership</span><span class="font6"> videos are frequently assigned the function as utterance-final marker rather than audible comma particle as they are primarily employed at the end of utterance to mark an end of it.</span></p>
