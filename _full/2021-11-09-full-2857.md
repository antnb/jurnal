<h3><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>ABSTRAK</span></h3>
<p><span class="font3">Artikel berikut melaporkan usaha-usaha yang telah dilakukan dalam menghasilkan respons kromatografi gas (KG) guna mendapatkan hasil analisis senyawa-senyawa steroid yang andal. Usaha-usaha itu adalah proses untuk memperoleh LOD dan LOQ serendah mungkin, menjaga linearitas respons, keterulangan, dan keandalan dari analisis. LOD yang ditunjukkan oleh prosedur yang telah dijalankan dalam percobaan ini adalah 0.0002 ng, yaitu dari menginjeksikan 1</span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font3">L sampel dengan konsentrasi terendah sebesar 0.2 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font3">gL<sup>-1</sup>, sementara LOQnya adalah 0.001 ng (yakni dengan menginjeksikan 1</span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font3">L sampel dengan konsentrasi terendah sebesar 1 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font3">gL<sup>-1</sup>). Ini berarti bahwa analisis ini mampu mendeteksi atau menentukan sampel dari lingkungan yang konsentrasinya jauh lebih kecil melalui pemekatan selama preparasi, seperti umumnya preparasi kontaminan dalam sampel air. Terbukti pula bahwa analisis dengan KG ini andal dengan linearitas tinggi dalam jangkauan konsentrasi steroid yang diteliti, yakni 0-10 mgL<sup>-1</sup>.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Kata Kunci : rhizodegradasi, limbah minyak, laju, kinetik</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h3>
<p><span class="font3">This paper reports the process conducted to maintain a high quality of GC responses in obtaining reliable data on steroid analyses of environmental samples. Works including setting up the Limit of Detection (LOD) and Limit of Quantitation (LOQ), maintaining linearity, controlling the reproducibility, and determining its reliability are described. It is evident that in this work the LOD of the GC is 0.0002 ng (by 1 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font3">L injection of 0.2 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font3">gL<sup>-1</sup> samples) and the LOQ is 0.001 ng (by 1 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font3">L injection of 1 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font3">gL<sup>-1</sup> samples), which means it is capable of detecting much lower concentrations of steroid compounds if the samples undergo 2000 times pre-concentration during preparation. Under the range investigated the GC provides reliable quantitative linear responses to all steroid compounds.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Keywords : rhizodegradation, oil waste, rate, chinetic</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">One of the most significant steps in the steroid analysis is the quantitative determination of each compound in the samples. In this project the analysis is carried out using a gas chromatograph with a mass spectrometer (GC/MS). The success of the analysis therefore depends on the reproducible performance of the GC/MS (Borjesson, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">et.al</span><span class="font4">.,1998; Nichols, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">et.al</span><span class="font4">., 1996). For this reason there are basic protocols to be carried out to assure the quality of the GC outputs.</span></p><a name="caption2"></a>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>Limit of Detection (LOD) and Limit of Quantitation (LOQ)</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">LOD is the lowest concentration detectable by the analytical method, and LOQ is the lowest concentration that can be quantitatively analysed (Nguyen, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">et.al</span><span class="font4">., 1995). The LOD and LOQ of the GC/MS is determined by analysing standard solutions over a range of concentrations until the instrument cannot detect any of the compounds injected (Piocos, E. A. and de la Cruz, A. A. 2000). In these analyses the concentration ranges chosen were 0.01 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font4">gL<sup>-1</sup>- 10 mgL<sup>-</sup>.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>Linearity.</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">Linearity of an analysis shows the correlation between the response and the amount of the analytes. Good quality analysis should give as high linearity as possible. The linearity of GC response is tested by analyzing a range of known concentration of analytes under the same protocol (Jayasinghe, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">et.al</span><span class="font4">., 1998; Cathum and Sabik, 2001). The linearity of the GC responses to the concentrations of each compound is shown by a calibration curve or statistically by the linear correlation coefficient (R<sup>2</sup>). The analysis of samples should be carried out within the linear ranges of the curve to maintain accurate quantization of the samples (Nichols, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">et.al</span><span class="font4">.,1996). Calibration standards should be included along with every sample analysis.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Reproducibility of the GC responses and silylation recovery.</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">Along with linearity, the variation of GC responses to the same sample is a measure of precision of the instrument. The variation is determined by the “coefficient of variation (C.V.)” of responses (represented by areas) of 5 or more injections of a single sample. The C.V. is calculated using the formulae:</span></p><a name="caption3"></a>
<h1><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>Sd</span></h1>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">C.V = —x</span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"> 100%</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">where </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Sd</span><span class="font4"> is the standard deviation and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">X</span><span class="font4"> is mean response</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The C.V. of the five injections is defined as the C.V. of the GC and used as a measure of the GC reproducibility for a particular compound. The lower the C.V. the better the reproducibility.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The silylation process changes steroids to their trimethylsilyl (TMSi) ether derivates to reduce their boiling points, however, the extent of the reaction cannot be quantitatively measured (Nguyen, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">et.al</span><span class="font4">., 1995). Thus reliability of the derivatisation can only be indirectly determined as an inseparable parameter of the “reproducibility of GC responses and silylation process” which is obtained by calculating the C.V. of multiple GC responses to replicates of silylation products. For example, five silylated</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">standard solutions are each analysed five times using the GC, and the C.V. of the twenty five responses are adopted as the measure of the combined GC and silylation reproducibility.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>Reliability of the analytical method</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">Using external standards for quantitative analysis, especially of environmental samples, can lead to uncertainty. This is because of the differences in matrices of samples and standards and in treatments applied to them. Standard addition method is ideal for analysing environmental samples because both unknown and standard compounds share the same matrix and treatments (Jayasinghe, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">et.al</span><span class="font4">., 1998; Cathum and Sabik, 2001). The drawbacks of using this method are time and reagent use as each replicate sample must be analysed across the range of concentrations expected.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">A compromise can be drawn by combining both methods, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">i.e.</span><span class="font4"> using external standards to determine the concentrations of the isolated compounds, and the standard addition method to calculate the percentage of recovery of the compounds from the samples. By determining the recovery, the reliability of the analytical method by which the compounds are isolated is determined. This knowledge is then used to recalculate all concentrations obtained by the external standard method of the expected concentrations in the original samples.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">In summary, to minimise the uncertainty of the results produced by external standard determination, the recovery efficiency of the analytical method used to isolate the compounds from samples needs to be determined, and this is carried out using the standard addition method.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">METHODS</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>Limit of Detection (LOD) and Limit of Quantitation (LOQ)</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">The lowest concentration of each steroid that could be detected (by the GC/MS) was determined as the LOD of the corresponding steroid, and the lowest concentration that could be quantified was the LOQ of the steroid. Both LOD and LOQ were determined by analysing</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">standard solutions of steroid mixture as detailed below.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Stock solution of 1000 mgL<sup>-1</sup> steroid mixture (cholestane, coprostanol, epicoprostanol, cholesterol, cholestanol, ethyl cholesterol, and stigmastanol was prepared with chloroform as a solvent. To prepare the 10 mgL<sup>-1</sup> solution, 1.0 mg of the above solution was placed in a 100 volumetric flask, and made to volume with chloroform, homogenized. The 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2.5, and 5 mgL<sup>-1</sup> solutions were prepared by serial dilution of the 10 mgL<sup>-1 </sup>standard solution. The 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 5.0, and 10 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font4">gL<sup>-1</sup> solutions were prepared by serial dilution of the 0.01 mgL<sup>-1</sup> solution.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Three 1 mL aliquots of each solution were derivatised (silylated) as described in Suprihatin, 2004 and were analysed using GC/MS. The column used was AT™ -5MS (Alltech), a low bleed capillary column with low polarity containing 5% Phenyl and 95% Dimethyl Polysiloxane.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>Linearity</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">The linear regressions of the GC responses (in unit counts) obtained from the above analyses against concentrations of each steroid were analysed using Microsoft® Excel.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>Reproducibility</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">1 mL aliquot of the 10 mgL<sup>-1</sup> standard solution was silylated and analysed 5 times using the GC/MS.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>Reliability of the extraction method</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">Analyses were carried out in triplicate.</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark24"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark25"></a>Standard Addition Method on sewage sample</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">To 250 mL sample of lagoon influent collected from Bolivar STP was added 0, 50, and 100 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font4">g of each standard (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">i.e.</span><span class="font4"> 0, 500, and 1000</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font4">L of 10 mgL<sup>-1</sup> the standard solutions). (When concentrated to 1 mL for silylation process these samples would have additional steroid concentrations of 0, 5, and 10 mgL<sup>-1</sup>).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The mixtures were equilibrated for 24 hours, and then analysed as described in Suprihatin, 2004.</span></p>
<h3><a name="bookmark26"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark27"></a>Environmental Samples.</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">To 2 L sample of river water was added 0, 1, 2, and 5 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font4">g of each standard (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">i.e.</span><span class="font4"> 0, 100, 200, and 500 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font4">L of 10 mgL<sup>-1</sup>of the standard solutions). Upon volume reduction during the analysis, the added standards concentrations would be 0, 1, 2, and 5 mgL<sup>-1</sup>.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The mixtures were equilibrated for 24 hours and analysed as detailed in Suprihatin, 2004.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">RESULTS AND DISCUSSION</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark28"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark29"></a>Limit of Detection (LOD) and Limit of Quantitation (LOQ)</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">The limit of detection of the GC obtained are: 0.0002 ng (equal to 1 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font4">L of 0.2 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font4">gL<sup>-1</sup> silylated mixture) for all steroid analysed. The corresponding LOQs are 0.001 ng (equal to 1 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font4">L of 1 </span><span class="font1">µ</span><span class="font4">gL<sup>-1</sup> silylated mixture) for all compounds. The LOD is improved by 250 times that of the initial LOD, but more importantly for quality control, the LOQ and its ratio to the LOD are also improved. The current LOQs are lower than the earlier LOQs and are 4.6 times of the LODs, while the old LOQs were 10 to 20 times of the corresponding LODs. The suggested ratio is 4 (Clesceri, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">et.al</span><span class="font4">.,1998). This means that following column and power board replacement much lower concentrations could be quantified.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark30"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark31"></a>Linearity</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">Initially tests for linearity of the responses to steroids (cholestane, coprostanol, epicoprostanol, cholesterol, cholestanol, ethyl cholesterol and stigmastanol) were conducted in the concentration range of 0-1000 mgL<sup>-1</sup>(not reported here). The concentration range was then</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">lowered to suit the concentrations of steroids in the environmental samples and to avoid overloading of the column. The new range was 0-10 mgL<sup>-1</sup>. The sewage samples, which were expected to contain high concentrations of steroids, were addressed by sample dilution.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark32"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark33"></a>Sensitivity and Reproducibility</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">Concern arose about the sensitivity and especially the reproducibilty of the GC responses when large variations were obtained from multiple injections of a single standard mixture of 10 mgL<sup>-1</sup> (not shown). An experiment was then conducted to calculate the size of this variation. A newly silylated 10 mgL<sup>-1</sup> standard solution was injected 5 times in a row under a variety conditions, and the resulting coefficient of variation (C.V.) is shown in Figure 2.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The first series of the column diagram (‘w/o blank’) represents 5 injections of a single standard without blank injections between samples (Figure 2). The high C.V. suggests there are carryovers from the previous injections into subsequent ones, and a simple test to find out whether it was caused by the column or the injector was carried out by injecting a sample following by running the GC/MS without</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">sample. It was concluded that the carryover was caused by the injector. Series 2 on the same diagram (Figure 2. ‘w/ blank’) shows the results of attempts to remove the carryover, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">i.e</span><span class="font4">. by injection of blanks between two samples. There was great improvement in C.V. However, injection of blanks in between is not practical for sample analysis, and so two other methods were tested, namely changing the relay method from splitless to split/splitless and in addition doubling the injector wash time (‘double wash’). The results show that changing the relay method improved the C.V. only slightly, while the combination of changing relay method and doubling the wash time resulted in a lower C.V. and thus more reproducible responses. On the basis of this experimental testing, it was decided that this final condition, namely split/splitless injection with 50 seconds wash time, was to be adopted for all future work.</span></p>

<h3><a name="bookmark36"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark37"></a>COCLUSION</span></h3>
<p><span class="font4">Considerable effort and resources were delivered to the development of the quality control protocols both on the GC analysis and for the analytical method. This was without doubt essential to conduct a robust analysis with reliable and reproducible results.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The work resulted in a significant improvement in the performance of the system. The LOQ/LOD ratio was improved from 10-20 (previous work, not shown) to as low as 5 which is close to the recommended value by the Standard Method (Clesceri, </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">et.al</span><span class="font4">.,1998). Combined with the linearity of the responses at all anticipated steroid concentrations, the GC was capable of responding in a quantitatively linear manner to the steroid contents both in environmental (low range) and sewage (higher range) samples.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The great improvements in sensitivity and reproducibility contributed to the success in</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">using the GC/MS for quantitative analyses. The sensitivity increased up to 40 times during the practice of the quality control protocol. This means the system is able to detect low concentration with much greater accuracy, as long as the amount injected is not lower than the LOQ (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">i.e.</span><span class="font4">. 0.001 ng). This finding is mostly valuable for the analyses of environmental samples, which generally contain low concentrations of pollutants including steroids.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The recoveries of steroids by the standard addition method demonstrate that the analytical method used to isolate steroids from both environmental and sewage samples is reliable. The method is capable of isolating all compounds tested with low standard deviations.</span></p>
