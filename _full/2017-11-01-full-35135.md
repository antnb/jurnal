<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Penelitian ini berjudul“Analisis tipe-tipe semantik dari kelas kata sifat pada kolokasi kata sifat yang mengandung kata benda dengan mengacu pada novel Victims of Violence” bertujuan meneliti dan menganalisis tipe-tipe semantik pada kolokasi kata sifat yang mengandung kata benda.Data penelitianini di ambildari novel yang berjudul Victims of Violence.Novel ini dipublikasikan pada 7 April 2003. Ada 42 contoh data yang dimasukkan dalam analisis ini.Selain itu, untuk menganalisis makna pada data ada tiga tipe utama yang terdapat pada kolokasi yaitu based wholly on the meaning of the item,based on the range and based on the strictest sense. Contoh lainnya dimasukkan kedalam kolokasi based on the range karena mereka dapat bersama dengan jenis kata yang memiliki bebarapa fitur semantik yang sama.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Backgraund of the Study</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Semantic is the study of meaning. It is a wide subject within the general study of language. To link a word with another word is very important to create a meaning. A word cannot stand by itself because it does not have meaning. A word can have meaning when it entries a sentence or a clause. The individual words combine together as phrases and sentences in a language. The word combination comes from the two words or more which combine and linked eachother. As we know, there are many kinds of word combinations. However, this study focuses on collocation.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The collocation is concerned with how words go together (Larson, 1984:155). The collocation is categorized into six different types, they are; verb + noun, adjective + noun, noun + noun, adverb + adjective and verb + adverb. Collocation is concerned with how words are combined together here; a word cannot just combine with other words without looking at the meaning carried by a word. However, this study only focuses on adjective noun. Adjective noun means in some instance, more than one adjective (or more than one form of the same adjective) can collocate with the same noun. For example, the colocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">light</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">bag</span><span class="font2">. The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">light</span><span class="font2"> denotes weight which means the bag is light. In this case, thenoun adjective collocation can be found in many literary works. One of them is a novel entitled </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Victims of Violence</span><span class="font2"> by Joan Jonker.</span></p>

<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Meaning of Collocations A. The Meaning of Collocation</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Based Wholly on the Meaning of the Item</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">loud voice </span><span class="font2">comes from the combination of adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">loud</span><span class="font2"> and the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">voice</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Loud </span><span class="font2">means producing much noise, easily heard, forcing people to notice them or it (Hornby, 1995: 698).</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Voice</span><span class="font2"> means sounds produced through the mouth by a person speaking or singing. As this collocation belongs to the type based on the whole meaning. The meaning is derived from the combination of the two words of adjective and noun. So, the collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">loud voice</span><span class="font2"> means sound produced by a person speaking which much noise, easily heard.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">only answer </span><span class="font2">comes from the combination of adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">only</span><span class="font2"> and the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">answer. Only</span><span class="font2"> is used to give a limitation of something in term of its number. The adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">only</span><span class="font2"> refers to no one or nothing else that exists or present when it is used in isolation (Hornby, 1995: 810). </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Answer</span><span class="font2"> means the thing said or written in response to reply or solution (Hornby, 1995: 115). As this collocation belongs to the type based on the whole meaning, the meaning is derived from the combination of the two words of adjective and noun. So, the collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">only answer</span><span class="font2"> means limitation of thing said in response to reply.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">black print </span><span class="font2">comes from the combination of the adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">black</span><span class="font2"> and the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">print.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Black</span><span class="font2"> means the color that has the very dark color, like night or coal (Hornby, 2010: 138). </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Print</span><span class="font2"> means producingletters, on paper using a machine that puts ink on the surface (Hornby, 2010: 649). As this collocation belongs to the type based on the whole meaning, the meaning is derived from the combination of the two words of adjective and noun. So, the collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">black print</span><span class="font2"> means producing letters in paper using a machine with a dark color.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">British rail </span><span class="font2">comes from the combination of adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">British</span><span class="font2"> and the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">rail. British</span><span class="font2"> means relating to Great Britain or the United Kingdom, or to its people or language (Hornby, 2010: 1132). </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Rail </span><span class="font2">means steel bar on which trains run (Hornby, 2010: 662). As this collocation belongs to the type based on the whole meaning, the meaning is derived from the combination of the two words of adjective and noun. So, the collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Britis rail</span><span class="font2"> means steel bar on which trains runs located at England.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark29"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark30"></a>B. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Meaning of the Collocational Based on the Range.</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">big words </span><span class="font2">comes from the combination of adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">big</span><span class="font2"> and the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">words</span><span class="font2">. This adjective noun belongs to the based on the range because the meaning which is created is derives from the secondary meaning. The adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">big</span><span class="font2"> means large in size, extent or intensity (Hornby, 1995:105). The noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">words</span><span class="font2"> means written or spoken unit of language or thing that you say (Hornby, 2010: 613). The adjective noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">big words</span><span class="font2"> mean words that arent in someone’s limited vocabulary in which he or she would be accused as trying hard to sound intellegent. In other words, it end up as nonsense.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">young hearts </span><span class="font2">comes from the combination of adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">young</span><span class="font2"> and the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">heart</span><span class="font2">. This adjective noun is based on the range because the meaning which is created is derived from the secondary meaning. The adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">young</span><span class="font2"> means not far advanced in life, growth, development, etc. (Hornby, 1995:1389). The noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">heart</span><span class="font2"> means organ that pumps blood around the body (Honby, 2010: 306). The adjective noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">young heart </span><span class="font2">points to the children as they are typically innocent.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">wooden box </span><span class="font2">comes from the combination of the adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">wooden</span><span class="font2"> and the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">box. </span><span class="font2">This adjective noun belongs to the range because the meaning which is created is derived from the secondary meaning. The adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">wooden</span><span class="font2"> means made of wood; the hard material that the trunk and branches of a tree are made of (Hornby 2010: 512). The noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">box</span><span class="font2"> means container made of wood, cardboard,etc., usually with a lid, used for holding solid things (Hornby, 2010: 147). The adjective noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">wooden box </span><span class="font2">has the similar meaning to </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">coffin. Coffin </span><span class="font2">means box in which a dead body is buried or cremated.</span></p>

<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">C. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Meaning of the Collocational Based on the Strictest Sense, Involving Neither Meaning Nor Range.</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">good hiding </span><span class="font2">comes from the combination of the adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">good</span><span class="font2"> and the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">hiding. </span><span class="font2">This collocation is considered collocation based on the strictest sense because the words which collocate each other suit the context it occurs. The adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">good</span><span class="font2"> has the meaning of showing the high quality of something or pleasant; that you enjoy or want (Hornby, 1995:512). The noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">hiding</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">means physical punishment of being beaten (Hornby, 2010:309). It shows that the collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">good hiding </span><span class="font2">naturally means term for the act of threatening someone by hitting or slapping. The context supports that the collocation means that way. The adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">good</span><span class="font2"> means the sarcasm. The collocation </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">good hiding</span><span class="font2"> is easily understood since they occur each other by the context. In contrary, the adjective </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">nice</span><span class="font2"> which has the same meaning to </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">good</span><span class="font2"> cannot collocate with </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">hiding</span><span class="font2"> to create the same meaning as it is not easily understood and does not sound natural eventhough they are synonyms.</span></p>
