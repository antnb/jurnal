<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">Creation of new superior varieties of mango through the empowerment of local genetic resources requires information about the potential properties of mango germplasm, including DNA polymorphism and its genetic diversity. This research aimed to obtain basic data on DNA polymorphism, genetic relationship, genetic similarity level, and molecular accession of Bali’s unique local mango. Sampling was conducted in four regencies in the Province of Bali. DNA preparation, PCR, and microsatellite analysis were carried out at the Laboratory of Genetics and Plant Breeding, Faculty of Agriculture, Gadjah Mada University. It found 44 accessions of mangoes in Bali. The test results with 10 pairs of SSR markers showed that all primers produced polymorphic loci. There were 825 amplified DNA bands. Primer of AY31 produced the highest number of loci, which were 14 loci, while AY21 produced the fewest loci, i.e., 3 loci. Forty-four mango accessions showed a genetic similarity coefficient of 0.27 to 0.97. At a coefficient of 0.27, accessions were divided into 2 major groups: group A and group B. Group A consisted of two accessions, namely, KRA-005 and BDG-006 (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera foetida</span><span class="font5"> Lour.), which had a similar coefficient of 0.657. Group B was divided into two smaller groups, namely, groups B1 and B2, at a similarity coefficient of 0.342. Group B1 consisted of 39 accessions, while group B2 consisted of 3 accessions. Accessions Madu Anggur, Gading, Sambuk Mengwi, Kakul and Pakel Sulangai were identified as having unique alleles.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Keywords:</span><span class="font5"> Polimorphism</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">, g</span><span class="font5">enetic similarity, Mango, DNA, Microsatellite</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">Creation of new superior varieties (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">varietas unggul baru</span><span class="font5"> or VUB) of mango by empowering local genetic resources requires information about the potential properties of mango germplasm. Information about the potential properties is obtained through exploration and characterization of mango germplasm. Furthermore, the information obtained becomes the basis for consideration</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">in developing conservation, breeding, management and sustainable use of mango genetic resources.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Characterization of mango germplasm in Indonesia has been carried out by a number of researchers from Indonesian Tropical Fruit Research Institute and various universities in Indonesia, for example, characterizations that were conducted by Kostermans and Bompart (1993) in the West</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Kalimantan and South Kalimantan regions, Sumiasri </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">. (2006) in Madiun Regency, Rai </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">. (2008) in the Bali region specifically for </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera Caesia</span><span class="font5"> Jack., Krisnawati (2008) in the Central Kalimantan region, Sumarsono </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">. (2012) in Banyumas Regency, Fitawati </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">. (2013) in the Central Sumatra region, and Oktavianto </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">. (2015) in Kediri Regency. Characterization was also carried out on mango germplasm collection in the Mango Experimental Garden in Kampung Cukur Gondang, Pasuruan Regency (Maltuchah and Zainudin, 2007; Fitawati </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2010; Zainudin </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2010; Utami </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2012; Tasliah </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2013; Karsinah </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2015). However, given the vast territory of Indonesia and the limited budget and availability of research resources, not all mango germplasm can be characterized, including those mango germplasm in the Province of Bali. Bompard and Schnell (1997) reported that there were 5 species of mangoes found in the Province of Bali. The five species are </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera indica </span><span class="font5">L., </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera foetida</span><span class="font5"> Lour., </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera caesia</span><span class="font5"> Jack, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera odorata</span><span class="font5"> Griff., and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera lalijiwa</span><span class="font5"> Kosterms. A quite indepth study of the characteristics of the </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera Caesia</span><span class="font5"> Jack was carried out by Rai </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">. (2008). However, species of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera indika</span><span class="font5"> L., </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera foetida </span><span class="font5">Lour., </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera odorata</span><span class="font5"> Griff., and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera lalijiwa</span><span class="font5"> Kosterms are still not</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">much studied so that basic data on DNA polymorphism, genetic similarity, genetic relationship, and the molecular uniqueness of mango germplasm in Bali are very limited.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">This research aims to obtain basic data on DNA polymorphism, genetic relationship, and the level of genetic similarity, and to obtain accessions of unique local mangoes in Bali that molecularly and potentially may be developed as New Superior Varieties.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>Research Location and Time</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">Field research (on farm characterization) was conducted in 4 (four) regencies in the Province of Bali, Indonesia, namely, Buleleng, Karangasem, Badung, and Klungkung. Meanwhile, DNA preparation, PCR, and microsatellite analysis were conducted in the Laboratory of Genetics and Plant Breeding at the Faculty of Agriculture, Gadjah Mada University. The study was conducted for 13 months from July 2018 to August 2019.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>Materials and Research Tools</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">Exploration equipments that were used included GPS, digital cameras, books of mango descriptors, scissors, label paper, plastic straps, paper bags, cool boxes, and stationery. Meanwhile, DNA analysis equipments were mortar, centrifugation</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">machines, micro pipettes, micro tubes, ovens, MJ Research PCT 100 PCR machines, gel electrophoresis, and Beckman Coulter® CEQ-8000 genetic analyzer. The materials used were young leaves, extraction buffer, EDTA, ice, lysis buffer, dithiothreitol, chloroform isoamil alcoholyl, sodium acetate, isopropanol, 70% ethanol, PCR buffer, forward primer, reverse primer, and Taq DNA Polymerase.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Determination of Sampling Location</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">Accession of Bali’s local mangoes was obtained through exploration at predetermined sampling locations. The sampling location was determined by the stratified random sampling method (Chaudhuri and Stenger, 2005). Sampling was done in two stages. The first stage was to determine the location of the district. Meanwhile, the second stage was to determine the sub-district sampling locations in each selected district. The research sample was taken from 4 (four) districts that were sorted by the number of sub-districts with local mango accessions. Each district was chosen from 4 sub-districts with the highest number of villages with mango accession. Mango plant leaf samples were taken from each accession that was found in each</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">village. Plants that were selected were plants that were more than 10 years old, in healthy conditions, and classified as the best among plants on the same accession in the vicinity.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>Scope of Bali’s Local Mangoes</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">Determination of local mango accession refers to the scope proposed by Rai </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">. (2016) that Bali’s local mangoes belong to the Bali’s local fruit group. As defined by Article 1 number 6 of the Bali Province Regional Regulation Number 3 of 2013, Bali’s local fruit referred to in this research refers to all types of fruits developed and cultivated in Bali.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>Molecular Characterization</span></h1>

<h1><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>DNA Isolation and PCR Analysis</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">A total of 1 to 2 g of young leaves from each accession was extracted until DNA samples were obtained. The DNA isolation procedure used cetyl trimethyl ammonium bromide (CTAB) buffer extraction, following the Doyle and Doyle (1990) protocols. Dithiothreitol was added to prevent oxidation of phenolic compounds during extraction. The PCR reaction was carried out in a total volume of 20 μl.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">DNA multiplication stages were initiation of denaturation at 95°C for 3 minutes, followed by 34 cycles consisting of denaturation at 94°C for 1 minute, primer attachment at 50°C for 1 minute, and extension of base at 72°C for 2 minutes (Schnell </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2005). The final stage of the PCR process was the final extension at 72°C for 5 minutes and incubation at 4°C. To see whether an amplified DNA appeared, electrophoresis using 2% agarose gel was performed. Primers, which produced bands in</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">agarose gel, were used for DNA fragment analysis.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>Analysis of DNA Fragments</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">DNA fragments were detected on a genetic analyzer (Beckman Coulter® CEQ ™ 8000). The procedure for preparing PCR product samples for loading and procedures for running CEQ 8000 followed the protocol established by Thomson (2004). Samples were analyzed by multiplexing, i.e., mixing PCR amplification products with two kinds of green and blue fluorescent primers in one well. PCR products with a certain volume (according to the optimization results) of each primer were mixed in one well, then 0.5 μl of CEQ internal size standard (400 bp) labeled in red and SLS with a final volume of 40 μl were added.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Fragment size analysis results on CEQ 8000 were processed using CEQ Fragment Analysis software. Then, binning was carried out. Binning is grouping DNA</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">fragments (alleles) based on the number of repeats of certain DNA motifs (for example, repeat of threads from two, three, or four bases pairs) flanked by a pair of microsatellite primers.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>Data Analysis</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">DNA concentration and purity of Bali's local mango accession were measured using the Gene Quant 1300 spectrophotometer. DNA concentrations obtained were high, i.e., more than 100 ng/µL, even most DNA oncentrations reached thousands of ng/µL. The purity of DNA compared to impurities, including protein impurity, was measured by the wavelength of λ260/280, while the purity of DNA compared to impurity in the form of RNA was measured by the wavelength of λ260/280. Good DNA purity ranges from 1.8 to 2.2.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Analysis of mango germplasm accessions was based on the level of allele similarity between accessions, using the genetic distance formula described by Nei </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">. (1983). The dendrogram was generated from the binary data of the mango plant molecular scoring results using the unweighted pair group method with arithmetic mean (UPGMA) using NTSYS 2.02 software.</span></p>

<p><span class="font5">Based on species, 44 accessions of Bali’s local mangoes come from 4 species, namely, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera indica</span><span class="font5"> L., </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera odorata</span><span class="font5"> Griff., </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera foetida</span><span class="font5"> Lour., and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera lalijiwa</span><span class="font5"> Kosterms. Accession of the species of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera indica</span><span class="font5"> L. dominates mango germplasm in Bali with 38 accessions or 86.36%. This finding corroborates the results of research by Bompard and Schnell (1997) that reported that there were 5 species of mangoes found in Bali. The five species were </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera indica</span><span class="font5"> L., </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera foetida </span><span class="font5">Lour., </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera caesia</span><span class="font5"> Jack, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera odorata</span><span class="font5"> Griff., and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera lalijiwa </span><span class="font5">Kosterms. Specifically on </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera caesia </span><span class="font5">Jack, Rai </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">. (2008) have conducted quite in-depth studies. However, on the other 4</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">species, no in-depth molecular studies have been carried out.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark26"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark27"></a>DNA Concentration and Purity</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">DNA concentration and purity of 44 Bali’s local mango accessions were measured using &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;the &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;GeneQuant &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;1300</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">spectrophotometer. DNA concentrations obtained were high, i.e. more than 100 ng/µL. Most of concentrations even reached thousands of ng/µL. Good DNA purity ranges from 1.8 to 2.2. From the results of the quantification, it was known that the DNA purity of the 44 accessions of the mango analyzed was quite good because it was in the range 1.8 - 2.2.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark28"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark29"></a>DNA Polymorphism</span></h1>

<h1><a name="bookmark32"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark33"></a>The Unique Accession of Mango Germplasm</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">Among 44 accessions of Bali’s local mangoes, there are five individuals that have one unique allele, as presented in Table 3.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">The five accessions are BLL-028, BDG-002, BDG-003, BDG-004, and BDG-006. These alleles were mostly amplified by different primers, but some were identified in the same primers at different loci (AJ68 in accessions BDG-004 and BDG-003). Unique alleles are alleles that are only owned by one individual/accession at a specific locus size, and are not owned by another individual. BLL-028 (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Madu Anggur</span><span class="font5">) is a superior variety that has been released by the government or is classified as non-landraces. Whereas, BDG-002 (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Gading</span><span class="font5">), BDG-003 (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Sambuk Mengwi</span><span class="font5">), BDG-004 (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Kakul</span><span class="font5">), and BDG-006 (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Pakel Sulangai</span><span class="font5">) are accessions of landraces. In principle, accessions that have</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">unique alleles can be a characteristic of the accessions concerned and have the potential to be developed as plant breeding material</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">and combined with other superior properties so that in the long run, they can be developed into New Superior Varieties.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark34"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark35"></a>CONCLUSION</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">It can be concluded from this study that 44 mango accessions were found in Bali. Thirty four accessions or 77.27% are landrace accessions. The rest are nonlandrace accessions. The accession of Bali’s local mangoes originated from 4 species, namely, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera indica</span><span class="font5"> L., </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera odorata</span><span class="font5"> Griff.</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">, Mangifera foetida</span><span class="font5"> Lour., and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera lalijiwa</span><span class="font5"> Kosterms. Ten primers</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">produced polymorphic loci. There were 825 amplified DNA bands. AY31 Primer produced the highest number of loci, which was 14 loci, while AY21 Primer produced the fewest loci, i.e., as many as 3 loci. Forty-four mango accessions showed a genetic similarity coefficient of 0.27 to 0.97. At a coefficient of 0.27, it was divided into 2 major groups, namely, group A and group B. Group A consisted of two accessions, namely, KRA-005 and BDG-006 (species of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mangifera foetida</span><span class="font5"> Lour.), which had a similarity coefficient of 0.657. Group B was divided into two smaller groups, namely, groups B1 and B2 at a similarity coefficient of 0.342. Group B1 consisted of 39 accessions, while group B2 consisted of 3 accessions. Accessions Madu Anggur, Gading, Sambuk Mengwi, Kakul and Pakel Sulangai were identified as having unique alleles.</span></p>
