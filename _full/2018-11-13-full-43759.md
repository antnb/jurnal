<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>Abstract</span></h2>
<p><span class="font2">This study entitled </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">The Use of Singlish Discourse Particle ‘lah’ in SGAG’s Videos </span><span class="font2">is aimed to analyze the types and function of discourse particle in SGAG’s videos. The data were taken from the conversation in SGAG’s video collection. Descriptive qualitative method was applied to analyze the types and functions of discourse particle ‘lah’. The theories used to analyze it are the classification of the type of discourse particle theory by Wong, role of particles theory by Ling and Deterding and Context of Situation by Halliday. The result of analysis shows that there were 62 discourse particle lah in the SGAG’s video collection from May 28<sup>th</sup> 2017 until October 10<sup>th</sup> 2017. The major type of discourse particle lah was Propositional lah (33 occurrences) and the function is more often as a pragmatic role (56 occurrences) rather than a syntactic function (6 occurrences).</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Keywords: Singaporean English, Discourse Particle, Lah</span></p>

<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Kata kunci: Singaporean English, Discourse Particle, lah</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">often gets misunderstood by people</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">. Singlish</span><span class="font2"> is a language variety in Singapore, not a whole Singaporean English. There are two varieties of English in Singapore.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The first one is Singapore Standard English (SSE), which is similar to British English, as the Standard English in Singapore. The second one is Singapore Colloquial English (SCE) or </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Singlish</span><span class="font2">. Harada (2009:70) stated that English is the second language in Singapore, while Mandarin, Chinese Dialects (Hokkien, Teochewan and Cantonese), Malay and Tamil are the first languages in Singapore.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Singapore Standard English is derived from British English. Based on the history according to Langford and Brownsey (1998:176), Singapore was a British colony for nearly 140 years. There were many factors why English spread in Singapore. Based on Brown and Ling (2005:359), those factors are the enhancement factors of English communication in government service, harbors, commercial companies and banks. In addition, English is facilitated by the existence of English-medium schools. All of those factors caused English to gain its own position in Singapore. The high use of English in several important institutions makes the learning and use of English by the population necessary.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">One of the unique features in </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Singlish </span><span class="font2">is the discourse particle. In general, discourse particlesalso known as </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">tags,</span><span class="font2"> occur at the end of a sentence. Their presence changes the meaning or the tone of the sentence, but not its grammatical meaning. Particles are noted for keeping their tones regardless of the remainder of the sentence.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Ling and Deterding (2003:65) called discourse particle as an utterance final particle or audible comma particle, because discourse particle sometimes occurs at the end of the sentence as utterance final particle, and in some other cases discourse particles occur in the middle of the</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">sentence as audible comma particle. Lim (2007:469) points out that, discourse particles are the influences of Malay, Hokkien and Cantonese. Malay and Hokkien contributed the particles </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah, ah, </span><span class="font2">and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">what</span><span class="font2">. Cantonese contributed </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lor, hor, leh, meh</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ma.</span><span class="font2"> Those discourse particles have their own meanings and functions.</span></p>

<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Analysis of Type and Function Of Discourse Particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Lah</span></p>

<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Impositional </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Lah</span></p></li></ul></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Data 1</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Title 1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: When You Plan The</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Perfect Proposal But…</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Setting &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: On the call (Bobby and</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Kenny)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Kenny : Yo Bobby what’s up!</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Bobby : </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Eh</span><span class="font2"> Bro, do you think she’d want a public proposal or not?</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Kenny : For what? So she can reject the proposal in public </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">meh</span><span class="font2">?</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Cannot </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Bobby : </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Aiya</span><span class="font2">, then how?</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The dialogue occurs when Bobby and Kenny were talking on the phone about how to plan a perfect proposal for Kenny’s Girlfriend. There are two participants involved in the conversation; Bobby and Kenny. Bobby is a close friend of Kenny, who is planning to propose his girlfriend. Bobby helps him to make an unforgettable proposal and gives Kenny an idea to make a public proposal, but Kenny does not agree because he is afraid if his girlfriend rejects it in public so he would be so embarrassed. As the result, Kenny said ‘cannot’ with the additional discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> with a low tone.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The type of discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> in the dialogue performed by Bobby and Kenny can be classified as Impositional </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2">, while the function can be classified as the utterance final particle. The discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> in the dialogue is used as the speaker’s (Kenny’s) tool to change the addressee’s (Bobby’s) idea forcefully. Kenny is using the discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> to emphasizing his words to make the addressee understand that he does not want a public proposal. In other words, Kenny presents his emphasis attitude.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark27"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark28"></a>5.2.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Propositional </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Lah</span></h2></li></ul>

<p><span class="font2">Title 3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: When You Run Out of</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">Setting &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: Tifannis’s room</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">get a bit too much. Like there’s this one time, I asked him to</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">The conversation occurred when a couple, Charles and Tifannis, are getting an interview &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;by &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;SGAG &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;about</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">datalackaphobia</span><span class="font2">, a condition where you have the fear of being deprived of sufficient data. It affects 6 in every 10 Singaporeans. SGAG’s interviewer sat down with them to find out more about his battle with the condition. There is nothing serious about the interview. It is made as a representative of Singaporean who are addicted with internet data, as they become frustrated and depressed when their internet data is running out. Charles is used to suffer from </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">datalackahobia</span><span class="font2">, which made him become grumpy. When Tifannis is getting interview, she said that actually his boyfriend is quite a sweet person, but sometimes it (the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">datalackaphobia</span><span class="font2">) can get a little bit too much. She asks him to check the timing of a movie when they were hanging out, and he just refused.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The types of discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> in the dialogue performed by Tifanni to the interviewer about his boyfriend who is suffering from </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">datalackaphobia</span><span class="font2"> can be classified as Propositional </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2">, while the function can be classified as the audible comma particle. She put the additional </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah </span><span class="font2">in the end of the sentence to strengthen her opinion by emphasizing that her boyfriend</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">is actually a good boyfriend, but sometimes it (the datalackaphobia) gets too much. In this case, the speaker (Tifannis) is using the discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> to emphasize her utterance to the addressee (the interviwer).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark29"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark30"></a>5.2.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Persuasive </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Lah</span></h2></li></ul>

<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2">!</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">market myself </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ah</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The conversation occurs between mother and son. The mother is asking his son, Charles, to accompany her to go to the market, but Charles refuses because he is still streaming a show using the house’s wifi. The mother keeps persuading Charles and asks him to watch the show on the bus. Charles still refuses by making excuses that if he uses his data, it means that he would be wasting money. As a result, the mother goes to the market by herself.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">There is two discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> in the dialogue which can be classified as persuasive </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2">, while utterance final particle is classified as the function. First, the mother puts the additional </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> in the end of the utterance to strengthen her utterance and uses the persuasive </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> to</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">persuade Charles to go to the market with her. There is a friendliness attitude in her utterance since she persuades Charles without an imperative. When Charles refuses, the mother keeps trying to persuade him by saying that he could watch it on a bus with the additional </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> in the end of the utterance. In this case, the mother presents an impatience attitude which she really wants Charles to accompany her to the market.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark31"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark32"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">This last chapter presents the conclusion drawn from the research analysis in the previous chapter. The answer to the research questions have been discovered in the previous chapter. Based on the analysis, it can be signified that discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> which occurs more frequently is Propositional </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2">, which reached 33 occurrences. Then, followed by the occurrences of impositional </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> which is 17 occurrences and the lowest is persuasive </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> which is 12 occurrences. It can be concluded that the discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> in SGAG’s videos tend to be used to emphasize an idea, suggestion or advice rather than to request the addressee to change their action, while it is rarely used to request or persuade people. This tendency occurs because the discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> is mostly used by the speakers to end or to conclude the conversation.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Meanwhile, from the result obtained, there were 56 occurrences of discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> as utterance-final particle (pragmatic role), while the discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> as an audible comma particle (syntactic function) only reached 6 occurrence. It could be concluded that the function of discourse particle </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lah</span><span class="font2"> in this analysis emerged more often as a pragmatic role than a syntactic function.</span></p>
