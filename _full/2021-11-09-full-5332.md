<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>Abstrak</span></h1>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Alasan memilih judul ini karena bahasa Inggris sangat kaya dalam penggunaan bahasa kiasan yang dapat dipertimbangkan sebagai salah satu yang paling sulit dan bagian yang paling menarik dalam kosa kata bahasa Inggris. Jurnal ini menyangkut dengan jenis bahasa kiasan yang ditemukan dalam novel, cara menerjemahkan bahasa kiasan dan juga konsep kesetaraan yang ditemukan ketika menerjemahkan bahasa kiasan dengan menggunakan teori yang dikemukakan oleh Larson. Temuan menunjukkan bahwa ada lima jenis bahasa kiasan ditemukan yaitu metonimia, sinekdok, idiom, eufemisme, dan hiperbola dalam novel. Dalam analisis, hanya satu contoh yang digunakan di setiap jenis bahasa kiasan untuk menghindari analisis berulang. Dalam proses penerjemahan, bisa ditemukan beberapa cara bagaimana data yang diterjemahkan, misalnya terjemahan dengan mengubah bentuk atau struktur kata atau kalimat dan terjemahan dengan memberikan penambahan atau pengurangan kata atau kalimat dari sumber ke novel hasil terjemahan. Dalam hitungan kesetaraan, ada dua konsep yang ditemukan ketika menganalisis data. Mereka adalah kesetaraan leksikal ketika konsep diketahui dan kesetaraan leksikal ketika konsep tidak diketahui dalam hasil terjemahan. Setelah melakukan analisa, hasil yang didapat adalah terdapat lima jenis bahasa kiasan yang berdasarkan pada teori terjemahan yang dikemukakan oleh Larson dan dianalisa dalam novel The Lightning Thief dan atau Pencuri Petir dalam bahasa Indonesia. Jenis bahasa kiasan tersebut adalah metonimia, sinekdok, idiom, eufemisme dan hiperbola. Setiap bahasa kiasan dianalisa dengan menggunkan masing-masing satu contoh untuk membuat analisa menjadi lebih mudah untuk dimengerti.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci: bahasa kiasan, terjemahan, kesetaraan bahasa</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Every language especially English is very rich in the use of figurative language. Larson (1998:6) recommends that a translator also needs to develop sensitivity to the use of figurative languages in the TL and use them naturally to make the translation lively and keep the style of the SL. In the way to make an interaction with the society, people write</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">books. The writers, of course, are using some beautiful words and sentences to make their books more interesting and figurative languages are the solution of it.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Problem of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">According to the background, there are three problems in this study, those are:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;What types of English figurative languages were found in the novel?</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;What lexical equivalents were found when translating the figurative languages from </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">The Lightning Thief</span><span class="font1"> to </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Pencuri Petir</span><span class="font1">?</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Aims of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Every scientific of writing should have its own purpose and so did this study. This study tried to establish the following aims:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To explain the types of English figurative languages found in the novel.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To describe the concepts of equivalent found when translating the figurative languages from </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">The Lightning Thief</span><span class="font1"> to </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Pencuri Petir</span><span class="font1">.</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Method</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Research method is a procedure in doing research. The research in this study was a qualitative research which all the data were collected directly from the source and emphasize on the descriptive explanation.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>4.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Data Source</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">According to the need of analysis, the data in this study were taken from the novel written by Rick Riordan entitled </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">The Lightning Thief</span><span class="font1"> which was published by Disney in 2006 with 375 pages and also its Indonesian version translated by Femmy Syahrani entitled </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Pencuri Petir</span><span class="font1"> which was published by Mizan Media Utama in 2008 with 454 pages.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>4.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Method and Technique of Collecting Data</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The methods that applied in this study were library research method and the data were collected in some steps in order to obtain appropriate and sufficient data.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>4.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Method and Technique of Analyzing Data</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The collected data were classified from these types, whether they were a Metonymy, Synecdoche, Idioms, Euphemism, Hyperbole, Metaphor, or Simile. Then, the data were analyzed by explaining the way of how it translated and also its translation equivalence, whether using the equivalence when concepts in the SL text are known (shared) or unknown in the TL text.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>5.1.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Metonymy</span></h1></li></ul></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In translating the data above, from English </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“on that happy note, it’s time for lunch.”</span><span class="font1"> to Indonesian </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“Dengan akhir cerita yang bahagia itu, sekarang waktu makan siang.”,</span><span class="font1"> the translator changed the form of the underlined word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">note</span><span class="font1"> in SL into the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">cerita</span><span class="font1"> in TL. Literally, the translation of the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">note</span><span class="font1"> is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">catatan</span><span class="font1"> in Indonesian and the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">cerita</span><span class="font1"> is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">story</span><span class="font1"> in English. In this case it was not translated literally because the meaning of the sentence was unnatural. So, in order to make it natural, the translator changed the form of the underlined word figuratively. In this example, the translator used the concept in the SL which is known (shared) in the TL, specifically in the way of using related words as equivalent. In this case, the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">note</span><span class="font1"> in SL is closely related or has a close meaning to </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">cerita</span><span class="font1"> in TL.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>5.1.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Synecdoche</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In translating the data above, from English </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“I could tell from her tone that the last time hadn’t gone well.”</span><span class="font1"> to Indonesian </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“Dari nadanya Aku bisa menebak, bahwa yang terakhir kali ini tidak berjalan lancar.”</span><span class="font1"> the translator translated the underlined word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">tone</span><span class="font1"> to </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">nada</span><span class="font1"> in order to maintain its meaning and made it natural. Besides, it can be translated literally </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">(tone = nada)</span><span class="font1"> in order to naturalize the meaning of the sentence.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In this example, the translator used the concept in the SL which is known (shared) in the TL, specifically in the way of using generic-specific words. In this case, the </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">tone</span><span class="font1"> in SL takes a role as a more specific term, while </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">nada </span><span class="font1">in TL takes a role as a more generic term.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>5.1.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Idiom</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The sentence in the SL above is being used in a figurative way. The idiomatic expression </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">hit the sack</span><span class="font1"> by 7<sup>th</sup> edition Oxford Advanced Learner’s Dictionary means </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">to start doing something and continues very quickly and successfully; goes to bed.</span><span class="font1"> If this idiom is translated into Indonesian </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">hit the sack</span><span class="font1"> become </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">tidur</span><span class="font1">. In this example, the translator uses the concept in the SL which is known (shared) in the TL, specifically in the way of using related words as equivalents. In translating the idiom above, the translator changed the form and the meaning of the words idiomatically. In Indonesian, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">tidur</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">membenturkan diri ke kasur</span><span class="font1"> have a very close relationship in terms of the meaning.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">However if the translator used </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">membenturkan diri ke kasur</span><span class="font1"> the translation would not be natural so in this case, it is replaced by </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">tidur </span><span class="font1">because both sentences are very close related in terms of the meaning. The meaning of the idiom </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">hit the sack</span><span class="font1"> in Indonesian is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">membenturkan diri ke kasur.</span><span class="font1"> So it can be also referred to </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">tidur</span><span class="font1"> in order to make the reader more understand about the meaning and make it more natural in TL. In another word, it has the same meaning with the meaning that is intended by the idiom in the source context.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark24"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark25"></a>5.1.4 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Euphemism</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In translating euphemism the translator should recognize the euphemistic nature of the SL expression and then translated with an appropriate and acceptable expression of the TL whether euphemistic or direct. In this case, from English </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">He looked at the picture of his mother, the only person who had loved him, and who was now gone</span><span class="font1"> to Indonesian </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Dia melihat ke arah foto ibunya, satu-satunya yang mencintainya, dan yang sekarang sudah tiada,</span><span class="font1"> the translator only changed the form of the underlined word. Literally, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">gone</span><span class="font1"> means </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">pergi</span><span class="font1"> in Indonesian, but according to the context of this sentence, it can be referred to or has the same meaning with </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">died</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In this example, the translator uses the concept in the SL which is known (shared) in the TL, specifically in the way of using related words as equivalents. In some context, the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">gone</span><span class="font1"> is a synonym of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">died</span><span class="font1">. So it can also be translated into </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">tiada</span><span class="font1"> instead of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">mati, meninggal, or tewas</span><span class="font1"> because it can be more acceptable for the readers.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark26"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark27"></a>5.1.5 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Hyperbole</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">From the example above, the expression </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">burst my bubble</span><span class="font1"> belongs to hyperbole. It is formed by two words, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">burst</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">bubble.</span><span class="font1"> Burst as a verb means t</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">o break open or apart, especially because of pressure from inside; to make something break in this way (Hornby, 2005:201). Bubble</span><span class="font1"> in this expression means </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">full of feelings like happy, excitement, enthusiasm, high spirit or expectancy</span><span class="font1"> (Oxford, 1989:146). In this case, it is only used as an</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">effect of exaggeration to refer to the sadness. The implicit meaning may be Percy living in hope of something that Annabeth.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In translating the data above, from the expression </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Annabeth gave me a cautious look. She didn’t want to burst my bubble</span><span class="font1"> in English to </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Annabeth melemparkan tatapan hati-hati kepadaku. Dia tak ingin membuyarkan harapanku</span><span class="font1"> in Indonesian, the translator changed the form of the words to be more descriptively, so the reader can be easily to know the meaning of this expression. It can be seen in the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">burst</span><span class="font1"> in the SL which is translated into </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">membuyarkan</span><span class="font1"> in the TL.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark28"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark29"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">There are found five types of figurative languages which are similar to the Larson’s theory in analyzing the figurative languages in Riordan novel’s The Lightning Thief. Those are metonymy, synecdoche, idiom, euphemism, and hyperbole. In the data analysis, each types was explained by giving one example, to give a clear description of the type itself and also to avoid too many data and repeated analysis. In regard to equivalent, there are three concepts found when analyzing the data. Besides the lexical equivalent when concepts are shared and lexical equivalent when concepts are unknown in TL as introduced by Larson (1998:169), there is also concept of literal equivalent. In the data, it can be seen that the lexical equivalent when concepts are shared, specifically in terms of using related words as equivalent, is found more if compared with the others.</span></p>
