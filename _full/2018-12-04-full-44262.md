<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h1>
<p><span class="font6">Kudzu plantation (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Pueraria phaseoloides</span><span class="font6">) as </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">legume cover crop</span><span class="font6"> is one of alternatives in coastal sandy land conservation. The crops are known to associate with a root nodule-forming bacteria (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizobium</span><span class="font6"> sp.) which give some benefits to nutrient cycling i.e. : atmospheric N</span><span class="font3">2 </span><span class="font6">fixing and play role as soil conditioner; soil Nitrogen enrichment; nutrient cycling; and increasing other nutrients availability. A research to study the isolation dan bacterial inoculum multiplication from wild kudzu root nodules, compatible isolates screening and selected isolates multiplication, and examining the form, amounts and most proper inoculum application method was conducted in Greenhouse and Laboratory of Microbiology in Yogyakarta province.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">The research were held in four phases : (1) isolation, purification and characterization of isolates; (2) reinoculation dan compatibility testing of isolates to kudzu seeds; (3) inoculum multiplication; and (4) examination of the form, amounts and most proper inoculum application method. Physical and biochemical properties of the isolates were observed during the isolation phase. Infection and nodulation activity were observed during the reinoculation phase. Indirectly counting of the microbial numbers to obtain the cell numbers was conducted during the inoculum multiplication. While infection and nodulation activity and plant growth were observed during the inoculum testing phase.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Isolates purification on Yeast Mannitol Agar + </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">congo-red</span><span class="font6"> media gave 5 different isolates named RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">1, RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">2, RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">3, RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">4, RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">5. The RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">4 isolate had the highest compatibility to the kudzu seeds (number of effective nodules &gt;100 per plant), followed by RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">5 (medium compatibility, number of effective nodules 50–100 per plant), RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">1 and RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">3 (low compatibility, number of effective nodules &lt;&nbsp;10). Isolate RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">2 was incompatible to the kudzu.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Optimum cell numbers was reached in 48 hours incubation time. Application of broth/liquid inoculum of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizobium</span><span class="font6"> sp. has advantages over solid inoculum (in peat) as it gives the highest number of nodules, and the optimum dosage was 2 – 4 ml per plant. The highest infection-nodulation activity and plant growth were reached in 4 ml inoculum per plant (direct application) or 2 ml inoculum per plant (weekly applied in two weeks).</span></p>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Keywords</span><span class="font6">: isolation, characterization, inoculum formulation, </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizobium sp</span><span class="font6">., Kudzu</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h1>
<p><span class="font6">Coastal sandy land of the southern region of Yogyakarta Special Regency is one</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">of the most critical lands that meets several restriction for crop cultivation. Nevertheless, this area provides huge potential to managed</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">and used to improve the local/domestic revenue. The main obstacles to manage the critical area are : low soil fertility; low in soil mineral content; unfavorable microclimates; high wind and water erosion; few in propagules of soil fertilizing microbes; and low quality and quantity of managing human resources.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Cultivation of kudzu (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Pueraria phaseoloides</span><span class="font6">), familia Fabaceae,as a legume cover crop (LCC) is one of the alternatives to conserve the critical coastal land. The crops are known to associate to a root noduleforming bacteria (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizobium</span><span class="font6"> sp.) which give some benefits to nutrient cycling i.e. : atmospheric N</span><span class="font3">2 </span><span class="font6">fixing and play role as soil conditioner; soil Nitrogen enrichment; nutrient cycling; and increasing other nutrients availability. Nitrogen accumulation on topsoil is reach 8 to 14 times higher if compared to the biomass nitrogen content. The more the biomass production brings the higher Nitrogen mineralization in soil, and significantly increase the nutrient cycling, reduce the nutrien leaching, and increase the other mineral content (i.e. K content) in topsoil (Lehmann </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font6">., 2000).</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Kudzu is a beneficial herbs, not just an invaders,which give benefits to human (leaves for tea, bean for coffee and tempeh, flower for vegetable food ingredients),livestock forage, industrial fiber and textile. According to Ashton (2017), kudzu contains a variety of</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">phytochemicals, which are potent antioxidant compounds found naturally in plants, that help to prevent and treat human disease. Kudzu contains the phytochemicals quercetin, which has antihistamine and anti-inflammatory properties, and genistein which works as a free radical scavenger. The most important phytochemicals are, however, the isoflavone compounds -- daidzein, daidzin, tectorigenin and puerarin.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">The kudzu LCC plantation are also meet the ecosystem benefits such as protecting the topsoil from erosion, soil bulking agents, increasing soil organic matters, increasing the fertilizing microbe propagules, improve soil moisture, increasing mineral availability, and improving soil aeration. The wide and deep root system of kudzu take the important roles for prevent soil erosion, reduce water run-off to prevent landslides, and to increase land productivity. The kudzu plants are also known to be a well adapted plants to critical dry climate and have the ability to grow on extremely acidic or basic soil.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">The success of Kudzu cultivation in extremely critical coastal sandy land can be increased by inoculating root nodule forming bacteria. Application of indogenous inoculum from wild Kudzu significantly improved the infection activity and increased plant growth (Nike-Triwahyuningsih </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font6">., 2005). The research obviously gave a clear evidence that</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">the Kudzu root nodules indigenous can be used as a source of natural inoculum.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Availability of information on the inoculum form, amount and application method are important part of this research, in order to provide sources of inoculum. Therefore the research was aimed to : (1) isolate the rhizobial bacteria from the root nodules of wild kudzu to obtain the effective isolates; (2) characterize and determine the pure isolates and to reproduce the inoculum; (3) reinoculate the pure isolates to obtain the selected effective isolates to stimulate the root nodules formation, and (4) reproduce the selected inoculum and to test the form, amount and application method to obtain the effective ones.The targetes of the research</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">were : (1) isolation and characterization of the indigenous isolates; (2) inoculum reproduction; (3) formulation and reinoculation on the cultivated Kudzu.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></h1>
<p><span class="font6">Several materials were used in this research. Wild kudzu plants with intact root nodules used as source of indigenous inoculum. Yeast Mannitol Agar (YMA) was used to reproduce inoculum, and Yeast Mannitol Broth (YMB) to cultivation, isolation and enumeration of Rhizobium spp. Kudzu seeds used to test the effectivity of the selected pure inoculum in coastal sandy soil media. The overal procedures and phases of the research were as follow:</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Phase I : Harvesting of wild Kudzu in coastal sandy land to provide the root nodules and indigenous isolates. Isolation and purification of isolates on YMA to obtain the pure isolates that will be compatibility tested in phase III.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Phase II : &nbsp;1. Characterization and determination of all the pure isolates grew on the</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">YMA. The physical properties (colony form, edges, elevation, and inner structure) and physiological properties of bacterial isolates (gram, acid neutralization) were observed.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font6">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Reproduction of inoculum I. Every purified isolate than multiplied in YMBto obtain the optimum amount of inoculum (the amount of cells reachup to 10<sup>7</sup> – 10<sup>8</sup> CFU/ml).</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font6">Phase III : Reinoculation and compatibilty testing of isolates to Kudzu planted in coastal sandy soil media, to collect the selected pure isolates. The experiment was arranged in a single factorial completely randomized design to test the compatibilty of each isolate.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Phase IV : Reproduction of inoculum II in order to multiply the selected compatible inoculum. The multiplication process was conducted to obtain the sufficient amount of inoculum.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Phase V : Examination on concentration, form and application method of selected inoculum. The experiments were simultaneously conducted and arranged in factorial completely randomized design with three replications.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Experiment 1 : to examine the concentration and inoculum form, Experiment 2 : to examine the concentration and application method.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">All the influences of treatments were observed on :</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Infection and nodulation activity</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Kudzu plant growth</span></p>

Application of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">congo-red</span><span class="font6"> is as indicator of colony viability, where it means that the colony is viable. </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizobium</span><span class="font6"> sp. usually has colony diameter 1-5 mm, and all the purified colony have diameter 1-2 mm, that indicated the slow growing strains. The colony form (circular), elevation (plate-convex) and mucus (abundant) are as stated by Elkan (1987). But, the inner structure of the purified Rhizobium colony (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">finely granular</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">coarsely granular</span><span class="font6">) is differ from the one stated by Elkan (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">opaqe</span><span class="font6"> and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">transclusent</span><span class="font6">). This character indicated that the Rhizobium of Kudzu probably has different strain.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">The cell characterization results showed that all the purified isolates has a gram negative properties. According to Tortora (2001), </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizobium</span><span class="font6"> sp. is gram negative, and it indicates that the strain has the hydrophobic lipopolysaccharide cell wall, so it capable to resist to the unfavorable environment. This is why the strain have capability to survive in the environment with extremely dryness, low nutrient level and low acidity.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">According to the aerobocity properties, all the Rhizobium isolated are microaerobic which obviously different from anaerobic character as stated by Elkan (1987), and this probably came from different strain of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizobium</span><span class="font6"> sp. Isolates of Kudzu </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizobium </span><span class="font6">sp. need litlle oxygen as an energy source and seemly can survive in coastal sandy soil with extremely dryness and lack of nutrients.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">All the Rhizobium isolates showed the capability to reduce nitrite and nitrate based on the nitrification testing. This indicates that all the isolates capable in forming root nodules and sinthesizing proteins. And according to the fermentation test, all the isolates capable to reduce amylum, glucose and sucrose, which mean that all the isolates have the capability to reduce carbohydrate and producing energy needed for cell growth and development.</span></p>

<p><span class="font6">The effective root nodules are characterized by the red-pink color whenever it squeezed. The red color come from the leghemoglobin (legHb or symbiotic Hb), a nitrogen carrier or oxygen carrier and hemoprotein found in nitrogen fixing leguminous root nodules. It is produced by legumes in response to the roots being colonized by nitrogen-fixing rhizobacteria (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizobium</span><span class="font6">), as part of the symbiotic interaction between plant and bacterium.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Roots not colonized by </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Rhizobium</span><span class="font6"> do not synthesize leghemoglobin. Leghemoglobin has close chemical and structural similarities to hemoglobin, and, like hemoglobin, is red in colour. The holoprotein (protein + heme cofactor) is widely believed to be a product of both plant and the bacterium in which the apoprotein is produced by the plant and the heme (an iron atom bound in a porphyrin ring) is produced by the bacteroids (O’Brian et al., 1987). Some evidence, however, suggests that the heme moiety is also produced by the plant (Santana et al., 1998). The morphological photograph of the Kudzu root nodule are shown in Fig. 1 and Fig. 2. Nodules attached to the root by a long stalk (A) or short stalk (B), and the legHb producing bacteroids live in cortical cells of root nodules.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark21"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark22"></a>CONCLUSIONS</span></h1>
<p><span class="font6">Based on the observation, it can be concluded that:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font6">1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;From the isolation and purification of Rhizobium bacteria from wild Kudzu roots obtained 5 selected isolates, there are : RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">2 (incompatible isolate); RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">1 and RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">3 (low-compatibility isolates), &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">5 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(medium</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font6">compatibility isolate) and RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">4 (high compatibility isolate).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font6">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To multiply the number of inoculums, the isolates should be incubated on the Yeast Mannitol Broth (YMB) medium for 48 hours as the number of cells has reached the maximum.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font6">3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;From RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">4 reinoculation testing (high compatibility) it is known that broth inoculum has advantages over solid inoculum (in peat) because it gives the highest number of nodules.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font6">4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The number of equivalent inoculums 2 and 4 ml/plant gives the same effect on infection activity and bacterial nodulation of isolate RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">4 and RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">5, and gives the same effect to the growth of inoculated plants with RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">5.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font6">5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;For reinoculation of RP-</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Etp</span><span class="font6">5 to the Kudzu plants (medium compatibility), can be used as inoculum with concentration of 4 ml/plant directly applied at planting time or concentration 2 ml/plant weekly applied in 2 weeks.</span></p></li></ul>
