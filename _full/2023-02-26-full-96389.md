<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Abstract</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Wabi sabi is one of Philosophy in Japan. It’s about appreciation to the beauty of imperfection. Wabi sabi can be found in so many aspects in Japanese people’s life such as chanoyu, ikebana, haiku, or fairytale of Japanese children. This article aims to know how the oldman gets the spirit of life by wabi sabi to cheer his life. Hanasaka Jiisan is used in this research as a source of data. The result is the beauty of wabi sabi is appeared by the oldman’ house, the falling leaves, the dead trees, and the falling flowers. Furthermore, wabi sabi as the spirit of life can be found in the old man who lives with his wife, the old man who takes care of a dog, the old man who takes the ash of mortar home, and the old man who throws ash all over the dead trees and it makes the lord of local castle so happy.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Keywords</span><span class="font2">: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Hanasaka Jiisan; simplicity of life; spirit of life; wabi sabi</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Abstrak</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Wabi sabi merupakan salah satu filosofi Jepang yang mengapresiakan keindahan dalam ketidaksempurnaan. Wabi sabi dapat dijumpai di berbagai aspek hidup orang Jepang seperti chanoyu, ikebana, haiku, atau dongeng anak-anak Jepang. Artikel ini bertujuan untuk mengetahui bagaimana seorang kakek mendapatkan semangat hidup melalui wabi sabi. Dongeng Hanasaka Jiisan merupakan sumber data artikel ini. Hasil yang diperoleh adalah keindahan wabi sabi tampak dari wajah rumah Kakek, daun-daun yang berguguran, pohon-pohon yang mati, dan bunga sakura yang berjatuhan. Selanjutnya, wabi sabi sebagai semangat hidup dapat diketahui dari sang Kakek yang hidup bersama istrinya, sang kakek yang merawat seekora anjing, sang kakek yang membawa pulang abu lesung, dan sang kakek yang melempar abu ke pepohonan yang telah mati dan membuat penguasa kastil bahagia.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Kata kunci</span><span class="font2">: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Hanasaka Jiisan; kesederhanaan hidup; semangat hidup; wabi sabi</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Introduction</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Wabi sabi</span><span class="font3"> （written as </span><span class="font1">侘寂 </span><span class="font3">or </span><span class="font1">侘び寂び </span><span class="font3">） consist of two separate words reflected aesthetic value, based on the perspective of literature, culture and religion. Finding beauty in simplicity, as well as a spiritual depth and tranquility in distancing oneself from the physical world, is what wabi is all about. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Sabi</span><span class="font3"> is more concerned with the passage of time,</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">with the way that all things grow and decay and how ageing alters the visual nature of those things (Kempton, 2019: 30).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The feeling of remote isolation that comes with living in nature and the paradoxical beauty of imperfection (like a broken cup fixed with gold through kintsugi) is the original meaning of wabi. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Sabi</span><span class="font3">, depending on the context, can mean “withered,” “lean,” or “cooled” but more often refers to the beauty of aging – like the changing hue of wood, the comeliness of rust, the delicate droop and drying of roses in the sun (Longhurst, 2018: 61; Adek et al., 2020; Yurt &amp;&nbsp;Basarir, 2020).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">In historical meaning, </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi</span><span class="font3"> means the solitude of living in nature; and languishing. But in current meaning, It means simple rusticity; subdued beauty; freshness; tranquility; peculiarities or blemishes that are delightful. Sabi in historical meaning are wizened; wilted; rusted, and in current meaning are beauty and calm in what is mature or aged; pleasure felt when appreciating the imperfect (Suzuki, 2020: 33).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Wabi sabi</span><span class="font3"> is a concept in Japanese society that portrays the floating, changing, and imperfect beauty of nature all around us. Instead of looking for perfect beauty, we should look for it in things that are flawed, incomplete. This is why Japanese people value imperfection so much, for example, falling in love with an irregular or cracked teacup. only imperfect, incomplete, and ephemeral things can truly be beautiful because only those things truly resemble nature (Garcia and Miralles, 2016: 191). The key point is that there are some things over which we have no control, such as the passage of time and the ephemeral nature of the world around us. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Wabi sabi</span><span class="font3"> teaches us to appreciate the beauty of imperfection as an opportunity to grow (Garcia and Miralles, 2016: 193).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Wabi-Sabi</span><span class="font3"> is often associated with a sense of peace, together with the natural development of life. Maintaining that life and the understanding that things are impermanent, enables us to love the beauty and sadness of it (Mori, 2019: 14).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Some principles to understand and feel </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi sabi</span><span class="font3"> as a way of life: 1) proceeding to use old objects but caring for them as if they were old friends, 2) learning to say 'no' to social or commercial opportunities that may not be what they appear to be, 3) avoiding getting frustrated by life’s imperfection, 4) taking things as they are, 5) Living in tune with nature, accommodating twist in fate and making the best out of change in a life which you are in harmony with yourself and with all that surrounds you, including your</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">friends and family, 6) practicing some form of art that allows you to adhere to the wabi sabi aesthetic principles (Suzuki, 2020: 24-25).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">So many Japanese fairytales describe about </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi sabi</span><span class="font3"> in their story. One of is </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Hanasaka Ojiisan</span><span class="font3">. It tells about the old man and his wife who lovingly cherish the lost dog. But the dog died because it was killed the greedy old man.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Wabi sabi</span><span class="font3"> teaches us to accept who we are and what we have now, no more, no less, and to love ourselves exactly as we are</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">.</span><span class="font3"> The </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi-sabi</span><span class="font3"> philosophy inspires people to accept simplicity wholeheartedly and appreciate what we already have. (Kempton, 2018:38). Through </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Hanasaka Jiisan</span><span class="font3">, we know the beauty of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi sabi</span><span class="font3"> that can grow the spirit of someone’s life.</span></p>

<p><span class="font3">A fairy tale entitled Hanasaka Jiisan is a simple and light children's fairy tale. Nevertheless, it is found out that there is a concept of the beauty of wabi sabi as the spirit of Grandpa's life, as the main character in the story.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark13"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark14"></a>4.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Beauty of </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Wabi Sabi</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">The old house occupied by a pair of kind-hearted grandparents is a picture of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi. Wabi </span><span class="font3">is a picture of the beauty of simplicity. Suzuki (2020:33) defines </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi</span><span class="font3"> with a more modern meaning as rustic simplicity. In the story of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Hanasaka jiisan</span><span class="font3">, it is told that an old man lives with his wife.</span></p>
<p><span class="font0">昔むかしあるところに、正直者のおじいさんとおばあさんがくらしていました。</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Mukashi mukashi aru tokoroni, shoujikisha no ojiisan to obaasan ga kurashite imashita.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Once upon a time, there lived an honest old man and his wife.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The word </span><span class="font1">くらしていました</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">kurashite imasu</span><span class="font3"> (to live), shows that the grandparents live together in one place. In the story it is not explained in detail where the grandparents live, but the portrayal of a kind-hearted grandfather figure (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">shoujikisha no ojiisan</span><span class="font3">) can describe that grandfather is living peacefully with his wife somewhere in a certain place. That statement is supported by the story of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Hanasaka Jiisan</span><span class="font3"> in the following video</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Picture 1</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/96389-1.jpg" alt="" style="width:202pt;height:111pt;">
<p><span class="font2">The beauty of Rustic Simplicity</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The picture above shows the grandpa's house standing alone in a quiet village surrounded by vegetable gardens and trees. Next to the house, there is only a Buddha statue adding to the impression of simplicity of natural beauty. This is in accordance with the wabi portrayal of the beauty coming out from simplicity.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Furthermore, the beauty of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">sabi</span><span class="font3"> can be seen from the falling leaves in the video portraying the grandfather's sadness due to the death of his dog because of being killed by his neighbor.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">怒った意地悪じいさんは犬を殺してしまいました。おじいさんとおばあさんは それを知ると大変悲しみにくれ、庭の隅に墓を作り</span><span class="font3">…</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Okotta ijiwaru Jiisan wa inu wo sashite shimaimashita. Ojiisan to obaasan wa sore wo shiruto taihen kanashimi ni kure, niwa no sumi ni haka wo tsukuri…</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">'That angry cunning grandpa killed the dog. Knowing this, the kind-hearted grandparents felt great sadness, they made a grave for the dog at the end of the yard…'</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Picture 2</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/96389-2.jpg" alt="" style="width:148pt;height:67pt;">
<p><span class="font2">the beauty of fallen leaves</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The shadow of the black leaves falling in the video describes the sabi according to Kempton (2019: 30), ‘passage of time, with the way that all things grow and decay and how ageing alters the visual nature of those things’. The togetherness of grandfather and dog who had lived together and had passed brings memories that live and grow in grandfather's mind.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The beauty of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi sabi</span><span class="font3"> is then seen when grandfather passes by a withered tree carrying mortar ashes.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">…</span><span class="font1">ふいに風が吹き、灰が枯れ木にかかったかと思うと</span><span class="font3">…</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">…Fui ni kaze wo fuki, hae ga kareki ni kakattaka to omouto...</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">‘… The wind blows, the ashes from the mortar stick to the withered tree…’</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The withered tree (kareki) is a description of Longhurst's sabi, (2018: 61) that is “withered” refering to the beauty of aging. Withered tree describes a tree that has gone through a phase of growing, blooming, until finally aging and withering.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Picture 3</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/96389-3.jpg" alt="" style="width:179pt;height:65pt;">
<p><span class="font2">the Beauty of Withered Tree</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The beauty of the </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">sabi</span><span class="font3"> from Longhurst (2018: 61) is also seen when the cherry blossoms fall beautifully.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">…</span><span class="font1">おじいさんは村の枯れ木に灰をかけ、次々と花を咲かせていきました。</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">… Ojiisan wa mura no kareki ni hai wo kake, tsugi tsugi to hana wo sakasete ikimashita.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">…The Grandfather took the ashes and scattered them on the withered trees in the village, and one by one the Cherry blossoms bloomed on the trees.’</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Picture 4</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/96389-4.jpg" alt="" style="width:181pt;height:93pt;">
<p><span class="font2">The beauty of Cherry blossoms</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Blooming flowers (hana wo sakasete ikimashita) indicate sabi, in which time goes by or the existence of a life process that has passed. In this case the process refers to the process of the cherry blossoms blooming until the flowers fall to the ground beautifully, as shown in Figure 4.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark15"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark16"></a>4.2</span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Wabi-sabi</span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"> as the Spirit of Life</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">Some of the principles of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi sabi</span><span class="font3"> according to Suzuki (2020: 24-25) as seen from the </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Hanasaka Jiisan</span><span class="font3"> story include the grandfather's principles of living life in the following ways.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">1) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Living in tune with nature</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">The description of where grandfather lives in Figure 1 clearly shows that grandfather lives in a simple village. This simplicity can also be seen from Grandpa's quiet house and the Buddha statue beside Grandpa's house.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">2) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Averting from becoming frustrated by life's defects</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">There is no sadness for grandparents who do not have a child. When they see a confused dog, without thinking, Grandpa takes the initiative to treat the dog like his own child, and loves it.</span></p>
<p><span class="font0">子どもがいなかった二人は、「この子をわしらの子どもと思って、大事に育てよう。 」と言い、大変かわいがりました。</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Kodomo ga inakatta futari wa, ‘kono ko wo washira no kodomo to omotte, daijini sodateyou’ ti ii, taihen kawaigarimashita.’</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">'The two elderly people who did not have children said affectionately, &quot;Let's treat this dog like our own child.&quot; '</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">Picture 5</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/96389-5.jpg" alt="" style="width:169pt;height:95pt;">
<p><span class="font2">The Old man and his dog</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">3) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Continuing to use old object but looking after them like old friends</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">The behavior of the grandfather who takes the mortal ashes shows that he treats the ashes like an old friend. The ashes are mortals made from trees that grow over his dog's grave. Eventhough it is no longer mortal, with a sad feeling, Grandpa still puts the mortal ashes in a container and brings them home.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">…</span><span class="font0">臼はすっかり灰になって いました。悲しみながらもその灰を持ち帰る</span><span class="font3">…</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">… usu wa sukkari hai ni natte imashita. Kanashimi nagara mo sono hai wo mochi kaeru…</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">‘…the mortar has turned to ashes. The old man brought the ashes of the mortar home with a sad feeling…'.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Picture 6</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/96389-6.jpg" alt="" style="width:179pt;height:80pt;">
<p><span class="font2">The Old Man Saves the Mortar Ash</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">4) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Taking things as what they are, accommodating fate's twists, and making the best of change in a life that is harmonious with everything around it</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">The sad grandfather turns happy when he learns that the mortar ashes he brings can turn a wilted tree into a flowering tree. He also sprinkles the ashes all over the withered trees in the village, so that the landlord who sees it feels the extraordinary beauty of the village and grants grandpa gifts.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">…</span><span class="font0">ふいに風が吹き、灰が枯れ木にかかったかと思うと、不思議なことにぱっと花が咲 きました。 「これは面白い。」おじいさんは村の枯れ木に灰をかけ、次々と花を咲か せていきました。</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">…Fui ni kaze wo fuki, hae ga kareki ni kakattaka to omouto, fushigi na kotoni patto hana ga sakimashita. ‘kore wa omoshiroi’ Ojiisan wa mura no kareki ni hai wo kake, tsugi tsugi to hana wo sakasete ikimashita.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">‘… The wind blew, the ashes of the mortar clung to the dead tree, and miraculously flowers grew and bloomed on the tree. &quot;This is interesting&quot;. The Grandpa took the ashes and scattered them on the dead trees in the village, and one by one the flowers bloomed on those trees.'</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The Grandpa’s utterance &nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="font1">「これは面白い」</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">kore wa omoshiroi</span><span class="font3"> ‘this is</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">interesting’, gives rise enthusiasm in grandpa's mind to turn his sadness into an interesting and meaningful situation, namely to make the village beautiful with many blooming cherry blossoms.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark17"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark18"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Hanasaka Jiisan</span><span class="font3"> fairy tale portrays the beauty of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi sabi</span><span class="font3">, which is a picture of the peaceful village where grandpa lives, the leaves falling when the dog dies, the trees wither, and the cherry blossoms fall. The </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">wabi sabi</span><span class="font3"> principle carried out by grandfather results in enthusiasm for grandfather in living the twists and turns of his life.</span></p>
