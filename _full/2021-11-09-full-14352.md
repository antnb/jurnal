<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Kalimat adalah aspek penting dalam berbahasa, karena kita berbahasa dalam bentuk kalimat dan berkomunikasi satu sama lain menggunakan kalimat. Dalam kalimat terdapat sekelompok kata yang digabungkan untuk membentuk sebuah makna yang dapat mengekspresikan pernyataan, permintaan, perintah, seruan, dan lain lain. Dalam membuat sebuah kalimat yang baik, aspek yg paling penting untuk diperhatikan adalah tatabahasa, tatabahasa mengatur kata untuk berubah bentuk dan bergabung satu sama lain untuk membentuk satu kalimat utuh yang baik. Dalam sebuah kalimat terdapat kata kerja, kata benda, kata sifat, adverbia, dan lain lain. Dari sekian banyak aspek kata, adverbia adalah salah satu elemen untuk didalami dan dipahami, karena adverbia dapat secara langsung maupun tidak langsung merubah ekspresi dan struktur kalimat berbahasa inggris dari segi tata bahasa. Adapun dari sekian banyak adverbia yang ada, dalam penelitian ini hanya terfokus pada adverbia tingkatan yang mana menggunakan novel dari Joseph Conrad yg berjudul The Rescue sebagai sumber data, dalam tujuan untuk menganalisa bentuk, fungsi dan posisi dari adverbia tingkatan yg dapat muncul dalam sebuah kalimat.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci: adverbia tingkatan, bentuk, posisi, fungsi.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">There were many aspects to be notified in composing one good sentence, such as: tenses, verb, noun, adjective, adverb, etc. From those aspects, adverb is one of the elements to be comprehend, since adverb can affect the expression and structure of English sentence grammaticaly. Adverbs have various types. Morphologically, adverb can be classified into three parts; they are: simple adverb, complex adverb, and compound adverb. Eckersley (1965 : 252) stated that “adverb can be divided into eight, such as: adverb manner, adverb of place,</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">adverb of time, adverb of frequency, adverb of degree, adverb of affirmation, relative adverb, and interrogative adverb”. And this study focuses only on adverb of degree.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Adverb of degree can be devided into two types: Derivational form and Non-Derivational form, and have several function such as: modifier of adjective, adverb itself, noun phrase, determiner, prepositional phrase modifier, and etc. The position is also important, since the adverb of degree can be found in various positions; the position of adverb in sentence will affect the function of adverb itself. Therefore, it is important to analyze the form, function, and position of the adverb of degree, so we can use it properly and corectly in all forms of communication. Those are the main reason why adverb of degree is chosen to be topic of this writing.</span></p>

<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Analysis of Adverb of Degrees in Joseph Conrad’s “</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">The Rescue</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">” Novel</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<h1><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>5.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Forms of Adverb of Degree</span></h1></li></ul></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Adverb of degree can also be devided into two types: Derivational form and Non-Derivational form:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>5.1.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Non-Derivational Forms</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;She seemed </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">quite</span><span class="font1"> surprised by that view. (Conrad, 1920 : 129)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;It can’t be </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">very</span><span class="font1"> late yet, “he said”. (Conrad, 1920 : 262)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">c. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;They would have trusted me quick </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">enough</span><span class="font1">. (Conrad, 1920 : 215)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In sentences (a),(b) and (c) the non-derivational adverbs of degree were formed from pure single words, no suffixes or other distinguishable markings differentiate them from other parts of the sentence.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>5.1.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Derivational Forms</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;They were mysterious, but she felt certain they were </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">absolutely</span><span class="font1"> true. (Conrad, 1920 : 131)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Its approaches are </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">extremely</span><span class="font1"> difficult for a stranger. (Conrad, 1920 : 61)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In sentences (a),(b) above are adverb of degree in derivational forms, using suffixation process by adding –</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">ly</span><span class="font1"> to the adjectives so they all belong to derivational forms.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Function of Adverb of Degree</span><br><br><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark24"></a>5.2.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adverb of Degree as Adjective Modifier</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;They were mysterious, but she felt certain they were </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">absolutely true</span><span class="font1">. (Conrad, 1920 : 131)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;.., </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">quite natural</span><span class="font1"> in a girl so young and elevated to such a high</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">position. (Conrad, 1920 : 233)</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In the sentence above there are adverbs of degree functioning as adjective modifiers and both pre-modifies adjectives.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark25"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark26"></a>5.2.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adverb of Degree as Adverb Modifier</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;.., clad in spotless white suit, came </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">just then</span><span class="font1" style="text-decoration:underline;"> </span><span class="font1">from forward where he had been busy with the anchors. (Conrad, 1920 : 54)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;.., and had noted the lay of the land and the position of the dangers </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">so carefully </span><span class="font1">that, though at... (Conrad, 1920 : 74)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In sentence (a) the non-derivational adverb of degree </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">just</span><span class="font1"> pre-modifies adverb of time </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then.</span><span class="font1"> And (b) adverb of degree </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1"> pre-modifies adverb of manner </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">carefully</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark27"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark28"></a>5.2.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adverb of Degree as Prepositional Phrase Modifier</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;But even </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">far into</span><span class="font1" style="text-decoration:underline;"> the present century</span><span class="font1"> they have had successors. (Conrad,1920 : 15)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;You stranding </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">just on that spot of the whole coast</span><span class="font1"> was my bad luck. (Conrad, 1920 : 42)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In sentence (a) the adverb of degree </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">far</span><span class="font1"> pre-modifies prepositional phrase </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">into the present century.</span><span class="font1"> And in (b) the adverb of degree </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">just</span><span class="font1"> pre-modifies prepositional phrase </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">on that spot of the whole coast.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark29"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark30"></a>5.2.4 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adverb of Degree as Determiner Modifier</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">a....was profoundly troubled about those very things though she, too, felt desperate enough to welcome </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">almost any</span><span class="font1"> solution. (Conrad, 1920 : 310)</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">b. D’Alcacer was a man of </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">nearly forty</span><span class="font1">, lean and sallow, with hollow eyes and a dropping brown moustache. (Conrad, 1920 : 110)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In sentence (a) the adverb of degree </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">almost</span><span class="font1"> pre-modifies the determiner </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">any,</span><span class="font1"> and in sentence (b) the adverb of degree </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">nearly</span><span class="font1"> pre-modifies cardinal numeral </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">forty,</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark31"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark32"></a>5.2.5 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adverb of Degree as Noun Phrase Modifier</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The silence in the cabin lasted </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">only a few seconds</span><span class="font1">. (Conrad, 1920 : 42)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;It stayed there for </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;text-decoration:underline;">quite a long time</span><span class="font1">. (Conrad, 1920 : 347)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In sentence, (a) the adverb of degree </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">only</span><span class="font1"> pre-modifies the noun phrase </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">a few seconds, </span><span class="font1">in (b) the adverb of degree </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">quite</span><span class="font1"> pre-modifies the noun phrase </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">a long time.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark33"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark34"></a>5.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Position of Adverb of Degree</span><br><br><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark35"></a>5.3.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Initial Position</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Nearly</span><span class="font1"> right, I think, sir. (Conrad, 1920 : 24)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Too</span><span class="font1"> hard for our common fearful heart. (Conrad : 374)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In example, (a) and (b) above, the adverb of degree occurs in the initial position, at the very beginning of the sentence, initiates the other part of the clause.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark36"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark37"></a>5.3.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Medial Position</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;He had come up </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">only</span><span class="font1"> for a moment, (Conrad, 1920 : 20)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The coast was </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">completely</span><span class="font1"> deserted to all appearance. (Conrad,1920:37)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In sentence, (a) above, the adverb of degree appear in the middle of the sentence after the verb </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">come</span><span class="font1">. And in (b) the adverb of degree appears in the middle right before the main verb </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">deserted</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark38"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark39"></a>5.3.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Final Position</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Wouldn’t he treat a stranger </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">fairly</span><span class="font1">? (Conrad, 1920 : 46)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Mrs Travers nodded at him </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">slightly</span><span class="font1">. (Conrad, 1920 : 269)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In example (a) and (b) above, the adverb of degree appears in the final position, after the main verb, and appears at the very end of the sentence.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark40"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark41"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Based on the analysis above, there are several significant adverbs of degree in Joseph Conrad “The Rescue” novel that can be drawn as a conclusion .</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">There are two forms of adverb of degree occuring in the novel, non-derivational form which is formed from pure word, and drivational form, formed mostly by simply adding suffix –ly to the adjective. Based on the function, in Joseph Conrad’s novel, the adverb of degree is commonly used to modify other part of speech such as noun, prepositional phrase, adjective, determiner, cardinal numeral, and other adverbs. In terms of position, the adverb of degree can be found in all positions of the sentences: initial position, medial position, and final position, and in each position of adverb have their own functions.</span></p>
