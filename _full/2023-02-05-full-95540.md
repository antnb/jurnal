<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>Abstract</span></h2>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font2"> sp. is a zoonotic intestinal protozoa in orangutans which is transmitted through contaminated water (waterborne disease). This study aims to determine </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font2"> sp. which infects the sumatran orangutan (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo abelii</span><span class="font2">) and the bornean orangutan (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo pygmaeus</span><span class="font2">) in North Sumatera Wildlife Park. The samples used were 3 orangutan fecal samples from different locations, two bornean orangutans in Medan Wildlife Park and one sumatran orangutan in Pematang Siantar Animal Park. Samples were examined using the floatation method and the modified Ziehl-Neelsen method. The results showed that from 3 samples of orangutan feces (sumatran and bornean orangutan), 2 positive tails from bornean orangutans were found containing </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font2"> oocysts, namely the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">C. parvum</span><span class="font2"> species. In this study, it can be concluded that the orangutans in Medan Wildlife Park, the bornean orangutan were infected with cryptosporidiosis.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Keywords: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font2"> sp.; bornean orangutan; </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo abelii</span><span class="font2">; </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo pygmaeus;</span><span class="font2"> sumatran orangutan</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>Abstrak</span></h2>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font2"> sp. adalah protozoa pencernaan yang bersifat zoonosis pada orangutan yang ditularkan melalui air yang terkontaminasi (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">waterborne disease</span><span class="font2">). Penelitian ini bertujuan untuk mengetahui </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font2"> sp. yang menginfeksi orangutan sumatera (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo abelii</span><span class="font2">) dan orangutan kalimantan (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo pygmaeus</span><span class="font2">) di Taman Satwa Sumatera Utara. Sampel yang digunakan sebanyak 3 sampel feses orangutan dari lokasi yang berbeda, yaitu 2 ekor orangutan kalimantan di Taman Margasatwa Medan dan 1 ekor orangutan sumatera di Taman Hewan Pematang Siantar. Sampel diperiksa dengan menggunakan metode floatasi dan metode </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">modified</span><span class="font2"> Ziehl-Neelsen. Hasil penelitian menunjukkan dari 3 sampel feses orangutan didapatkan 2 ekor positif mengandung oosista </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font2">, yaitu spesies </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">C. parvum</span><span class="font2">. Pada penelitian ini dapat disimpulkan bahwa orangutan yang ada di Taman Margasatwa Medan, yaitu orangutan kalimantan terinfeksi kriptosporidiosis.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Kata kunci: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font2"> sp.; orangutan Kalimantan; orangutan Sumatera; </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo abelii</span><span class="font2">; </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo pygmaeus</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">Indonesia is one of the countries with high primate diversity. One of the primates that are threatened with extinction is the orangutan. There are three extant orangutan species, two of which are on the island of Sumatera (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo abelii</span><span class="font3"> and </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo tapanuliensis</span><span class="font3">) and one on the island of Kalimantan (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo pygmaeus</span><span class="font3">) (Condro </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2021). Orangutans are classified by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) as critically endangered or critically endangered because they have experienced rapid population decline (Chua </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2020).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">One of the efforts of ex-situ conservation is to carry out captive breeding with the aim of education and promotion of conservatives through the management of animal parks or zoos. In Medan, there is Medan Wildlife Park which has long been known to have tourism potential, has attractions such as typical animals from North Sumatera (Tamba and Prana, 2020). In addition, in Siantar, there is also a breeding place, namely Pematang Siantar Animal Park (THPS). In 2018, there were 2 orangutans (1 male and 1 female) in Pematang Siantar Animal Park. However, in 2021 there will be only one male orangutan because another orangutan has died. The habitat environment between natural and enclosures in zoos is very different. The parameters for the success of ex-situ conservation are seen from the number of orangutans that are able to reproduce and survive with the treatment and treatment given at the time of conservation (Tarigan </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2021).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Primate populations in animal parks act as potential reservoirs for pathogens that infect humans, such as viruses, bacteria and parasites that can cause zoonoses. Several studies have reported that the enteric protozoan pathogen, namely </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Giardia intestinalis</span><span class="font3">, </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Entamoeba histolytica </span><span class="font3">and </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. can cause gastrointestinal enteritis in primates (gibbons, orangutans, marmosets, gorillas</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">and chimpanzees) (Menu </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2021). </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. is a zoonotic protozoan. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. can cause diarrhea that is acute to chronic diarrhea (Maryanti, 2017). Transmission of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporodium</span><span class="font3"> sp. can occur in animals and humans when consuming contaminated water (waterborne disease) (Xiao and Feng, 2008).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Until now there has been no report regarding the identification of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. in sumatran and bornean orangutans, so it is necessary to identify </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. which infests sumatran and bornean orangutans in North Sumatra Wildlife Park as an effort to improve health and estimate potential zoonotic risks as well as to maintain the life of protected orangutans.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">RESEARCH METHODS</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Place and Time of Research</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">This research was conducted in January 2022 in three locations, the Medan Wildlife Park, Pematang Siantar Animal Park, North Sumatra and Parasitology Laboratory of the Faculty of Veterinary Medicine, Syiah Kuala University, Banda Aceh.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>Research Tools and Materials</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">The tools used in this study were ice boxes, smartphone cameras, refrigerators, masks, gloves, sterile bottles/plastics, digital scales, mortar, stirring rods, spatulas, centrifuges, centrifuge tubes, centrifuge tube racks, tea filters, dropper drops glasses, measuring instrument, object glass, cover glass, lens tissue, CX-3 microscope, label paper, markers, and pen.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The materials used in this study were 3 fecal samples, consisting of 2 samples of the bornean orangutans feces taken at Medan Wildlife Park and 1 sumatran orangutan feces samples from Pematang Siantar Animal Park, North Sumatra, methylene blue 0,1%, NaCl saturated, aquades, acid alcohol 3%, formalin 10%, methanol, carbol fuchsin 1% , immersion oil, and water.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>Research Sample</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">The samples used in this study were fresh fecal samples taken from 3 orangutans, including 2 bornean orangutans, male and female in Medan Wildlife Park and 1 male sumatran orangutan at Pematang Siantar Animal Park, North Sumatra.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>Research Procedure Sampling method.</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">Stool samples of sumatran orangutan and bornean orangutans were taken immediately after the orangutans defecated using a spatula by taking the middle or inner part of the pile of feces as much as ± 3 g and put into bottles/plastic samples filled with 10% formalin solution in a ratio of 1:1, then homogenized and stored in an ice box. Furthermore, the samples in the ice box were brought aseptically to Parasitology Laboratory of the Faculty of Veterinary Medicine, Syiah Kuala University for examination.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>Sample Inspection Method Floatation method.</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">The principle of the floatation method is that the specific gravity of the protozoa is lighter than the specific gravity of the solution used, so that the protozoa will float on the surface. Fecal samples taken as much as ± 2 g were put into a mortar, added with distilled water and stirred until homogeneous. The preparation was filtered, then put into a centrifuge tube up to tube and centrifuged at 1500 rpm for 5 minutes. The supernatant was discarded, then saturated NaCl was added and stirred until homogeneous. Spin again at 1500 rpm for 5 minutes. The preparation was placed on a tube rack perpendicularly and dripped with saturated NaCl using a pipette until the surface of the liquid in the tube became convex and left for 3 minutes. Paste the object glass on the convex surface carefully and then flip it quickly. The surface of the object glass was covered with a cover glass and examined for gastrointestinal protozoa under a microscope.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>Ziehl-neelsen modified method.</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">Fecal samples taken ± 1 g were put into the mortar, added with distilled water and crushed until homogeneous, then filtered through a tea filter and dripped 2-3 drops on the object glass. Then, the preparation is made like a thin blood smear and waits for it to dry. After drying, the preparation was fixed in methanol for 3 minutes, and was flooded with carbol fuchsin for 15-20 minutes, then washed with running water. The preparation is rinsed again with acid 3% alcohol and allowed to stand for 15-20 seconds, then washed with running water and waited for it to dry. Then dripped with methylene blue into the preparation for 1 minute and observed under a microscope. The surface of the object glass was closed using a cover glass and examined for </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. under a microscope with a magnification of 400x or 1000x.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">Species Identification</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Protozoa identification is done by looking &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;at &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;the &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;morphological</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">characteristics as well as the size and shape of the protozoa. Furthermore, to determine the species of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium </span><span class="font3">sp. measurements were made by connecting a sigma camera to the microscope and then displayed on the monitor screen and measurements were made using the Topup view application .</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">Data Analysis</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The research data obtained were tabulated for further descriptive analysis.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>RESULTS AND DISCUSSION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">Examination of the fecal from sumatran orangutan and bornean orangutans using the floatation method and the modified Ziehl-Neelsen method to detect the oocysts of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. which is presented in Table 1.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Based on Table 1, it can be seen that from 3 samples of orangutans, 2 orangutans from Kalimantan (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo pygmaeus</span><span class="font3">) was positive </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium parvum</span><span class="font3"> using the floatation method and the modified Ziehl-Neelsen method.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Infection </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">C. parvum</span><span class="font3"> in bornean orangutans has been reported by Mynářová </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">. (2016), there were 2 orangutans positive </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">C. parvum</span><span class="font3"> from 298 samples. Species identification in this study was carried out by observing the characteristics, morphology, and shape of the protozoan then measurements were made to determine the species of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium </span><span class="font3">sp.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Based on Figure 1, the oocyst of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. observed with Ziehl-Neelsen method are round, pink in color and have a size of 2.21 × 2.25 μm. In Figure 2, the </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. oocyst observed by the floatation method are round with a size of 2.43 × 2.65 μm. According to Mufa </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">. (2020), oocyst </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> spp. has a round, oval shape and 2-6 μm in diameter, while the size of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium parvum</span><span class="font3"> oocysts is 2-3.2 μm in diameter and spherical (Mehlhorn, 2012; Taylor </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2016). Based on this, the species </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. observed was </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">C. parvum</span><span class="font3">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium parvum</span><span class="font3"> is a species that has been found in orangutans (Cibot </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2021) and the most pathogenic species in humans with more than 90% of cryptosporidiosis cases in humans worldwide and is responsible for almost all causes of cryptosporidiosis (Khan </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2018; Zhao </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2019).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> is a zoonotic protozoan commonly found in primates. As a result, there is a potential for transmission of this pathogen to humans and vice versa (Zhao </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2019). Close and frequent contact between orangutans and cage staff can increase the risk of transmitting parasitic diseases from orangutans to cage staff or vice versa. Primates with high levels of human contact are more often infected with </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3">, for example semi-wild and captive animals (Mynářová </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2016).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Infection </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium parvum</span><span class="font3"> in the bornean orangutan (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo pygmaeus</span><span class="font3">)</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">at Medan Wildlife Park was caused by several factors, like water supply, environment, and cage maintenance management. A drinking holder with an open container made of cement is placed in the cage, allowing contamination of the drinking water which is a factor causing cryptosporidiosis (waterborne disease). This is in accordance with the statement of Wahdini </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">. (2021), who stated that poor water quality contributed to the high prevalence of digestive protozoan infections. Meanwhile, Osman </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">. (2016) reported that consumption of untreated water tripled the risk of protozoal infection compared to water treated with chlorite. However, chlorination of drinking water could not kill oocysts </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. (Maryanti, 2017). An effective disinfectant to kill </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. are ammonia, formol saline 10%, hydrogen peroxide 3%, and commercial bleach 50%. In addition, freezing below freezing point and heating at a temperature &gt;65 °C for 30 minutes can also kill it (Wijayanti, 2017).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The condition of the orangutan cage in the Medan Wildlife Park is cleaned once a day in the morning. The cage staff did not wear personal protective equipment such as masks and gloves to prevent contamination of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. oocysts. According to Rachmawati (2019), the habit of washing hands, cutting nails, using personal protective equipment such as masks and gloves, and cleaning the cage regularly are steps to prevent oocyst contamination.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Apart from poor maintenance management, cryptosporidiosis can also be infected through contamination of food given to orangutans. At Medan Wildlife Park, orangutans are given fresh food in the form of fruits and vegetables, such as papaya, banana, corn, kale, and mustard greens once a day. Orangutans are also given additional feed such as boiled eggs, honey and milk when stocks become available. The feed is given directly to the orangutans without being cleaned first.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Contamination of the feed given to orangutans may occur because the fruits and vegetables given are grown in areas that have been contaminated with the oocyst </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. This is in accordance with the statement of Zhao </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">. (2019) that cryptosporidiosis can be transmitted through water and food, such as fruits, vegetables, and milk contaminated with the oocyst </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">According to information received, the bornean orangutan in Medan Wildlife Park came from the Ragunan Zoo in 2019 and was never given antiprotozoal drugs and antibiotics. The orangutans were only given vitamins. Athaillah </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">. (2021) stated that the high prevalence of protozoal infestations could occur because primates were never given antiprotozoal drugs.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. is one of the opportunistic intestinal protozoa which is generally asymptomatic or causes mild symptoms and is self-limited in immunocompetent individuals (Wijayanti, 2017). This is similar to this study where the bornean orangutan infected with cryptosporidiosis showed no symptoms (asymptomatic) and had a soft stool consistency, like solid but not hard.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The clinical symptoms and severity of cryptosporidiosis can vary widely, largely depending on the patient's immune status. Patients with immune system disorders, especially patients with Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)/ Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS) have a high mortality rate (Zhao </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2019). The infection can become chronic and lifethreatening with frequent defecation leading to dehydration. In severe cases, the oocyst of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. can infected the digestive tract (epithelial cells of the small intestine villi), it can also reach the respiratory tract, liver, bladder, and pancreas (Fayer, 2004). Antiretroviral therapy can be given to increase the immune system and reduce symptoms of cryptosporidiosis (Mohammed </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2017).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Sumatran orangutan (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo abelii</span><span class="font3">) in Pematang Siantar Animal Park was not found oocysts </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. with the floatation method and the modified Ziehl-Neelsen method. This is probably due to good management of cage maintenance, provision of drinking water and feed at THPS. Orangutan cages are cleaned twice a day, in the morning and evening. Orangutans are given regular deworming medicine every 3 months and herbal deworming medicine once a month such as papaya leaves and chinese petai which are mixed into their daily diet. Feed is given in the morning (once a day) in the form of bananas, papaya, sapodilla, cucumber, corn, salak, oranges, and long beans which are washed first. On Wednesdays and Sundays, they are given additional feed, namely one boiled egg and coconut.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Oocyst of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. is the infective stage that is commonly found in the feces of infected humans and animals. Diagnosis can be made by finding oocysts from feces through floatation or stool concentration methods. Fresh stool specimens can be concentrated with sheater sugar solution and preparations with cover glass examined under a microscope. In this study, the floatation method and the modified Ziehl-Neelsen method were used. In the floatation method, positive results were obtained or the discovery of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. oocysts. This method must have a high eye sensitivity to look for </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. considering how small they are under a microscope.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The modified Ziehl-Neelsen method is a simple and specific method to detect </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp. (Maryanti </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2014) which will be colored reddish-pink and easily distinguished from the background (Kusumasari and Syairaji, 2020). This method has an accuracy of about 37-90% (Mahardianti </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2020). The same thing in this study, in the modified Ziehl-Neelsen method the discovery of oocysts </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> sp.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">was pink under a microscope at 1000x magnification.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark24"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark25"></a>CONCLUSION AND SUGGESTIONS</span></h2>
<h2><a name="bookmark26"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark27"></a>Conclusion</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">From the results of the examination of 3 samples of orangutan feces at different locations, 1 sumatran orangutan (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo abelii</span><span class="font3">) at Pematang Siantar Animal Park and 2 bornean orangutans (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Pongo pygmaeus</span><span class="font3">) at Medan Wildlife Park, it was found that 2 bornean orangutans were infected by the protozoan </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium parvum</span><span class="font3">.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark28"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark29"></a>Suggestion</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">It is necessary to carry out good and correct managaement of cage maintenance to prevent cryptosporidiosis and further serological research is needed, such as ELISA (Enzyme-linked Immunosorbent Assay) to confirm </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Cryptosporidium</span><span class="font3"> oocyst species from samples obtained.</span></p>
