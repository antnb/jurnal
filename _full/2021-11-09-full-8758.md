<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>Abstrak</span></h1>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Jurnal ini berjudul kata sambung dalam Novel &quot;&nbsp;Jika saya tinggal &quot;&nbsp;oleh Gayle Forman. Penelitian ini mengenai jenis kata sambung yang terdapat di novel dan fungsi kata sambung yang digunakan dalam novel . Tujuan dari jurnal ini adalah untuk mengetahui jenis kata sambung dan untuk menjelaskan fungsi kata sambung yang digunakan dalam novel . Kata sambung diambil dari novel berjudul “Jika saya tingga”l oleh Forman ( 2009) . Pengumpulan data dilakukan dengan metode dokumenter . Dalam menyelesaikan proses pengumpulan data , pencatatan teknik diterapkan . Data yang terkumpul dianalisis secara deskriptif . Data untuk penelitian ini merupakan data kualitatif . Teori utama yang digunakan dalam penulisan ini adalah teori kata sambung oleh Halliday dan Hassan dalam buku mereka &quot;&nbsp;Kohesi dalam bahasa Inggris &quot;&nbsp;( 1976) , teori mencakup jenis jenis kata sambung. Selain itu , jurnal ini juga menggunakan teori dari Stern , dalam Bukunya Garis Tata Bahasa Inggris dengan Latihan dan Kunci Jawaban ( 2003) menjelaskan fungsi kata sambung. Temuan ini menunjukkan empat jenis kata sambung yang dikemukakan oleh Haliday dan Hassan , yaitu: tambahan , berlawanan , bersifat sebab dan sementara . Ada dua kesimpulan dinyatakan di sini , berdasarkan masalah yang dianalisis . Pertama , empat jenis kata sambung dikemukakan oleh Haliday dan Hassan yang menemukan novel ini , dengan berbagai jenis . Kedua , kata sambung dalam novel ini memiliki beberapa fungsi , yaitu: kata sambung menggabungkan satu kata denagn yang lain , kata sambung menggambungkan satu klausa dengan yang lain , kata sambung menggabungkan satu kalimat denagn yang lain , dan kata sambung menggabungkan satu frasa dengan satu frase yang lain .</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci: Kata sambung , tambahan , berlawanan, sebab, sementara</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font0"><a name="bookmark3"></a>1.</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In communication we have to consider grammar, situation, and the audience. The message must be well-structured so that it can be easier to understand. To make a well structured sentence, we have to organize with grammatical rules. In communication, there is a transfer of information whether it is spoken or written. The written text demonstrates how the language is exploited or manipulated in order to achieve the intended goal of the writer. To form understandable English is not an easy task, even for those educated in the language. The difficulties occur in using their English, especially in understanding conjunctions. A conjunction is a way of linking different parts of text to create cohesiveness. It is difficult to learn to use conjunctions correctly in a foreign language. Most English conjunctions have several different functions and these may correspond to several different conjunctions in another language.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Specifically this study will discuss types and functions of conjunctions found in the novel </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“If I Stay”</span><span class="font1"> by Forman. Based on the theory that is proposed by Halliday and Hasan (1976:320) a conjunction is somewhat different from other cohesive relations. It is based on the assumption that there are in the linguistic system form systematic relationships between sentences. There are a number of ways in which the system allows a text to connect meanings. There are four types of conjunction; additive, adversative, causal, and temporal.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font0"><a name="bookmark15"></a>5. </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Analysis of Conjunction and Its Function In Novel “If I Stay”</span><br><br><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark16"></a>5.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Types of Conjunctions in Novel “If I Stay”</span></h1></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Additive</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In this type conjunction, there are three kinds of items which can be noted from the data source, such as the additives </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">and, neither</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">or</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">When </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and</span><span class="font1"> is used alone as a cohesive item, as distinct from </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and then</span><span class="font1">, etc, it often seems to have the sense of ‘there is something more to be said from the previous sentence’ (Halliday and Hasan, 1976:245). It can be seen clearly in the following sentences.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font0">1.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Mom hands me a steaming mug </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and</span><span class="font1"> the newspaper. (GF, 2009:5, L.18)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font0">2.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;If you want, I’ll pick you up at five-thirty </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and</span><span class="font1"> we’ll drive to Portland together. (GF, 2009:35, L.9)</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">b. Or (simple Alternative Additive)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">With </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">or,</span><span class="font1"> relation distinction between the external and the internal plan is perhaps more clear-cut in the basic meaning of which of the conjunctive or relation is alternative. In the data source there are some items of the application of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">or</span><span class="font1">, such as being used in the following sentences.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font0">1.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;It isn’t even that my cello is at school </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">or</span><span class="font1"> that I could stay home and watch TV. (GF, 2009:10, L.22)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font0">2.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;What springs into my mind are those news reports about tornadoes </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">or </span><span class="font1">fire. (GF, 2009:16, L.21)</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adversative</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The core meaning of adversative relation is “contrary to expectation”, which may come from the content of what is being said (external or from the communication process (internal). In order to express this sense the writer applies two adversative items in five stories such as: </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">but</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">only.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;But (Simple Adversative ‘Proper’)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">but</span><span class="font1"> is used to link the two sentences that have different senses, called ‘contrastive’. In general, the adversative conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">but </span><span class="font1">indicates the meaning of ‘contrary to expectation’. This conjunction has external aspect since it expresses the meaning of ‘as against’.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font0">1.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Mom and Dad offer to let me drive, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">but</span><span class="font1"> I say no. (GF, 2009:11, L.18)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font0">2.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I guess it was okay to be in arrested development with one kid, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">but </span><span class="font1">with two, time two grow up. (GF, 2009:12, L.15)</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Only (Simple Adversative)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">only</span><span class="font1"> in the conjunctive relation is apart from simple adversative relation. Beside the conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">only,</span><span class="font1"> the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">yet</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">although</span><span class="font1"> belong to the simple adversative conjunction. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Only</span><span class="font1"> can be used as a focusing adverb and it refers to different part of a sentence and normally comes before a subject that it refers to.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font0">1.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I’ve shown them to Adam </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">only</span><span class="font1"> after we’d already been together for five months. (GF, 2009:10, L.4)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font0">2.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;That’s what we did the last time I went to Portland with him, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">only </span><span class="font1">it was warmer then. (GF, 2009:32, L.2)</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Causal</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In general, the causal relation indicates the relation between sentences that express what follows. This is the result or consequence of previous relation. For the conjunctive items that belong to causal relation, there are no significant varieties that can be noted. The following discussion includes items, such as: </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so, therefore, accordingly, because, for</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. So (Simple Causal)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1">, as simple expressions of causal relation is used in the story, such as being used in the following sentences.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font0">1.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;That’s why I practiced so much, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1"> I could rush myself on solid ground and then work out the details from there. (GF, 2009:35, L.21)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font0">2.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I’m not dead and the heart monitor is sleeping along, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1"> I assume I’m not, but I’m not in my body. (GF, 2009:43, L.10)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">This conjunction is the reversed form of the causal relation, in which the presupposing sentence expresses the cause. Within the sentence, it is natural to find the structural expression of cause going in either direction. With the cohesive relation between sentences, however within the text unfolded reflects the typical sequence. The conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">because</span><span class="font1">, as simple reversed expressions of causal relation is used in the story in the following sentences:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font0">1.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;She’ll probably think I’m absent </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">because</span><span class="font1"> I stayed out late listening to Adam and shooting Star in Portland. (GF, 2009:31, L.13)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font0">2.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;He says that jazz is punk for old people; I guess that explains it, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">because</span><span class="font1"> I don’t like punk. (GF, 2009:43, L.3)</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.4 Temporal</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Temporal conjunction indicates that the relation obtaining between sentences are concerned with time. Temporal relation indicates the events in the text are related in terms of the time of the occurrence. This relation is expressed internally by its simplest form </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then.</span><span class="font1"> The other temporal expressions are such as (</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and</span><span class="font1">) </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then, aftherthat, afterwards, since, before, after.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">a. And Then (Simple Sequential Temporal)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">As mentioned before, conjunctive </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then</span><span class="font1"> or </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and then</span><span class="font1"> connect the sentences which are related in time. It can be seen from examples below that this conjunctive item relates the two, where the second item can be interpreted occurs after the first event.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font0">1.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;I played lightly at first </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and then</span><span class="font1"> with more force and speed as the song now playing in my head increased in intensity. (GF, 2009:60, L.24)</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font0">2.</span><span class="font1"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;She’d exploded like a thunderstorm, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and then</span><span class="font1"> be fine again. (GF, 2009:70, L.20)</span></p></li></ul>

<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">As proposed by Halliday and Hasan in their book </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“Cohesion in English” </span><span class="font1">(1976:238), there are four types of conjunction: Additive relation: </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and</span><span class="font1">, and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">or</span><span class="font1">; Adversative relation: </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">but</span><span class="font1">, and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">only</span><span class="font1">; Causal relation: </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1">, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">because</span><span class="font1">, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">for,</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then</span><span class="font1">; and Temporal relation: </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and then, after that, afterwards, since, before,</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">after</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The conjunctions found in the novel “</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">If I Stay”</span><span class="font1"> have some functions. The functions are conjunction joins one word to another, conjunction joins one clause to another, conjunction joins one sentence to another, and conjunction joins one phrase to another. It is as a theory proposed by Stern in his book entitled “</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">An Outline of English Grammar with Exercises and Answer Key”</span><span class="font1"> (2003:101).</span></p>
