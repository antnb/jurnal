<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>INTISARI</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Millettia pinnata</span><span class="font8"> L. Panigrahi atau </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Pongamia pinnata</span><span class="font8"> L. Piere adalah tumbuhan legum yang menghasilkan biji dengan kandungan minyak yang cocok untuk biodiesel. Fenologi dan polinasi pada tanaman ini dipantau dan dimanipulasi untuk meningkatkan pembentukan biji dan hasil panen. Musim berbunga di Kununurra berlangsung selama satu bulan dari awal Oktober dan pembungaan terjadi tidak bersamaan baik pada satu tanaman maupun di antara tanaman yang berbeda. Dua spesies lebah lokal sebagai polinator, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Megachile</span><span class="font8"> sp., dan </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Nomia</span><span class="font8"> sp., memiliki tingkat kunjungan yang sangat rendah (6-13 kunjungan) dengan puncak waktu kunjungan dari jam 9.00 sampai jam 10.00 dan periode aktivitas mencari makan yang pendek (dari jam 6.00 sampai 12.00). Hal ini mungkin terjadi karena sedikitnya volume nektar yang dihasilkan (1.0 ± 0.04 µl) dan tingginya temperatur di Kununurra. Viabilitas polen yang tinggi (85 ± 3%) menurun tajam menjadi 10 ± 3% dengan penyimpanan selama satu tahun pada suhu 4<sup>o</sup>C, tetapi hanya sedikit menurun menjadi 62 ± 3% pada suhu penyimpanan -20<sup>o</sup>C dan -80<sup>o</sup>C. Kunjungan lebah madu (</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Apis mellifera</span><span class="font8">) meningkatkan hasil panen biji dari 296 menjadi 4.981 g/pohon, tetapi hasil panen ini masih rendah dan sangat bervariasi.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci: morfologi bunga, fenologi, lebah madu, Millettia pinnata, viabilitas polen, hasil panen biji</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Millettia pinnata</span><span class="font8"> L. Panigrahi syn. </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Pongamia pinnata</span><span class="font8"> L. Pierre is a leguminous tree, which produces seed oil suitable for biodiesel. Phenology and pollination were monitored and manipulated to increase seed set and yield. Flowering time in Kununurra occurred for one month from early October and flowering was asynchronous within and between trees. Two legitimate, native bee pollinators, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Megachile</span><span class="font8"> sp. and </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Nomia</span><span class="font8"> sp., had very low visitation rates (6 and 13 visits) during the peak period from 09.00 – 10.00 and short foraging activity (from 06.00 – 12.00). This was possibly due to the small nectar reward (1.0 ± 0.04 </span><span class="font2">µ</span><span class="font8">l) and high summer temperature in Kununurra. High pollen viability (85 ± 3%) decreased substantially following storage for 1 year at 4<sup>o</sup>C to 10 ± 3%, but only slightly to 62 ± 3% at either -20 <sup>o</sup>C or -80 <sup>o</sup>C. Introduced honey bees (</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Apis mellifera</span><span class="font8">) increased seed yield from 296 to 4981 g/tree, but it remained relatively low and highly variable</span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Keywords: flower morphology, phenology, honey bee, Millettia pinnata, pollen viability, seed yield</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Millettia pinnata</span><span class="font8"> (L). Panigrahi syn </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Pongamia pinnata</span><span class="font8"> L. (Pierre) is one of the few leguminous trees that produce seeds with high oil content, therefore, it is useful for biodiesel. This new tree crop has multiple uses, including many by-products and extracts from the seed, which could improve profitability of farming systems and commercial plantations (Scott </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2008). Information gathered from natural and ornamental trees indicates this crop has substantial potential for successful biodiesel production (Murphy </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2012). However, little is known about yield of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata</span><span class="font8"> in plantation environments, in particular the impact of factors such as flowering, pollination and pod set on seed yield. These were investigated in mature trees in Kununurra Western Australia, which are in the oldest plantations in Australia.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Millettia pinnata</span><span class="font8"> originates from India (Sujatha </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2008) and across Asia into the Pacific (Morton,</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">1990; Scott </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2008). Flowering time varies with individual trees across locations (Dhillon </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2009; Raju and Rao, 2006), but the diversity of flowering time is poorly understood. The monsoonal tropics of northern Australia have a distinct hot wet and cooler dry season. Most rain falls from November till March with an annual rainfall of 800 mm (Bureau of Meteorology, 2010). Mean maximum monthly temperatures range from 30°C in the coolest month of July to 39°C in November, mean monthly minimum temperatures range from 15°C in July to 26°C in December and phenology will need to be determined under these climatic conditions. Flowers take 11 months to develop into mature seeds (Arpiwi </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2012). Hence, reproductive phenology requires investigation.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Millettia pinnata</span><span class="font8"> belongs to Family Fabaceae, which has unique floral architecture (Aronne </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2012). Bisexual, zygomorphic flowers have one large standard petal, two light-purple wing petals and two white keel</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">petals. Standard petals have a hook-like structure at the base to hold nectar. Anthers and stigma are enclosed but separated in the boat-shaped keel petals (Raju and Rao, 2006). These require tripping agents to bring about pollination and the most common, legitimate pollinators are bees (Galloni </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2007). Legitimate pollinators will need to be identified and managed for </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata </span><span class="font8">plantations.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">Pollen viability can be limited across-pollination. Pollen viability can be reduced by temperature extremes in the field (Kakani </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2005; Austin </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 1996) or prolonged low temperatures suitable for storage (Astarini </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 1999). Superior genotypes of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata </span><span class="font8">has been sought (Arpiwi </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2012) and controlled cross-pollination required for breeding was assisted by pollen storage. Viable pollen can be distinguished from dead pollen using fluorescence microscopy (George </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">at al</span><span class="font8">., 2009).</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">The aims of this study were to investigate phenology, pollination and the rule of native and introduced pollinators on seed set and seed yield. Pollen viability under different time and temperatures were also studied.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>Flowering penology and floral traits</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8">Plants (20) were randomly selected within the Forest Products Commission collection of Kununurra (Lat 15° 46´ S, Long 128° 44´ E) in tropical, northern Western Australia. Flowering phenology was observed from the beginning (first week of October 2012) until the end (first week of November 2012) of the flowering season on 20 selected trees. Flower characteristics, including length of inflorescence and number of flowers and buds in an inflorescence, were examined on 30 inflorescences from three trees. Timing and duration of flower opening were noted by removing open flowers from an inflorescence to avoid recounting. Time of dehiscence, when anthers began to disperse pollen, was measured on 20 mature buds (one day before flower opening) in hourly interval from 06.00 to 10.00. Buds were opened, and anthers removed and observed over paper using a 10x hand lens. Stigma receptivity was measured in mature buds (20) and open flowers (20) at 07.00, 12.00 and 16.00 using Peroxtesmo Ko indicator paper (Macherey-Nagel GmbH &amp;&nbsp;Co, Germany) according to the method by Dafni and Maues (1998). Nectar was collected from bagged inflorescences before flower opening to avoid insect removal of nectar. When flowers opened bags were removed and 30 flowers from 3 trees and nectar was collected using a micropipette and the volume measured.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Flower visitors</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8">Flower visitors to three selected trees were observed. Their activities, including mode of approach, forage collected and contact with anther and stigma were recorded. The number of visits by each visitor was monitored at hourly intervals for 30 min from 06.00 to 17.00. Visitors were captured and identified in the Entomology Section of the Western Australian Museum.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>Pollen viability</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8">Pollen viability and storage potential were examined using a method by George </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font8"> (2009). Nine inflorescences were harvested from three trees and air dried over paper for 4 h. Anthers were cut from flowers, bulked and air dried (48 h) in a desiccator containing silica gel. Samples were stored at 4<sup>o</sup>C, -20<sup>o</sup>C or -80<sup>o</sup>C for 0, 90, 180, 270 and 360 days. Following storage, anthers were suspended in 1 ml of 20% sucrose and agitated to release pollen, then left for 10 min to hydrate pollen grains. A microscope slide was prepared with a drop of 1% flourescein diacetate in acetone, evaporated and then a drop of pollen suspension was added. Staining was viewed under a Zeiss Axioplan fluorescence microscope MC 80 with a blue filter combination. Viable pollens fluoresced bright yellow-green while non-viable pollens were opaque green or did not fluoresce. Viability was calculated as percentage of fluorescent pollen over total pollen grains.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>Pod and seed yields</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8">Pod and seed were harvested in two consecutive years to study the influence of introduced honey bees on pod and seed set. Five hives were introduced to a </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata </span><span class="font8">plot containing 10 year-old trees planted into laser-leveled irrigated plots at 3.6 m x 6 m spacing. Bee hives were placed 6 m apart from the trees early in October 2008 during flowering time. Pods were hand harvested from 20 trees in September 2008 and 2009 respectively and weighed. These will give yields comparison with and without introduced honey bees.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>Data analysis</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8">Mean and standard errors of floral traits were calculated using Microsoft Excel. Pollen viability at different storage temperatures and durations were subjected to analysis of variance using Genstat 14<sup>th</sup> (VSN International, UK). Mean and standard error of mean were determined.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">RESULTS</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>Flowering phenology and floral traits</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8">Flowering in Kununurra commenced in the first week of October and lasted until the first week of November and it varied within a tree and between trees. Within a tree new flowers bloomed few days after the first bloomed, while between trees the difference could be up to eighteen days. Inflorescences were 16.5 ± 0.3 cm long with 78 ± 1.2 flowers (Figure 1). Flowers opened from 06.00 to 10.00 in the morning and they remained open until 17.00. Each flower opened for one</span></p>
<div><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/12091-1.jpg" alt="" style="width:108pt;height:213pt;">
<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">Figure 1</span><span class="font5">. Inflorescences of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mil-lettia pinnata</span></p>
</div><br clear="all">
<p><span class="font8">day only, there were 10-15 flowers open per inflorescence per day, and flowers opened in acropetal order (from base to tip) within the inflorescence. All flowers within an inflorescence opened within 9 ± 0.3 days and the ovary began to swell into a pod 8 ± 0.5 days after the flower opened. Anthers started to dehisce at the mature bud stage, one day before flower opening. Stigmas were receptive in mature buds just before opening and in open flowers from 06.00 to 17.00. Nectar volume was 1.0 ± 0.04 </span><span class="font2">µ</span><span class="font8">l per flower and it was available from 06.00 to 11.00 with only a negligible amount detected after this time.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>Flower visitors</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8">Three species of local insects visited flowers, and these were native bees, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Megachile</span><span class="font8"> sp. and </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Nomia</span><span class="font8"> sp., and a wasp </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Polistes</span><span class="font8"> sp. (Figure 2). During the monitoring period from 06.00 to 18.00 more visits were recorded of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Apis mellifera</span><span class="font8"> than </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Megachile</span><span class="font8"> sp., followed by </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Nomia </span><span class="font8">sp and </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Polistes</span><span class="font8"> sp. (Figure 3). Foraging activities started at 06.00 and most flower visitors had their peak activity of 6-12 visits between 09.00 and 10.00 on the bright, sunny days while </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Apis mellifera</span><span class="font8"> made about 100 visits at this time. Flower visits began to decrease after 10.00 and there were no visits after 12.00.</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/12091-2.jpg" alt="" style="width:249pt;height:84pt;">
<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">Figure 2. </span><span class="font5">Flower visitors to </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Millettia pinnata</span><span class="font5"> in plantations at Kununurra: a) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Apis mellifera</span><span class="font5">, b) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Megachile</span><span class="font5"> sp., c) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Nomia</span><span class="font5"> sp. and d) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Polistes </span><span class="font5">sp.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Apis mellifera, Megachile</span><span class="font8"> sp. and </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Nomia</span><span class="font8"> sp. visited both mature buds and open flowers, where keel petals provided a platform for landing. These bees tripped the keel petals from the front without side-working and moved toward the base of the standard petal for nectar collection. Their weight freed stigma and stamens and these contacted with ventral side of the bees carrying pollens from previously visited flowers, then cloud of pollens were ejected explosively dusting the ventral sides of the bees. </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Apis mellifera</span><span class="font8"> collected pollen and carried it in the pollen baskets on their hind legs, but </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Megachile </span><span class="font8">sp. and </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Nomia</span><span class="font8"> sp carried pollen in the underside of abdomen. The wasp (</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Polistes</span><span class="font8"> sp.) foraged on petals for nectar droplets with side-working, but without body contact with the stigma. Wasps foraged for nectar by perforating the calyx and very rarely tripped the keel. There was a maximum of seven wasp visits at the peak time between 9.00 and 10.00.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>Pollen viability</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8">Pollen viability was influenced by storage temperature and duration (P&lt;0.001, Figure 4). Viability of fresh pollen was 85 ± 3%. Within three months of storage at 4<sup>o</sup>C viability decreased substantially to 58 ± 4% and this</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/12091-3.png" alt="" style="width:227pt;height:304pt;">

(b) from 06.00 to 17.00 on </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Millettia pinnata</span><span class="font5"> grown in plantations at Kununurra</span></p>

At lower temperatures viability declined at a slower rate and it did not change within 90 days but reduced to 67 ± 4% after 180 days (-20<sup>o</sup>C) and 270 days (-80<sup>o</sup>C ). <h2><a name="bookmark26"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark27"></a>DISCUSSION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8">With some variations between trees flowering in Kununurra commenced in the first week of October and ended during the first week of November. There was considerable overlap in the duration of flowering between trees, even though the first flowers in inflorescences opened at different times within a tree. In some tree crops, such as apricot, asynchrony of flowering reduces fruit set (Austin </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 1998) but the limited asynchrony in </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata</span><span class="font8"> should not limit cross pollination as there were some flowers open on several trees at any one time.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">Native pollinators were very rare in Kununurra, and only two species of legitimate pollinators were observed on </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata</span><span class="font8"> trees, namely two bees </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Megachile</span><span class="font8"> sp. and </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Nomia</span><span class="font8"> sp. Both </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Megachile</span><span class="font8"> sp. and </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Nomia</span><span class="font8"> sp. triggered the pollen release mechanism when they landed on enclosed keel petals and depressed these petals while seeking nectar at the base on standard petal. The bees grasped the flower and their abdomens always made contact with the stigma while foraging allowing for transfer of pollen from previously visited flowers. Local bees were legitimate pollinators, who probably transferred pollen resulting in cross fertilizations and successful seed set. A single species of wasp, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Polistes</span><span class="font8"> sp. also visited flowers, but this insect was an illegitimate pollinator and it did not trigger the pollen release mechanism.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">Insects, foraged on flowers, reached a peak activity between 09.00 and 10.00 with 6-12 visits. The low foraging activity was possibly due to the low volume of</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">nectar and high daytime temperatures in Kununurra during flowering. High diversity, abundance and activity of pollinators are usually related to copious nectar availability (Bhattacharya, 2004). In Kununurra only 1 </span><span class="font2">µ</span><span class="font8">l of nectar was available per flower from 06.00 to 11.00 and negligible amounts were detected thereafter. This may be due to the extreme temperatures in Kununurra with the highest maximum of 40.8<sup>o</sup>C in October which could lead to evaporation of nectar (Herrera, 1990) and reduced insect activity.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">Overall, the activity of introduced honey bees was much greater than native bees. Whilst there are 800 bee species in Western Australia, most of them are solitary (Houston, 2011). Solitary bees are more specialized pollinating particular species of flowers. They have shorter lifespans and have morphological and behavioral traits that fit the host plants but they are often ineffective pollinators for non-host plants. In contrast, honey bees live in hives of thousands of individuals (Austin </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 1996), colonies live year-around and they are generalists and rely on many flower species for survival (Westerkamps and Gottsberger, 2000).</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">Pollen viability was high at 85% and this should have been adequate for cross-pollination and seed set. The number and viability of pollen grain deposited onto the stigma was not a limiting factor for seed set in </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata </span><span class="font8">(Arathi </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 1999). Pollen viability usually remains high over the short periods of time required for dispersal and cross-pollination (Astarini </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 1999; Sahai, 2009), and is not likely to be of concern in </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata</span><span class="font8">. Pollen storage for breeding purposes was possible at freezing temperatures and it was well maintained at -20<sup>o</sup>C and -80<sup>o</sup>C.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">Facilities for -20<sup>o</sup>C are more readily available and given there was no difference in viability between -20<sup>o</sup>C and -80<sup>o</sup>C after a year, -20<sup>o</sup>C is recommended. Storage at 4<sup>o</sup>C is possible for short periods of less than three months as it retains viability at about 60%. Pollen storage at the -20<sup>o</sup>C is recommended here as it would preserve pollen within and between flowering seasons or in transit to one or more distant regions. Long term storage of viable pollen is important to preserve resources that can be utilized for breeding programs and genetic engineering (Alba </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2011).</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">Pod and seed yield were low and highly variable. These parameters improved with the introduction of honey bees but remained problematic. Pod and seed yields were about 17-fold higher with, than without honey bees. Reliance on local pollinators was not a viable option for high yields in Kununurra. The provision of additional honey bees was required to increase pollination and seed set. Pollination is obviously a critical factor for seed or fruit set in most plants, especially for commercial production of seed from </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata</span><span class="font8"> (Dhillon </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2009; Raju and Rao, 2006).</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">Only a third of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata</span><span class="font8"> trees produced seeds under irrigated plantation conditions indicating inadequate pollination in Kununurra. Similarly in India, observations indicate a large proportion of trees did not produce any seeds in natural populations, however lack of quantitative</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">data for seed number and tree age makes comparisons difficult (Sharma </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font8">., 2011). Presumably insects are present in natural forests but there may be competitions or other factors influencing their activity on </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata</span><span class="font8">.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark28"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark29"></a>CONCLUSION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font8">Flowering time of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">M. pinnata</span><span class="font8"> in northern Western Australia occurred at the end of the hot, dry season, from early October to early November. Two local bees, namely </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Megachile</span><span class="font8"> sp. and </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Nomia</span><span class="font8"> sp. were identified as legitimate pollinators visited flowers. The low foraging activities of the local bees may have been due to high temperatures and low nectar availability. Introduced honey bees, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Apis mellifera</span><span class="font8">, was legitimate pollinators, and they substantially increased pollination and seed yield and are recommended for commercial production systems. Pollen viability was high and it could be stored short term at 4°C and long term at -20°C. Seed yield was variable and low. Further research is required to increased seed yield.</span></p>
