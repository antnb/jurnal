<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">The aim of this study was to investigate genetic diversity and relationship of guava (local name: jambu biji) genotypes grown in Bali, Indonesia, based on Random Amplified Polymorphic DNA (RAPD) markers. Twelve guava cultivars namely Jambu biji ‘Australia’, ‘Bangkok Merah’, ‘Bangkok Putih’, ‘Cokorde’, ‘Dadu 1’, ‘Dadu 2’, ‘Getas Merah’, ‘Kamboja’, ‘Kristal’, ‘Pipit’, ‘Susu’ and ‘Variegata’ were collected from nine Regencies in Bali using survey and explorative method. Ten decamers RAPD primers were employed to distinguish between the 12 cultivars and determine their genetic relationships. A dendogram was constructed using coefficient dissimilarity analysis based on phylogenetic analysis using parsimony (PAUP). The twelve cultivars were grouped into 2 main clusters and five smaller clusters. Variation between genotypes of guava local will be good sources for future crop improvemen.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">Keywords</span><span class="font4">: DNA markers, RAPD analysis, guava cultivars</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font5">Guava (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Psidium guajava</span><span class="font5"> L, family Myrtaceae) is one of the 50 most well-known edible fruit (Chandra &amp;&nbsp;Mishra, 2007) and are commonly cultivated in tropical region including Indonesia. Guava is an allogamous and highly heterozygous fruit crop. Many variations occur in local Indonesia guava as a result of traditional seedling selection by local people. Vegetative propagation was done rarely, so that variations within cultivars are quiet high. In Indonesia, guava mainly</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">grows as backyard trees, not an intensive farming. Guava can be consumed fresh as table fruit, or processed in guava juice, jelly, jam and sweets.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Demand for guava in Indonesia is high, both for fresh fruit and processing into juice. Guava fruit is rich in dietary fiber, contain high vitamin A, C, calcium, potassium and iron (Valdes </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">. 2007). Guava also high on quercetin, which is effective to increase blood thrombocyte and therefore the fruit juice is a common</span></p>
<p><span class="font0">ISSN ONLINE: 9 772303 337 008</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">supplement for dengue fever patients in Indonesia. In addition, guava leaves extract contain tannin which is effective to treat acute diarrhea and gastroenteritis (Soedarya 2010) and are commonly use in Balinese community.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Diversity of local guava in Indonesia needs to be investigated to be able to select favorable and superior genotypes for further cultivation and improvement. Morphological traits such as leaf shape, leaf size, fruit size and fruit color are important phenotypic markers to select superior genotypes. However, morphological traits may change with the agronomic treatment and growth environment. Therefore, molecular characterization needs to be developed to distinguish between genotypes. RAPD marker has been used to distinguish guava genotypes in India (Chandra and Mishra, 2007), Bangladesh (Ahmed </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2011), Taiwan (Chen </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2007) and Kenya (Kidaha </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2014). RAPD marker has also been employed on wani, a native fruit of Bali (Rai </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2008) and cauliflower (Astarini </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2002; Astarini </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2006). In this study, RAPD analysis will be employed to distinguish guava genotypes that are grown in Bali,</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Indonesia.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">The use of RAPD technique on perennial plants would increase selection efficiency since it can be done on young plants. RAPD data will complement morphological data and has a great potential on developing germplasms collection data base (Rai </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2008).</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>MATERIALS AND METHODS Study site and sampling</span></h2>
<p><span class="font5">Guava samples were taken from 9 Regencies of Bali, Indonesia, namely Badung, Bangli, Buleleng, Tabanan, Gianyar, Klungkung, Karangasem, Jembrana and Denpasar. Sites altitude ranging from 200 – 700 m above sea level, and temperature ranging from 20 – 30<sup>o</sup>C. Purposive sampling technique (Rai et al., 2008) was employed and selected trees were tagged for study.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>RAPD analysis</span><br><br><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark10"></a>DNA extraction</span></h2>
<p><span class="font5">Guava young leaves were collected from field and placed in plastic bag containing silica gel. Leaves were kept in ice box until arriving in lab and then keep in -20<sup>o</sup>C freezer until use. Guava leaves were washed under running tap water and then dried. Guava leaf, 0.1 gram, was</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">weighed and crushed using mortar and pestle, then 800 µl of extraction buffer (CTAB, consists of 2% CTAB, 1.4 M NaCl, 100 mM Tris HCl pH 8, 20 mM EDTA pH 8, 1% β-mercaptoethanol</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">that has been incubated on 65<sup>o</sup>C waterbath for 30 minute) was added. Sample mixture was then incubated on 65<sup>o</sup>C waterbath for 60 minute, and shake gently every 10 minutes during incubation. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Samples were then</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">centrifuged for 15 mins at 12,000 rpm and the supernatant were mixed with equal volumes of chloroform: isoamyl alcohol (24:1). The mixture was centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 15 mins. Supernatant were taken gently and mixed with an equal volume of cold isopropanol and incubated at -20<sup>o</sup>C for at least 1 hour. The nucleic acid was pelleted by centrifugation for 10 minute at 12,000 rpm and the pellet washed with 500 µl of 70% ethanol and then centrifuged for 5 minute at 12,000 rpm. The DNA pellet was air dried and resuspended in 50 -100 µl TE buffer + 1% RNAse on 37<sup>o</sup>C waterbath for 60 minute. Purified DNA was then stored at 4<sup>o</sup>C refrigerator until use.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark11"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark12"></a>DNA Quantification</span></h2>
<p><span class="font5">Purified DNA was quantified using Gene Quant to find out DNA</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">concentration obtained. Reference was set using psdH2O. Dilution factor (psd H</span><span class="font2">2</span><span class="font5">0) approximately 1998 µl and DNA about 2 µl was entered into cuvette and then entered into Gene Quant to measure light absorption at 260 nm wavelength so DNA concentration and DNA-RNA ratio can be determined.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark13"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark14"></a>DNA dilution</span><span class="font5">.</span></h2>
<p><span class="font5">DNA dilution was done with the aim to obtain DNA concentration according to amplification requirement, i.e 2.5 ng/µl. DNA solution was added with psdH2O until required DNA was achieved.</span></p>

<p><span class="font5">A dendogram showing relationship among 12 cultivars was generated (Fig. 2). Two major clusters were obtained. First cluster consists of ‘Variegata’ and ‘Australia’ cultivars, which were on separate cluster with 10 other cultivars, with 0.49 coefficient dissimilarity. ‘Variegata’ and ‘Australia’ cultivars have a similar fruit shape (round), red flesh and spreaded seed. Although their fruits are very similar, cultivars of ‘Variegata’ and ‘Australia’ have different flower colour, i.e. white for ‘Variegata’ and red for ‘Australia. Leaf shapes are similar but ‘Variegata’ has a combination of white and green on its leaf, while ‘Australia’ has solid green colour. Cultivar of ‘Australia’ in particular, has specific characteristics, i.e. its stem and fruit has purplish red colour. Fruit productivity is very low,</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">and therefore this cultivar often use as ornamental fruit plants in pot. Cultivar</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">of ‘Variegata’ also often us as ornamental pot plants as it has colorful leaf, produce a lot of fruit but plain taste.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Although cultivar ‘Pipit’ was in one big cluster with the 9 other cultivars, it has a quite distant relationship with the other, as revealed by their dissimilarity coefficient of 0.39. ‘Pipit’ has distinct characteristics; smallest fruit compared to others and has white flesh with yellow colour thin skin. Ripe fruits have a beautiful fragrance and sweet taste.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Cultivars of ‘Getas Merah and ‘Bangkok Merah’ are very closely related, as revealed by their coefficient dissimilarity of 0.2. Name ‘Bangkok’ means the plant was originally from Bangkok, Thailand. ‘Getas Merah’ is a</span></p>
<p><span class="font0">ISSN ONLINE: 9 772303 337 008</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">result of a cross between ‘Bangkok Merah’ with local guava from Pasar Minggu, Jakarta. ‘Getas Merah’ has many good fruit characteristics including bright red flesh, thick flesh, sweet taste with nice fragrance. The plant has a strong rooting system and highly responsive to fertilizer, and good level of pest and disease resistance, so that it has high fruit productivity</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Cultivars of ‘Dadu 1’ and ‘Dadu 2’ were in the same cluster with coefficient dissimilarity about 0.26. Both genotypes have pale pink flesh, thick flesh and small number of seed. The fruit shape was a bit different,</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">where ‘Dadu 2’ has a round shape, while ‘Dadu 1 has narrower fruit shape toward the base of the fruit.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Another group are cultivars of ‘Kamboja’, ‘Kristal’, ‘Bangkok Putih’ and ‘Susu’. All cultivars have white and thick flesh with low seed number. Cultivars of ‘Bangkok Putih’ has a crunchy texture and sweet taste. It has light green skin colour when ripe. Cultivar of ‘Susu’ has a soft flesh and therefore will be a perfect choice for processing into jam, juice or jelly.This study demonstrated that RAPD analysis provide a simple and reliable method for cultivar identification.</span></p>
