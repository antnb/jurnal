<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstrak</span></h1>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Penelitian ini berjudul </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">The Nominalizing Suffixes Found in John Green’s Novel “The Fault in Our Stars</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">”. Tujuan dari penelitian ini adalah untuk mengidentifikasi akhiran yang membentuk kata benda serta menjelaskan fungsi dan maknanya dalam kalimat. Data diambil dari sebuah novel yang ditulis oleh John Green berjudul The Fault in Our Stars diterbitkan di New York oleh Dutton Books di tahun 2012. Teori utama yang digunakan adalah teori yang dikemukakan oleh Quirk (1973) tentang proses afiksasi. Sebagai tambahan, penelitian ini juga menggunakan teori terkait yang dikemukakan oleh Bauer (1983), Katamba (1993), dan Frank (1972). Hasil dari penelitian ini menunjukkan bahwa terdapat tujuh akhiran “class maintaining” dari kelas kata benda seperti akhiran –hood, -ship, -er, -ism, -ist, -ess, and –ette. Namun, dalam akhiran “class changing” terdapat sembilan akhiran dari kelas kata kerja menjadi kata benda seperti akhiran –ation, -al, -er/-or, -ment, -ure, -ant, -ion, -ing, -ence/-ance, dan dari kelas kata sifat menjadi kata benda seperti akhiran –ness, -cy, -ity, -dom, dan –th.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Kata Kunci: </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Morfologi, Derivatif, Kata Benda</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Words are the basic units of language. Words can be complex or simple. The complex words can be broken down into smaller units as morphemes. The study about the way in which words can be broken down into morphemes is called morphology. In morphology there is a term called affixation. That affix morphemes can be divided into two major functional categories, namely inflectional affixes which do not change referential or cognitive meaning and derivational affixes which can change the word class and also the meaning to which the base belongs. This study focuses on discussing the nominalizing suffixes to find out which word classes can be changed into nouns in the novel “The Fault in Our Stars” by John Green. Noun is chosen since it has a lot of members, the largest number in English word classes. In order to understand sentences in English better, it is necessary to know about the process of word formation, mainly the process that causes the formation of new lexemes by suffixation. That is why the</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">derivational suffixes particularly, in the derived nouns are important and interesting to be analyzed.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Method</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The data source of this study were taken from the novel “The Fault in Our Stars” written by John Green which consist of 25 chapters. This novel was chosen as the data source since it contains several numbers of nominalizing suffixes. This study used documentation method through reading and note-taking in collecting the data. The method was done by reading the novel and finding out the words which contain of nominalizing suffixes. The words which are derived nouns were underlined. Finally, the data were collected and noted. Qualitative method was used to analyze the data in this study. First, the data were classified based on the suffixes and then those words were seperated from their suffixes to know the base form. Then, the bases of the words were grouped into their word classes to ascertain in what type of derivation the words belong to. Lastly, their meanings and functions in the sentences were analyzed.</span></p>

<p><span class="font2">5.1.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;De-nominal Noun</span></p></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–hood</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- Most would live into </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">adulthood</span><span class="font2">, as Patrick had. (Green, 2012:5)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The underlined word above is the new lexeme which is constructed from the noun base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">adult</span><span class="font2"> and suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–hood</span><span class="font2"> is added to it to form the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">adulthood</span><span class="font2">. This addition does not change the grammatical class of the base. The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">adulthood</span><span class="font2"> can be determined as a noun because in the sentence it functions as the object of preposition. The derived noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">adulthood</span><span class="font2"> has the meaning of indicating </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">status.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ship</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- Your coup becomes a </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">dictatorship</span><span class="font2">. (Green, 2012: 311)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The attachment of suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-ship</span><span class="font2"> forms a new lexeme </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">dictatorship</span><span class="font2"> which belongs to the noun class from the noun base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">dictator</span><span class="font2">. That word can be determined as a noun because it functions as a complement in the sentence. The derived noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">dictatorship </span><span class="font2">tends to have the meaning of indicating </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">condition</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">c. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–er</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- I just can’t admit it because I’m a </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">teenager</span><span class="font2">” (Green, 2012: 28)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The underlined word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">teenager</span><span class="font2"> is a new lexeme which is formed through a derivational process by adding suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–er</span><span class="font2"> to the base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">teenage</span><span class="font2"> which belongs to noun</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">. </span><span class="font2">The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">teenager</span><span class="font2"> can be determined as a noun because it functions as a complement in the sentence. The suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–er</span><span class="font2"> means indicating </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">varied meaning</span><span class="font2"> as in the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">teenager.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">d. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ism</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- His </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">enthusiasm</span><span class="font2"> was adorable (Green, 2012: 147)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">enthusiasm</span><span class="font2"> which is derived from the base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">enthusiast</span><span class="font2"> belongs to the noun class. The base form is added by the suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ism</span><span class="font2"> to form </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">enthusiasm</span><span class="font2"> which also belongs to the noun class. The new lexeme </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">enthusiasm</span><span class="font2"> can be determined as a noun because it functions as subject. From the example above</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">enthusiasm</span><span class="font2"> indicates the meaning of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">attitude.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">e. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ist</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- When the </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">scientists</span><span class="font2"> of the future show up at my house with robot eyes and they tell me to try..(Green, 2012: 258)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The underlined word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">scientists</span><span class="font2"> is the new lexeme which is constructed from the noun base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">science</span><span class="font2"> and the suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-ist</span><span class="font2"> is added to it to form the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">scientists</span><span class="font2">. <ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">f. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ess</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- The </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">hostess’s</span><span class="font2"> eyes lit up as Augustus and I walked toward her. (Green, 2012:162)</span></p></li></ul>

<p><span class="font2">g. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ette</span></p></li></ul>

<p><span class="font2">The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">cigarette</span><span class="font2"> is formed from the base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">cigar</span><span class="font2"> and belongs to the noun class. <h1><a name="bookmark15"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark16"></a>5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Class Changing Derivational Suffixes</span></h1>

The meaning of derived noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">embarrassment</span><span class="font2"> is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">state.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<p><span class="font2">h. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ing</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- I considered </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">lying.</span><span class="font2"> (Green, 2012:23)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The underlined word above is constructed from verb base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lie. T</span><span class="font2">he suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ing</span><span class="font2"> is attached to the base to form the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lying.</span><span class="font2"> The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lying</span><span class="font2"> can be determined as a noun</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">because it functions as the object of the verb in the sentence. The derived noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">lying </span><span class="font2">indicates the meaning of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">activity.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">i. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ence/-ance</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- “</span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">Ignorance</span><span class="font2"> is bliss,” I said (Green, 2012:220)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The underlined word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ignorance</span><span class="font2"> is the new lexeme which is constructed from the verb base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ignore</span><span class="font2"> and the suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ance</span><span class="font2"> is added to it to form the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ignorance</span><span class="font2">. The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ignorance</span><span class="font2"> can be determined as a noun because in the sentence it functions as the subject. The derived noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ignorance</span><span class="font2"> means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">state of.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.2.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;De-adjectival Noun</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ness</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- The </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">darkness</span><span class="font2"> is still complete. (Green, 2012:133)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The attachment of suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ness</span><span class="font2"> forms the new lexeme </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">darkness</span><span class="font2"> which belongs to the noun class from the adjective base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">dark</span><span class="font2">. That word can be determined as a noun because it functions as the subject of the sentence. The derived noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">darkness</span><span class="font2"> tends to mean </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">quality</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–cy</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- “See, Isaac, if you just take-we’re coming to the curb now-the feeling of </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">legitimacy</span><span class="font2"> away from them..(Green, 2012:229)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">legitimacy</span><span class="font2"> is a form noun which is formed from the base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">legitimate </span><span class="font2">and to which the suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–cy</span><span class="font2"> is added. The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">legitimate</span><span class="font2"> is categorized as an adjective. It can be determined as a noun because it functions as the object of the preposition. The derived noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">legitimacy</span><span class="font2"> indicates </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">state of.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">c. &nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ity</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- The gentle </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">familiarity</span><span class="font2"> felt wrong, somehow. (Green, 2012:92)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The underlined word above is constructed from the adjective base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">familiar. </span><span class="font2">When the suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ity</span><span class="font2"> is attached to the base, the noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">familiarity</span><span class="font2"> is formed</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">.</span><span class="font2"> The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">familiarity</span><span class="font2"> can be determined as a noun because it functions as the subject of the sentence. The derived noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">familiarity</span><span class="font2"> indicates </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">state.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">d. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–dom</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- “Some tourists think Amsterdam is a city of sin, but in truth it is a city of </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">freedom</span><span class="font2">. (Green, 2012:157)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The underlined word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">freedom</span><span class="font2"> is a new lexeme which is formed through the derivational process by adding suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–dom</span><span class="font2"> to the base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">free</span><span class="font2"> which is an adjective</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">.</span><span class="font2"> The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">freedom</span><span class="font2"> can be determined as a noun because it functions as the complement of the sentence. The suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–dom</span><span class="font2"> means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">condition</span><span class="font2"> as in the word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">freedom.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">e. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-th</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">- &nbsp;&nbsp;..looking at me as if to say I’m sorry, as if it were a crime to mention </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">death</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">to the dying. (Green, 2012:44)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The derived noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">death</span><span class="font2"> is the new lexeme which is formed from the adjective base </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">dead.</span><span class="font2"> Suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–th</span><span class="font2"> brings a changing process towards the grammatical class of the base from adjective into noun. The word </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">death</span><span class="font2"> can be determined as a noun because it functions as a complement. The meaning of derived noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">death</span><span class="font2"> is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">state.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark17"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark18"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">It can be concluded that the nominalizing suffixes found in the novel “The Fault in Our Stars” by John Green are suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-hood, -ship, -er, -ism, -ist, -ess, –ette, -ation, -al, -er/or, -ment, -ure, -ant, -ion, -ing, –ence/ance, -ness, -cy, -ity, -dom,</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–th.</span><span class="font2"> The functions of nominalizing suffixes are first, maintaining the class of word, in this case, from noun into noun (de-nominal noun). These suffixes can lead to a shift in the grammatical subclass of the base without moving it into a new word class; second, changing the word class, in this case, from verb into noun (de-verbal noun) and from adjective into noun (de-adjectival noun). These suffixes produce new lexemes which belong to different classes from the base. Each nominalizing suffix has its own meaning, for examples, the suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–ation</span><span class="font2"> means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">state, action</span><span class="font2"> or </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">institution</span><span class="font2">, the suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">– hood</span><span class="font2"> means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">status</span><span class="font2">, the suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">-th</span><span class="font2"> means </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">state</span><span class="font2"> or </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">condition,</span><span class="font2"> etc.</span></p>
