<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>Numerical Solution Of The SIRV Model Using The Fourth-Order Runge-Kutta Method</span></h1>

<p><span class="font2">Department of Mathematics, Faculty of Mathematics and Natural Sciences, Pattimura University e-mail:</span><a href="mailto:nonatjie72@gmail.com"><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">nonatjie72@gmail.com</span></a></p>

<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Abstract</span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">: </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">This study aimed to predict the spread of the Covid-19 virus in Maluku Province using the fourth-order Runge-Kutta method. The mathematical model of the spread of the Covid-19 virus is a system of differential equations which includes Susceptible (S) variables, namely human subpopulations that are susceptible to Covid-19 virus infection, Infected (I), namely human subpopulations infected with the Covid-19 virus, Recovered (R) namely subpopulation of people who have recovered and Vaccination (V) namely a subpopulation that has been vaccinated and is immune to the Covid-19 virus, used as initial values. The values of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">k, a, β, γ, ψ, μ, μ<sub>c</sub> </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">are parameter values that are numerically solved by the fourth-order Runge-Kutta method performed for 24 literations with </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">h</span><span class="font2"> = 0,01</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">. Data were obtained from the Maluku Provincial Health Office from March 2022 - November 2022. Based on the data obtained, the average of the data is used as the initial value, where </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S =</span><span class="font2"> 198.890, Z = </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">204, R</span><span class="font2"> = 172, and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">V =</span><span class="font2"> 7.693</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">. The initial and parameter values were substituted into the numerical solution and simulated using Matlab. The rate value of each class for the next 24 months for the Susceptible (S), Infected (I), and Recovered (R) classes has decreased until it approaches zero equilibrium. It shows that the subpopulation of the three classes no longer exists, and the Vaccinated (V) class has increased significantly because almost all of the population has been vaccinated in the next 24 months. It shows that after an individual is vaccinated, he does not return to being vulnerable.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Keywords</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">: Epidemic Model, Covid-19, Vaccination, Fourth Order Runge-Kutta Method, Numerical Solution.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>1.</span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Introduction</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">Coronaviruses encompass a family of viruses known to induce a spectrum of illnesses, ranging from common colds to more severe conditions such as acute respiratory syndrome (SARS). The identification of Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) as the causative agent for a cluster of pneumonia cases in Wuhan, China, was</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">reported by Chen et al. (2020) in late 2019. Subsequently, the virus disseminated not only within China but also globally. By February 2020, the World Health Organization (WHO) officially declared coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) as a worldwide pandemic. (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Situation Report-51 SITUATION IN NUMBERS Total and New Cases in Last 24 Hours</span><span class="font4">, n.d.)</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">There has been no cure found for Covid-19, so vaccines are one of the new initiatives launched to strengthen one's immunity. The emergence of vaccines has given many people hope to be free from the plague soon. Vaccination aims to activate antibodies so that they are immune to disease or only suffer from the mild disease (Liu et al., 2022). The mathematical model was first constructed by Kermack and Mckendrick (Kermack &amp;&nbsp;Mckendrick, 1927).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Therefore, more and more researchers are considering mathematical models in solving this Covid-19 spread case. Ndairou et al. also developed a mathematical modeling system to study the spread of COVID-19 in Wuhan, China (Ndaïrou et al., 2020). Prem et al. investigated the status of COVID-19 in Wuhan (Prem et al., 2020). In another analysis, Hellewell et al. identified successful isolation techniques for COVID-19 (Hellewell et al., 2020). Kucharski et al. proposed a mathematical model-based analysis of COVID-19, where the authors considered all positive cases in Wuhan, China, until March 5, 2020 (Kucharski et al., 2020). Liu et al. Examined the possible numerical assessment of the early reproductive rate of COVID-19 in their research. (Liu et al., 2020) Fanelli and Piazza utilized mathematical modeling to explore and forecast the onset of COVID-19 in the three most impacted countries in March 2020 (Fanelli &amp;&nbsp;Piazza, 2020). In a recent study, Chakraborty and Ghosh (2020) explored a hybrid ARIMA-WBF model for predicting the spread of COVID-19 in various countries worldwide. Shereen et al. (2020) delved into the characteristics, regional impact, and distribution of coronavirus within the human population. On a related note, Tahir et al. (2019) focused on investigating Middle East Respiratory Syndrome (MERS) caused by a coronavirus in humans. Additionally, Zhao and Chen (2020), as mentioned by Zhao et al., contributed to the study</span><span class="font0">.</span><span class="font4">Utilized mathematical modeling to examine the scope of the coronavirus pandemic. Shim et al. extended their study by investigating the transmission dynamics of 2019-nCoV in South Korea (Shim et al., 2020). Nuraini, N. et al., in their study, predicted that the end of endemic transmission of Covid-19 in Indonesia would end in April 2020, and the total number of cases would exceed 8000 (Nuraini et al., 2020). In the research, Nesteruk formulated an SIR epidemic model, statistically analyzed the parameters employed in the suggested model, and demonstrated strategies for managing these infections. (Nesteruk, 2020). Zu et al. used the sensitivity analysis method (Zu et al., 2020). They conducted predictions on the epidemic trend and transmission risk associated with COVID-19, assessing the effectiveness of various intervention strategies. Tang et al. (2020) specifically delved into the efficacy of quarantine and isolation measures, thereby determining the trajectory of the COVID-19 epidemic in the later stages within China. Ahmed et al. analyzed the mathematical model of COVID-19 in their study using numerical approaches and logistic models. They proposed three numerical techniques to solve the equation: the Euler method, second-order (RK2), and fourth-order (RK4) Runge-Kutta method (Ahmed et al., 2020). In another study by Okuonghae and Omame, they used numerical simulation to investigate the</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">effect of control measures, especially social distancing, mask use, and case detection (contact tracing and subsequent testing) on COVID-19 (Okuonghae &amp;&nbsp;Omame, 2020). Meanwhile, Foy et al. used the SEIR model to evaluate age-specific vaccine distribution strategies in India (Foy et al., 2021). On the other hand, research on an effective vaccine against COVID-19 continues at full speed. Several academic studies have been conducted in this context. Kaur and Gupta pointed out in their study that different types of vaccine strategies have been developed against COVID-19 and provided an overview of research to find an effective vaccine against the novel coronavirus affecting the global world, economy, health, and human life (Kaur &amp;&nbsp;Gupta, 2020)</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">In this study, a new model is introduced that refers to the modified model (Rijoly et al., 2022) in the form of adding a new variable of vaccine subpopulation class that shows the individuals who have been vaccinated. The benefit of vaccines for the human body is to stimulate the immunity of the human body and to protect them from Covid-19 viruses. Concentrating on the model, this study endeavors to identify the equilibrium points for each compartment in both disease-free and endemic states. Additionally, the research explores the stability of these equilibrium points obtained. (Sapulette, Lesnussa &amp;&nbsp;Rijoly, 2023). The last study was to use the numerical simulation for this model.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Methods</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">This type of research is quantitative. The data used in this study were sourced from the Maluku Provincial Health Office and the Maluku Provincial Statistics Agency. The data required includes the number of subpopulations in Maluku Province, the number of subpopulations born, the number of human subpopulations susceptible to Covid-19 infection, the number of human subpopulations who have been infected with Covid-19, the number of human subpopulations who have recovered or who have become immune to Covid-19, the number of human populations who died from Covid-19 infection, The count of subpopulations that have received vaccinations and developed immunity to Covid-19, along with the life expectancy of individuals in Maluku Province in the year 2022.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The research steps are as follows:</span></p>

<p><span class="font4">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Finding the parameters of the model that has been made</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font4">3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Inputting parameter values in the model based on the data obtained</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font4">4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Solving the SIRV model with fourth-order Runge-Kutta.</span></p></li>

<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Result and Discussion</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">The SIRV model described in this research consists of 4 subpopulations, namely subpopulation </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">S</span><span class="font4"> (Susceptible human subpopulation), subpopulation </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">I (</span><span class="font4">Human subpopulation</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">that has been infected with Covid-19), Population </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">R (</span><span class="font4">Human subpopulation that has recovered from Covid-19), and subpopulation </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">V</span><span class="font4"> (Vaccinated human subpopulation) with the following assumptions (Sapulette, Lesnussa &amp;&nbsp;Rijoly, 2023):</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font4">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The natural mortality rate was present in each subpopulation and was assumed equal to μ.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font4">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The susceptible subpopulation (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">S</span><span class="font4">) might increase due to births, which was assumed to be k.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font4">c.</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;μ<sub>c</sub></span><span class="font4"> is a death rate due to the interaction of individuals in the susceptible subpopulation (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">S)</span><span class="font4"> with individuals in the infected subpopulation (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">I</span><span class="font4">) cause by Covid-19.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font4">d. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Individuals in the </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Infected</span><span class="font4"> (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">I</span><span class="font4">) subpopulation moved to the </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">Recovered</span><span class="font4"> (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">R</span><span class="font4">) subpopulation with a recovery rate of β because they received treatment.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font4">e. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Those in the Recovered (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">R</span><span class="font4">) category would shift to the Vaccination (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">V</span><span class="font4">) subgroup as part of the process of receiving vaccination to reinforce their immunity.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font4">f. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Individuals within the susceptible subpopulation (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">S</span><span class="font4">) may also choose to get vaccinated to avoid contracting Covid-19, with a vaccination rate equivalent to </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">ψ</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font4">g. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Those who had recovered from Covid-19 and received vaccination would no longer be susceptible to the virus.</span></p></li></ul>

<h2><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">The conclusions of this study are as follows:</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The Fourth Order Runge-Kutta method was used to numerically solve the SIRV model, simulating the spread of the Covid-19 virus. This SIRV model comprises Susceptible, Infected, Recovered, and Vaccinated populations. From the model, the Matlab Software iteration calculation was carried out with the initial value of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">S<sub>0</sub></span><span class="font4"> = 198.890, ∕<sub>0</sub> = 204, Λ<sub>0</sub> = 172, dan V<sub>0</sub> = 7.693. Then, iterations were carried out starting from t<sub>0</sub> = 0 month to t<sub>24</sub> = 24 months.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Prediction using Matlab Software obtained results where, in the next 24 months, vulnerable individuals (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">S),</span><span class="font4"> infected individuals (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">1)</span><span class="font4">, and recovered individuals (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">R</span><span class="font4">) no longer exist, and vaccinated individuals amounted to 197,490 people. It means that the subpopulations (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">S</span><span class="font4">), (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">1</span><span class="font4">), and (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">R)</span><span class="font4"> will decrease slowly for the next 24 months while the population (</span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">V)</span><span class="font4"> will increase slowly for the next 24 months. So, it can be inferred that the spread of Covid-19 in Maluku Province is influenced by the vaccination rate.</span></p>
