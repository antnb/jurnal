<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstrak</span></h1>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kerja adalah sebuah kata yang menyatakan keadaan, tindakan. Hubungan antara predikat dengan argumennya disebut struktur semantis. Penelitian ini dilakukan untuk mengetahui struktur semantis kata kerja aksi, susunan konfigurasi kata kerja aksi dan peran semantis kata kerja aksi. Hasil dari penelitian ini adalah struktur semantis kata kerja aksi ditunjukkan oleh susunan konfigurasinya, kata kerja aksi mempunyai susunan konfigurasi sebagai kata kerja aksi, kata kerja aksi pengalaman, kata kerja aksi benefaktif, kata kerja aksi lokatif. Peran semantis kata kerja aksi adalah sebagai agent, experiencer, benefactive, object dan locative.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Kata Kunci: Aksi, semantis, kata kerja</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">A verb is a word that characteristically is the grammatical center of a predicate and expresses an act, occurrence, or mode of being. Semantic structures are basically a set of relationships between a central verb and a series of nouns. Semantically, the relation between the predicate and its arguments known as semantic role because each of its argument is assign a certain role by its predicate. This study exclusively analyzed the English Action Verbs. The Action Verbs have their own case frames configuration, semantic structure, semantic role, lexicalized, build in, and coreferential cases.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<h1><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Method</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The data of this study were taken from two novels, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">The Lightning Thief </span><span class="font2">written by</span><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rick_Riordan"><span class="font2"> Rick Riordan </span></a><span class="font2">(2005) and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Twilight</span><span class="font2"> By Stephenie Meyer (2005). Those novels provide data of the action verb in case grammar. The data source is chosen because it can fulfill the need of data that have relevance to the types of verbs according to the Case Grammar Matrix Model and there were the sources filled with many sentences connected with the English action verbs.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The data for this study were collected by using library research. The data also collected by reading the novel intensively, and then collecting the data in accordance with the parameters required, and searching for sentences in the data source that has only one single verb or one clause which then used as the data.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The data were analyzed by qualitative method. The method is used in order to explain the characteristics of the data and to find out the factor that stands behind the data itself. There are three techniques that are used to analyze the data: The first technique is identification. The relevant data were marked out. The second is classification. The collected data were classified in terms of the types of the verbs it involves, that is action verb. And then, sentences with the verbs which occur more than once eliminated from the data, because only one of them isused further. The third is analysis. The data were analyzed by using the Case Grammar Theory in order to identify the case frames of the verbs, to explain semantic structures of the verbs and also to identify the semantic roles of the verbs.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Result and Discussion</span></h1>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font2">5.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Semantic Structure of Action Verb</span></p></li></ul></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">She</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">closed</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">her eyes</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-O] (Riordan, 2005: 360)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">She</span><span class="font2"> sentence above expresses Agent case, as it is the animate doers of the activity. Syntactically, the Agent cases on the sentence above takes the function of</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">subject. The objects of the sentence which is also the Object case of the verbs’ case frame is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">her eyes.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Semantic Structure of Action Experiential Verb</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">He</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">told</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">us about the carvings on the sides</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-E-O]</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(Riordan, 2005: 5)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Tell</span><span class="font2"> is a communication verb which must be accompanied by three arguments. The first argument is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">He</span><span class="font2"> as the Agent case as this argument affects the occurrence of the experience which experienced by the Experiencer. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">About the carvings on the sides</span><span class="font2"> is the Object case and also the direct object of the sentence. It is the content of the experience. The experiential case is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">us</span><span class="font2"> as the one who undergoes the experience.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Semantic Structure of Action Benefactive Verb</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">Alice</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">bought</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">Edward new clothes</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-B-O]</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(Meyer, 2005: 248)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The Action Benefactive verb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">bought</span><span class="font2"> above involving three arguments. The first argument, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Alice</span><span class="font2"> functions as an Agent case, the one who did the activity or the cause of the gain. The second argument, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">new clothes</span><span class="font2"> is the Object Case of the verb, specifying the entity transferred. While the third argument, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Edward</span><span class="font2"> is the Benefactive case; the one who undergoes the gain.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.4 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Semantic Structure of Action Locative Verb</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">The headmaster</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">sent</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">my mom a letter</span><span class="font2"> the following week.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">+[-A-O-L] (Riordan,2005:19)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The action locative verb in the sentence above needs three arguments to accompany it. The first argument, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the headmaster</span><span class="font2">, which takes the function of the subject of the sentence, is the Agent case of the verb. A </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">letter</span><span class="font2"> is the Object case. It is the thing transferred or the thing that is changed its location by the Agent (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the headmaster</span><span class="font2">). The Location of the object is changed to a certain location, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">my mom </span><span class="font2">is the third argument as the Locative case.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.5 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Agent Case</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">In action verb analysis, the Agent Case is chosen as subject in the basic action verb, action experiential, action benefactive, and action locative.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">I</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">eat</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">hellhounds</span><span class="font2"> for breakfast. +[-A-O] (Riordan, 2005:139)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">I</span><span class="font2"> expresses agent case, as it is the animate doer of the action. The verb in this sentence, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">eat</span><span class="font2"> must be accompanied by an object. The object of the sentence is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Hellhounds</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The agent case is also as the subject in the Action Experiential Verb.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">Mr.D</span><span class="font2"> would </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">show</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">me worse things</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-E-O] (Riordan, 2005:76)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Show</span><span class="font2"> is the action experiential verb. It must be accompanied by the Agent Case. The Agent Case is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Mr.D</span><span class="font2">. This action verb also need object case. W</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">orse things</span><span class="font2"> indicate the object cases.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The Agent Case as the subject in Benefactive Verb shown in following example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">Mrs.Dodds</span><span class="font2"> would </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">give</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">me the evil eye.</span><span class="font2"> +[-A-B-O] (Riordan, 2005:5)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Mrs.Dodds</span><span class="font2"> functions as Agent, someone who did something. The verb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">give</span><span class="font2"> is action verb because it needs Agent and also the Object. The object is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the evil eye</span><span class="font2">. In addition, the Benefactive functions take by the nominal </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">me</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The locative case in the action verb also needs an agent as the subject.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">She</span><span class="font2">'d </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">send</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">me</span><span class="font2"> right back </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">to Yancy</span><span class="font2">. +[_A-O-L] (Riordan, 2005:10)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The Agent Case in the Action Locative Verb takes position as the subject of the sentence. The Agent case is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">She</span><span class="font2">. The object case is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">me</span><span class="font2">. The locative which specifies the location of an object is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">to Yancy</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.6 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Experiencer Case</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">I</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">spoke</span><span class="font2"> to </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">my brothers about it</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-E-O]</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(Meyer, 2005: 126)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The experiencer case cannot as subject in the example above. It accompanied by an agent and an object. The agent in the sentences above is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">I</span><span class="font2">. The verbs </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">spoke</span><span class="font2"> is Action Verb and this verb must be accompanied by the noun </span><span class="font1">162</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">which express the experiencer. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">My brothers</span><span class="font2"> is the experiencer which undergoes the psychological experience. In addition, the object as the content or stimulus for the experience is taken by </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">about it</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.7 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Benefactive Case</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">He</span><span class="font2"> could </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">give</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">me some pointers</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-B-O]</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(Meyer, 2005: 20)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In the example above, the nominal </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">He</span><span class="font2"> functions as the agent which has caused of the lost a respect to given object. The object is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">some pointers</span><span class="font2">. The benefactive noun which function as the undergoer of the gain something is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">me</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.8 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Object Case</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">In the Action Verb, the object case can occur with the basic action verb, experiential verb, benefactive verb, and also locative verb.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In the basic Action Verb, the object case accompanied with the agent case. In addition, the agent case as the subject.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">I</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">touched</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">its dorsal fin</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-O] (Riordan,2005:280)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In the sentence above, its </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">dorsal fin</span><span class="font2"> is the object. The object case in the action verb analysis always needs an agent. The agent case is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">I</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In the action experiential verb, the object cannot occur as the subject. the Object case may occur with the accompany of the agent and experiencer case.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">He</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">reminded</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">me</span><span class="font2"> of </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">a beach comber from Key West</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-E-O]</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(Riordan,2005:353)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The object case in the action experiential verb specifies the content for the experience or stimulus for the experience. The object is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">a beachcomber from Key West.</span><span class="font2"> The Agent in the action verb takes function as the subject and manifested by animate noun. The agent is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">He</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Me</span><span class="font2"> as the experiencer that specifies the undergoer of a psychological event of sensation, emotion, or cognition.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The object case also may occur with the benefactive verb in action verb</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">analysis. The object specifies the object which gained or loss.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">She</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">gave</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">Grover a nudge</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-B-O] (Riordan,2005:295)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The object case in the action benefactive verb always needs the accompany of the agent and the benefactive case. The object case is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">a nudge</span><span class="font2">. The agent is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">She</span><span class="font2">. The benefactive that undergoer of the gain is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Grover</span><span class="font2"> who gains the object transferred.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The object case takes the position in the middle of case as agent and locative case in the action locative verb. The object case in the action locative verb specifies the object in allocation, or undergoing change of position.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">Grover</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">took</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">his reed pipes out of his pocket</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-O-L]</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(Riordan,2005: 198)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">His reed pipes</span><span class="font2"> is the object of the case. The object case in the sentence above specifies the change of location. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Grover</span><span class="font2"> as the agent and also the subject of the sentence. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Out of his pocket</span><span class="font2"> is the locative case which specifies the change of location.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.9 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Locative Case</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">Grover</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">put</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">his hand on my shoulder</span><span class="font2">. +[-A-O-L]</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(Riordan,2005: 329)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The action locative verb needs the accompany of agent and object case. The agent of the sentence is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Grover</span><span class="font2">, the agent which specifies the instigator of the action. The object of the sentence is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">his hand</span><span class="font2">. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">On my shoulder</span><span class="font2"> indicates as the locative case which specifies the change of position.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.10 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Lexicalized Case</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">I</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">answered</span><span class="font2"> automatically.+[A-*O/O –lex] (Meyer,2005: 57) It means: I answered with an answer. It implies that the Agent said the answer. Therefore, the Object case is lexicalized into the verb.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.11 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Build in Case</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">He</span><span class="font2"> was </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">wearing</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">blue jeans</span><span class="font2">. +[A-O*L/L build in]</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">(Riordan,2005: 63)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">It means: He was wearing blue jeans on his body. We do not need to mention </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">on his body</span><span class="font2"> because the verb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">wearing</span><span class="font2"> already explain that we used on our body. So without using on his body, the meaning can be understood because it is impossible to say that we wear jeans on our nose (for example). Therefore, the locative is build in.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">5.12 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Coreferential Cases</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">For example: </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">I</span><span class="font2">'ll </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">get</span><span class="font2"> </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">your bag</span><span class="font2">. +[A-*B-O/A=B] </span><span class="font1">(Meyer,2005: 18)</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">It means:This is an example of the Agent case which is coreferential with the Benefactive case. Besides becomes the cause of the gain (Agent case), </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">I</span><span class="font2"> is also the one who undergoes the gain with respect to a given object (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">bag</span><span class="font2">, as the Object case).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">It can be concluded that the semantic structures of the English Action Verbs are shown by the case frame configuration as Basic action Verb [-A-O], Action Experiential Verb [-A-E-O], Action Benefactive Verb [-A-B-O], Action Locative Verb [-A-O-L]. The English Action Verbs have the case frame configurations as basic action verb, action experiential, action benefactive, and action locative. The semantic roles of the Action Verbs are agent, experience, benefactive, locative, and object.</span></p>
