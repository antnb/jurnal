<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ABSTRAK</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Tulisan ini berjudul </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“Cohesive Devices Used by Jay Gatsby in “The Great Gatsby” Movie Script” </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">yang bertujuan untuk mengidentifikasi jenis kohesi dan fungsinya yang digunakan oleh Jay Gatsby dalam naskah film “The Great Gatsby”. Sumber data diambil dari situs </span><a href="http://www.springfield.co.uk"><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">www.springfield.co.uk</span></a><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">. Penelitian ini menggunakan metode kualitatif dalam menganalisis data dan metode deskriptif dalam penyajian data. Dialog dari Jay Gatsby yang mengandung kohesi dikumpulkan dan dianalisis menggunakan teori “Cohesion in English”karangan Halliday dan Hasan (1976) dan “Introduction to Discourse Studies” karangan Renkema (2004). Hasil dari penelitian ini menunjukan bahwa terdapat 67 kohesi yang digunakan Jay Gatsby dalam naskah film “The Great Gatsby”. Kohesi grammatical merupakan yang paling banyak ditemukan dan jenis kohesi yang tidak ditemukan dalam dialog Jay Gatsby adalah leksikal kohesi general word dan hiponimi. Fungsi dari penggunaan leksikal kohesi adalah pemberi harmonisasi pada text sekaligus pengikat agar text tersebut memiliki makna.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci: kohesi gramatikal, kohesi leksikal, naskah film.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Discourse is the study of language above sentence. The highest unit of language in discourse analysis is text, and language is studied in its context. The connection of the sentences is connected with cohesive device. Cohesive devices are used to establish the links of the ideas. Halliday and Hasan (1976) described text connectedness in term of reference, substitution, ellipsis, conjunction and lexical cohesion.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">In this study, film script was used as the data source. According to Manfred (2003) a film is a multimedia narrative form based on a physical record of sounds and moving pictures. The movie that was chosen as the data source is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“The Great Gatsby” </span><span class="font1">2013. There are several reasons to choose this film script as the data source. This film script was chosen because it has a sufficient number of cohesion and the script is </span><span class="font0">139</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">feasible because cohesive devices exist in the dialogue of Jay Gatsby as the main character, although the types are different from each other. In addition, the contents of the script fulfill the requirements of the text.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<li>

<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>5.1 . The Analysis of Grammatical Cohesion Used by Jay Gatsby in </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“The Great Gatsby”</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"> Movie Script</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The data presentation of this part is divided into four categories: reference, substitution, ellipsis and conjunction.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>5.1.1 . Reference</span></h1>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.1.1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Personal Reference</span></p></li></ul></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Ewing </span><span class="font1">is a funny genius, </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">he </span><span class="font1">can play anything.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above, there is anaphoric personal reference from the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“he”.</span><span class="font1"> The presupposed item is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“Ewing”</span><span class="font1"> in the preceding text. The relation made from this reference is anaphora since it refers back to the text for its interpretation. The function of this reference is to indicate personal pronoun </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“he”.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.1.2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Demonstrative Reference</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">TOM: &nbsp;&nbsp;Oh yes, I understand that you went to </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Oxford</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;Uh yes, I went </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">there</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above there is a demonstrative reference, that is, the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“there”.</span><span class="font1"> The presupposed item is</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“Oxford”</span><span class="font1"> in the preceding sentence. The relation made from this reference is anaphoric since it refers back to the text for its interpretation. This demonstrative reference is called adverbial demonstrative, in which</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“there”</span><span class="font1"> is used to indicate the location or place.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.1.3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Comparative Reference</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;You must know the faces of </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">many </span><span class="font1">people you've heard of.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above, there is comparative reference, that is, the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“many”.</span><span class="font1"> The comparative reference word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“many”</span><span class="font1"> is adjective functioning in the nominal group as numerative. The function of this reference is to indicate </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“more than one people”.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>5.1.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Substitution</span></h1>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.2.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Nominal Substitution</span></p></li></ul></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;If it wasn't for the mist, we could see </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">the green light</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">DAISY : &nbsp;&nbsp;What green light?</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;The </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">one </span><span class="font1">that burns all night at the end of your dock.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above there is nominal substitution, that is, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“one”.</span><span class="font1"> The presupposed item is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“the green light”</span><span class="font1"> in the preceding text. The function of this kind of cohesion is to substitute the noun </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“the green light”</span><span class="font1"> to avoid its repetition.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.2.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Verbal Substitution</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">NICK : &nbsp;&nbsp;Well, I'll </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">call you up.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Please </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">do</span><span class="font1">, old sport. Please do. I suppose that Daisy will call too.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above, there is verbal substitution, that is, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“do”.</span><span class="font1"> The presupposed item is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“call you up”</span><span class="font1"> in the preceding text. The relation made from this</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">substitution is anaphora since it refers back to the text for its interpretation. The function of this substitution is to indicate the verbal substitution </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“do”</span><span class="font1"> and to avoid repetition.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.2.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Clausal Substitution</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;You think it's too much?</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">NICK : I think </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">it's what you want.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;I think </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">so </span><span class="font1">too.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above, there is a clausal substitution, that is, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“so”.</span><span class="font1"> The presupposed item is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“it’s what you want”</span><span class="font1"> from Nick’s utterances. The relation made from this substitution is anaphoric since it refers back to the text for its interpretation. The function of clausal substitution is to replace a previous clause </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“it’s what you want” </span><span class="font1">in Nick’s utterances to avoid repetition.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark24"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark25"></a>5.1.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ellipsis</span></h1>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.3.1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Nominal Ellipsis</span></p></li></ul></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;Your face is familiar. Weren't you the 3rd Division during the war?</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">NICK: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Uh, yes, the 9<sup>th</sup> </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">battalion</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;I was in the </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">7th</span><span class="font1">. Excuse me. I knew you looked familiar. Having a good</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">time, old sport?</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above, there is nominal ellipsis, that is, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“7<sup>th</sup>”.</span><span class="font1"> The presupposed item is “</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">battalion</span><span class="font1">” in the preceding sentence. The function of ellipsis is to omit the word that has been mentioned specifically to avoid repetition. In this sentence,</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“7<sup>th</sup>”</span><span class="font1"> is enumerative, standing for </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“7<sup>th</sup>”</span><span class="font1">battalion.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.3.2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Verbal Ellipsis</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">TOM : &nbsp;&nbsp;You've been seeing him... for five years?</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;No, no. No, not seen. Not seen, we </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">couldn't </span><span class="font1">but... both of us loved each</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">other all that time, didn't we?</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above there is verbal ellipsis from the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“couldn’t”.</span><span class="font1"> The omitted words are </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“have seen”,</span><span class="font1"> indicating </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“we couldn’t”</span><span class="font1"> standing for </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“we couldn’t have seen”</span><span class="font1">. The function of this ellipsis is to avoid repetition.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.3.4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Clausal Ellipsis</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">TOM &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: &nbsp;&nbsp;Nope, plenty o' gas. Well, if I run out, I'll stop at the drugstore. I hear you</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">can buy anything at a drugstore nowadays.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">DAISY &nbsp;: &nbsp;&nbsp;You take Nick and Jordan.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;I suppose you </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">can</span><span class="font1">, yes.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above, there is clausal ellipsis from the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“can”.</span><span class="font1"> The omitted word in Tom’s utterance is the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“buy anything at a drug store nowadays”. </span><span class="font1">It indicates that the word “you can” stand for </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“you can buy anything at a drugstore nowadays”.</span><span class="font1"> The function of this ellipsis is to avoid repetition of a clause.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark26"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark27"></a>5.1.4 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conjunction</span></h1>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.4.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Additive Conjunction</span></p></li></ul></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;No, old sport. No, you see... she never loved you. She only married you</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">because I was poor </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">and </span><span class="font1">she was tired of waiting, it was a terrible, terrible mistake, but in her heart, in her heart she never loved any one but me.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above there is the additive conjunction, that is, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“and”</span><span class="font1"> in the second sentence “I was poor </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and</span><span class="font1"> she was tired of waiting”. The function of this conjunction is to add new information to the statement or fact that has been given previously.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.4.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adversative Conjunction</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;I will tell you God's truth. God's truth about myself. I am the son of some</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">very wealthy people from the Middle West. Sadly, all of them are dead now. I was brought up an American, </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">but </span><span class="font1">educated at Oxford because all my ancestors had been educated there for many years, you see it's a family tradition.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above, there is an adversative conjunction, that is, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“but”</span><span class="font1"> in the fifth sentence. The function of this adversative conjunction is to indicate contrast between the old information “I was brought up an American” and the new one “educated at Oxford because all my ancestors had been educated there for many years, you see it's a family tradition”.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.4.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Temporal Conjunction</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;No, I... You see, </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">after </span><span class="font1">we left New York, she was... she was very</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">nervous, she thought... that driving would steady her. But... this woman, she rushed out at us. It all happened so quickly. It wasn't her fault, Daisy. No one must know that Daisy was driving. Promise me.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above, there is a temporal conjunction, that is, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“after”. “After” </span><span class="font1">in this sentence relates two clauses in terms of timing of their occurrence. The function of the temporal conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“after”</span><span class="font1"> is to indicate the sequence of the time of “what happened after they left New York”.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.1.4.4 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Causal Conjunction</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;No, old sport. No, you see... she never loved you. She only married you</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">because </span><span class="font1">I was poor and she was tired of waiting, it was a terrible, terrible mistake, but in her heart, in her heart she never loved any one but me.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above, there is a causal conjunction, that is, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“because”</span><span class="font1"> in the third sentence. The function of the causal conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“because”</span><span class="font1"> in this sentence connects new information to what has already been given in terms of cause and effect relationship. The effect of “she only married you” is the result of “I was poor and she was tired of waiting”.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark28"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark29"></a>5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Analysis of Lexical Cohesion Used by Jay Gatsby in </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“The Great Gatsby” </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Movie Script</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The analysis of cohesive device in </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“The Great Gatsby”</span><span class="font1"> movie script is classified in accordance with the types of lexical cohesion. The data presentation is divided into two categories: reiteration and collocation.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.2.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Reiteration</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Data 1</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;Excuse me. I can't talk now, </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">old sport</span><span class="font1">. You must know what a </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">small</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">town </span><span class="font1">it is. Listen to me! Listen to me! They said a </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">small town</span><span class="font1">. Listen to me. He is of no use to us if Detroit is his idea of a </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">small town</span><span class="font1">, you understand? We'll chat later, </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">old sport</span><span class="font1">. All right.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above, there is reiterated item by the use of the words </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“small town”</span><span class="font1"> that are repeated three times and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“old sport”</span><span class="font1"> that is repeated twice. In the text above, the words “small town” that are repeated are preceded by the definite article “a”. The function of this cohesion is to indicate repetition and build cohesiveness between the sentences.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Data 2</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;But of course, you don't need to take my word for it, old sport. At lunch</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">today, I'm going to introduce you with one of New York's most distinguished businessmen, in Mr. Meyer Wolfsheim. My good friend. And he will </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">confirm </span><span class="font1">all I have told you, </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">and vouch </span><span class="font1">for my good character.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above there is a reiteration through the use of synonymy to the words </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“confirm”</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“vouch”.</span><span class="font1"> The function of this cohesion is to express two words that have the same meaning in the sentence using a different word. The reiterated words in this sentence indicate synonymy between </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“confirm”</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“vouch”</span><span class="font1"> which have the same meaning “give assurance”.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">5.2.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Collocation</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;I have a man in England who buys me clothes.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">DAISY : &nbsp;&nbsp;I've never seen anything like this.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">GATSBY: &nbsp;&nbsp;Something for the lady. He sends over a selection at the beginning of</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">each season. This is </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">silk</span><span class="font1">. This is </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">flannels</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">From the data presented above there is a collocation; they are </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">clothes, silk</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">flannel</span><span class="font1">. The type of collocation that occurs in the data above is inclusive collocation since the words include the limit specified. The word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“clothes”</span><span class="font1"> is inclusive to the words </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">silk</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">flannel</span><span class="font1">; they refer to the type of clothes. The function of this collocation is to make the bond of meaning from the sentences in the text so they have the same reference meaning.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark30"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark31"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Based on the analysis above and discussion of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“Cohesive Device Used by Jay Gatsby in “The Great Gatsby” Movie Script”</span><span class="font1">, the total number of cohesion found in the utterances expressed by Jay Gatsby as the main character in “The Great Gatsby” movie script is 67 ties of cohesion, consisting of 35 ties of reference, 4 ties of substitution, 4 ties of ellipsis, 21 ties of conjunction, 1 tie of lexical cohesion reiteration synonymy, 1 tie of lexical cohesion reiteration repetition and 1 tie of lexical cohesion collocation. The cohesive chains that were not found in the data source are lexical cohesion in terms of super ordinate and general word. The function of the grammatical cohesions are to indicate reference pronoun, addition, time order, spatial order, concession in argument, cause and effect, comparison, and contrast. The function of lexical cohesion is to build cohesiveness between the sentences and to make the bond of meaning from the sentences in the text so they have the same reference meaning.</span></p>
