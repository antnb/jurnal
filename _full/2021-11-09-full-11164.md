<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font11" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h2>
<p><span class="font11">In order to analyze and to study expressions of the Al-tolerant genes, we have examined five clone genes that were isolated from soybean cv. Lumut. Soybean cv. Lumut and Slamet, </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">Centrocema pubescens, Pennisetum purpureum</span><span class="font11"> and </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">Escherichia coli</span><span class="font11"> were selected for futher analysis. Based on the DNA sequencing, searching enzyme restriction sites and searching DNA homology with the genebank database; the clones encoding: (1) Catalase (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gmali</span><span class="font11">12, that function as an antioxidant), (2) Proliferating cell nuclear antigen like protein/PCNALP (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gmali</span><span class="font11">15, that involved as one of transcriptional regulator in the eucaryotic cell cycle), (3) Growth hormone (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gmali</span><span class="font11">22, this gene may play a role on stimulation of cell growth/development), (4) Amine oxidase (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gm</span><span class="font11">AO, genebank accession number AF313622, a gene that function as amine oxidation and/or antioxidant), and (5) Aminoacyl peptidase (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gm</span><span class="font11">AP, genebank accession number AF091304, a serine protease gene). Expressions of the clone genes either on forage crops or </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">Escherichia coli</span><span class="font11"> indicated that all of the clones are basic genes, but its expression increased with aluminium induction (Al-induced genes) and involved in detoxification to Al stress. From this research, we also found similar responses between oxidative stress and Al stress to gene responses.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font11" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font11">Aluminum (Al) is regarded as one of the main toxic factors which exist in most acidic soil in Indonesia (Notohadiprawiro, 1983), even of the world, comprising 1x10<sup>9</sup> hectares in the tropical and cool temperature regions (Van Wambeke, 1976) or approximately 8% by Weight (Moller </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font11">., 1984). Most Al in soil is insoluble, associated with complex aluminosilicates and oxides. However, under acidic soil condition (pH &lt;&nbsp;5) Al is converted from insoluble forms into soluble Al <sup>+3</sup> (Marschner, 1991; Driscoll and Schecher, 1990; and Kinraide, 1991), which block growth of plant roots (Rajaram and Villegus, 1990; Kinraide and Ryan, 1991; Foy </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font11">., 1978; Wagatsuma </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font11">., 1987; and Taylor, 1991). Thus, Al toxicity is one of the most important soil problems that limits plant growth, particularly in the tropical regions.</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">Approximately 40% of the world’s arable soils are too acid, and Indonesia has over 47,6 millions hectares. A problem that is becoming increasingly severe, because of the use of nitrogenous fertilizer, industrial pollution and acid rain (Van breeman, 1985). Eventhough, normal rainfall can also cause acidification of soils by promoting the leaching of basic cations such as Ca<sup>+2</sup>, Mg<sup>+2</sup>, K<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> (Foy, 1984). Thus, Al toxicity is one of the most important soil problems that limits plant growth, particularly in the tropical regions (Kochian, 1995; Taylor, 1995; Matsumoto, 2000).</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">Identifying genetic resistance to aluminium toxicity would be a valuable contribution toward the development of tolerant crops in the tropical areas,</span></p>
<p><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">Soybean, Forage, E. coli</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">especially in Indonesia. In these low pH aluminosilicate soils, the susceptibility of field crops to aluminium toxicity leads to the inhibition of root growth into the lower soil horison. Aluminium saturates the charged sites of the soil particle and, along with the restriction of root growth, acts to impede cation exchange with subsoil elements (Ca<sup>+2</sup>, K<sup>+</sup>, and Mg<sup>+2</sup>), which are critical for normal plant development. Determining the molecular basis of tolerance to increase levels of aluminium in certain crops (such as soybean) poses a significant challenge.</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">Soybean is one of important crops in Indonesia. Its specific material for Indonesian tradisional food such as tempe, tofu, sauce and soybean milk have brought the soybean to an important position in Indonesian nutrition. Demand for soybean is increasing with the increase on protein need due to improvement program on Indonesian nutrition. The development of animal husbandry in Indonesia have also increased the demand of this crop. Unfortunately, the increase in demand for soybean can not be responded by the sufficient increase in production of this crop. This research was conducted to support soybean breeding programs by molecular approach. Considering the importance of molecular information on soybean tolerance to Al, we proposed the research on molecular biology of soybean tolerance to al stress as follow-through from previous research, by two approach: (1) Analysis of the Al-tolerant and (2) Study of expression of the cloned genes. The genes also have been evaluated to forage crop by northern/slot blot hybridization (heterologous approach) and </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">E. coli</span><span class="font11">.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font11" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>RESEARCH METHOD</span></h2>
<p><span class="font11">The research consist of two programs: Research I (Analysis of Al-tolerant genes) and Research II (Study expression of Al-tolerant genes).</span></p>
<p><span class="font11" style="font-weight:bold;">Research 1</span><span class="font11">. There are 3 steps in this program: (a) Analysis of clone genes by nucleotide sequencing, (b) Analysis of clone genes by searching restriction enzyme sites and (c) Analysis of clone genes by searching homology with GeneBank database.</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">DNA synthesis for chain-termination sequencing is carried out two steps. In the first, the primed strand of DNA is extended and at the same time labelled by the incorporation of dye-nucleotide. . In the second step, dideoxynucleotides are added to the population of labelled DNA molecules (ranging in length from a few to many hundreds of nucleotides) and synthesis continues until a ddNTP is incorporated, thus terminating the chains.</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">Analysis of clone genes by nucleotide sequencing was started with cDNAs cloned from our previous study that is not analyzed yet (Anwar, 1999). Plasmid cDNAs cloned are prepared using the alkaline lysis method (Sambrook </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font11"> 1989). The selected cDNA clones was sequenced by dideoxynucleotide chaintermination method (Sanger </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font11"> 1977).</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">Analysis of clone genes by searching restriction enzyme sites using the amino acid and restriction enzyme sites software that have been developed by DCRG-team database, which provided information about analysis of DNA especially for searching of restriction enzyme sites, start and stop codon, amino acid sequence, including number of ATGC and amino acid.</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">Analysis of clone genes by searching homology with GeneBank database. The resulted cDNA sequences are then compared to the existing genes sequences in Genebank. First, we access to the NCBI (National Center for Biotechnology Information) website (http:// </span><a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov"><span class="font11">www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov</span></a><span class="font11">), and then select GeneBank database for searching similarity/homology sites for nucleotide sequence (BLAST program/BLAST web). Finally, follow instruction provided in the web electronic guide till resulted kinds of the genes.</span></p>
<p><span class="font11" style="font-weight:bold;">In Research II</span><span class="font11">, expression of the cloned genes have been studied by (a) using mRNA analysis by northern/slot blot hybridization method both on soybean and forage crop and (b) </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">Escherichia coli’s </span><span class="font11">exposed to Al toxic level. There are 4 steps for analysis of transcript level/mRNA analysis i.e: (a) Planting material, (b) total RNA Isolation, (c) probe preparation and (d) northern/slot blot hybridization.</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">Planting material was planted described by Anwar (1999). Total RNA was isolated from the root tips (</span><span class="font9">± </span><span class="font11">5 mm) and/or leaf of soybean and forage crop treated and untreated with Al<sup>+3</sup>, using phenol/SDS method (Ausabel </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font11">., 1987).</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">Northern/Slot Blot Hybridization. Total RNA (10-15 µg) samples was denatured with glyoxal and DMSO, and followed incubation in 65<sup>o</sup>C for 15 minutes. Then,</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">the RNA was transferred to Hybond-N+ membranes (Amersham) by Slot-Hybridization (prior to use, the slot must be cleaned with 0.1 N NaOH and washed by steril water- DEPC treated). Probes was prepared from cDNA inserts isolated from agarose gels and labelled by non radioactive system (ECL-system). Hybridization was performed as described in Virca </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font11"> (1990). The filter was washed twice with 2xSSC+0.4%SDS for 10 min at 42<sup>o</sup>C, and twice with 2xSSC for 5 min at room temperature. Filter was stripped by immersion in warm (60<sup>o</sup>C) 0.1% SDS and reprobed up to three times as described by Sambrook </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font11"> (1989).</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">For Expression of clone genes by identifying tolerant-genes with its expression on </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">Escherichia coli</span><span class="font11"> to Al toxic level was implemented by addition of Al toxic level on LB (Luria Bertani) culture (2% bactotryptone, 0.5% yeast extract, 10 mM NaCl and 1% bactoagar. First of all, to set up assay for Al stress, </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">E. coli</span><span class="font11"> and </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">E. coli</span><span class="font11"> containing vector was cultured in LB with various Al treatment (0-500 ppm). Assay for Al-toxic level based on the reduction of </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">E. coli’s </span><span class="font11">growth on media at least 75% from control (without Al). Secondly, all of the clones was cultured at LB plate with addition of Al-toxic level based on previous study for 2 days. The clones that involved to Al tolerance was indicated by </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">E. coli</span><span class="font11"> (contained the clones) growing well in the selected media.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font11" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Expression of Al-tolerant Genes</span></h2>
<p><span class="font11">Plants show spesific responses to many kinds of stress (biotic and abiotic) including aluminium stress. Genes response to Al stress will be reflected by increasing transcription (production mRNA) level of one or more genes. The molecular basis of these responses has not been completely worked out but there are clear examples of the expressions of many induced genes by aluminium stress.</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">Based on slot blot analysis (Figure 6), all of the genes are basic genes (appear at all of control media/media pH 6.0 without Al) , but its expression increased with Al stress (media pH 4.0 with Al stress). Clone g</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">mali</span><span class="font11">12 is coding catalase which involved as antioxidant. This result indicated that genes response to Al stress is similar with oxidation stress responses. This novel information is useful for genetic engineering. Similar result from the genes are expressed on </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">Escherichia coli.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font11" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>CONCLUSIONS</span></h2>
<p><span class="font11">From this research, we concluded that nucleotide and sequencing of Al-tolerant genes are coding: (1) Catalase (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gmali</span><span class="font11">12, that function as an antioxidant), (2)</span></p>
</div><br clear="all">
<div>
<p><span class="font11">Proliferating cell nuclear antigen like protein (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gmali</span><span class="font11">15, that involved as one of tanscriptional regulator in the eucaryotic cell cycle), (3) Growth hormone (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gmali</span><span class="font11">22), (4) Amine oxidase (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gm</span><span class="font11">AO, genebank accession number AF313622), and (5) Aminoacyl peptidase (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gm</span><span class="font11">AP, genebank accession number AF091304)</span></p>
<p><span class="font11">Expression of Al-tolerant genes (</span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gmali</span><span class="font11">12, </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gmali</span><span class="font11">15, </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gmali</span><span class="font11">22, </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gm</span><span class="font11">AO, and </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">gm</span><span class="font11">AP) either on plant or </span><span class="font11" style="font-style:italic;">Escherichia coli</span><span class="font11">, indicated that all of the clones are genes response to Al-induction and involved in detoxification to Al-stress.</span></p>
