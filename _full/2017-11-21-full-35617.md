<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstract</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">This study has two aims, those are to analyze: (1) the classification of phrasal verbs found in a short story and the classification of their translations in Indonesian done by some English teachers from some primary schools in Denpasar, and (2) how the meaning of phrasal verbs in SL help to determine their equivalents in TL. The classification and the meaning of phrasal verbs are proposed by McArthur and Atkins (1975). Based on the analysis there were three categories of 34 phrasal verbs found in the data source, those are: intransitive verbs, transitive separable verbs, and transitive fused verbs. The results found that most of the phrasal verbs are included into transitive separable verbs and from the three categories of phrasal verbs; they were translated mostly into transitive verbs. Meanwhile, the meanings of phrasal verbs in SL determine the suitable equivalents for the phrasal verbs in TL since there were variants equivalents given in the translations.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Keywords</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">: Phrasal verbs, Translation, Words meaning</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>I INTRODUCTION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">Multi words expression, and especially phrasal verbs, can assess the level of English language proficiency. But it can be difficult to both understand and remember for the non-English speaker. Phrasal verbs are used by all native English speakers with great frequency because the verbs are colloquial. They are used casually, in everyday speech, or in order to express vivid, emotional and frequently slangy points, to conjure up special metaphoric relationships and jokes and to label actions in such daily activity. Phrasal verbs are the combinations of simple monosyllabic verbs (put, take, get, etc) and members of a set of particles (on, up, out, etc) (McArthur and Atkins, 1975). There is no universal definition of phrasal verbs , but some linguists qualify phrasal verbs as a combination of a verb and a preposition or an adverbial particle whereas other only consider a phrasal verb as a verb followed by an adverbial particle (Darwin and Gray, (1999); Sawyer, (2000) in Riquel (2014)). The combination is nowadays called phrasal because, on paper, it represents the appearance of a two – word phrase rather in a single item. Although it looks like a phrase, it functions in many respects like a single word, although under certain conditions other items (direct object, adverbs) can come between verbs and particle (McArthur and Atkins, 1975:iii). Since phrasal verbs derive from verbs and their particles that followed, the classification of phrasal verbs also can be classified into verbs classification: intransitive and transitive verb. Transitive verbs are next divided into separable and fused verbs. (pg. v)</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The meaning of phrasal verbs, typically, is not obvious from the meanings of the individual words themselves. But some of them may emphasize the meaning of the verb that builds them. This is important to understand, since translation is also about analyzing the meanings of words as state by Larson (1998) in Hatim and Munday (2004) ‘a process which begins with the ST, analyses this text into semantic structure, and then restructures this semantic structure into appropriate receptor language forms in order to create an equivalent receptor language text’. Moreover, Nida (1969) mentioned that the meaning of a word or phrase from source language which is translated into the target language is very important to be analyzed in order to have the message which is delivered clearly understood.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Translation focuses in the process of translating and the product of translation as stated by Shuttleworth and Cowie (1997) in Hatim and Munday (2004:3) for the definition of translation. Catford (1965) suggested the theory that can be used to analyze the product of translation is the equivalence theory, which is divided into two parts: textual equivalent and formal correspondence.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The product of translation that discussed here was the translation of phrasal verbs into Indonesian from some primary English teachers. The analysis of classification of phrasal verbs found in the source text with the classification of their translations and the meanings of phrasal verbs to determine their equivalents, which used the theory proposed by McArthur and Atkins (1975), are the main discussions of this study.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>II MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">The data were taken from the translations made by some primary schools English teacher who translated a short story entitle moles taken from internet </span><a href="http://www.oxfordenglish.com.br/oxford-wp/index.php/phrasal-verb-story-moles/"><span class="font4">(</span><span class="font4" style="text-decoration:underline;">http://www.oxfordenglish.com.br/oxford-</span></a><span class="font4" style="text-decoration:underline;"></span><a href="http://www.oxfordenglish.com.br/oxford-wp/index.php/phrasal-verb-story-moles/"><span class="font4" style="text-decoration:underline;">wp/index.php/phrasal-verb-story-moles/</span><span class="font4">)</span></a><span class="font4"> that contained 34 phrasal verbs which then can be categorized in three categories: intransitive verbs, transitive separable verbs, and transitive fused verbs. There are two reasons why the translations of phrasal verbs from these teachers were used. Firstly, these teachers, which come from some private primary schools, are frequently used translating technique in teaching vocabularies to their students. This means that they can produce the product of translation, particularly for the translation of phrasal verbs. Secondly phrasal verbs have different meanings from the verbs that form them. Therefore, there must be variants of equivalents for the phrasal verbs found in the source text into target language.</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">The data were collected through the observation method and supported by the note – taking technique. First, the phrasal verbs found in the source text and their equivalents in target language were classified into their categories. Second, the data that have been noted-down were listed and analyzed the suitable equivalents based on the meaning of the phrasal verbs in SL. However, not all the data were analyzed here. To analyze the data, a descriptive qualitative method was used.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>III RESULTS AND DISCUSSION</span></h2>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">3.1 &nbsp;P</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">HRASAL </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">V</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">ERBS</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">According to McArthur and Atkins (1975) phrasal verbs are usually combination of simple, monosyllabic verbs and members of a set particles. They are called so because the combination presents the appearance of a two-word phrase rather than a single item. A phrasal verb also consists of adverb that modifies or changes the meaning </span><a href="https://www.usingenglish.com/glossary/phrasal-verb.html"><span class="font4">(</span><span class="font4" style="text-decoration:underline;">https://www.usingenglish.com/glossary/phrasal-verb.html</span><span class="font4">)</span></a><span class="font4">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">3.1.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;C</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">LASSIFICATION OF </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">P</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">HRASAL </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">V</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">ERBS</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">Since all verbs are initially classified into intransitive and transitive verbs, these categories are also applied for phrasal verbs (McArthur and Atkins, 1975: v). Intransitive means that the verbs do not need object, meanwhile transitive verbs need object. In Indonesian language, there are also these categories of verbs with the same characteristics: depend on having an object or not (Chulsum and Novia, 2006: 711).The position of the object in a transitive verb is then separated into two more categories: separable verbs and fused verbs (McArthur and Atkins, 1975: v).</span></p>
<p><span class="font4">Separable phrasal verb means that the object can be inserted between the verb and its particle or placed after the phrasal verbs, meanwhile in the fused verb; an object cannot be inserted between the verb and its particle, but must be placed after them.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">3.2 &nbsp;I</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">NTRANSITIVE </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">P</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">HRASAL </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">V</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">ERBS</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">Data 1</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">SL: Every time she </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">drops in</span><span class="font3">, he goes to great length to avoid talking to her</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">TL: a. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Setiap kali dia </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">mampir</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">, Ronnie selalu menghindarinya.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">b.</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Setiap dia </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">berkunjung</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">, anakku Ronnie selalu menjauh…….,</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font3">c.</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Setiap kali dia </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">masuk</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">, dia berusaha keras untuk tidak berbicara dengannya.</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">In this sentence, there is no object followed the phrasal verb, therefore the words </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">drops in </span><span class="font4">is included into category of intransitive verbs, since it does not require an object (McArthur and Atkins, 1975).The equivalents are also categorized as intransitive verb because they are not followed by objects after the verbs. The meaning of words </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">drops in </span><span class="font4">above is </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">visit casually </span><span class="font4">(McArthur and Atkins, 1975: 42) which then the closest equivalents from words given above are </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">mampir</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">berkunjung</span><span class="font4">, because these also have the sense of meaning visit casually.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Data 2:</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">SL: She’s got to </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">call round </span><span class="font3">her mother’s house to…..</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">TL: a. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">dia sudah </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">menelepon </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">ibunya….,</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">b.</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Dia mendapat </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">panggilan dari </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">ibunya,</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font3">c.</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Dia mendapat </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">telepon </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">dari ibunya</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">This phrasal verb is also included into intransitive verb because it is followed not by an object, but an adverb </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">her mother’ house</span><span class="font4">. Meanwhile the translations are divided into two word classes: a. </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">menelpon</span><span class="font4"> is the verb which included into transitive verbs and are followed by words ibunya which is consider as objects, b. </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">panggilan</span><span class="font4">, and c. </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">telepon</span><span class="font4"> are categorized as noun or they can be said as the objects of their verbs: </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">mendapat</span><span class="font4">. The meaning of this phrasal verb is </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">visit casually </span><span class="font4">(McArthur and Atkins, 1975: 20), but none of the equivalents above have the same meaning as in SL. Therefore, those are not the suitable equivalents for </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">call round</span><span class="font4">. It should be </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">mampir </span><span class="font4">or </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">berkunjung </span><span class="font4">as mentioned in data 1.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">3.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;T</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">RANSITIVE </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">S</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">EPARABLE </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">P</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">HRASAL </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">V</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">ERBS</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">Data 1</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">SL: If our client receives that bill, he’s going to </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">call off </span><span class="font3">the Zebra deal.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">TL: a</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">. Jika klien kita menerima tagihan itu, dia akan </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">membatalkan perjanjian </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">yang sudah disepakati,</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">b. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Jika klien kita menerima tagihan, dia akan </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">memanggil pengacara</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">.</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">Transitive Separable verbs mean those which, under certain conditions, may have an item inserted between the verb and particle (McArthur and Atkins, 2004: v). Therefore, the words </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">call off </span><span class="font4">is categorized as transitive separable verb because the object, in this case </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">Zebra Deal</span><span class="font4">, can be inserted between the verb </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">call </span><span class="font4">and particle </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">off </span><span class="font4">or can be put after the phrasal verb. Meanwhile the equivalents are </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">membatalkan</span><span class="font4"> which included in transitive verb since it followed by object </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">perjanjian</span><span class="font4">, and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">memanggil</span><span class="font4"> is included as transitive verbs as well because it is also followed by object: </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">pengacara</span><span class="font4">. This phrasal verb has meanings: </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">abandon </span><span class="font4">or </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">cancel </span><span class="font4">(McArthur and Atkins, 1975: 20). Then the suitable equivalent from words in TL above is </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">membatalkan </span><span class="font4">because it has the same meaning as in SL.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Data 2:</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">SL: It seems that someone has been making up story that our company is in financial trouble and </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">handing it out </span><span class="font3">to the Press</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">TL: a. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">……….dan </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">menyerahkannya </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">kepada pers, </span><span class="font3">b....</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">…….dan </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">menyampaikannya </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">kepada pers, </span><span class="font3">c. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">………dan </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">memberikannya </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">kepada pers</span></p>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">Handing it out </span><span class="font4">is a transitive separable where the object can be put after the phrasal verb or inserted between the verb and its particle (McArthur and Atkins, 1975:68). In this sentence, the object is </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">it</span><span class="font4">, and placed between the verb and its particle. The equivalents given are also as transitive verbs because followed by object </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">–nya</span><span class="font4">, which refers, the same as in SL, to </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">the story that has been made up</span><span class="font4">. The phrasal verb </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">handing out </span><span class="font4">has a function as figurative informal word with the meaning: </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">offer </span><span class="font4">or </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">dispense </span><span class="font4">(McArthur and Atkins, 1975: 68). In this sentence, </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">offer </span><span class="font4">is the perfect meaning. Then all words in TL above are close equivalents for this phrasal verb.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">3.4 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;T</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">RANSITIVE </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">F</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">USED </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">P</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">HRASAL VERBS</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font3">Data 1</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">SL: She wants you to </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">look after </span><span class="font3">the kids tonight</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">TL: a. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Dia ingin anda </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">menjaga </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">anak – anak malam ini,</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">b.</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Dia ingin anda untuk </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">mengurus </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">anak – anak malam ini,</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font3">c.</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Dia ingin anda </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">menengok </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">anak – anak malam ini</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">Look after </span><span class="font4">in the sentence above is a transitive fused verb, because the object is only able to put after the phrasal verbs. The translations given are also included in transitive verb with their objects: </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">anak – anak</span><span class="font4">. The meaning of phrasal verb above is </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">take care of </span><span class="font4">or </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">be temporarily responsible for </span><span class="font4">(McArthur and Atkins, 1975: 83). So, the suitable equivalents are </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">menjaga</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">mengurus</span><span class="font4">, instead of </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">menengok</span><span class="font4">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Data 2</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">SL: I </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">look forward to </span><span class="font3">seeing you at the usual place, Mr. Green</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">TL: a. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Saya </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">berharap bisa bertemu </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">dengan anda di tempat biasa, Mr. Green,</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font3">b.</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Saya </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">menunggu bertemu </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">dengan anda di tempat biasanya, Mr. Green, </span><span class="font3">c. </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Saya </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">akan menemui </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">anda di tempat biasa, Mr. Green</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font4">The phrasal verb above is a transitive fused verb as well. It cannot be inserted by the object in between the words. The object must be after the words. The translations are categorized as intransitive verbs for </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">berharap bisa bertemu</span><span class="font4"> and </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">menunggu bertemu</span><span class="font4">. But it differs for the last translation </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">menemui anda</span><span class="font4">, it is a transitive verb words with the object anda. Its meaning in SL is </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">anticipate eagerly </span><span class="font4">(McArthur and Atkins, 1975: 83). Therefore the suitable equivalent is </span><span class="font4" style="font-style:italic;">berharap bisa menemuimu</span><span class="font4">.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>IV CONCLUSION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font4">From the analysis that have been done, it can be concluded that there were three categories of 34 phrasal verbs found in the text source, those are (1) intransitive verbs, which need no objects that follow, (2) transitive separable verbs, which the object can be placed between or after the verbs and (3) transitive fused verb, with the object that must be placed after the verbs. The equivalents for these phrasal verbs were mostly translated into transitive verbs which require objects in the usage. There were many variants equivalents given in the translations. Many words which have the same sense of meaning were chosen to be the equivalents for the phrasal verbs found in the text source. But the suitable equivalents obviously can be determined from the meanings that the phrasal verbs in source language have. Those help to give the closest equivalents in the target language.</span></p>
