<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Penelitian ini berjudul Directive Illocutionary Acts Found In Breaking Dawn Novel. Data dalam penelitian ini diambil dari novel fiksi berjudul Breaking Dawn oleh Stephanie Meyer. Novel ini terpilih sebagai sumber data karena novel ini menarik untuk dianalisa dan merupakan novel terlaris Teori dari Bach dan Harnish digunakan untuk menganalisa jenis direktif yang ditemukan dalam Breaking Dawn novel. Hasil analisis menunjukkan bahwa ada beberapa jenis tindakan direktif digunakan oleh pembicara dalam mengarahkan seseorang untuk melakukan sesuatu.Masing-masing memiliki karakteristik sendiri.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Kata Kunci: direktif tindakan, konteks situasi, respon pendengar.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of The Study</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Language as a system of communication plays a crucial role in every aspect of human life. Through language we can express our ideas in our mind and confess our feelings in order to interact with other people or even society. When the speakers do not only produce utterance that contains of word and grammatical structure but also perform or expect actions through their utterance, it is called a Speech Act.The part that would be emphasized in this study is the illocutionary acts especially the directive illlocutionary acts. The speaker has directly performed an illocutionary acts if the intended act is recognized as such by the hearer, which is then communicated. For example, “It is very cold here”. The speaker might intend to describe the room situation, in this case the illocutionary force would be “describing”. He might also intend to request someone who sits</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">near the window or door to close it. This sentence might produce more than one illocution, either direct illocution or indirect ilocution.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark11"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark12"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Directive Illocutionary Acts Found in </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Breaking Dawn</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> Novel</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">There are a number of directives illocutionary acts which are found in the novel.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Directive Illocutionary Acts of Requirement</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Dialogue:</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Bella: </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“You’re going to stay away from her,”</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Jacob: “I can’t do that!”</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The analysis is as follows:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(i)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The belief that his utterance, in virtue of his authority over hearer, constitutes sufficient reason for hearer to act.</span><span class="font2"> The speaker (Bella) has the authority toward Jacob, in which he was the person whom Bella who used to love and now he is falling in love with her baby Renesmee. She uttered the utterance to command him, and believes that Jacob would follow the instruction given but regrettably he did not.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">(ii)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The intention that hearer do act because of speaker’s utterance.</span><span class="font2"> By uttering, “</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">You’re going to stay away from her,” </span><span class="font2">Bella wanted Jacob to do as what she was commanded. Additionally, Jacob did not take the action because of Bella’s utterance.</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Directive Illocutionary Acts of Prohibitive</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Dialogue:</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Edward: </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“You’re not going alone,”</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Bella: </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">They won’t hurt me,”</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">To ensure that it can be classified into type of prohibitive, the analysis as as follows:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">(i) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The belief that his utterance, in virtue of his authority over hearer, constitutes sufficient reason for hearer not to act.</span><span class="font2"> From the utterance above, it can be explained that the speaker (Edward) has really close relationship with the hearer (Bella). Regarding to the conversation, it can be said that Edward as Bella’s husband has authority toward her wife, in which he does not allow Bella to meet Volturi alone.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">(ii) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The intention that because of speaker’s utterance hearer not to do act. </span><span class="font2">In this case, Edward prohibited Bella not to go alone to find Volturi. However, Bella was convinced her husband to let her go alone.</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Directive Illocutionary Acts of Permissive</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Dialogue:</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Bella: ”Get some rest, please,”</span><span class="font2"> she went on. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“There’re a few beds upstairs, you’re welcome to any of them”</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Jacob: “Thanks, Bells, but I’d rather sleep on the ground. Away from the stench, you know.”</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The analysis is seen as follows:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(i)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The belief that his utterance, in virtue of his authority over hearer, entitles hearer to act.</span><span class="font2"> As the speaker, Bella expresses the belief that he has the authority toward Jacob to say this. She permitted Jacob to use any room upstairs that he wants to take a rest.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">(ii)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The intention that hearer believe that speaker’s utterance entitles him to act.</span><span class="font2"> In this case, Bella expresses her intention to allow Jacob to take a rest using upstairs room.</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.4 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Directive Illocutionary Act of Requestive</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Dialogue:</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Jacob: “Yeah. Bye, Jared. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Hey, tell my dad that I’m okay, will you? And that I’m sorry and that I love him”</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Jared: “I’ll pass that along</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The analysis can be seen as follows:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">(i)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The desire that hearer do act.</span><span class="font2"> In performing directive acts of requestive in data above, the speaker (Jacob) wants the hearer (Jared) to do something. In this case Jacob asked Jared to deliver his message to his father that he was really okay and miss him so much.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">(ii)</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The intention that hearer do act because of speaker’s desire.</span><span class="font2"> Jacob uttered the utterance above with intention that Jared will do something</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">based on what is being requested by Jacob, that is, to tell his father how much he miss him.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
