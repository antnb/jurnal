<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Five Japanese Synonymous Verbs </span><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">GO</span></h2>

<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">Abstract</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">The proper use of synonymous words is a problem that is often faced by foreign language learners. Native speakers of a language often experience difficulties when asked to explain the differences in synonymous words. They use their linguistic instincts to differentiate the use of these words, or what in Japanese is called kan</span><span class="font0"> 勘</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">. This paper raises the synonym of the verb 'go' which in Japanese has five words, namely omomuku</span><span class="font0"> 赴く</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">, irassharu</span><span class="font0"> いらっしゃる</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">, mairu</span><span class="font0"> 参る</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">, kaeru </span><span class="font0">帰る</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">, and iku</span><span class="font0"> 行く</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">. This paper uses a qualitative descriptive method. Synonyms of these verbs will be observed based on the frequency of their use in writing and oral languages in two Japanese corpora. In addition, the meaning components of these verbs are mapped to see the range of use of each of the verbs. Based on observations on the frequency of use, it is known that these five verbs have differences in the variety of languages used, namely: omomuku have the lowest frequency of use in both written and spoken registers. Meanwhile, based on observations on the mapping of meaning components, the five 'go' verbs also show differences, namely: iku is the verb with the most meaning components. Thus, it can be concluded that these five synonymous verbs 'go' cannot completely replace each other. There are some usage limitations in the presence of each of these verbs.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Introduction</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">Japanese learners often encounter difficulties when facing words that are synonymous in Japanese. The difficulty is regarding the proper use of each of those synonymous words. Sudaryanto (2015:70) explains that &quot;synonym&quot; means the same information, similar meaning, and different forms. Some previous studies regarding Japanese synonymous words has been conducted. Kato (n.d.), in her research titled &quot;Nihongo Kyouiku no Shomondai - Ruigigo Bunseki Shikoudoushi 'mezasu' to 'nerau' -&quot;, examined the directive verbs of Japanese, namely </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mezasu</span><span class="font5"> and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">nerau.</span><span class="font5"> Miyata (n.d.), in his research titled &quot;Gairaigo 'meritto' to sono ruigigo no imihikaku - shinbun wo shiryo to shite -&quot;, examined the meaning comparison of the loanword in Japanese, namely </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">meritto</span><span class="font5"> and its synonyms, namely </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">chousho, rieki,</span><span class="font5"> ad </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">riten</span><span class="font5">. The source of this study was the Asahi, Mainichi, and Yomiuri newspapers from 2003 to 2005. Miyata's study is a continuation of the previous Miyata and Tanaka Studies in 2006 that examined similar things, namely the loan-word </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">risuku</span><span class="font5"> and its synonyms, namely </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">kiken</span><span class="font5"> and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">kikensei</span><span class="font5">. Gumbira (2013), in her research entitled &quot;Analysis of the Meaning of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Omou</span><span class="font5"> and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Kangaeru</span><span class="font5"> Verbs as Synonyms&quot;, examined the synonymous form of Japanese verbs </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">omou</span><span class="font5"> and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">kangaeru</span><span class="font5"> that both can be translated into </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">berpikir</span><span class="font5"> in Indonesian. Gumbira's study (2013) aims to find out the differences and similarities of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">omou</span><span class="font5"> and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">kangaeru</span><span class="font5"> verbs in terms of structure, meaning, and whether the two can replace each other in sentences. Shiba and Cho (2017), in their research titled &quot;Koopasu ni</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Motozuita Ruigigo Bunseki - 'Miotosu', 'Misugosu', 'Minogasu' wo rei ni -.&quot;, examined three synchronous verbs in Japanese, namely </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">miotosu</span><span class="font5">, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">misugosu</span><span class="font5">, and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">minogasu</span><span class="font5">. Shiba and Cho's study (2017) primarily relates to the colocation information and grammar patterns of the three synonymous verbs. A study of these three synonymous verbs was actually already conducted by Nagashima in 1981. Nagashima's study (1981) at that time showed similarities and differences between these three synonymous verbs through the characteristics of the subjects and objects of those verbs. However, Shiba and Cho (2017) criticized nagashima 's study (1981) as still unclear showing differences in nuance and behavior of the subjects of the three synonymous verbs. Zarifa, Herniwati, and Sutjiati (2017), in their research entitled &quot;Analysis of the Misuse of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Taisetsu na, Daiji na</span><span class="font5">, and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Juuyou na</span><span class="font5">&quot;, examined the misuses of three Japanese adjectives that have the meaning of 'important' by students in the fourth semester at the University of Education Indonesia. Zarifa, Herniwati, and Sutjiati (2017) mentioned two factors that are the cause of the misuse among these three adjectives. First, the students do not understand the difference in the use of these three adjectives; second, the students do not understand the difference in the meaning of 'important' that exists in the context of the sentence. Based on the literature studies that have been done, it can be said that the potential of synonymy studies can still be developed further as done through this research.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">This paper raises five Japanese verbs that Backhouse (2016:112-113) says have similar meanings, namely </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">omomuku</span><span class="font2">赴く</span><span class="font5">, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">irassharu</span><span class="font2">いらっしゃる</span><span class="font5">, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mairu</span><span class="font2">参る</span><span class="font5">, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">kaeru</span><span class="font2">帰る</span><span class="font5">, and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">iku</span><span class="font2">行く</span><span class="font5">. These five verbs can be said to be synonymous because according to Momiyama (in Sutedi, 2008:129), there are four ways of identifying synonymy, one of which is: several words when translated into foreign languages will be one word. For example, the word </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">oriru</span><span class="font5"> and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">kudaru</span><span class="font5"> in Japanese can be regarded as synonymous because they can be translated into &quot;turun&quot; (‘to go down’) in Indonesian. The five verbs that are discussed in this paper if translated into English can be translated into “to go”. The problem raised in this paper is about the level of synonymy of the synonymous verbs observed through differences in the frequency of use in the corpora and the meaning components.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Methods</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">In order to observe differences in frequency of use, the data used in this paper were taken from two Japanese corpora, namely the Balance Corpus of Contemporary Written Japanese (BCCWJ) and Corpus of Spoken Japanese (CSJ). The BCCWJ itself contains over one hundred million words and has been launched to the public since December 2011 (Maekawa et al., 2014). Whereas, CSJ contains around seven million and more words. Baker (2010, 4-5) explains that the word &quot;corpus&quot; comes from Latin meaning 'body'. In English it has a single form of corpus and a plural form of corpora. Corpus is a collection of languages that occur naturally and are stored as computer files. The presentation of observations regarding differences in frequency of use is presented in the form of a bar chart. Explanations of the numbers in tables and diagrams are given using words or sentences to describe and elaborate the findings, and to draw conclusions. The meaning components of each synonymous verb are examined from their usages found in the </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Sanseido Daijirin</span><span class="font5"> dictionary.</span></p>

<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">3.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Meaning Components on </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;">Five Synonymous Verbs </span><span class="font4" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">GO</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">The meaning of each verb examined in this paper is examined in an online monolingual Japanese dictionary, namely </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Sanseido Daijirin</span><span class="font5"> </span><a href="https://www.weblio.jp/content/%e8%b5%b4%e3%81%8f"><span class="font5">(</span><span class="font5" style="text-decoration:underline;">https://www.weblio.jp/content/</span><span class="font5">)</span></a><span class="font5">. The followings are the meaning of each verb.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font4">3.2.1</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;omomuku</span><span class="font1">赴く</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">In using modern Japanese, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">omomuku</span><span class="font5"> has two meanings, namely:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font4">a.</span><span class="font5"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;go somewhere (</span><span class="font1">ある場所に向かって行く</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">aru basho ni mukatte iku</span><span class="font5">)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">Example: (1) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Kyouto ni omomuku</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">京都に赴く</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">‘going to Kyoto’</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Besides being able to use with a concrete place, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">omomuku</span><span class="font5"> can also be used to indicate the meaning of ‘going to an abstract place’.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Example: (2) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Ninchi e omomuku</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">任地へ赴く</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">’going to one’s place of work’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font4">b.</span><span class="font5"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;towards a situation (</span><span class="font1">ある状態に向かう</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">aru joutai ni mukau</span><span class="font5">)</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">Example: (3) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Byouki mo kaihou ni omomuku</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">病気も快方に赴く</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">’getting better from illness’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font4">3.2.2</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;irassharu</span><span class="font1">いらっしゃる</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">irassharu</span><span class="font5"> is a form of respect (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">keigo</span><span class="font1"> 敬語</span><span class="font5">) of the verbs </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">iku</span><span class="font1"> 行く </span><span class="font5">‘go', </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">kuru</span><span class="font1">来る</span><span class="font5">‘come', and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">iru</span><span class="font1">居る </span><span class="font5">‘exists'. In Japanese, the form of respect is used to respect the speech partner and the person being discussed. In other words, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">irassharu</span><span class="font5"> is usually used with the subject of the second person or third person.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Example: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(4) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Sensei wa Rondon e irassharu</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">先生はロンドンへいらっしゃる</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">‘teacher goes to London’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">(5)</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Kaisha kara kochira e irassharu </span><span class="font1">会社からこちらへいらっしゃる </span><span class="font5">‘coming here from the office’</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">(6)</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Go-jitaku ni irassharu </span><span class="font1">御自宅にいらっしゃる </span><span class="font5">’being at home’</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">3.2.3</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;mairu</span><span class="font1">参る</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mairu</span><span class="font5"> has many meanings. Related to the meaning of 'go', the </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mairu</span><span class="font5"> verb is a form of degrading one's own actions (first person) when expressing </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">iku</span><span class="font1">行く </span><span class="font5">‘go’ and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">kuru</span><span class="font1">来る </span><span class="font5">‘come’ to respect the speech partner. </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mairu</span><span class="font5"> is also used to convey the meaning of 'pilgrimage'. In addition, this </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mairu</span><span class="font5"> verb can function as a </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">sondaigo</span><span class="font1"> 尊大語 </span><span class="font5">(utterances used to show the social strata of the speaker and his/her speech partner) when used by people who have higher social status to people with lower social status. In use with its function as </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">sondaigo</span><span class="font5">, the </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mairu</span><span class="font5"> verb is usually used in the form of an order, namely </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">maire</span><span class="font5"> (see sample data (10) and (11)).</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Example: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(7) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mata ashita ni-ji ni mairimasu</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">また明日</span><span class="font4">2</span><span class="font1">時に参ります</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">‘I will come again tomorrow at 2’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">(8)</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Hai, sugu sochira e mairimasu </span><span class="font1">はい、すぐそちらへ参ります </span><span class="font5">‘Okay, I will go there right away’</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">(9)</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Bodaiji ni mairimasu </span><span class="font1">菩提寺に参ります</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">‘going in a pilgrimage to Bodaiji’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">(10)</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Washi wa ato kara iku kara omae wa saki ni maire </span><span class="font1">わしはあとから行くからお前は先に参れ</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">‘because I will go there later, you go first’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">(11)</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Hayaku kochira e maire </span><span class="font1">早くこちらへ参れ </span><span class="font5">‘come here quickly’</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">3.2.4</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;kaeru</span><span class="font1">帰る</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Kaeru</span><span class="font5"> has two meanings, namely:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">a. </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Hajimeni &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;ita &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;tokoro, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;matawa &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;moto &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;ita &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;basho &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;ni</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">modoru</span><span class="font1">初めにいたところ、またはもといた場所にもどる </span><span class="font5">‘going back to original place’ Example: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(12) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Go-ji ni wa kaettekuru</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">五時には帰ってくる</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">‘coming home at 5’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">(13)</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Kokoku ni kaeru </span><span class="font1">故国に帰る</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">‘going back to one’s own country’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">b. </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Yatte kita hito ga soko o tachisaru</span><span class="font1">やってきた人がそこを立ち去る </span><span class="font5">‘a person who came to a place is leaving that place’</span></p>
<div>
<p><span class="font5">Example:</span></p>
</div><br clear="all"></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">(14)</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Kyaku ga kaeru</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">客が帰る</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">‘the guest is leaving’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">3.2.5 </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">iku</span><span class="font1">行く</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Iku</span><span class="font5"> (yuku), like the </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mairu</span><span class="font5"> verb, has many meanings, both lexical meaning and metaphorical meaning such as (15) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Osaka e yome ni itta musume</span><span class="font1">大阪へ嫁に行った娘 </span><span class="font5">‘my daughter will get married in Osaka’. Regarding the meaning of ‘go’, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">iku</span><span class="font5"> has three meanings,</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">namely:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">a.</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Hito, doubutsu, norimono ga hanashite no iru basho kara tooku e idou suru</span><span class="font1">人・動物・乗り物が話してのいる場所から遠くへ移動する &nbsp;</span><span class="font5">‘humans, animals,</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">vehicles move away from the speaker’</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Example: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(16) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Kore kara ginkou e iku</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">これから銀行へ行く</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">‘I will go to the bank now’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">b.</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Hito, doubutsu, norimono ga mokuteki no chiten ni mukatte susumu mata itaritsuku</span><span class="font1">人・動物・乗り物が目的の地点に向かって進むまた至りつく </span><span class="font5">‘humans, animals, vehicles heading or moving towards the destination’</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">Example: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(17) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Puuru ni oyogi ni iku</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">プールに泳ぎに行く</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">‘going for swimming in swimming pool’</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">(18)</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Kyoto o mita ato Nara e iku </span><span class="font1">京都を見たあと奈良へ行く </span><span class="font5">‘going to Nara after seeing Kyoto’</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">(19)</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Osaka kara Kyushu e iku ressha </span><span class="font1">大阪から九州へ行く列車</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">‘train that going to Kyushu from Osaka’</span></p>
<div>

<p><span class="font5">Example: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(20) </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Gaitousha ni wa yakusho kara tsuuchi ga iku hazu da</span></p>

<h3><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Novelties</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">This paper discussed not only the meaning components of the verbs, but also the usage in the corpus. Unlike previous studies which only emphasis on one of those two, this study combined the two so that more comprehensive understanding regarding the comparison among those synonymous verbs can be alerted.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h3><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h3></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">Based on observations on the frequency of use and meaning components, the five synonymous verbs 'go' in Japanese show differences. It can be stated that these verbs cannot completely replace each other. There are limits to usage that require the presence of each of these verbs. Therefore, Japanese language learners must be careful in using these verbs so they can convey their intended meaning correctly to their speech partner.</span></p>
