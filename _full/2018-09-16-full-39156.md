<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstrak</span></h2>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Penelitian yang berjudul The Syntactic Functions of Relative Clauses in A Game of Thrones Novel ini mengangkat dua pokok masalah yakni; tipe klausa relatif yang ditemukan di novel dan fungsi sintaksis dari klausa relatif yang terdapat dalam novel tersebut. Penelitian ini bertujuan untuk menemukan tipe-tipe klausa relatif yang muncul dalam novel serta menjelaskan fungsi-fungsi sintaksis klausa relatif yang ditemukan. Pengumpulan data diambil langsung dari novel A Game of Thrones yang ditulis oleh G. R. R. Martin pada tahun 1996. Data-data yang terkumpul kemudian dianalisis secara deskriptif dan kualitatif. Penelitian ini menerapkan dua teori. Teori utama diusulkan oleh Oshima dan Hogue dalam buku mereka berjudul Writing Academic English (1999). Teori tersebut dipakai untuk mengulas fungsi sintaksis dari klausa relatif. Teori pendukung diusulkan oleh Radford dalam bukunya Transformational Syntax: A Student Guide to Chomsky’s Extended Theory (1988). Teori kedua ini digunakan untuk menemukan tipe klausa relatif dalam novel. Penerapan pohon diagram dalam penelitian ini adalah untuk memberikan gambaran jelas mengenai fungsi sintaksis dari klausa relatif. Berdasarkan masalah pertama, penelitian ini menghasilkan bahwa semua tipe klausa relatif ditemukan dalam novel; terbatas, tidak terbatas, dan bebas. Permasalahan kedua menghasilkan bahwa keenam fungsi sintaksis dari klausa relatif semua ditemukan kecuali bagian dari salah satu fungsi yakni klausa relatif pada frasa kualitas tidak ditemukan.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Kata kunci: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">fungsi sintaksis, kata ganti relatif, klausa relatif.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>Abstract</span></h2>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">This study is entitled The Syntactic Functions of Relative Clauses in “A Game of Thrones” Novel. It was formulated two problems; types of relative clauses found in the novel as well as the syntactic function of relative clauses found in the novel. It was aimed at finding out the types of relative clauses occurring in the novel and at explaining the syntactic functions found in the novel. The data were collected from A Game of Thrones novel written by G. R. R. Martin in 1996. The data were then analyzed descriptively and qualitatively. There were two theories applied in this study. The main theory was proposed by Oshima and Hogue in their book Writing Academic English (1999). The theory was used as the instrument to answer the syntactic functions of relative clauses. The supporting theory applied in this study was proposed by Radford in his book Transformational Syntax: A Student Guide to Chomsky’s Extended Theory (1988). The theory was used to discuss the second problem. The data were presented using tree diagram in order to give clearer images about the syntactic functions. This study showed that based on the first formulated problem the types of relative clauses occurring in the novel were restrictive, non-restrictive and free relative clauses. Based</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">on the second formulated problem, it showed that the functions of relative clauses appearing in the novel were all the six functions but the relative clause in quality phrases did not exist.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Keywords: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">relative clause, relative pronoun, syntactic function.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">In using grammar, it is a must to know what particles create sentences since grammar greatly deals with sentences.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The most important particle of a sentence is a clause. A clause may be an independent clause, or a dependent clause. In terms of the dependent clause, the clause can be differentiated based on their grammatical functions as nominal clause, adverbial clause, and relative clause (Leech, 1983: 211). Oshima and Hogue (1999: 209) say that a relative clause is a dependent clause that functions as an adjective; it modifies a noun or pronoun. For this reason, a relative clause is also similarly known as an adjectival clause.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">A relative clause is introduced by its relaters. There are two types of relaters; relative pronoun and relative adverb. The relative pronoun consists of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">who, whom, whose, which,</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">that</span><span class="font2">. On the other hand, relative adverbs consist of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">where, when</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">why</span><span class="font2">. Quirk </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font2"> (1985, 366) point out that a relative clause has two types namely restrictive relative clause and non-restrictive relative clause. The semantic relation between a clause and its relater may be either restrictive or non-restrictive.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">A formal distinction between a restrictive and non-restrictive relative clause is found sporadically across languages or only an intonational distinction where the relative clause follows the head noun (Comrie, 1989: 139). Persian is a language with a formal distinction which requires the suffix </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">–i</span><span class="font2"> on the head of a restrictive relative, but not on the head of a non-restrictive (Comrie, 1989: 139). On the contrary, the semantic</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">distinction applies equally to prenominal adjectives in English (Comrie, 1989: 139).</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Therefore, in English there is no such an affix or a formal distinction to mark whether a relative clause is restrictive or the opposite. Apart from the types, Oshima and Hogue (1999: 212) state that in each different kind, the relative pronoun has a different function.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">This study was interesting since there is still confusion among English learners in using relative clauses. Through the discussion of this study, it was expected that the use of relative clauses would be clearer and easier to apply.</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark26"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark27"></a>5.2.1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Relative Clause as Subjects</span></h2></li></ul></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a) He groped for words </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">that did not come</span><span class="font2"> (Martin, 1996: 7)</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/39156-3.jpg" alt="" style="width:203pt;height:158pt;"></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The relative clause above functions as the subject of the noun preceding it, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">words</span><span class="font2"> yet it functions as the direct object within the complex sentence. Consider the complex sentence when separated into two simple sentences: a. He groped for </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">words </span><span class="font2">b. </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">Words</span><span class="font2"> did not come</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">words</span><span class="font2"> in the complex sentence is the subject of the verb phrase </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">did not come</span><span class="font2">. The noun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">words</span><span class="font2"> is replaced by the relative pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">that</span><span class="font2"> when both sentences are united. The relative pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">that</span><span class="font2"> also becomes the subordinator of the complex sentence. The relative clause is positioned at the back which means that it is in the final position. The structural relationship corresponds between the object of the main clause and the subject of the relative clause. Subsequently, it builds up an object-subject connection (o – s).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark28"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark29"></a>5.2.2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Relative Clause as Objects</span></h2></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a) They found the places </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">that her father had promised.</span><span class="font2"> (Martin, 1996:</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">293)</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/39156-4.jpg" alt="" style="width:203pt;height:192pt;">
<p><span class="font2">The relative clause functions as the object for the noun preceding it, </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the places</span><span class="font2">. Consider the complex sentence if separated to clearly see the function of the relative clause:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;They found </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">the places</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Her father had promised her </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">the places</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The function of the second NP </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the places</span><span class="font2"> is clearly seen from the fragment above. The second NP </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">the places</span><span class="font2"> which is replaced by the relative pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">that</span><span class="font2"> is the object of the VP </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">had promised</span><span class="font2"> done by the subject of the relative clause, the NP </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">her father</span><span class="font2">. The position of the relative clause is in the final position. The structural connection is between the object of the relative clause and the object of the main clause. This leads to the formation of object-object (o – o) connection.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark30"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark31"></a>5.2.3. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Relative Clause as Object of Preposition</span></h2></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a) You are tried and found guilty of the crimes </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">for which you stand accused</span><span class="font2">….. (Martin, 1996: 420-421)</span></p></li></ul><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/39156-5.jpg" alt="" style="width:203pt;height:227pt;">
<p><span class="font2">The relative pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">which</span><span class="font2"> appears after the preposition </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">for</span><span class="font2"> and it simply makes the relative clause become the</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">object of the preposition. Consider the relative clause if the preposition is moved:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a. You are tried and found guilty of the crimes </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">which you stand accused for </span><span class="font2">This form is possible in a conversation or in more informal way than that of the first complex sentence.</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark32"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark33"></a>5.2.4. Possessive Relative Clause</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">a) He rode three passes at Lothor Brune </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">whose armor was drab</span><span class="font2">. (Martin, 1996: 295)</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/39156-6.jpg" alt="" style="width:203pt;height:186pt;">
<p><span class="font2">The possessive relative clause </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">whose armor was drab</span><span class="font2"> detected within</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">the complex sentence modifies the NP </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Lothor Brune</span><span class="font2"> preceding it. The function of the relative pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">whose</span><span class="font2"> is as the subject of the relative clause. Consider the complex sentence if separated:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;He rode three passes at </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">Lothor Brune </span><span class="font2">b. </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">His</span><span class="font2"> armor was drab</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">It is clearly seen in the fragment that the possessive relative pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">whose </span><span class="font2">functions as the subject of the relative clause since the possessive relative pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">whose</span><span class="font2"> refers to the NP </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Lothor Brune</span><span class="font2"> which is the subject of the clause and it is him who has the armor. The position of the possessive relative clause is in the final position. The structural connection is linked between the object of the main clause and the subject of the relative clause leading to the objectsubject (o – s) connection.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2.5. Relative Clause in Quantity Phrases</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">a) There were quicksands and snakes, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">none of which stopped Arya</span><span class="font2">. (Martin, 1996: 141)</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/39156-7.jpg" alt="" style="width:204pt;height:205pt;">
<p><span class="font2">The relative clause</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">detected in</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">the complex sentence above is called Relative Clause in Phrases of Quantity. The relative pronoun is found within a quantity phrase. </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">None of which</span><span class="font2"> refers to the nouns before it; quicksands and snakes. The relative pronoun shows that</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">none of the things mentioned before stops the subject. Consider the fragment: a. There were </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">quicksands and snakes </span><span class="font2">watching from the trees</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">b.</span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;They</span><span class="font2"> did not stop Arya</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark34"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark35"></a>5.2.6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Adverbial Relative Clause</span></h2></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a) Brandon had been twenty </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">when he died</span><span class="font2"> (Martin, 1996: 43)</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/39156-8.jpg" alt="" style="width:203pt;height:147pt;"></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The relative adverb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">when</span><span class="font2"> is found within the complex sentence. It is intended to modify the NP </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">twenty </span><span class="font2">preceding it. See the complex sentence if separated:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2">a. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Brandon had been </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">twenty</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font2">b. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Brandon died at </span><span class="font2" style="text-decoration:underline;">twenty</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">When they are combined into one single complex sentence, they need a relativiser and in this case the relative adverb </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">when</span><span class="font2"> is used. It is to reduce the repetition of the age </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">twenty</span><span class="font2"> as well as to combine both simple sentences.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark36"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark37"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">The finding of the discussion answering the first formulated problem of this study, which was to find the types of relative clause found in the novel </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">A Game of Thrones</span><span class="font2"> was that there are three types of relative clause contained in the novel; restrictive relative clause, non-restrictive relative clause, and free relative clause. The frequency of presence of each type is different. Restrictive and non-restrictive are the</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">most frequent types used whereas the free type is less frequent.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The second formulated problem was to find the syntactic function of relative clause based on the six functions; as subjects, objects, object of preposition, possessive relative clause, relative clause in quantity and quality phrases, and relative adverb by Oshima and Hogue. They were all found in the novel. However, there is a part of a function which does not appear in the novel; the relative clause in quality phrase. It is only relative clauses in quantity phrases which were found.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Moreover, the most frequent functions appearing in the novel are all of them but the relative adverb and relative clause in quantity phrases. There are a few data for both functions. It is also found that within a complex sentence, the structural relationships linked between the main clauses and the relative clauses were as the subject or object of the relative clause itself as well as the subject or object of the main clause.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The aims of this study were expected to be real since the discussion had done its best to show and discuss each function and type so that the use of relative clause would not be confusing and would be easier to apply.</span></p>
