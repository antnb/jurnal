<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Abstract</span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"> -- </span><span class="font5">Quite a lot of approaches have been utilized to study humor texts; yet, none has been from an ecolinguistics’ perspective. This article specifically intends to scrutinize a particular humor text from Papua called </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> on the ground of dialectical ecolinguistics as developed by Bang, Door, et al. (1993). The main purpose of this article is to describe the elements of core contradictions (i.e. race, age, sex, class, authority, ideology, town-country, private-public, and culture-nature) found in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5">. The result of the analysis shows that all the nine elements of the core contradictions in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> are found in the bio-logics dimension indicating that the bio-logics dimension is the most dominant dimension in the world of the </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> owners (i.e. the people of Papua).</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Keywords</span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">: </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Core Contradictions, Mob of Papua, Dialectical Ecolinguistics</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Introduction</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Mob</span><span class="font5"> is a typical humor discourse from Papua. At first, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> was a part of oral traditions, meaning that it was spread through the words of mouth. However, technological advance helps delivering </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> into bigger audiences. Nowadays, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> can be found in various media: either electronic media (such as local television and local radio) or short message services and internet. In other words, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> can now be referred to as not only an oral text but also a written text.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Even though </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> can be categorized as humor text, there is a different focus of discussion about humor in this article from previous studies on other humor texts. Previous studies on humor texts mostly focused the discussion on contributing factors to humorous aspects in the texts; while the main focus of discussion here is rather on how humor text is viewed from an ecolinguistics’ perspective. Ecolinguistics in this article refers mainly to Dialectical Ecolinguistics (DE) as developed by Bang and Door (1993). The article will specifically address the elements of the core contradictions in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5">.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Theoretical Framework</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">This particular section will cover about important concepts used in the article and the theories applied for the analysis.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>2.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Concepts: </span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Mob</span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"> and Triple Dimensions of Environment</span><br><br><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark8"></a>2.1.1</span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Mob</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">The concepts of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> here are: (1) “its owner’s (i.e. Papuan folks) knowledge of their world, community, faith, culture and tradition, which is informally learned and creatively expressed through words as well as interactively and dynamically created and communicated to its audiences in order to make them laugh when reading or listening to it”; and (2) ”secondary folklore”, it is because </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> is prone to changes and development. The dynamics of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob </span><span class="font5">nowadays make it more known, documented, and developed, thanks to printing media, mobile phones, and the internet.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark9"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark10"></a>2.1.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Triple Dimensions of Environment</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">The concept of triple dimensions of environment here refer to the three main points of the Odense Mashab’s concept of environment: (1) ideological environment (mental order, also known as ideo-logics dimension); (2) biological environmemt (physical order, also known as bio-logics dimension); and (3) sociological environment (sosial order, also known as sosio-logics dimension) and the dialectical relation among the triple dimensions.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark11"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark12"></a>2.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Theoretical Framework</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">The theory applied here is the theory of Dialectical Ecolinguistics, using the Model of Core Contradictions. Applying the Core Contradictions Model from the DE theory as theoretical framework means that the analysis focuses on the nine elements of core contradictions of social praxis and their triple dimensions. These nine elements are race, age, sex, class, authority, ideology, town-country, private-public, and culture-nature; while the triple dimensions are biologics, socio-logics, and ideo-logics dimensions.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">The following picture presents the Core Contradictions Model.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">105</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/47408-2.png" alt="" style="width:68pt;height:37pt;"><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/47408-3.jpg" alt="" style="width:307pt;height:166pt;">
<p><span class="font5">Picture 1: Core Contradictions of the Social Praxis (Source: Bang and Door, 1993) The nine elements of the core contradictions deal with the following aspects.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">1) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Race: referring to differences in colors, culture, and ethnicity, or conflicts between issues of integration vs apartheid.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">2) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Age: referring to old-young dichotomy or things related to life-cycle phases, from childhood to adulthood, or to parents, grandfather/grandmother, etc.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">3) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Sex: referring to male-female dichotomy, fertile-infertile; as well as heterosexualhomosexual.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">4) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Class: referring to conflicts of positions, rights, and compentencies within a social order system.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">5) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Authority: referring to position conflicts in relation to authority relations, such as teacherstudent relation, doctor-patient relation, or parents-children relation.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">6) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Ideology: referring to various philosophical points of view, religions, values, yudicial laws and ethics, or paradigms of true knowledge and sciences.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">7) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Town-Country: referring to different lifestyles and life settings as well as to different production and consumption styles of foods and goods. It also refers to different ecology.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">8) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Private-Public: referring to various domains in social order related to different yudicial and economic condition among individuals or communities.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">9) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Culture-Nature: referring to different lifestyles related to the complexity of media and other artificial products.</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<p><span class="font5">On the ground of Titscher et al (in Mautner, 2008:35) the research data can be categorized as “universe of possible texts”. Data collection process in this research can be referred to as “cyclical process” as pointed out by Bauer and Aarts (in Mautner, 2008: 35). <p><span class="font5">This research employed documentation as its data collection method as proposed by Danandjaja (1997:191). The documentation method is the first stage of the data collection, intended to archive or document. Besides, the research adopted the documentation method from Sarwono (2006) and Bungin (2008) which stated that this method may refer to both document study method or online data search. Document study method employed in the research referred to searching of written data sources on </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5">; while online data search method referred to information searching on </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> by utilizing internet.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">This research employed triangulation technique to check on the data validity. According to Moleong (2002:178), triangulation referred to data validity checking technique which made use of other components beyond the data themselves. Those components functioned both as background check and as comparison to the data. Triangulation consisted of four types, which are triangulation of sources, methods, examiners, and theory. This research utilized the triangulation of sources (see Sugiyono 2009:83). The triangulation of sources here meant the research data were obtained from different sources using the same technique.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">The analysis method applied for this research can be categorized within the group of the text and language analysis as well as the group of the cultural-theme analysis. From the text and language analysis, the research applied both Discourse Analysis/Textual Analysis and Critical Discourse Analysis. From the cultural-theme analysis, the research applied the analysis techniques which contained Domain Analysis, Taxonomic Analysis, and Componential Analysis</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">. </span><span class="font5">Besides, the use of the cultural-theme analysis for the research related to the fact that this research viewed the research object (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5">) as part of Papuan culture in a form of folklore.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark15"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark16"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Discussion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">As previously mentioned, the core contradictions consist of nine elements: race, age, sex, class, authority, ideology, town-country, private-public, and culture-nature. Those nine elements are found within the triple dimensions of environment in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5">, which are, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">bio-logics</span><span class="font5"> dimension, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">sosio-logics</span><span class="font5"> dimension, and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">ideo-logics</span><span class="font5"> dimension. All the nine elements of core contradictions are found at the bio-logics dimension in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5">; while only three elements of core contradictions (sex, class, authority) are present at the socio-logics dimension, and one element (authority) at the ideo-logics dimension.</span></p>

<p><span class="font5">“tanah”, “gunung”, “hutan”, “sungai”, “muara sungai”, “langit”, “gua”, “angin”, “hujan”, “mentari”, “pelangi”, “lembah”, “kali”, “laut(an)”, “bulan”, “padang pasir”, “batu”, “terumbu karang”, “bintang”, “pantai”, “batubara”, “kebun”, “kolam ikan”, “sawah” ,“bahasa”, “bahasa kampung”, “bahasa Inggris”, “bahasa Indonesia”, “bahasa India”, “bahasa Belanda”, “layang-layang”, “kelereng” “FB” “lagu”, “pantun”, “syair”, “kata mutiara” “acara peresmian”, “ultah”, “</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">valentine</span><span class="font5">”, “kado”, “piala dunia”</span></p></td></tr>

<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">1) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Race: The Race Element is identified to be existing within the living things groups. The element is present in the environment of human beings under the aspects of “race/ethnicity” and “family names.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">2) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Age Element is identified to be existing in the living things groups for the environment of human beings. It is present under the aspects of “terms of address”, “nicknames’, and “ages”.</span></p></li></ul>

<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font5">3) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Sex Element is found at the living thing group, in the environment of human beings. It is present under the aspects of “personal names”, “occupation”, “kinship”, “sex”, “race/ethnicity”, “nicknames”, “terms of address”, and “Bible figures”.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">4) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Class Element is found at the living thing group, in the environment of human beings. It is present under the aspects of “occupation”. The element is also found at non-living thing group at material/matter environment and spiritual/supernatural environment.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">5) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Authority Element is identified at both the living thing and the non-living thing groups. In the living thing group, the element is found in the environment of human beings under the aspects of “personal names with respect attribute”, “occupation”, “kinship”, and “terms of address”. In the non-living thing group, the element can be found at three kinds of environments. The first one is in the environment of the universe under the aspects of “territorials”; in the material/matter environment under the aspects of “general facilities”; and the the spiritual/supernatural environment.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">6) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Ideology Element is identified in the environment of non-living thing. It is present at the spiritual-supernatural environment (at liturgical aspects</span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">/</span><span class="font5">Christianity with regard to the dogma/concept, worship services attributes, and symbols), and at the cognitive-creative environment (at the aspects of citizenship/ideology/law).</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">7) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Town-Country Element is identified in the non-living thing group. The element is found within two types of environments: a. the environment of the universe; which includes the aspects of “nature and climate” as well as “teritorials”; and the environment of the material/matter under the aspects of “housings” and “general facilities”.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">8) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Private-Public Element is found in the non-living thing group. The element is present at the environment of material/matter under the aspects of “general facilities” and “transportation”.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font5">9) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The ninth element, the Culture-Nature Elemet is found in the environment of the non-living thing. It presents in three types of environments: a. the environtment of the universe, at the aspect of “the universe/climate”; the environment of material/matter at the aspects of “foods”, “drinks”, “cooking spices”, “housings”, “home appliances”, “clothing and jewelry”, “stationary”, “tools/(edged) weapons”, “general facilities”, “industry”, “transportation facilities”, “electronic devices”, “basic items/staple”, “furniture”, “cellular world”, “money”, “solid matter”, “chemical substance”, “infrastructure”; and c. the cognitive-creative environment.</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">Berger and Luckman (1990:27-28) states that everyday lives present themselves as reality</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">as interpreted by humans and possess subjective meanings to them as a coherent world.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Everyday lives are basically a world made of thoughts and actions and therefore, maintained as “real” by those thoughts and actions. The basics of knowledge in the everyday lives are what is so called “objectivation”. Objectivation itself refers to the process of objectifying subjective meanings and it is this process which in the end leads to intersubjectivity. Thus, it can be said that what is presented in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> may convey the world of thoughts and actions of its owner.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">The world is objectified through language use in everyday lives. Language, through its</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">lexicons, remarks the coordinates of its speakers’ community lives. In other words, it can be</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">110</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/47408-8.png" alt="" style="width:65pt;height:31pt;">
<p><span class="font5">assumed that the bio-logics dimension is the most dominant dimension in the world of the </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob </span><span class="font5">owners (i.e. the people of Papua). This is so since all the nine elements of the core contradictions as mentioned in the table above are found in the bio-logics dimension. Then, it is understandable why physical environments (here: nature, land, and all their riches) are invaluable treasures to the people of Papua. The result of this research, indeed, confirms that this physical environment (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">bio-logics</span><span class="font5"> dimension) is not merely an element of core contradiction namely Culture-Nature, but it is a realization of all the core contradiction elements in the lives of the people of Papua.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark17"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark18"></a>5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Novelty</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">Novelty aspect of this research refers to its empirical finding with regard to the elements of core contradictions. The nine elements of core contradictions in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5"> are found in the environments of both living and non-living things. In the Dialectical Ecolinguistics’ perpective, the environment is usually addressed as the triple dimensions of environment, consisting of three dimensions, namely </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">bio-logics</span><span class="font5"> dimension, </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">socio-logics</span><span class="font5"> dimension, and </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">ideo-logics</span><span class="font5"> dimension. The environments of both living and non-living things are parts of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">bio-logics</span><span class="font5"> dimension.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark19"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark20"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion and Recommendation</span><br><br><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>6.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">Based on the discussion on the core contradictions in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5">, it can be concluded that the nine elements of core contradictions (i.e. race, age, sex, class, authority, ideology, town-country, private-public, and culture-nature) are all found in the bio-logics dimension of the environment, both in the group of living things and in the group of non-living things.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>6.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Recommendation</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font5">This article only presents the elements of core contradictions as shown by the lexicons found in the texts of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">mob</span><span class="font5">. Core Contradictions Model is actually one of the four models of analysis offered by the Dialectical Ecolinguistics (DE) theory. Therefore, it might be interesting to see how these elements are tackled using other models of approaches available in the DE (i.e. Dialogue, Triple References, Semantic Matrix).</span></p>
