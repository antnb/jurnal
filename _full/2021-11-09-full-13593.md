<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font2"><a name="bookmark1"></a>ARTICLE</span></h1>
<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>EGG PRODUCTION, QUALITY AND CHICK COLOUR PLUMAGE OF FIVE COLOURS OF KAMPONG HENS FED GOOD QUALITY FEED</span></h2>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">IM Mastika, AW Puger, IKM Budiasa and IGL Oka</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Faculty of Animal Husbandry, Udayana University</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Jl. PB. Sudirman, Denpasar, Bali.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">e-mail: </span><a href="mailto:made.mastika@gmail.com"><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">made.mastika@gmail.com</span></a></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">ABSTRACT</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">An experiment was carried out to study the egg production, quality and chick colour plumage of five phenotypic plumage colours of Kampong hens fed good quality feed. A Randomized Block Design consisted of five treatments and five replicateswere used in this experiment. A total of 25 maturated kampong chickens consisted of five plumage coloursi.e. Black (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Selem</span><span class="font5">), Red (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">biying</span><span class="font5">), Mix colour (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">brumbun</span><span class="font5">), white plumage with yellow leg (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">putih siung</span><span class="font5">), and white plumage with white leg (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">putih kedas</span><span class="font5">) and five cockerels with respective colours were used in this experiment. Hens were housed individually in enrich cage. Mating procedure was done in a special mating cage where each cockerel with respective colour stays all the time. Hen was bring into the mating cage and left there for 24 hours and repeated after 5 days, until hens were going into brood. A good quality feed contained 17% CP and 2750 kcal ME were offered to all hens and fresh water were provided </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">ad libitum</span><span class="font5">. Observation was made for 14 weeks.This study showed that in one laying cycle except the mixed plumage colour hens produced similar number of eggs while the mix colour was the least. All parameters measured for egg quality from those five plumage colours were similar. Observation of the chicks colour at one day old chick (DOC) showed that black plumage, white plumage with yellow leg, and red plumage hens had 100%, 100% and 81% chicks respectively had similar colour to their respective parents. While those mix colour and white plumage with white leg were only had 38.89% and 30.2% chicks with similar colour to their respective hens. After 8 weeks old, the colour of the chicks from respective colour has changed where black, red, white plumage with yellow leg had 70%, 77.5% and 93.75% chicks with the same colour to their respective hens. While those mix colour and white plumage with white leg had 53.8% and 17.6% chicks with the same colour to their parent. It was concluded that black, red, and white plumage with yellow legs had dominant gen in kampong chickens.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Keywords: </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Kampong chickens, chick colour distribution, egg production, egg quality</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font6">The role of kampong chickens in providing protein from meat and eggs in Bali and Indonesia in general is well documented. In Bali, kampong chickens plays a very important role due to the fact that kampong chicken is not only providing egg and meat for consumption but more importantly it is also used for ceremonial purposes as well as for hobby (cock fighting). The total number of kampong chicken in Bali was reported about 4.6 million (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Dinas Peternakan, 2010</span><span class="font6">). Bali communitieswhere mostlyare Hindusneedingkampong chicken in their daily life for ceremonial functions, such as “Mecaru”, make an offering (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">meyadnya</span><span class="font6">) namely </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Dewayadnya, Resiyadnya, Manusayadnya, Pitrayadnya</span><span class="font6">and </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Bhutayadnya (</span><span class="font6">Nitis</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">,</span><span class="font6"> 2006</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">)</span><span class="font6">. The Chicken needed are those who have five plumage colours namely:</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">Red (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Biying</span><span class="font6">), Black (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Selem</span><span class="font6">), White withyellow legs (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Putih siungan</span><span class="font6">), White with white legs (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Putih kedas</span><span class="font6">), and Mixed colour (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Brumbun</span><span class="font6">). Those five colours follows the positions of the offerings that is red in the South (for the God of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Brahma</span><span class="font6">), black in the North (for the God of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Wisnu</span><span class="font6">), yellow in the West (for the God of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Mahadewa</span><span class="font6">),white in the East (for the God of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Iswara)</span><span class="font6">, and mixed colour in the Centre (for the God of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Siwa</span><span class="font6">) (Utarayana, 1993; Budaarsa, 2009). These functions are common and frequently practiced by the Hindus community in Bali, as well as for hobby or entertainment such as cock fighting purpose. Although the needs of the five colours of kampong chickens are a lot particularly during the ceremonial activities, there is no special production center of those types of chickens at present in Bali. Kampong chickens are commonly raisedextensively and are left to scavenge their feedsin the field so</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">their production is relatively low (Mastika, 2004). To anticipate those needs, an experiment was carried out to study egg and chicken of the five phenotypic plumage colours of kampong chickens when fed good quality feed (15-17% protein)and housed in the intensive farming system to increase the production.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></h2>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">Chickens. </span><span class="font6">Twenty five mature hens (kampong chickens) comprising of five plumage colours namelyRed (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">biying</span><span class="font6">), Black (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">hitam)</span><span class="font6">, White with yellow legs (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">putih siungan</span><span class="font6">), White with white legs (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">putih kedas</span><span class="font6">) and Mix colours (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Brumbun</span><span class="font6">) were used in this experiment. They were housed individually in “ enriched floor cages” (Pavlik </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font6">., 2007, Sarica </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font6">., 2008). Each cage was provided with feed and water containers, perching stick and nesting box. The chicken cage size is 100x75cm wide, with 80 cm height.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">Feed and Water</span><span class="font6">. Feed were formulated using a mixture of Charoen concentrate, yellow corn, rice brand,additional vitamin and mineral so those mixture containedapproximately17% CP and 2750 kcal ME as suggested by Mastika (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">unpublished</span><span class="font6">) and this feed is considered as good quality feed for kampong chickens. Feed and water were provided </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ad libitum</span><span class="font6">, but on the other hand feed and water intake were weighed and recorded every day.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">Mating System</span><span class="font6">. In order to have fertile eggs, each hens of the same colour with the cockerels were mated individually in a mating cage. Hens were placed first then followed by the cockerels. This method were performed every day by replacing the hens, until all the hens have been mated and wascarried out continuouslyuntil the hen is going into brood. The similar system of mating was employed to the other colour group.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">Egg production</span><span class="font6">. Egg production was counted and recorded daily for all hens and at the third day of production, one egg from each hen was taken for egg quality measurement.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">The measurement of egg quality was carried out following the method explained by Stadleman </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font6">. (1973) and Hughes (1974). At the end of production cycle, the birds would have started brooding, but feed and water were provided </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">ad libitum</span><span class="font6"> and consumption were measured daily to study the pattern of feed and water intakes during brooding time. After 21 days of brooding time, the baby chickens that have hatched were counted and let the hens in the nest for 2-3 days until all the egg hatched. At one day old, chicken (DOC), the colourof the plumage were recorded. Hatched and unhatched eggs were then calculated and all this data were tabulated and analyzed.</span></p>
<p><span class="font6" style="font-weight:bold;">Design of the experiment and statistical analyses</span><span class="font6">. A Complete Randomized Design consisted of five treatments (5 plumage colours) as mentioned above (Red, Black, White with yellow leg, White with white leg and mixed colour) and 5 replicates each was used in this experiment. All data were recorded, tabulated, and analyzed using analysis of variance. Whenever significantly differenceswere found, analyses will becontinued using Duncan’s Multiple Range Test (Lu Chih Chang, 1982).</span></p>

Siregar and Sabrani (1980) also reported that egg production of kampong chickens was 30—60 eggs/year.</span></p>

It was interesting to note that the number of infertile eggs of mixed colour hens (</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">brumbun)</span><span class="font6"> was the highest (4,7 out of 11,5 eggs) when compared to others. Although the feed given was the same in quality and quantity, probably those differences were due to the different genetics background of the hens. Scott </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font6">. (1982) has reported that the efficiency of vitaminsutilization especially riboflavin in chickens was different among species. He stated that those differences due to the ability of protein bonding to a specific vitamin such as a riboflavin binding protein, which are very important in influencing embryo development</span></p>
<p><span class="font6">thus hatchability of the eggs. He postulated that this condition is controlled by a simple recessive autosomal gene in the homozygote state.</span></p>

<p><span class="font6">During brooding period a natural physiological process occurred in which hens has spent their body energy deposit (glycogen, fat, protein) in their body as source of heat for brooding their eggs, so their body weight will drop about 171,6 g after brooding has completed (Table 5). The nature of nutrient depletion on kampong chicken hens is similar with wild birds which were reported by Mrosovsky and Sherry (1980). They state that as in nature, several species of birds self-induce a period of fasting known as molting, where they lose up to 50% of their body mass. After the completion of the molting process, the reproductive system is rejuvenated allowing the birds to enter into an egg production cycle. Similar process probably happened to the kampong chicken while brooding reduced feed and water intake so reduced body weight about 30%.</span></p>
