<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>THE EQUIVALENT OF ENGLISH FIGURATIVE LANGUAGES INTO INDONESIAN WITH REFERENCE TO MRS DALLOWAY</span></h1>
<p><span class="font1">By :</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Ni Ketut Sudiani</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">English Department</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Faculty of Letters and Culture Udayana University</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Abstrak</span></p>
<p><span class="font0" style="font-style:italic;">Penerjemahan sebuah karya sastra, khususnya novel merupakan sebuah tantangan tersendiri bagi seorang penerjemah. Diperlukan sebuah ketelitian serta kedalaman pemahaman akan dua budaya guna menemukan kesepadanan antara kata bahasa sumber dengan bahasa target. Novel Mrs. Dalloway karya penulis Inggris ternama Virgina Woolf merupakan objek penelitian ini karena novel ini dibaca luas di seluruh dunia dan kerap dikenal sebagai karya yang tingkat kesulitannya cukup tinggi sebab menggunakan cukup banyak bahasa kiasan. Dalam penelitian ini dibahas mengenai jenis-jenis bahasa kiasan dan strategi yang digunakan untuk memperoleh kesepadanan kata antara bahasa sumber dan bahasa target. Penelitian ini mengacu pada teori yang diciptakan oleh Larson yaitu tiga pendekatan dalam menemukan kesepadanan kata dalam proses penerjemahan. Adapun hasil dari penelitian ini adalah metonymy, bahasa kiasan yang paling banyak ditemukan dalam novel. Hasil kesepadanan kata dari terjemahan bahasa sumber ke bahasa target terbilang baik. Makna kata dalam bahasa sumber tersampaikan dengan baik ke bahasa target.</span></p>
<p><span class="font0" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci : bahasa kiasan, kesepadanan kata, penerjemahan</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Figurative language is one of the most important parts in translation, translating novel in particular, as the words or expressions in the source language often have a figurative sense. There are number of novels which were translated from English into Indonesian. One of them is Mrs. Dalloway written by Virginia Woolf. This book was chosen as the data source because it has been a favourite one since it was first published in 1925 and the most misunderstood as well (Molly Hoff 2009: vii). Its author often used words or expressions that have a figurative sense. It is slightly difficult to understand the source language as they are many terms that are unknown to the Indonesian readers. Therefore, it is needed to find the equivalent of figurative language in doing the translation of the source language to the target language.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">According to those issues, this study was compiled to analyse the figurative language in Woolf’s Mrs. Dalloway and the strategies that were applied in finding an appropriate lexical equivalent. This analysis applied the theory of three approaches in choosing adequate lexical equivalent that proposed by Larson.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Problems of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Concerning the background given above, this study has two problems to discuss, as follows:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;What types of figurative languages are found in Woolf’s Mrs. Dalloway and its translation into Indonesian?</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;What strategies are applied in finding the adequate lexical equivalent of figurative languages translation in Woolf’s Mrs. Dalloway?</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font1"><a name="bookmark7"></a>3.</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Aims of the Study</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">These are two aims intended to be discovered in this study:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To identify the types of figurative languages found in Woolf’s Mrs. Dalloway and its translation into Indonesian.</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To analyse the strategies applied in finding the adequate lexical equivalent of figurative languages translation in Woolf’s Mrs. Dalloway.</span></p></li></ul>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Method</span><br><br><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark10"></a>4.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Data Source</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The data of this study were taken from figurative language in the English and Indonesian translation of Mrs. Dalloway written by English writer Virginia Woolf (new edition : 2000), published by Oxford University Press (New York). Its translation into Indonesian entitled Mrs. Dalloway (2006) was translated by Josephine, and was published by Jalasutra (Yogyakarta).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark11"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark12"></a>4.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Method and Technique of Collecting Data</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The library research method was used in collecting the data. Some techniques were applied in completing the method. It started by reading the whole part of the novel intensively and accurately, and then focused on the words and</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">phrases that have figurative senses. That technique was continued by taking a note of those figurative languages and classifying them in a form of comparative chart.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark13"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark14"></a>4.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Method and Technique of Analyzing Data</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The collected data were analysed descriptively by using qualitative method. The analysis of this study took several steps. The data were numbered into a comparative chart, classified into a specific comparative chart to be the related data to the research subject, then the data were analyzed according to the theory used in this study.</span></p>

<li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">5.1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Figurative Language</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">5.1.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Metonymy</span></p></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>
<table border="1">
<tr><td style="vertical-align:top;">
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Source Language</span></p></td><td style="vertical-align:top;">
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Target Language</span></p></td></tr>
<tr><td style="vertical-align:bottom;">
<p><span class="font1">1. Or in a wood, making </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">the kettle boil</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">-</span><span class="font1">; (Woolf 2000 : 130)</span></p></td><td style="vertical-align:bottom;">
<p><span class="font1">Atau di hutan, menunggu </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">air mendidih dari ketel</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">,</span><span class="font1">; (Josephine 2006 : 162)</span></p></td></tr>

<p><span class="font1">The word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">kettle</span><span class="font1"> has a figurative sense meaning </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">water</span><span class="font1">. Therefore, to transfer the meaning properly, it is translated into </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">‘air mendidih dari ketel’. Kettle</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">water</span><span class="font1"> are related because kettle is a form of container for water. It is categorized as metonymy.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h1><a name="bookmark15"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark16"></a>5.1.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Synecdoche</span></h1></li></ul>
<table border="1">

<p><span class="font1">1 </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">The feet </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">of those people busy about their activities</span><span class="font1">. (Woolf 2000 : 116)</span></p></td><td style="vertical-align:bottom;">
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Kaki-kaki </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">orang yang sibuk dengan segala kegiatan mereka </span><span class="font1">(Josephine 2006 : 144)</span></p></td></tr>
</table>
<p><span class="font1">The word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">feet</span><span class="font1"> has a figurative sense based on part-whole relationship. In the novel, it is described that </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">the feet of those people busy about their activities</span><span class="font1">. In this case, one specific member, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">the feet,</span><span class="font1"> of the class </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">body,</span><span class="font1"> is substituted for body. Logically, the feet are inseparable to the other part of the body. It is right that someone used her/his feet to move from one place to another place. Therefore, the</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">author used </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">‘the feet’</span><span class="font1"> in specific to depict the activities of the busy people. Indeed, the author is really talking about </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">‘the person’</span><span class="font1"> who is busy, not merely </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">‘the feet’</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>

<p><span class="font1">..pengemis-pengemis paling terhina yang duduk di tangga </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">(terus minum-minuman sampai makin tenggelam dalam kemiskinan)</span><span class="font1"> juga melakukan hal yang sama; (Josephine 2006 : 4)</span></p></td></tr>

<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Drink their downfall</span><span class="font1"> is an expression that cannot be understood literally. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Drink</span><span class="font1"> is defined as the action of taking liquid into your mount and swallows it (Hornby 1998 : 468), while downfall means the loss of a person’s money, power, social position, etc (Hornby 1998 : 460). It would not make sense if someone renders the meaning by combining those two dictionary definitions. Actually drink their downfall has an idiomatic meaning, which is drinking rapidly until it drops, until the drinker lose that she/she has.</span></p>

</table>
<p><span class="font1">The expression </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">unspeakable aged</span><span class="font1"> is used to exaggerate the age of someone, as if that there is no exact word to describe how old someone is. It is beyond description, inexpressible. In the story, it was told that Clarissa felt very young and at the same time, she felt very old. It happened when she walked through the St James’s Park, wondered about her decision not to marry Peter.</span></p>

<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">5.1.5 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Euphemism</span></p>
<table border="1">
<tr><td style="vertical-align:top;">

In the story, it was told that actually The Queen of England sat inside the car. It did not commonly happened there, which made people suddenly leave their work and stood in crowd to see The Queen. The </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">greatness</span><span class="font1"> is a polite word to refer to The Queen.</span></p>

<li>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Lexical Equivalents when Concepts are Shared</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2.1.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Nonliteral Lexical Equivalent</span></p></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>

Indeed, the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“berkaca”</span><span class="font1"> does not have a figurative sense any longer, but the meaning is remained.</span></p>

Therefore, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“empat puluh lima kilogram”</span><span class="font1"> was used to reach the lexical equivalent. It was needed to add description to transfer the proper meaning from the source language into the receptor language.</span></p>

However, the closest one is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">“yang Agung”,</span><span class="font1"> the exact word to name the Queen of England. This figurative language was translated using related words as equivalent, where the concept is shared.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark24"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark25"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h1></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Metonymy is the most figurative language that was found in the novel, than hyperbola, idiom, synecdoche, and euphemism. As noted, there are more than 250 figurative languages written in that novel. By using figurative languages, the author is successful to give the soul and emotion to the words, to the expression that were used to describe the personalities of each characters, the environment, etc. Therefore, whoever read the novel, they will not put it down before finish it.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">According to the theory of three approaches in finding the lexical equivalents, nonliteral lexical equivalent, descriptive phrases, related words as equivalent, generic – specific words, secondary and figurative senses, equivalence by modifying a generic word, equivalence by modifying a loan word, and equivalence by cultural substitutes and key terms are used. Among those ways, the nonliteral lexical equivalent is the most strategy applied in finding the adequate lexical equivalent.</span></p>
