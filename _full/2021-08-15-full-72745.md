<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>Malassezia sp.</span><span class="font3"> Infection Prevalence in Dermatitis Dogs in Badung Area</span></h1>
<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font2"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Putu Henrywaesa Sudipa<sup>1</sup>, Ketut Tono Pasek Gelgel<sup>1</sup>, and Putu Devi Jayanti<sup>2</sup></span></h2>
<p><span class="font1"><sup>1</sup>Departement of Veterinary Pathobiology, Faculty of Veterinary Medicine, Udayana University, Denpasar, Indonesia</span></p>
<p><span class="font1"><sup>2</sup>Departement of Veterinary Clinical Diagnose, Clinical Pathology and Radiology, Faculty of Veterinary Medicine, Udayana University, Denpasar, Indonesia Corresponding Author: </span><a href="mailto:henrywaesa@unud.ac.id"><span class="font1">henrywaesa@unud.ac.id</span></a></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Abstract</span><span class="font1">. Malasseziosis is a common fungal infection in dogs and it is secondary to the initial underlying dermatitis infection. These infections can worsen the prognosis of disease in dogs. The study was conducted on 26 dogs that were treated in several veterinary clinics in the Badung, Bali. This study aimed to determine the incidence rate of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp.</span><span class="font1"> in dogs with dermatitis. Samples were collected using tape smear method from the skin lesion and then by microscopic examination. The results were tabulated and analyze descriptively. The results showed that 15 of the 26 samples of dogs tested positive were infected with </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp.</span><span class="font1"> (58%). Infection is more common in male (60%) and geriatric dogs (40%). Lesions were more common in the ear, limb, vaginal and inguinal region of infected dogs</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Keywords:</span><span class="font1"> dog; tape smear; </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">I. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;INTRODUCTION</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Dogs are one of the pets that are very close to humans. The benefits of raising dogs are stated to increase the owner's motivation to exercise and can reduce stress levels [1]. In addition, in almost every home in the Balinese community it is reported that they raise dogs for various purposes. However, along with its development, many health problems have been observed in dogs, especially skin health problems.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Dermatitis cases are commonly observed in Bali with various infectious agents. This is supported by the statement of Wiryana et al. [2] which states the incidence of dermatitis caused by various infectious agents in street dogs in Bali is relatively high. Dermatitis cases on stray dogs in Bali generally cause skin lesions in the form of primary, secondary, and mixed disorders [3]. One of the infectious agents of concern and can cause problem to pets and pet owners is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1"> is a fungus (yeast) which is a normal and commensal flora found in the skin layers of mammals and birds [4], [5]. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1"> is classified as a dimorphic fungus since it is found in yeast and mycelial phases. This fungus is a lipophilic fungus that forms colonies in the stratum corneum of the skin [4], is oval or ellipsoidal with thick walls and without pseudomycelium production [6]. This type of leaf can be classified into 7 (seven) species. These species include </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia pachydermatitis, M. furfur, M. sympodialis, M. globosa, M. restricta, M. sloff </span><span class="font1">and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">M. obtusa</span><span class="font1"> [7], [8]. However, Guillot and Bond [5]</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">reported that </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1"> now forms a unique cluster with 18 (eighteen) species that live on the skin and mucosa of vertebrates.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">As a normal flora of the skin, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. generally found in limited numbers on the soles of the feet and ear canal [8], [9]. This causes </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. can still be isolated on the skin along the external ear canal without the occurrence of external otitis infection [10], [11]. Adiyati and Pribadi [12] stated that </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. is one of the opportunistic flora because it can cause mycosis infection in favorable conditions. However, if the conditions are favorable, there will be expansion of the lesion and infection.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Infection caused by </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. can occur either superficial or systemic. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia spp</span><span class="font1">. Infection can be a primary or secondary disease. The predisposition factors are increasing in the production of sebum from the skin of infected animals or decreasing in the quality of sebum, bacterial infection of the skin, accumulation of moisture and damaging that occur simultaneously [13], [6], hypersensitivity, endocrinopathy, and the presence of keratinization disorders [9], [5].</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. referred to as pathogenic microorganisms in animals and humans. The transition from commensal to pathogenic can occur in dogs with certain conditions [5]. Zoonotic events occur due to direct contact of infected dogs with humans. Morris et al. [14] stated that the transfer of the infection is common from infected pet dogs to their owners.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp.</span><span class="font1"> can infect various breeds of dogs but infection is more commonly reported in Westies, shih-tzu, basset hounds, and cocker spaniels [9]. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. Many have been reported in pet dogs or stray dogs in Indonesia, however the data on the prevalence of Malassezia infection in Bali has not been widely reported. Observing the impact of the losses caused by infection with </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. in dogs, this study aims to determine the prevalence of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. in dogs with dermatitis, especially in the Badung area, Bali.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">II. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;RESEARCH METHODS</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Data Collection Method</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Data were collected by examining skin samples of dermatitis dogs. Samples were collected from 26 dermatitis dogs treated at the veterinary clinic in the Badung area, Bali. The examination was carried out by conducting a general examination by observing clinical symptoms in each dog. Dogs that have skin infections were subjected to a skin cytology examination using the tape smear method, a simple diagnostic method that can be done to detect </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia spp</span><span class="font1">. [15], [5]. Data regarding sex, age, and location of lesions were recorded. Data related to age were divided into young age (≤ 12 months); adults (12-36 months); and geriatrics (≥ 36 months). The data were then described descriptively.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Tape Smear Examination</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Skin cytology examination aims to determine the presence of inflammatory cells, bacterial infections or fungal infections [9]. Samples were collected using tape smear method. This technique applies transparent plaster media (tape) which was affixed twice to the part of the skin lesion that is suspected of being infected with fungi. The plaster was attached to a sterile object glass [14]. The sample was then stained using </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Methylene Blue</span><span class="font1"> [4] and observed under a microscope with 400X magnification. Through microscopic examination, identification of the infecting fungus is then carried out. If present </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia </span><span class="font1">species such as </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia pachydermatis</span><span class="font1"> is characterized by its round to oval or classical peanut shape with monopolar budding. This lipophilic, non-lipid-dependent, non-mycelial saprophytic yeast organism is most often associated with </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia dermatitis</span><span class="font1"> (malasseziasis or </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia</span><span class="font1"> overgrowth) in dogs. Other </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia</span><span class="font1"> may uncommonly be noted as a cause of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia dermatitis </span><span class="font1">such as </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sympodialis</span><span class="font1">, which is smaller than </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia pachydermatis</span><span class="font1"> and has a more rounded bulbous shape and narrower-based monopolar budding [24]. The results of the were tabulated and explained descriptively.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">III. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;RESULT AND DISCUSSION</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">From the results of microscopic examination of the skin cytology, 26 dermatitis dogs collected using the tape smear method, showed positive results for </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. in 15 dogs (58%). The results of microscopic examination of the tape smear results were observed that the cell morphology was round to oval with thick walls and had hyphae with short and not straight stems (Figure 1). Microscopically, there was more than 5 </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. so the sample dogs tested positive for </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malasseziosis</span><span class="font1">. Nardoni et al. [16] stated that the detection of 1 </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. in 1 field of view showed a positive result of infection. Meanwhile, Borkar et al. [15] stated if more than 5 </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp.</span><span class="font1"> in the same field of view showed a positive result on the microscopic examination of the skin at 100X magnification.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia</span><span class="font1"> has a morphology that can be distinguished from other types of fungi. On microscopic examination, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia</span><span class="font1"> cells will be seen in the form of round cells with thick walls, monopolar buds, having hyphae with short and not straight stems [12], [8]. Seetha et al. [4] states that </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia</span><span class="font1"> cells are round, oval, or cylindrical in diverse sizes with 1 to 8µm diameter and reproduces asexually.</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/72745-1.jpg" alt="" style="width:231pt;height:196pt;">
<p><span class="font1">Figure 1. Microscopic examination results with 400X magnification of skin cytology samples in dermatitis dogs. The results showed positive </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia spp</span><span class="font1">. (Black Arrows)</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Based on gender, the positive results for </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. 15 dermatitis dogs consisted of 9 males (60%) and 6 females (40%) dogs (Table 1). It can be concluded that male dogs are more at risk of infection with </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. compared to female dogs. The same thing was reported by Seetha et al. [4] who stated that the cases of dermatitis due to </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia</span><span class="font1"> infection were higher in male dogs than female dogs. This statement is also supported by the research results of Conkova et al. [6] who stated that the</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">prevalence of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia pachydermatis</span><span class="font1"> infection was relative higher in dogs </span><span class="font0">of the male sex than in females.</span></p>

<p><span class="font1">Based on the age level of the infected dog, the highest infection results were found at the age above 36 months with an incidence percentage of 40%. The prevalence of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia pachydermatis</span><span class="font1"> infection is relatively higher in geriatric dogs than in young and adult dogs [6]. The more common infections in geriatric dogs may be attributed to the lower immunity of geriatric dogs compared to both young and adult dogs. Bond et al. [17] stated that both innate and specific immune responses play a significant role in the body's resistance to skin infections caused by fungi. Cellular immunity plays a vital role in the defense and recovery of conditions caused by exposure to </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia spp.</span></p>

<p><span class="font1">In this study, it was observed that there were several locations where the body of the dog had dominant skin lesions including the ears, vagina and inguinal, neck, abdomen, head, extremities, and back so that cytological samples were taken at these locations (Table 2).</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Lesions were more commonly observed in the ear (20%), vaginal and inguinal (20%), and extremities (20%) areas. This is in line with the statement of Nardoni et al. [18] as a potential pathogen, </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia spp</span><span class="font1">. can be isolated from the skin around the lips, chin, anal sac, vagina, rectum, and external ear canal [19]. Seetha et al.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">[4] &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;stated the initial infection of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia spp</span><span class="font1">. seen in the abdominal area of infected animals and then spread to other body areas such as axillary and inguinal.</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Parts of the body including the ears, extremities, vagina and inguinalis have a higher humidity level, which causes infectious lesions to develop more commonly in these areas than in other areas of the body. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia </span><span class="font1">development factors are associated with several environmental conditions, such as elevated temperature and humidity in the skin area or the location of animal rearing [6]. Matousek et al. [20] and Borkar et al. [15] stated that fungal colonies increased at high pH in certain skin areas. These results differ from reports of other researchers which state that </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia</span><span class="font1"> infection is higher in samples collected from the dorsal skin area [6] and the skin of the neck area [4]. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia spp</span><span class="font1">. produce keratinase enzymes and other enzymes that can digest protein complexes in the skin thus allowing these fungal species to grow deeper into the stratum corneum in animal skin and cause inflammatory reactions [4].</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Clinical signs at the beginning of infection are characterized by the presence of alopecia, local and general erythema, erythematous papules and macules, crusted skin, and the presence of scale [6]. In this study, clinical symptoms were observed in sample dogs suspected of being infected with </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia spp.</span><span class="font1"> in the form of hair loss, especially in the ears, extremities and back, moderate pruritus, scale and plaque, superficial oily skin, and skin hyperpigmentation. This is in line with Coyner's [9] he mentioned dogs with </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">malassezia spp</span><span class="font1">. generally develop clinical symptoms in the form of alopecia, lichenification, and generally, oily skin accompanied by a scale. Mild to high degrees of pruritus have been observed in infected dogs [21]. This is supported by Rostaher's [11] statement that almost all dogs with </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. will cause pruritus. Skin hyperpigmentation was observed in the ventral neck, axillary, and inguinal limbs [12]. Color changes have also been observed in the paws of infected dogs, where the skin of the claw area will be brownish red [11].</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The development of clinical sign is often associated with an imbalance in the normal homeostatic immunity of the infected animal [5]. Besides other predisposing factors that can trigger infection, namely an increase in sebum production or a decrease in the quality of sebum on the skin of infected animals, bacterial infection of the skin, accumulation of moisture and skin epidermal damage, hypersensitivity, endocrinopathy, and keratinization disorders [13], [6], [9]. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. Infection can worsen the case of dermatitis due to other agents. Gaitanis et al. [22] and Sihelska et al. [19] stated that </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. can participate as a cause of seborrheic dermatitis, dandruff, and folliculitis.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp.</span><span class="font1"> Infection is reported to be zoonotic from either infected animal to humans, especially animal owners if they have direct contact. Low immunity predisposes to easy transfer of infection between animals to humans. Pathological conditions in humans have been reported such as pityasis versicolar, seborrheic dermatitis, folliculitis, and systemic infections [19].</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. Infection can be local or general. </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. Infection can be chronic and allows for repeated exposure so that it requires a long therapy [17]. Antifungal treatments are generally used successfully to control fungal overgrowth [23]. This in turn affects the choice of antifungal drugs to be used. Sihelska et al. [21] stated that topical antifungal drugs are preferred for local fungal infections. Whereas for more general infections it is necessary to use systemic antifungal drugs and other necessary therapies that can support increased body immunity.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">IV. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;CONCLUSION</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1">. prevalence in dogs’ relative high in Badung Area with (58%), infects male dogs (60%) and geriatric dogs (40%), with lesions commonly found in the ear, limb, vaginal and inguinal areas with an incidence of 20% each. Dog age and sex predispose to the spread of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Malassezia sp</span><span class="font1"> infection in dogs.</span></p>
