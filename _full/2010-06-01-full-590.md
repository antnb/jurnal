<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>SUPPRESSION ABILITY OF CRUDE EXTRACT DERIVED FROM</span></h1>
<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>MARINE BIOTA AGAINST </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> f.sp. </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">vanillae</span></h1>
<p><span class="font9">I K</span><span class="font8">ETUT </span><span class="font9">S</span><span class="font8">UADA</span><span class="font9">, N</span><span class="font8">I </span><span class="font9">W</span><span class="font8">AYAN </span><span class="font9">S</span><span class="font8">UNITI </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Study Program of Agroecotechnology, Faculty of Agriculture, Udayana University, Bali</span></p>
<p><span class="font15">ABSTRACT</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">The objective of this research was to investigate suppression ability of marine biota extracts against </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font13"> f.sp. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">vanillae</span><span class="font13"> of vanilla stem rot. Samples were collected at intertidal zones and in the depth of 1-7 m from seven beaches in Bali. Screening of active compounds of biota extracts were conducted using inhibition zone of well diffusion method on Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA). The extract was tested </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">in-vitro</span><span class="font13"> in PDA medium using completely randomized design with three replicates. The methanolic extract of </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp. was able to suppress the growth of </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">F. oxysporum</span><span class="font13"> f.sp. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">vanillae</span><span class="font13"> effectively</span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">,</span><span class="font13"> with minimum inhibition concentration (MIC) of 0.05 %. The extract inhibited colony growth diameter and total mycelial dry weight.</span></p>
<p><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Keywords</span><span class="font13" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">: </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font13"> f.sp. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">vanillae, Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp.</span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">, well diffusion method.</span></p>
<p><span class="font15">INTISARI</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">Penelitian ini bertujuan untuk mengungkapkan aktivitas antimikroba ekstrak biota laut terhadap </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font13"> f.sp. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">vanillae</span><span class="font13"> penyebab busuk batang pada tanaman vanili. Sampel biota diambil dari daerah intertidal dengan kedalaman 1-7 m pada 7 pantai di Bali. Skrining aktivitas antibiotik dilakukan menggunakan zone hambat dengan metode difusi sumur pada media PDA. Pengujian dilakukan secara in vitro dengan rancangan acak lengkap dengan tiga ulangan. Penelitian ini membuktikan bahwa ekstrak methanol </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp. mampu menekan pertumbuhan </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">F. oxysporum</span><span class="font13"> f.sp. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">vanillae</span><span class="font13"> dengan konsentrasi hambat minimum (MIC) 0,05%. Ekstrak ini menekan diameter pertumbuhan koloni dan total berat kering miselium.</span></p>
<p><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci: Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font13"> f.sp. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">vanillae</span><span class="font13">, </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp., metode difusi sumur</span></p>
<p><span class="font15">INTRODUCTION</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">The increase of international competitive on trading triggers the effort of producers to produce vanilla beans in high quality without the presence of recidual pesticides. Controlling </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font13"> f.sp. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">vanillae, </span><span class="font13">(the pathogen of vanilla stem rot) using sinthetic compounds would result in high pesticide residue on agricultural products. This method is no longer accepted in a sustainable agriculture (Hadisutrisno, 2004).</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">The use of natural product derived from marine organism is promising, because the bioactive compound is generally safe and biodegradable. More over, the secondary metabolites from marine organisms are unique and often better than those of terrestrial biota (Anggadiredja, 2004). Those facts trigger people to explore antimicrobes from marine biotas particullarly from sedentary organisms. These organisms could not hinder from predator, therefore they develop defence mechanism by producing secondary metabolites including antifungial materials.</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">Some antibiotics derived from marine macro- and micro-algae have been developed, such as trichlorethane</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">and perchlorethane (Marasneh </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font13">., 1995). Some antifungi also have been extracted from marine biotas such as </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Sargassum despiense, Turbinaria decurrense, Cystoseira tamariscifolia</span><span class="font13"> and able to suppress </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium, Penicillium,</span><span class="font13"> and </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aspergillus</span><span class="font13"> sp. (Mabrouk </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font13">, 1985; Souhaili </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font13">, 2004).</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">At the intertidal zones of Serangan and Canggu beach, Bali, 25 species of algae, 27 sponges, and 7 coralls have been collected. The extract of these biotas has been fpund to have an inhibition activity against some fungi and bacteria (Suada </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font13">, 2008).</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">The purpose of this research was to investigate the suppression activities of the extract derived from marine biotas against </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font13"> f.sp. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">vanillae (Fusarium)</span><span class="font14">, </span><span class="font13">the causal agent of vanilla stem rot.</span></p>
<p><span class="font15">MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></p>
<p><span class="font14">Strain of Fungi and Marine Biota Samples</span></p>
<p><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13"> (</span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font13"> f.sp. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">vanillae</span><span class="font13">) was isolated and identified in Agricultural Biotechnology Laboratory, Udayana University from infected vanilla plants collected from the field in Tabanan, Bali. Single</span></p>

<p><span class="font13">spore isolation technique was applied to obtained single strain of </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13">. Samples of Marine biotas were collected at intertidal zones in the depth of 1-7 m at seven beaches (Serangan, Batu Bolong, Tanah Lot, Menjangan, Lovina, Tukad Abu, and Nusa Penida) of Bali Province.</span></p>
<p><span class="font14">Medium and Culture Conditions</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">The medium used for isolation of </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13"> was Matuo medium (Matuo, 1972). The medium consisted of 1.00 g K</span><span class="font10">2</span><span class="font13">PO</span><span class="font10">4</span><span class="font13">, 0.50 g KCl, 0.50 g MgSO</span><span class="font10">4</span><span class="font13">.7H</span><span class="font10">2</span><span class="font13">O, 0.01 g Fe-Na-EDTA, 2.00 g L-Asparagine, 20.00 g D-Galaktose, and 1.00 L aquadest. The medium was sterilized at 121<sup>o</sup>C for 20 min. Antimicrobe substances were added by 1.00 g PCNB 75% WP, 0.50 g Oxgall, 1.00 g Na</span><span class="font10">2</span><span class="font13">B</span><span class="font10">2</span><span class="font13">O</span><span class="font10">7, </span><span class="font13">and 0.30 g Streptomycin sulfate. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13"> were then cultured in potato dextrose agar medium (PDA, </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Nissui</span><span class="font13">) and potato dextrose broth (PDB, </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">DIFCO</span><span class="font13">) pH 4.5.</span></p>
<p><span class="font14">Well Diffusion Method</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">Two mililitres of spore suspension of </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13"> (2x10<sup>5 </sup>conidia/ml) were placed into petri dishes, then 20 ml PDA were added , shaked gently until the conidia well mixed. Over the solid medium of PDA wells were made by cork borer Ø 5 mm. The wells were filled with 40 µl conidium extracts in various concentrations , then incubated at room temperature for 48 h (Berghe and Vlietinck, 1991; Rios </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font13">., 1988). The inhibition zone appeared on the medium was examined. Through this experiment the minimum inhibition concentration (MIC) was determined.</span></p>
<p><span class="font14">Fresh Biomass Extraction</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">Two grams of marine biotas (Figure 1, Table 1) collected from 7 beaches were chopped, ground, then 5 ml solvents were added for maceration. Solvents used in this experiment were n-hexan, chloroform, aceton, ethanol, methanol, and water. The filtrate was put in eppendorf tubes, centrifuged at 5000 rpm and the supernatan was transferred into new eppendorf tubes. The filtrate aerated and bioassayed using well diffusion method (as described above) against </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13"> with the concentration of 50% (w/v) on PDA medium.</span></p>
<p><span class="font14">Dry Biomass Extraction</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">The extract </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp. was found as the most active extract and showed the widest inhibition zones toward the growth of </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13"> sp. Its dry material was then tested. The biota biomass was air dried (up to 12% water content, undirect sun light), then ground into powdered. Maceration of the powdered biotas was diluted in methanol at the ratio of 1:2 (w/v) at room temperature for 24 h. The filtrate was evaporated in vacuum rotary evaporator at 40<sup>o</sup>C. The crude extract obtained was freeze dried.</span></p>
<p><span class="font14">Inhibition on Colony Growth Diameter</span></p>
<p><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13"> colony plug, 5 mm in diameter, of 3 days culture was placed on the centre of PDA medium in Petri dish in various extract concentrations (0, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15, and 0.20%). The cultures were incubated for 7 days at room temperature (fungi on controlled culture were growing and covered all surface of the medium). The diameter of growing colonies was measured daily.</span></p>
<p><span class="font14">Inhibition of Mycelial Dry Weight</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">The mycelia of the treatments above were harvested by adding 1% HCl, then oven dried at 80<sup>o</sup>C, up to constant weight. Mycelia harvested from every treatments above were weighed.</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">This experiment was also run in PDB liquid medium. The cultures were incubated for 7 days at room temperature and the mycelia were filterred using Whatmann paper no 2. The mycelia were oven dried at 80<sup>o</sup>C up to constant weight . All data were analysed with Complete Random Design and the treatments were analised using DMRT 5% differentiation.</span></p>
<p><span class="font15">RESULTS</span></p>
<p><span class="font14">Diversity of Marine Biotas Collected from Bali Beaches</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">Fifty three types of marine biotas belong to algae, sponges, and hydrozoa were found and collected from seven beaches in Bali. Twelve biotas exhibit antifungi against </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13"> e.g.: algae: </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Gracilaria arcuata, Eucheuma cottonii;</span><span class="font13"> spongess: </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Haliclona</span><span class="font13"> sp., </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Tedaria ignis,</span><span class="font13"> and </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Spongilla </span><span class="font13">sp.; corals: </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp. (Figure 1; Table 1).</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">MIC is the smallest concentration an extract that would inhibit the growth of microbes visually after two days incubation (Andrews, 2006). MIC is refers to susceptibility of microbe against ones extract. According to Hoffmann </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font13">. (1993) and Andrews (2006), the MIC extract less than 0.1% is reliable to be tested. Forexample, if the extract of </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp. in methanol exhibited inhibition zone of 58 mm, then its MIC less than 0.1% was tested for the next steps.</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/590-1.jpg" alt="" style="width:248pt;height:166pt;">

<p><span class="font13">According to LIPI (The Indonesian Institute of Science ) identification, the </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp. contains the most active compound as the marine animal, and its classification is as follows: Kingdom Animalia, Phylum Cnidaria (Coelenterata), Class Hydrozoa, Ordo Hydroida, Subordo Thecata, Family Aglaopheniidae, Genus </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13">, Spesies </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp.</span></p>

<p><span class="font15">DISCUSSION</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">Biodiversity of marine biota found at Bali beaches containing bioactivity compounds were 12 species that comprised of algae, sponges and corrals. The most effective one was animal marine biota </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia </span><span class="font13">sp. with MIC value of 0.05% based on inhibition on germination spore of </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13">. Based on Hoffmann </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font13">. (1993) and Andrews (2006) criterias, the extract with MIC less than 0.1% is reliable to be tested, therefore</span></p><img src="https://jurnal.harianregional.com/media/590-3.jpg" alt="" style="width:248pt;height:155pt;">
<p><span class="font6">Figure 3. The effect of </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font6"> sp. extract on </span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font6"> mycelia</span><span class="font6" style="font-style:italic;">.</span><span class="font6"> A: Colony affected by extract at concentrations of 0%, 0.05%, 0.1% (w/v) and methanol; B and C: Control; D, E, and F: abnormal mycelia: spirally wrinkled at 0.1% concentration.</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">methanol extract of </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp. could be tested for the next steps.</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">Figure 2 and Table 2 showed that the extract significantly inhibited the colony growth and total inhibition (100%) occurred at concentration of 0.15%. The inhibition occurred up to day seventh of treatments. This result suggested that the extract is fungitoxic.</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">The extract significantly suppressed the dry weight of mycelia compared to control. The higher the concentration of treatments, the higher the suppression against mycelial dry weight. The mycelial dry weight at 0.15% was significantly lower than other treatments. The mycelia on treatments 0.15% and above were not able to grow. Therefore, the mycelia dry weight counted at treatments 0.15% and 0.20% was the mycelia from the original inoculums. This means that the extract was able to inhibit </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium</span><span class="font13"> at the mycelium stages.</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">One symptom of inhibition on mycelium growth was the wrinkled of mycelium which was caused by plasmolysis that occurred particularly on young myceliua (Figure 3). According to Sugiharso (1980) that bioactive compounds of fungicides commonly cause plasmolysis on </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium.</span><span class="font13"> The bioactive compound acts as chelating agent of membrane molecule and caused leaking in membrane cell. Consequently, K<sup>+</sup>, Na<sup>+</sup>, Cl<sup>-</sup>, Ca<sup>+2</sup> ions from cytoplasm come out of the cell. This could happen due to the outer cell osmotic pressure was higher than inner cell. Therefore water run out of cell and cell then becomes dry and wrinkled. Furthermore, Stenersen (2004) stated that the common symptoms caused by antifungi compound are distortion of cell, swelling, and spiraly wrinkling cell.</span></p>
<p><span class="font15">CONCLUSSION</span></p>
<p><span class="font13">Among all marine biotas collected from Bali island beaches there were 12 species showed inhibition activity against </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Fusarium oxysporum</span><span class="font13"> f.sp. </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">vanillae</span><span class="font13">. The most active extract was from </span><span class="font13" style="font-style:italic;">Aglaophenia</span><span class="font13"> sp. with MIC of 0.05%. The extract was able to suppress colony growth and mycelial dry weight. The mycelia affected by the extract were wrinkled.</span></p>
