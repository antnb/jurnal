<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstract</span></h2>
<p><span class="font2">The title of this study is </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Positive Politeness Strategies Used by Characters in an Animation Movie entitled “Finding Dory”.</span><span class="font2"> This study is aimed at finding out the types of positive politeness strategies used to extend and respond by the characters to express the utterance in movie script and also what factors that influence the usage of positive politeness strategies in the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">“Finding Dory”</span><span class="font2"> movie. The analysis of this study is concerned with the utterance of positive politeness strategies of the characters. The data of this study were collected through conducting direct observation of the movie script </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">“Finding Dory”</span><span class="font2"> produced by</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pixar"><span class="font2"> Pixar Animation Studios </span></a><span class="font2">and directed by Andrew Stanton (2016). The collected data then were identified, classified, and analyzed qualitatively. The data derived from the script of the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">“Finding Dory”</span><span class="font2"> movie were collected and then, analyzed the polite expressions of characters used while doing conversation. After that, the selected data were analyzed based on the theory of politeness proposed by Brown and Levinson. Based on the result of the analysis, it was found that when extending and responding the utterance that contains positive politeness strategies, the characters used intensify interest to H (3), in-group identity markers (4), presuppose/raise/assert common ground (7), assert or presuppose S's knowledge of and concern for H's wants (9), offer, promise (10), be optimistic (11), including both S and H in the activity (12), give (or ask for) reasons (sub-strategy 13), assume or assert reciprocity (14). The most prominent factor that influences the characters to extend and respond the positive politeness strategies is the circumstances: sociological variables.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Keywords: Politeness Strategies, Positive Politeness, Face Threatening Acts</span></p>

<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">In communication, people have to consider the politeness principle, which is a set or systems in conversation when speaking. The principle is to consider the feeling of the hearer. In this case politeness is needed to reduce the factors of conflict or Face Threat. Polite utterances are often used in asking (requesting), offering, complaining, apologizing, because those request tend to threat to others.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">A speaker must show awareness of the hearer’s face and self-image through various strategies. To be successful in interaction with others, one has to follow some important strategies to be polite. People usually expect to be respected in social interactions. On this basis, there are four main strategies for performing Face Threatening Acts (FTA) as follows: Bald on Record, Positive Politeness, Off Record, and Negative Politeness.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">This study discusses Positive Politeness from an animation movie script entitled </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">&quot;Finding Dory&quot;</span><span class="font2"> by Andrew Stanton as a source of data because the movie set numerous records, including the</span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest-grossing_openings_for_animated_films"><span class="font2"> biggest animated opening of all time</span></a><span class="font2"> </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest-grossing_openings_for_animated_films"><span class="font2">in North America </span></a><span class="font2">and has become the biggest Pixar film in China. The dialogue of the movie script was analyzed in order</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">to get positive politeness strategies and ways of characters delivering the politeness strategies in the movie script.</span></p>

<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis of Positive Politeness Strategies in a Movie Script Entitled </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">“Finding Dory”</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">In this section, there are several types discussed and explained through the essence analysis of positive politeness strategies in the movie script entitled </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">&quot;Finding Dory&quot;.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>5.2.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis of strategy 3: Intensify interest to H</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Charlie: Dory, Dory, Dory! Kelp cake! Undertow, sweetie.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Jenny : </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;text-decoration:underline;">Remember, honey.</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> We have to stay away from the undertow.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The utterance is a directive speech (order) which indicates that the speaker (S) is telling the hearer (H) to pay attention and stay away from the undertow. Based on the speech, the words </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">'remember'</span><span class="font2"> and </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">'always'</span><span class="font2"> function to cause involvement for the H to pay attention to the speaker's speech.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>5.2.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis of strategy 4: Use ingroup identity markers</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Young Dory : Okay, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">daddy.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Charlie &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;: No, no. Not </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">daddy</span><span class="font2">, I'm</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">the nice fish that wants to be your friend, okay?</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The usage of group identity </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">'daddy'</span><span class="font2"> in the speech confirms that speakers(S) and hearers (H) have a balanced position and close relationship using group identity markers. Brown and Levinson (1987: 112) proposed that this</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">strategy includes in-group usage of address forms of language or dialect.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis of strategy 7:</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Presuppose/raise/assert common ground</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Jenny : </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Dory, </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;text-decoration:underline;">what happens if</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">...</span><span class="font2">You know, if you're gone for too long. And </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;text-decoration:underline;">what if</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"> </span><span class="font2">you get confused and that makes you distracted. And </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;text-decoration:underline;">what if</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">...</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Dory : Mom. Mom. I lose you again?</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The directive conversation (request) indicates that the speaker is engaged in a strategy by generating a perception (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">'what happens if / what if ...ʼ</span><span class="font2">, ) of her speech to bring the other person more involved in the interaction created by the speaker.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2.4 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis of strategy 9: Assert or presuppose S's knowledge of and concern for H's wants</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Jenny: You know, I like this Marlin already.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Dory :</span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Yeah. And then somehow we found Nemo. Or did he find me? I don't know. But, you know what, Nemo is the sweetest. He just never gives up on me no matter what. </span><span class="font2">What would Dory do?</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In a directive conversation (advise), it states that speaker and hearer are cooperators. The speaker (Dory) affirms and implies the knowledge of the hearer's desire as a shared desire. Dory (speaker) convinces her parents by telling how she and Marlin struggle to find Nemo in a long adventure.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark24"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark25"></a>5.2.5 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis of strategy 10: offer, promise</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Jenny : You found us.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Charlie: </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">I'm never letting you go again.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Jenny : Oh my missing girl, you're here.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The directive dialogue (tell) above shows that the speaker makes a promise. Through a statement to the hearer by saying </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">'I'm never letting you go again'</span><span class="font2">, the speaker tries to give positive confidence and face to the statement given to the hearer in order to redress the potential threat of positive face threatening acts.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark26"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark27"></a>5.2.6 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis of strategy 11: Be optimistic</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">Fluke : </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Oh boy, this is gonna be good! </span><span class="font2">What the... Gerald! Have you lost your marbles?</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Rudder : Don't get used to it, Gerald!</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The directive speech shows a sense of optimism. Fluke is very optimistic that they can escape together if they stop the truck by saying </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ʻOh boy, this is gonna be good!ʼ</span><span class="font2">.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2.7 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Analysis of Sub-strategy 12:</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Include both S and H in the activity</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Dory : </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;">Let's go, we are stopping that truck.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Fluke : This I've got to see. Rudder : Yeah, yeah, me too!</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">The speaker involves participants by using the pronoun </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">'we'</span><span class="font2">. By using </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">ʻweʼ </span><span class="font2">as an inclusive pronoun, the participants will feel involved in the activity suggested by the speaker (Dory).This strategy is done to save the face of the opponent.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<p><span class="font2">Jenny : Exactly!</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">In the utterance above, the speaker (Charlie) asks Dory and immediately gives reason to the hearer (Dory).By asking and giving these reasons, the speaker is being optimistic that the hearer can do it as a good cooperator by utilizing one of the activities that may be remembered and performed.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<p><span class="font2">The speaker expects and needs help from the hearers by doing FTA through command. By submitting to the instruction, the hearers will perform an action of the speaker’s desire and is followed by the </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">'effect'</span><span class="font2"> statement if the hearer fails to perform the action.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark30"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark31"></a>5.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Factors Influencing of Positive</span><br><br><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark32"></a>Politeness</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font2">There are some factors that influence speakers to use positive politeness strategy in order to criticize the hearer’s behavior.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark33"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark34"></a>5.3.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The Payoff: &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;a priory</span><br><br><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark35"></a>consideration.</span></h2></li></ul>

<p><span class="font2">face and to satisfy H’s positive face. There are some respects of the speakers that make attentions to the hearers.</span></p>

This strategy is used by the speaker because the speaker wants to satisfy the positive face of H.</span></p>
