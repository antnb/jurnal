<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus</span><span class="font5"> is an important genius for economy and human health but a susceptible genius against biotic and abiotic stresses, so it needs to improved programs. Different basal media (MS, ½MA, ⅓MS and DKW) and different kind and concentrations of plant growth regulators i.e. BA, KIN, 2ip, ZE and TDZ (0 – 2 mg/l) and NAA, IAA and IBA (0 – 2 mg/l) added with 30 g/l sucrose, 3 g/l active charcoal and 7.5 g/l bacteriological agar] were used for organogenesis include shooting and rooting, also callusing from nodal explant of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus</span><span class="font5"> l</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">atifolia</span><span class="font5">. MS medium supplemented with 1 mg/l BA, and 0.01 mg/l NAA is the best media for multiple shoot induction on nodal explants and elongation of them. Other cytokinins had not significant effects on shoot induction and multiplication. Using of 0.01 mg/l IBA instead of 0.01 mg/l NAA on medium with 1 mg/l BA, led to multiple shoot induction on nodal explant indirectly. Rooting was induced on DKW medium plus 1.5 mg/l NAA in the best way compared another media. Both direct and indirect organogenesis (multiple shoot induction) were carried out on media with very similar contents. So we can use very simple and practical methods tissue culture for different improved programs in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus</span><span class="font5"> genius genius.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Keywords</span><span class="font5">: </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus latifolia</span><span class="font5">, direct organogenesis, indirect organogenesis, micropropagation</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus</span><span class="font5"> fruits are one of the most important and the most economic fruits in Iran alike all over the world. They are very desirable duo to their very good pleasant aroma and taste also due to their role in nutritional benefits and human health. </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus </span><span class="font5">fruits contain a variety of vitamins, minerals, fiber, and phytochemicals such as carotenoids, flavonoids, and limonoids, which appear to have biological activities and health benefits. There is considerable evidence that </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus</span><span class="font5"> fruit have antioxidant</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">and antimutagenic properties and positive associations with bone, cardiovascular, and immune system health (Codoner – French and Valls – Belles 2010).</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus</span><span class="font5"> cultivation affects by many biotic and abiotic stresses. For example Witches Broom Disease of Lime (WBDL) as a biotic stress is a serious disease for some </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus</span><span class="font5"> species if not controlled. WBDL that its agent is “Candidatus </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Phytoplasma aurantifolia</span><span class="font5">” has been reported from Iran since 2000 (Bove </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2000). Application of tolerant and resistant rootstocks and</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">varieties is the best way to overcome some plant diseases such as WBDL especially by using micropropagation that is an alternative method of vegetative propagation. It offers many unique advantages over conventional propagation methods such as rapid multiplication of valuable genotypes, expeditious release of improved varieties, production of disease – free quality planting materials, non – seasonal production (round the year), higher yield, earlier and more vigorous sucker production, uniform and mass production in relatively short periods of time, germplasm conservation and facilitating their easy international exchange (Datta </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2017). Also genetic manipulation facilitates rapid plant improvement, compared to conventional breeding methods, especially in cases where changes through the addition of one or more genes are necessary, while preserving cultivar integrity(Dutt </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2011).</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Micropropagation is a method to produce genetically identical plantlets by using tissue culture techniques (Chieri, 2001). On the other hand tissue culture and micropropagation are an essential prerequisite for genetic manipulation.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">According to the mentioned above and emphasize to direct and indirect role of tissue culture in plant improvement programs (micropropagation and genetic manipulation respectively), using of tissue</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">culture methods for </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus</span><span class="font5"> genius genius is very imperative. In this research plantlet regeneration using nodal explant and two different form of organogenesis (direct and indirect) was studied in one of the most important species of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus</span><span class="font5"> named </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus latifolia</span><span class="font5">. It is necessary to mention that two different form of organogenesis (with or without callusing phase) was done with very small changes in content of media.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>MATERIALS AND METHODS</span></h1>
<h1><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>Preparation of plant materials and establishment of explants</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">One year old scions of </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus latifolia </span><span class="font5">were prepared from Jahrom, Fars province, Iran. Shoots with 12 – 15 cm length were used as explant. This manner for preparing explant (using shoots with 12 – 15 cm length was done in the second and the third years of establishment of scions in greenhouse).</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">After transferring of shoots measuring 12 – 15 cm length from greenhouse to laboratory and defoliating of them, shoots were cut to segment measuring 7 – 8 mm with one node. Segments were sterilized with 25% commercial hypochlorite sodium for 50 min then were rinsed in sterile distilled water 4 – 5 times. Explants were culture on MS (Murashige and Skoog 1962) media without any plant growth regulators.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Shoot induction and multiplication</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">MS medium supplemented with 0.01 -2 mg/l cytokinins consist of BA, KIN, 2iP, ZE and TDZ and 0.01 - 2 mg/l auxins consist of NAA and IBA plus 3% sucrose and 0.75% bacteriological agar were tested for shoot and multiplication. All of media were adjusted to pH 5.8 then were autoclaved at 120ºC. Media distributed in petri dishes measuring 9 cm. Each petri dish contained 9 explants with 4 replicates. The mean number of regenerated shoots and the height of them were recorded after one month.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>Elongation of regenerated shoots</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">When height of regenerated shoots reached to 1 cm, subculturing of them were acted. For subculturing, media were used consist of different combinations of 0 - 2 mg/l KIN, GA</span><span class="font2">3</span><span class="font5">, BA, NAA and IAA.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>Root induction at the base of regenerated shoots and whole plantlets regeneration</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">MS, ½MS and ⅓MS and DKW (Driver and Kuniyuki 1984) media with 0 - 2 mg/l auxins such as NAA, IBA and IAA were used for rooting at the base of regenerated shoots with sufficient growth (1 - 1.5 cm). </span><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">Adaptation of plantlets to natural environment</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Whole plantlets resulted from rooting of regenerated shoots, were taken out of glass media and were washed with running tap water for erasing of agar around the roots then plant out in clear disposable glass consist of sterilized soil and perlite (1:1). For moisture retention of plantlets the opening of the pots</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">(clear disposable glass) were covered with another clear disposable glass.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>Experimental design and data analysis</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">Experiments were set up in a completely randomized design and the means (the mean number and the mean height of regenerated shoots and roots) were compared with Duncan Multiple Range Test. Data were subjected to ±SD.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>RESULTS AND DISCUSSION</span></h1>
<h1><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>Effect of different types of cytokinins and auxins on shoot induction and multiplication</span></h1>
<p><span class="font5">Direct and indirect organogenesis (shoot formation) was accomplished in two forms, in the sense that the regenerated shoots with different length, number and morphological appearances were propagated on nodal explants. This difference related to cytokinin type at first. The effect of BAP compared to other cytokinins was incomparable [Fig 1. (a</span><span class="font2">1 </span><span class="font5">- a</span><span class="font2">2</span><span class="font5">)]. On MS media supplemented with 0.01 - 2 mg/l BAP at least 2 and maximum 8 regenerated shoots were sprouted from the single node of explants in the event that the number of regenerated shoots on MS medium, with 0.01 - 2 mg/l other cytokinins such as KIN, ZE, 2ip and TDZ was 2 maximum [Fig 1. b</span><span class="font2">1 </span><span class="font5">- b</span><span class="font2">4</span><span class="font5">)]. Also the regenerated shoots on MS medium with different concentration of BAP were big, and have 2 - 3 leaves with grown leaf blade but the regenerated micro shoots on MS medium</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">with different concentrations (0.01 – 2 mg/l) of said cytokinins were very small with very small leaves blade. Positive and potent effect of BAP on shoot induction is in agreement with reports of other researchers. What is important in the report of these researchers is that the positive effect of BAP on shooting in plant tissue culture includes, plants belonging to different families. For example [</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Cucurbita maxima</span><span class="font5"> Duch (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Cucurbitaceae</span><span class="font5">) (Lee </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2003)]<sup>,</sup> [</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Ruta graveolunse</span><span class="font5"> L. (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Rutaceae</span><span class="font5">) (Ahmad </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2010)], [(</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Lycopersicon esculentum</span><span class="font5"> L. (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Solanaceae</span><span class="font5">) (Rai </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al.</span><span class="font5">, 2012)], and [Japanese pear (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Rosaceae</span><span class="font5">) (Kadota </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2001)].</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Also how to use of cytokinin, solely and or plus other plant growth regulators was affective on shoot induction and status of it, so that on MS media with different concentrations of BAP increasing of BAP concentration led to increasing of the number of regenerated shoots (5.5 per explants on MS medium plus 2 mg/l BAP). But on MS media supplemented with different concentrations of BAP and 0.01 mg/l NAA increasing of BAP concentration led to decreasing of the number of regenerated shoots. So the maximum number of regenerated shoots resulted on MS medium with 1 mg/l BAP and 0.01 mg/l NAA (6.2 per explants) (Table 1). There are reports either similar our result or contradictory our result. In </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Chlorophytum borivilianum</span><span class="font5"> the increasing of BAP concentration (2 – 3 mg/l)</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">had significantly positive effect on shoot multiplication (Mehdi Farshad </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2014). But in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus lemon</span><span class="font5"> L</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">.</span><span class="font5"> cv. Kaghzi Kalan with increasing of BAP concentration shoot proliferation decreased (Komal </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font5"> 2013).</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">In addition of cytokinin types, auxin type influenced on the form of shoot initiation, also. As mentioned above due to the very good effect of BAP on shoot induction and multiplication of regenerated shoots, we focused on continuing of experiments using BAP. The continuing of experiments showed that how to use the BAP solely or with other plant growth regulators such as NAA, was affective on shoot induction and different form of it, also. What it seems in the first moment is, there is not any difference between media with different concentrations of BAP and NAA and different concentrations of BAP and IBA. As it can be seen the maximum number of regenerated shoots on media with similar proportion of cytokinin and auxin but different kind of them is equal. But there is an important difference between two media. On MS medium with 0.01 – 2 mg/l BAP and 0.01 mg/l IBA, callus induction on nodal explant in the place of nods were initiated at first then after 2 – 3 weeks with differentiation of calli cells to initial forms of vegetative meristem, and appearance of the initial forms of leaves from this meristem, indirect organogenesis was done [Fig 1. (c</span><span class="font2">1 </span><span class="font5">–</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">c</span><span class="font2">4</span><span class="font5">)]. Therefore with an inappreciable alternation in content of media direct organogenesis was altered indirect organogenesis. In another plant from </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus </span><span class="font5">genius, Sweet orange (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus sinensis</span><span class="font5"> L.), an inappreciable alternation in plant taxonomy or in fact genetic characteristics of plants (Navelina sweet orange variety and genetically close variety of it namely Pineapple sweet orange variety) using 3 mg/l BAP led to different results. 3 mg/l BAP led to efficient shoot organogenesis in Pineapple but not in Navelina. Furthermore different effects were observed when the auxin NAA was added to BAP – containing regeneration media. Although NAA addition at 0.5 mg/l enhanced cambial callus formation, number of shoots and their elongation in Navelina, the contrary effect was observed in Pineapple (Rodeiguez </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2008). It is clear that the reason of these observations is complex interactions between plants with different genetic characteristics and the effect of plant growth regulators.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5" style="font-weight:bold;">Elongation of regenerated shoots Length of regenerated shoots on induction media</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">In some induction media the length of regenerated shoots reached the approximately 4 cm (Fig 1.d</span><span class="font2">1</span><span class="font5">). However for attaining more growth of regenerated shoots, we sub cultured of them on media with new composition (Table 2). As it is perceived sub culturing of shoots on new media did not lead remarkable</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">result and longitudinal growth of shoot was very low and caused of weakness and wanes of shoots (on media containing GA</span><span class="font2">3</span><span class="font5">) . Our conclusion about effect of GA</span><span class="font2">3 </span><span class="font5">for longitudinal growth have been proved by other researchers (Perez – Tornero </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2009). In some cases duo to beneficial influence of active charcoal in tissue culture (micropropagation, orchid seed germination, somatic embryogenesis, anther culture, synthetic seed production, protoplast culture, rooting, bulb formation especially stem elongation) (Denis Thomas, 2008)., recently mentioned substance was added to media, but, no considerable result was obtained. Despite of many positive effects, negative effects have been reported about active charcoal in tissue culture. For example microspore embryogenesis in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Brassica oleraceae</span><span class="font5"> (Da Silva and Dias 1999), embryogenic callus in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Phoenix camariensis</span><span class="font5"> (Huong </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 1999) and micropropagation in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Gymnema sylvestre </span><span class="font5">(Komalavalli and Rao 2000). One of the most acceptable reason, for these observations is concentration of active charcoal from a range of 0.002 g/l to 150 g/l differed wildly in plant tissue culture<sup>18</sup>. It is usually stated that the rooting of little shoots especially in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus </span><span class="font5">genius is very weak and so an intermediate phase is necessary and so the difficulty of rooting is still one of the major obstacles to successful micropropagation (Samaan </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2014). According to results of this research</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">not only the little shoots have potential of rooting but also subculturing of them on media for elongation is not necessary and regenerated shoots on induction media grew successfully [Fig 1. d</span><span class="font2">2 </span><span class="font5">– d</span><span class="font2">4 </span><span class="font5">)].</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">The results of rooting experiments showed that, two important factors led to root induction that were, increased concentration of exogenous auxin and decreased concentration of media salts.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">On full – strength MS and DKW media, high auxin (NAA) concentration promoted in vitro rooting. Successful rooting numbering 3 – 4 on the base of each regenerated shoot was observed 2 – 3 weeks after culturing on above mention media. The roots were white and potent also and the mean length of them reached to 3 cm in the best condition in terms of the content of media (Fig 1. e).</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">Another successful medium for rooting, were media with decreased concentrations MS salt (½MS and ⅓MS) supplemented with IBA and IAA (Table 3). On these media rooting was occurred after 4 weeks approximately. The roots were thick and yellowish and numbering 2 – 3, but were unavailing (for plantlet production) because connection and cohesion, and the other hand vascular relation of them was very loose with the base of regenerated shoot so the most of them were cut during the transfer of plantlets</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">from culture glasses. So it is observed that auxin is necessary for rooting according to results of this research. There are many reports that show, auxin is necessary for </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus</span><span class="font5"> rooting. According to Katsias and Roussos (Kotsias and Koussos 2001). Dipping the base of the explants in a 50% ethanol solution of IBA at 1000 mg l for 5 s resulted in 80% rooting with subsequent 90% survival of these explants, during acclimatization under mist.</span></p>
<p><span class="font5">In two sweet orange (</span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus sinensis</span><span class="font5"> L. Osb), pineapple and pera root formation and development decreased in both sweet orange cultivars concomitant with the reduction of medium strength. The α-naphtaleneacetic acid was important during the root differentiation phase, but its combination with indole-3-butyric acid was essential for root elongation (Mendes </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font5">., 2011). Also in </span><span class="font5" style="font-style:italic;">Citrus sinensis</span><span class="font5"> L. Local Cv. effect of auxins and auxins like substance (IBA, NAA, IAA, 2, 4-D and 2, 4, 5-T) and type of cutting on rooting ability of orange cuttings were tested. The results showed that the overall rooting responses was higher in the treatment with IBA and NAA rather than with IAA, 2,4-D and 2,4,5-T (Khursheed and Salih 2007).</span></p>
