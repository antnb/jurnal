<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Abstract</span></h2>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Staphylococcus aureus</span><span class="font2"> (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font2">) is one of the common causes of bovine mastitis worldwide. Inappropriate use of antimicrobials in cattle farms contributes to the increase of the antimicrobial resistance of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font2">. This study aimed to investigate the antimicrobial resistance profiles of </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus </span><span class="font2">isolated from cattle milk. Twelve </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font2"> isolated from cattle milk in Bogor, Indonesia, were tested against seven antimicrobials using Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion susceptibility test. The results revealed that 58.3 % of isolates were multidrug-resistant </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font2">. The </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font2"> isolates were resistant to penicillin G (66.7 %), ampicillin (66.7 %), vancomycin (58.3 %), and bacitracin (58.3 %). Most of the isolates were susceptible to ciprofloxacin (91.7 %), gentamicin (66.7 %), and chloramphenicol (66.67 %). If clinically indicated, treatment of mastitis should consider the antimicrobial susceptibility of the causative agent.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Keywords: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Staphylococcus aureus;</span><span class="font2"> antimicrobials; resistance; bovine mastitis</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>Abstrak</span></h2>
<p><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Staphylococcus aureus</span><span class="font2"> (</span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font2">) merupakan salah satu penyebab utama mastitis pada sapi di seluruh dunia. Penggunaan antimikroba secara tidak tepat di peternakan sapi menyebabkan peningkatan bakteri yang resistan terhadap antimikroba. Penelitian ini bertujuan untuk menginvestigasi profil resistansi antimikroba </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font2"> yang diisolasi dari susu sapi. Dua belas isolat </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font2"> yang diisiolasi dari sampel susu sapi di Bogor, Indonesia, diuji terhadap tujuh antimikroba menggunakan metode agar difusi menurut Kirby-Bauer. Hasil penelitian menunjukkan bahwa 58.3 % isolat merupakan </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus </span><span class="font2">resistan terhadap beberapa antimikroba. Isolat </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font2"> yang diuji resistan terhadap penisilin G (66,7 %), ampisilin (66,7 %), vankomisin (58,3 %), dan basitrasin (58,3 %). Sebagian besar isolat sensitif terhadap siprofloksasin (91,7 %), gentamisin (66,7 %), dan kloramfenikol (66,67 %). Pengobatan mastitis harus mempertimbangkan resistansi antimikroba dari bakteri penyebab penyakit.</span></p>
<p><span class="font2">Kata kunci: </span><span class="font2" style="font-style:italic;">Staphylococcus aureus;</span><span class="font2"> antimikroba; resistan; mastitis</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">Bovine mastitis is one of the most common diseases affecting the quantity and quality of world milk production (Ruegg, 2017; Wang </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2022). Mastitis incurs various costs, including decreased milk production, condemnation of milk due to antibiotic residues, veterinary expenses, and culling of chronically infected cattle (Nurhayati and Martindah, 2015; Abebe </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2016). </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Staphylococcus aureus</span><span class="font3"> (</span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3">), a Gram-positive bacterium, is one of the common causes of bovine mastitis worldwide (Ruegg, 2017, Abebe </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al., </span><span class="font3">2016; Chaalal </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2016).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">A previous study reported that the treatment of subclinical and clinical mastitis in Indonesia is still not effective because it generally uses broad-spectrum antimicrobials without conducting a specific analysis of the causative agent</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">(Nurhayati and Martindah, 2015). Antimicrobials often used in farms include penicillin, tetracycline, aminoglycosides, and macrolides (Meutia </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2016). Inappropriate use of antimicrobials causes antimicrobial resistance, which is now a global problem. Previous studies reported that antimicrobial-resistant bacteria were isolated from cattle in Indonesia (Klobongona </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2019; Tyasningsih </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2021; Rotinsulu </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2022). In order to maximise the benefits of antimicrobial stewardship, continuous monitoring of bacterial pathogens for antimicrobial resistance is essential. The use of antimicrobials to treat bacterial infections caused by </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> in cattle farms encourages the investigation of antimicrobial resistance of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus </span><span class="font3">isolated from cattle milk.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">This study aimed to investigate the antimicrobial resistance patterns of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolated from cattle milk against penicillin G, ampicillin, vancomycin, gentamicin, bacitracin, ciprofloxacin, and chloramphenicol. Furthermore, the results of this study can help clinicians in selecting appropriate antimicrobials for treating mastitis caused by </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3">.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>RESEARCH METHODS</span></h2>
<h2><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Materials and Equipment</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">Materials used in this study were twelve </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolates, crystal violet, lugolâ€™s iodine, safranin, H</span><span class="font0">2</span><span class="font3">O</span><span class="font0">2 </span><span class="font3">3%, glucose fermentation test, 0.5 McFarland standard, blood agar, tryptic soy agar (TSA), mannitol salt agar (MSA), Mueller-Hinton agar (MHA), antimicrobial discs penicillin G (10 IU), ampicillin (10 Î¼g), vancomycin (30 Î¼g), gentamicin (10 Î¼g), bacitracin (10 IU), ciprofloxacin (5 Î¼g), and chloramphenicol (30 Î¼g). Equipment used in this study were anaerobic jar, sterile cotton swab, inoculation needle (Ã¶se), forceps, bunsen burner, vortex mixer, 37 <sup>o</sup>C aerobe incubator, sterile cabinet, refrigerator, and ruler.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">Identification of </span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Staphylococcus aureus</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Isolates used in this study were twelve </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolates from the bacterial culture collection of the Laboratory of Bacteriology, Division of Medical Microbiology, School of Veterinary Medicine and Biomedical Sciences, IPB University, Bogor, Indonesia. All </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus </span><span class="font3">were isolated from raw milk of cattle that had mastitis in Bogor, Indonesia. All isolates were re-assessed for species identification using standard bacterial culture procedure (Carter and Cole 1990; FDA 2005). Briefly, tested bacterial cultures were inoculated on blood agar plates and then incubated aerobically for 24 hours at 37 <sup>o</sup>C. Agar plates were examined for colony morphologic features, including colony shape, size, colour, and haemolytic characteristics. Single colonies with macroscopic phenotype suspected as Staphlycoccocci were subcultured on TSA and incubated for 24 hours at 37 <sup>o</sup>C. Further identification was performed using Gram staining and biochemical reactions. Only Gram-positive cocci bacteria arranged in clusters were included for the further biochemical test, consisting of catalase test, glucose &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;fermentation &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;test &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;in</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">microaerophilic &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;condition, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;colony</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">morphology on manitol salt agar, and coagulase test (Carter and Cole 1990; FDA 2005).</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>Antimicrobial Susceptibility Test</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">The antimicrobial resistance test of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> was conducted using the Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion susceptibility test protocol according to Clinic Laboratory Standard Institute (CLSI 2020). Bacterial suspension equal to 0.5 McFarland standard was inoculated on MHA using a sterile </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">cotton swab</span><span class="font3"> over the entire agar surface. Afterwards, seven antimicrobial discs with known concentrations (penicillin G, ampicillin, vancomycin, gentamicin, bacitracin, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;ciprofloxacin, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;and</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">chloramphenicol) were placed on the MHA plate using sterile forceps. Agar plates were incubated aerobically at 37 <sup>o</sup>C for 24 hours. The antimicrobial susceptibility test was</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">performed in triplicates for each isolate. Interpretation of susceptible (S), intermediate (I), dan resistant (R) was conducted based on the size of the inhibition zone according to the breakpoints recommended by </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute</span><span class="font3"> (CLSI 2020).</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>Data Analysis and Interpretation</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">Data were processed using Microsoft Excel 2016. Results were analyzed descriptively and visualised using tables and figures.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>RESULTS AND DISCUSSION</span></h2>
<h2><a name="bookmark18"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark19"></a>Results</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">All twelve isolates were confirmed as </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> based on their macroscopic phenotype on the agar plate, microscopic morphology, and biochemical properties. All isolates formed double zone haemolysis on blood agar plates (Figure 1), were Grampositive cocci in clusters, catalase positive, glucose positive, coagulase positive, and had yellow colonies on MSA (Figure 2).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The antimicrobial susceptibility test was conducted against seven antimicrobials belonging to various antimicrobials classes, namely penicillin G (beta-lactams), ampicillin (beta-lactams), vancomycin (glycopeptides), gentamicin (aminoglycosides</span><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">)</span><span class="font3">, bacitracin (polypeptides), ciprofloxacin (fluoroquinolones), and chloramphenicol (phenicols) (Figure 3).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">The antimicrobial susceptibility test revealed that the tested </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolates had various antimicrobial resistance patterns. Seven isolates (58.3 %) were multidrug-resistant </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3">. Interestingly, one isolate (SA08) was resistant to all tested antimicrobials, while another isolate (SA04) was susceptible to all tested antimicrobials (Table 1). Most isolates were resistant to penicillin G (66.7 %), ampicillin (66.7 %), vancomycin (58.3 %), and bacitracin (58.3 %). Almost all isolates were susceptible to ciprofloxacin (91.7 %), and more than half of the isolates were</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">susceptible to gentamicin (66.7 %), and chloramphenicol (66.7 %) (Figure 4).</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark20"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>Discussion</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">Most of the isolates were resistant to the two tested beta-lactam antimicrobials, namely penicillin G and ampicillin. These results correlate with studies of bovine mastitis in Asia where 77.3 % </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> in China were resistant to penicillin G and ampicillin (Li </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2009), and 66 % </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> in Korea were resistant to penicillin (Nam </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2011). Additionally, 35.4 % and 15.1% of dairy cattle herds in Canada had antimicrobial-resistant </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus </span><span class="font3">against penicillin G and ampicillin, respectively (Saini </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2012). Both antimicrobials were previously commonly used to treat bacterial infections in cattle (Li </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2009). However, the increase of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> resistance against beta-lactams antimicrobials argues against their use in cattle with mastitis caused by </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3">.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Methicillin is one important antimicrobial belonging to the betalactamase antimicrobial class (CLSI 2020). Methicillin-resistant </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> (MRSA) strain is resistant to most beta-lactam antimicrobials, which makes the treatment challenging (Fessler </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2010; Khairullah </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2020). MRSA associated with bovine mastitis was first described in 1975 (Devriese and Hommez, 1975). Currently, MRSA has been reported worldwide (Fessler </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2010; Nam </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2011; Khairullah </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2020). It would be interesting to investigate the antimicrobial susceptibility of the </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolates in this study against methicilin.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">More than half (58.3 %) of the </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus </span><span class="font3">isolates in this study were resistant against vancomycin. Vancomycin resistance was also reported by Sashidaran </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> (2011) in Malaysia and Daka </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> (2012) in Ethopia. In Ethiopia, 23.5 â€“ 70 % of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus </span><span class="font3">isolates from cattle milk were resistant to vancomycin (Daka </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2012). Furthermore, over half (58.3 %) of </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolates in this study were resistant to bacitracin. A study in Algeria reported that 70 % of methicillin susceptible </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolated from milk were resistant to bacitracin (Chaalal </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2016).</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">In this study, most isolates were susceptible to ciprofloxacin, gentamicin, and chloramphenicol. These results correlate with a meta-analysis of bovine-associated </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> in China which reported that 63.8 %, 73.6 %, and 89.7 % </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolates were susceptible to quinolones, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;aminoglycosides, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;and</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">phenicols, respectively (Wang </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., 2022). A study in Korea showed that 88.1 % </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolates were susceptible to gentamicin (Nam </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2011), while another study in Germany reported that 68.7 % MRSA isolates were susceptible to gentamicin (Fessler </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al</span><span class="font3">., &nbsp;2010). In</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">Uganda, Staphylococci isolated from bovine mastitis cases were susceptible to ciprofloxacin and gentamicin (Kateete </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">et al.,</span><span class="font3"> 2013). This result implies that these antimicrobials are still effective in treating bovine mastitis caused by </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3">. However, antimicrobial stewardship should be considered in applying these drugs, especially for chloramphenicol which is a broad-spectrum antimicrobial.</span></p>
<p><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;">CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATION</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>Conclusion</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">This study revealed that multidrugresistant </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolates were isolated from cattle milk in Bogor Regency, Indonesia. These isolates were resistant to penicillin G (66.7 %), ampicillin (66.7 %), vancomycin (58.3 %), and bacitracin (58.3 %). However, most of the isolates were still susceptible to ciprofloxacin (91.7 %), gentamicin (66.7 %), and chloramphenicol (66.67 %). If clinically indicated, treatment of mastitis should consider the antimicrobial susceptibility of the causative agent.</span></p>
<h2><a name="bookmark24"></a><span class="font3" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark25"></a>Recommendation</span></h2>
<p><span class="font3">Further study is necessary to determine antimicrobial-resistant </span><span class="font3" style="font-style:italic;">S. aureus</span><span class="font3"> isolated from more samples and a wider area. Moreover, antimicrobial resistance genes</span></p>
<p><span class="font3">should be determined, especially for multidrug-resistant strains.</span></p>
