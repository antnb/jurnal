<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>Grammatical Analysis of Causal Conjunctions with Reference to Christina Jones’s </span><span class="font2" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Running The Risk</span></h1>
<h2><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>Ni Made Ayu Cynthianingrum</span></h2>

<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Abstrak</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Penelitian ini berjudul “Grammatical Analysis of Causal Conjunctions with reference to Christina Jones’s Running the Risk” bertujuan untuk mengidentifikasi tipe kata penghubung kausal dan mendeskripsikan fungsi dari tipe kata penghubung kausal dalam struktur tata bahasa yaitu frase dan klausa. Sebuah novel yang berjudul Running the Risk digunakan sebagai data dalam penelitian ini. Pengumpulan data menggunakan metode penelitian pustaka dan dianalisis secara kualitatif dan deskriptif. Teori konjungsi oleh Halliday and Hasan (1976) di dalam penelitian ini diaplikasikan dalam menganalisis tipe kata penghubung kausal dan struktur tata bahasa. Hasil dari penelitian ini menunjukkkan bahwa kedua tipe dari kata penghubung kausal yaitu eksternal dan internal terdapat di dalam data, namun hanya beberapa kategori yang dapat ditemukan. Tipe kata penghubung kausal yang ditemukan seperti kausal general dengan kata penghubung so, kausal reversed dengan kata penghubung for dan because, dan kausal kondisional dengan kata penghubung then. Sedangkan struktur tata bahasa yang terdapat di dalam data seperti frase partisipal, frase infinitive, frase kata kerja, klausa dependent, dan klausa relatif.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Kata kunci: konjungsi, kata penghubung kausal, struktur tata bahasa</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Background of the Study</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">Cohesion is a part of system of language that is related to semantic meanings within a text. According to Halliday and Hasan (1976: 6) cohesion is classified into two categories; there are lexical and grammatical cohesions. Lexical cohesion is cohesion that focuses on vocabulary which is divided into two categories, they are reiteration and collocation. Meanwhile, grammatical cohesion is the cohesion</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">that focuses on structural content which is divided into four categories such as reference, substitution, ellipsis, and conjunction. Conjunction is a connector system that connects word, phrase, and clause which contains information of a sentence. According to Halliday and Hasan (1976: 238), conjunction is classified into four categories, such as additive, adversative, temporal, and causal. Causal conjunction is a type of conjunction that is used as a connector</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">between two things; one is cause and other is effect. In a sentence, there is also language unit.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Language unit, especially in its grammatical structure, forms every unit into a complete sentence. According to Halliday and Hasan (1976: &nbsp;7), the</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">grammatical structure has four units, such as sentences, clauses, groups or phrase, and words. This study focuses on the analysis of causal conjunction, especially its types and grammatical structure, such as phrase and clause. Based on thetypes of causal conjunction and grammatical structure, a novel written by Christina Jones (1997) entitled </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Running the Risk</span><span class="font1"> was chosen because this novel implied abundance types of causal conjunction and grammatical structures.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1">1. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;What types of causal conjunctions are used in the </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Running the Risk</span><span class="font1">?</span></p></li>
<li>
<p><span class="font1">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;What are the kinds of grammatical structured units within which the causal</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">conjunctions are functioning in the novel </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Running the Risk</span><span class="font1">?</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>

<li>
<p><span class="font1">2. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;To describe types of causal conjunctions functioning within grammatical structured units in the novel</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Running the Risk</span><span class="font1">.</span></p></li></ul>

<h2><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>4. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Research Method</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">There are four aspects of researchmethod in this study, such as data source, method and technique of collecting data, method and technique of analyzing data, and method and technique of presenting data analysis. These methods are explained, as follows:</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>4.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Data Source</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">The data source of this study was directly taken from Christina Jones’s novel entitled </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Running the Risk</span><span class="font1"> (1997). </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Running the Risk</span><span class="font1"> novel was selected as a data source because this novel implied causal</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">conjunctions and grammatical structured units in the sentences that are related and can be analyzed to solve the problems of this study. It is good to discover the types of causal conjunction and its combination with grammatical structure to understand the hidden meaning of sentences within the novel.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>4.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Method and Technique of Collecting Data</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In this study, the method of collecting data was library research method. The data were collected using note taking technique. The technique is through three steps. First, the data were observed by reading and understanding the story of </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Running the Risk</span><span class="font1"> novel. Second, the data were collected by highlighting any key - themes which are related to this study. Third, the key - themes were written in a note paper based on their types of causal conjunction.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark16"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark17"></a>4.3 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Method and Technique of</span><br><br><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark18"></a>Analyzing Data</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In this study the data were descriptively analyzed usingthe</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">qualitative method. The data of this study was analyzed based upon the theory proposed by Halliday and Hasan (1976). The techniques of analyzing data in this study wereclassifying the components of causal conjunction into two types such as: external and internal based on the theory proposed by Halliday and Hasan (1976). Furthermore, identifying each type of causal conjunction based on the theory proposed by Halliday and Hasan (1976). Moreover, analyzing the combination between types of causal conjunction and grammatical structure based on the theory proposed by Halliday and Hasan (1976).</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">5. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Finding and Discussion</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;">
<li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">5.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Types of Causal Conjunction in </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Running the Risk</span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"> Novel</span></p></li></ul></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">In this analysis, there are examples of external and internal types of causal conjunction.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark19"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark20"></a>5.1.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;External</span><br><br><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark21"></a>5.1.1.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;General</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">‘I saw your tacho in the office </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">so </span><span class="font1">I knew you were back. Did everything go OK?’</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">(Jones, 1997: 46)</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">Conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1"> is classified into external sense of causal conjunction that focuses on the context of what is said before. The type of conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1"> in the sentence above belongs to simple category of general causal. As a type of simple category of general causal, the conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1"> contains two meanings, there are</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">for this reason </span><span class="font1">and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">for this purpose</span><span class="font1">. Based on the sentence above, the first clause </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">I saw your tacho in the office</span><span class="font1"> act as a cause to the whole sentence. It shows a cause that the subject I in this context is </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Georgia</span><span class="font1"> who saw a tacho on </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Rory’s</span><span class="font1"> table at the office. Meanwhile the second clause </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">I knew you were back</span><span class="font1"> act as a result to the whole sentence. It shows a result that </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Georgia</span><span class="font1">knew </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Rory</span><span class="font1"> was back to the office. In the sentence above, the conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1"> shows phenomena that constitute the context of what being said.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark22"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark23"></a>5.1.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Internal</span><br><br><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark24"></a>5.1.2.1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Reversed</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">‘I don’t want to get caught up in this. Once they start scrapping </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">for</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">the high ground we’ll all get dragged in.’</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">(Jones, 1997: 56)</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">for</span><span class="font1"> is classified into internal sense of causal conjunction that focuses on the speech situation. The type of the conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">for</span><span class="font1"> in the sentence above belongs to simple category of reversed causal. As a type of simple category of reversedcausal, the conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">for</span><span class="font1"> contains meaning </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">this is the reason for what was just said.</span><span class="font1"> The conjunction</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">for</span><span class="font1"> in the sentence above gives stress to the information implied in the first and second clauses. The first clause</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">once they start scrapping</span><span class="font1"> acts as a result to the whole sentence. It shows information that the subject</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">they</span><span class="font1"> in this context are </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Alan</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Beth</span><span class="font1"> starts scrapping. Meanwhile, the second clause </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">the high ground we’ll all get dragged in</span><span class="font1"> act as a cause to the whole sentence. It shows information that the high ground is causing </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Alan</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Beth</span><span class="font1"> to start scrapping and the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">we</span><span class="font1"> in the sentence above indicates to </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Georgia </span><span class="font1">and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Rory</span><span class="font1"> who get dragged into the scrapping.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark25"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark26"></a>5.1.2.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conditional</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">‘So you can go home, Georgia. There’s nothing else you can do at the moment. And if one of us has some sleep tonight </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">then </span><span class="font1">we’ll be more help tomorrow.’</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then</span><span class="font1"> is classified into internal sense of causal conjunction that focuses on the speech situation of the context. The conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then</span><span class="font1"> belongs to simple category of conditional causal. As a simple category of conditional causal, the conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then</span><span class="font1"> contains two meanings; they are </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">as a result of this or the reason for what was just said</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">for purpose</span><span class="font1">. The conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then </span><span class="font1">indicates something possibly it can be true or false according to the truth of its substances. The first clause</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">and if one of us has some sleep tonight</span><span class="font1"> acts as a cause to the whole sentence. It shows that if one of them has some sleep for that night. Meanwhile, the second clause </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">we’ll be more help tomorrow</span><span class="font1"> acts as a result to the whole sentence. It shows that they will be more help</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">tomorrow if one of them has a sleep that night.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">5.2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Grammatical Structured Units</span></p></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Functioning &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;of &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Causal</span></p>
<p><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">Conjunctions</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The receptionist, obviously waiting for her coffee break </span><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;">so </span><span class="font1">she could nip outside and have a Malboro, sucked longingly on her biro.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">According to the sentence above, the situation is in a hospital in winter day. There is a receptionist who is waiting for her break time. She wants to go outside her office then drinks and have cigarette to warm her body after working. In the sentence above, there are grammatical structures existing and combiningwith the causal conjunction expressed with conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1">. The grammatical structures in the sentence above are phrase and clause. The phrase in the sentence above is a participle phrase. The participle phrase is mentioned with the phrase </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">waiting for her coffee break.</span><span class="font1"> The phrase is a participle phrase because the phrase contains present participle that adds</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">description in the sentence. In the phrase above, the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">waiting</span><span class="font1"> is present participle and it is a simple verb that ends with </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">ing</span><span class="font1">, the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">for</span><span class="font1"> is modifier, and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">her coffee break </span><span class="font1">is object.</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">The other grammatical structure above is clause.The clause in the sentence above is a dependent clause </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">she could nip outside and have a Malboro.</span><span class="font1"> It is a dependent clause because the clause cannot stand independently. The clause needs adding any extra words to describe about the clause. This clause will have a complete description with additional first clause </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">the receptionist, obviously waiting for her coffee break.</span><span class="font1"> The dependent clause above contains subject and predicate verb, the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">she</span><span class="font1"> is a subject and the word </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">nip</span><span class="font1"> is the predicate verb.</span></p>
<ul style="list-style:none;"><li>
<h2><a name="bookmark27"></a><span class="font1" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark28"></a>6. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Conclusion</span></h2></li></ul>
<p><span class="font1">After analyzing sentences in </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">Running the Risk</span><span class="font1"> novel with implied causal conjunction and grammatical structure to find out types of causal conjunction and its combination with grammatical structures such as phrase</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">and clause within the sentences of data source, it can be concluded that:</span></p>
<p><span class="font1">First, based on the types of causal conjunction, it can be concluded that according to theory proposed by Halliday and Hasan (1976), both external and internal types were found. The external type found in the data source was the simple general category expressed with the conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">so</span><span class="font1">. Besides, the internal type found in the data source was simple reversed category expressed with the conjunctions</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">for</span><span class="font1"> and </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">because</span><span class="font1">, and simple conditional category expressed with the conjunction </span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">then</span><span class="font1">.Second, based on the grammatical structure in the data source, it can be concluded that according to the theory proposed by Halliday and Hasan (1976), two units of grammatical structure such phrase and clauses were found. The phrases found in the data source were participle phrase, infinitive phrase, and verb phrase.</span></p>
