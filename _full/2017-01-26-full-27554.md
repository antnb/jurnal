<h1><a name="bookmark0"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark1"></a>ABSTRACT</span></h1>
<p><span class="font7">Flavonol compounds, quercetin (</span><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">1</span><span class="font7">) and quercetin-3-</span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">O</span><span class="font7">-β-D-glucopyranoside (</span><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">2</span><span class="font7">) have been isolated from the ethyl acetate extract of </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium microphyllum</span><span class="font7"> leaves. The chemical structures of flavonol compounds were identified based on spectroscopic data and by comparison of spectral data obtained previously. The discovery of flavonol compounds in </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium microphyllum</span><span class="font7"> was shown in this study for the first time.</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">Keywords : </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium microphyllum</span><span class="font7">, quercetin, quercetin-3-</span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">O</span><span class="font7">-β-D-glucopyranoside, Lygodiaceae.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark2"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark3"></a>ABSTRAK</span></h1>
<p><span class="font7">Senyawa flavonol, quercetin (</span><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">1</span><span class="font7">) dan quercetin-3-</span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">O</span><span class="font7">-□ -D-glucopiranosida (</span><span class="font7" style="font-weight:bold;">2</span><span class="font7">) telah diisolasi dari ekstrak etil asetat daun </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium microphyllum.</span><span class="font7"> Struktur kimia senyawa flavonol diidentifikasikan berdasarkan data-data spektroskopi dan perbandingan data spektra yang diperoleh sebelumnya. Penemuan senyawa flavonol ini baru dilaporkan pada penelitian ini untuk pertama kali.</span></p>
<p><span class="font7">Kata kunci : </span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium microphyllum</span><span class="font7">, quercetin, quercetin-3-</span><span class="font7" style="font-style:italic;">O</span><span class="font7">-β-D-glucopiranosida, Lygodiaceae.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark4"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark5"></a>INTRODUCTION</span></h1>
<p><span class="font8">Susu A fern has survived since Paleozoic era and can adapt to variety environmental changes (Wallace et al</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">.,</span><span class="font8"> 1991), thus fern contain a lot of useful secondary metabolites, including flavonoids, steroids, alkaloids, phenols, triterpenoids, various kinds of amino acids and fatty acids (Zeng-fu et al</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">.,</span><span class="font8"> 2008). One from thousands of ferns species that have interesting pharmacological benefits is Lygodiaceae family. The genus belong to Lygodiaceae family only </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium</span><span class="font8"> (Guo-gang et al</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">.,</span><span class="font8"> 2012). Generally, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium</span><span class="font8"> genus is a group of ferns that spread and always propagate in other plants. </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium</span><span class="font8"> genus are different from other kinds of ferns because it</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">has roots that crawl on the ground rhizomes and fleshy and can only live in the open because they like the sunlight. Some plants from </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium </span><span class="font8">genus are invasive and has become a problem in a number of forest areas. It's growing fast and lack of predator makes these plants dominate, displacing wildlife, threatens biodiversity, and enhance the human-animal conflict (Zheng and Xing, 2009). One of the invasive species from </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodiu</span><span class="font8">m genus is </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">L. microphyllum</span><span class="font8">. Plants of this genus have a variety of properties that have been widely recognized, thus the utilization of plants from genus is quite expected. Some herbs of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium</span><span class="font8"> genus widely used by people one of them as traditional medicine as hepatitis medicine (Zheng and Xing, 2009), back pain, rheumatism</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">and treatment for kidney stones (Lee et al., 2008), expectorant, scabies, eczema and liver treatment (Upreti et al., 2009), diuretics, antiplasmodial, and treatment for lung and kidney (Upreti et al</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">.,</span><span class="font8"> 2009), laxative, headache and digestive disorders (Zheng and Xing, 2009). Phytochemical studies on the </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium</span><span class="font8"> genus had reported to contain a compound with unique structure and had diverse biological activities such as flavonoids (Zhang et al., 2006), phenolic glycoside (Ye et al., 2007), naphthoquinone (Chen et al., 2010), ecdysteroids (Zhu et al., 2009) and phenylpropanoid glycoside (Duan et al., 2012).</span></p>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">L. microphyllum</span><span class="font8"> based on ethnobotany and ethnopharmacological information provides many benefits in the field of reproductive health as well as simple patterns make the plant is classified as herbs are easily available so that benefit of these plants is required primarily to determine the chemical constituents of this plant. In our ongoing research to find new biologically active compounds from Indonesia </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Lygodium</span><span class="font8"> plants, we isolated and describe steroid compounds from the leaves of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">L. microphyllum</span><span class="font8"> (Kuncoro et al., 2015). In the further search for biologically active dompounds from the polar fraction, the ethyl acetate extract showed flavonoid constituents. In this paper we decribe isolation and structural elucidation of flavonols from the leaves of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">L. microphyllum.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">MATERIAL AND METHODS</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark6"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark7"></a>General</span></h1>
<p><span class="font8">Melting point measured on electrothermal melting point apparatus and not corrected. IR spectra were measured on Perkin-Elmer 1760X spectrophotometer, FT-IR on KBr. Mass spectra recorded with a mass spectrometer Water, Qtof HR-MS XEV<sup>otm</sup>. <sup>1</sup>H and <sup>13</sup>C NMR spectra are obtained by JEOL NMR 500 MHz, JEOL NMR ECZR 600 MHz used TMS as an internal standard. Chromatographic separation is carried out on silica gel 60 (Merck), octa desyl silane (ODS, Fuji silysia). TLC Plate filled with silica gel GF</span><span class="font5">254 </span><span class="font8">(Merck, 0.25 mm) and detection was obtained with the appearance of 10% H</span><span class="font5">2</span><span class="font8">SO</span><span class="font5">4 </span><span class="font8">in ethanol followed by heating and under ultraviolet-visible light at wavelength of 257 and 364 nm.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark8"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark9"></a>Plant Material</span></h1>
<p><span class="font8">The leaves of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">L. microphyllum</span><span class="font8"> was collected from forest areas in Samarinda, East Kalimantan in June 2014. The plant was identified by staff at the Faculty of Forestry, University of Mulawarman, Samarinda and sample specimens stored at the Faculty of Forestry, University of Mulawarman, Samarinda.</span></p>
<h1><a name="bookmark10"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark11"></a>Extraction and Isolation</span></h1>
<p><span class="font8">Dried leaves of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">L. microphyllum</span><span class="font8"> (3.54 kg) was extracted with methanol at room temperature. The methanol extract obtained was concentrated at low pressure to produce a concentrated methanol extract (526 g). The concentrated methanol extract was dissolved in water (4: 1) and partitioned successively with </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font8">-hexane, ethyl acetate, and </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font8">-butanol. Evaporation of the solvents resulted </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font8">-hexane (59 g), ethyl acetate (72 g) and </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font8">-butanol extract (54 g). The ethyl acetate extract (50 g) was separated using column chromatography with silica gel eluted with </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">n</span><span class="font8">-hexanel-ethyl acetatemethanol as a gradient solvent yield 22 fractions (A01-22). Fraction A10-15 were combined (920 mg) and separated using column chromatography on silica gel eluted with ethyl acetate: methanol (7:3) to obtained 22 fractions (B01-22). Fraction B02-05 were combined (420 mg) and further separated using flash column chromatography on silica gel eluted with a gradient ratio of solvent, ethyl acetate-methanol to obtained 20 fractions (C01-22). Fraction C05 (190 mg) was separated using column chromatography on ODS eluted with a gradient solvent ratio methanol-water to obtain compound </span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font8">(12.5 mg) and </span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font8">(5.4 mg) as a yellowish powder.</span></p>

<p><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">Quercetin-3-O-</span><span class="font1" style="font-style:italic;">β</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">-D-glucopyranoside</span><span class="font8"> (</span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">1</span><span class="font8">). <h1><a name="bookmark12"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark13"></a>RESEARCH AND DISCUSSION</span></h1>
<p><span class="font8">The ethyl acetate extract of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">L. microphyllum </span><span class="font8">leaves was fractionated by a classical column chromatography on silica gel and octa desyl silane adsorbens to yield quercetin (</span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">1</span><span class="font8">) and 3-</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">O</span><span class="font8">-</span><span class="font9">β</span><span class="font8">-D-glucopyranoside (</span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">2</span><span class="font8">).</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">Compound </span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font8">was isolated as a yellow amorphous powder. The TOFMS spectrum of </span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font8">gave ion peak at </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">m/z</span><span class="font8"> 303 [M+H]<sup>+</sup>, compatible with the molecular formula C</span><span class="font5">15</span><span class="font8">H</span><span class="font5">10</span><span class="font8">O</span><span class="font5">7</span><span class="font8">. Its UV absorptions in MeOH were consistent with the presence of 3, 5, 7, 3’, 4’-pentahydroxyflavone structure (Wang et al., 2010). In the <sup>1</sup>H NMR spectrum of </span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">1</span><span class="font8">, the aromatic region exhibited an ABX system at </span><span class="font9">δ</span><span class="font5">H </span><span class="font8">7.73 (1H, d, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=2.0 Hz, H-2’), 7.62 (1H, dd, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=2.0 and 7.5 Hz, H-6’) and 6.87 (1H, d, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=8.0 Hz, H-5’) due to a 3’,4’-disubstitution of B ring and a typical </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">meta</span><span class="font8">-coupled pattern for H-6 and H-8 protons </span><span class="font8" style="font-variant:small-caps;">(</span><span class="font2" style="font-variant:small-caps;">6</span><span class="font6" style="font-variant:small-caps;">h</span><span class="font8"> 6.17 and 6.37, d, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=2.0 Hz). The <sup>13</sup>C NMR indicated the presence of 15 atom carbons, the signal at </span><span class="font9">δ</span><span class="font5">C </span><span class="font8">177.3 was attributed to a carbonyl carbon placed at C-4, the other signals were 165.6 (C-7), 162.6 (C-5), 158.4 (C-9), 148.8 (C-4’), 148.1 (C-2), 146.3 (C-3’), 137.3 (C-3), 124.2 (C-1’), 121.8 (C-6’), 116.2 (C-5’), 116.0 (C-2’), 104.6 (C-10), 99.3 (C-6), 94.4 (C-8). The spectral data were compatible with those of quercetin (Fossen et al., 1998; Slimestad et al., 2007).</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">Compound </span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font8">was obtained as yellowish powder, m.p. 266-268 <sup>o</sup>C. The molecular formula was established to be C</span><span class="font5">21</span><span class="font8">H</span><span class="font5">20</span><span class="font8">O</span><span class="font5">12 </span><span class="font8">by TOFMS spectra (</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">m/z</span><span class="font8"> 465 [M+H]<sup>+</sup>) together with NMR spectra (Table 1), thus requiring twelve degrees of unsaturation. The UV spectra of </span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font8">showed </span><span class="font9">λ </span><span class="font8">maxima at 353 nm (band I) and 254 nm (band II) characteristic of the quercetin type (Slimestad et al., 2007). The <sup>1</sup>H NMR spectrum of </span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font8">showed three aromatic protons signals at </span><span class="font9">δ</span><span class="font5">H </span><span class="font8">7.71 </span><span class="font8" style="font-variant:small-caps;">(Ih,</span><span class="font8"> d, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=2.0 Hz, H-2’), 6.87 (1H, d, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=8.4 Hz, H-5’) and 7.58 (1H, dd, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=2.0, 8,4 Hz, H-6’) in the form of an ABX spin-system, suggesting a flavonol with 3’,4’-disubstituted B-ring, a pair of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">meta</span><span class="font8">-coupling proton signals at </span><span class="font9">δ</span><span class="font5">H </span><span class="font8">6.19 </span><span class="font8" style="font-variant:small-caps;">(Ih,</span><span class="font8"> d, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=2.0, H-6) and 6.38 (1H, d, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=2.0 Hz, H-8) for A-ring. It also showed signals for glucose moiety, it showed</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">signal at </span><span class="font9">δ</span><span class="font5">H </span><span class="font8">5.23 </span><span class="font8" style="font-variant:small-caps;">(Ih,</span><span class="font8"> d, </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=7.6 Hz, H-1’’) indicated that compound posses </span><span class="font9">β</span><span class="font8">-linked glucose.</span></p>
<p><span class="font8">The <sup>13</sup>C NMR spectra supported this hypothesis and showed 21 signal carbons including a carbonyl signal at </span><span class="font9">δ</span><span class="font5">C </span><span class="font8">179.5 (C-4), five methine sp<sup>2</sup>, eight sp<sup>2</sup> quartenary carbons and six sp<sup>3 </sup>oxygenated carbons. It revealed chemical shifts at </span><span class="font9">δ</span><span class="font5">C </span><span class="font8">135.6 (C-3), 163.0 (C-5), 165.9 (C-7), 145.9 (C-3’), 149.8 (C-4’) that suggested the 3, 5, 7, 3’, 4’ oxygenated flavone nucleus. It showed significant glucose signals at </span><span class="font9">δ</span><span class="font5">C </span><span class="font8">104.4 (C-1’’), 75.7 (C-2’’), 78.1 (C-3’’), 71.2 (C-4’’), 78.4 (C-5’’) and 62.6 (C-6’’). These functionalities accounted for eight out of the twelve degrees of unsaturation. The remaining four degrees of unsaturation were consistent to a flavonol with a sugar unit (Pereira et al., 2007).</span></p>

<p><span class="font8">The </span><span class="font9">β</span><span class="font8">-configuration for the sugar unit was determined based on the </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8"> value information for the anomeric proton (</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">J</span><span class="font8">=7.6 Hz). A detailed comparison of NMR data of </span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">1 </span><span class="font8">to those 3-</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">O</span><span class="font8">-</span><span class="font9">β</span><span class="font8">-D-glucopyranoside (Beck et al., 1999; Tang et al., 2000; D’Agostino et al., 1990) showed very similarity, thus compound </span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">2 </span><span class="font8">was identified as a quercetin-3-</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">O</span><span class="font8">-</span><span class="font9">β</span><span class="font8">-glucopyranoside and shown in this species for the first time.</span></p>

<h1><a name="bookmark14"></a><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;"><a name="bookmark15"></a>Conclusion</span></h1>
<p><span class="font8">Two flavonol compounds, quercetin (</span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">1</span><span class="font8">) and quercetin-3-</span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">O</span><span class="font8">- □ -glucopyranoside (</span><span class="font8" style="font-weight:bold;">2</span><span class="font8">) were isolated from the leaves of </span><span class="font8" style="font-style:italic;">L. microphyllum</span><span class="font8">. The phytocehmical studies of flavonol compounds from this species was shown for first time reported in this study.</span></p>
